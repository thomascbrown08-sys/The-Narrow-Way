About the Journey

This whole site is laid out as a road — the one John Bunyan imagined in The Pilgrim's Progress. If you have never read it, you have lost nothing; this page is the short catch-up. And if a phrase like wicket gate or slough sends you reaching for a dictionary, that is exactly what the glossary near the bottom is for.

The Pilgrim's Progress is an allegory — a story in which the people and places stand for something else — written by John Bunyan in 1678, much of it composed while he sat in an English jail for preaching without a license. In it, a man called Christian flees the City of Destruction with a great burden on his back and sets out for the Celestial City, meeting friends, enemies, and hard country along the way. It became one of the most widely printed and translated books in the English language, and has scarcely been out of print in the three centuries since.

We have borrowed its shape because it tells the truth about the Christian life: that it is a journey — walked over time, with real obstacles and real rest — and that you are already somewhere on the road. Each landmark on this site is one stop along that road. You do not need to have read Bunyan to walk it. But if his old English ever trips you, the rest of this page is here to help.

About the film. The animated Pilgrim's Progress (2019) was produced by RevelationMedia — it is sometimes confused with Angel Studios' faith films, but it is a separate production. It follows the same road we walk here, and features the actor John Rhys-Davies as Evangelist and the hymn-writer Kristyn Getty as the Interpreter. It runs under two hours and is suitable for families. RevelationMedia has long offered it to watch free — including a short-episode version that breaks the journey into roughly seven-minute stops — and it has also streamed free on services such as Plex and Hoopla, with rental and purchase available from the usual stores. Availability shifts over time, so a quick search for "Pilgrim's Progress RevelationMedia" will point you to the current options.

About the editions. The original is written in seventeenth-century English, which is part of its charm but can be slow going. Plenty of updated, modern-English editions exist, along with illustrated retellings for children and free audiobook recordings (LibriVox among them). Any of them is a fine way in — and the glossary below covers the handful of old words and symbols you will meet whichever you choose.

A Small Glossary of the Road

Old words, and what they stand for

Bunyan wrote in the language of his century and named everything for what it means. Here is a short legend — first the few words that have drifted out of everyday use, then the road's main symbols and what they represent.

Old words worth knowing

Wicket gate
A wicket is a small gate or door — often a person-sized one set into or beside a much larger gate. Bunyan's Wicket Gate is the narrow entrance onto the way; he has in mind Jesus' words about the narrow gate in Matthew 7:13–14.
Slough
A swamp or bog; a patch of deep, wet mud. (In this sense the word rhymes with "now.") The Slough of Despond is the mire of discouragement just outside the City.
Despond
An older form of "despondency" — sinking dejection, the loss of hope.
Delectable
Delightful, deeply pleasing. The Delectable Mountains are the high country with the sweetest views of the journey.
Beulah
A Hebrew word meaning "married," taken from Isaiah 62:4. The Land of Beulah is the restful borderland in sight of the Celestial City.
Apollyon
Greek for "the Destroyer" — the name given in Revelation 9:11 to the angel of the abyss. Bunyan's dragon-like adversary in the Valley of Humiliation.
Vanity Fair
A fair — a market — devoted to "vanities," meaning worldly goods and pleasures. Bunyan coined the phrase; a novelist borrowed it for a famous book some 170 years later.
Celestial
Heavenly. The Celestial City is heaven itself — the pilgrim's destination.

The road's symbols

Christian & his burden
Christian is the traveler — an everyman believer — and the burden on his back is the weight of sin and guilt he carries until it falls away at the Cross.
The City of Destruction
The life he must leave behind: the world as it stands under judgment.
Evangelist
The guide who first points Christian toward the way — the gospel, given a face.
The Wicket Gate
The narrow entrance onto the road; the beginning of faith.
The Interpreter's House
A house of instruction, traditionally understood as the work of the Holy Spirit, who opens the eyes to see.
The Cross & the Sepulchre
The place where the burden rolls away into the empty tomb: the gospel itself.
The roll (or scroll)
Christian's assurance of salvation — given at the Cross, read for comfort, and, on this road, lost and recovered on the Hill.
Palace Beautiful
The church: rest, fellowship, the records of the saints, and the armory of the creeds.
The two valleys
The Valley of Humiliation is spiritual conflict (where Apollyon attacks); the Valley of the Shadow of Death is spiritual darkness and danger, walked through to the morning.
Doubting Castle & Giant Despair
Captivity to doubt and despair — escaped by a key called Promise, which is the promises of God.
The Delectable Mountains
High country near the journey's end, with clear sight and the firmest assurance.
The Enchanted Ground
Air that makes pilgrims drowsy: the pull of spiritual complacency.
The River of Death
Death itself, crossed before the gates of the City.
The Celestial City
Heaven, where the journey ends and the King is.

That is the legend for the map. The road itself begins where every pilgrim's does — at the City of Destruction — and you are welcome to start there, or to pick up at whatever point on the map matches where you actually stand.
