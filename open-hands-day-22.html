Day 22

What Marriage Is Meant to Be

This explains why a man leaves his father and mother and is joined to his wife, and the two are united into one. Ephesians 5:25, 28, 31–32 (NLT) For husbands, this means love your wives, just as Christ loved the church. He gave up his life for her...

In the same way, husbands ought to love their wives as they love their own bodies...

As the Scriptures say, 'A man leaves his father and mother and is joined to his wife, and the two are united into one.' This is a great mystery, but it is an illustration of the way Christ and the church are one. Genesis 2:24 (NLT)

We have been studying faithfulness in relationships — friendship, chastity, discernment, becoming the right kind of person. Today we look at the destination: what is marriage actually for?

Genesis 2:24 is remarkable because of when it occurs: before the Fall. Marriage was designed into creation itself, not invented as a remedy for loneliness or as a social institution. It predates brokenness. "The two are united into one" — one flesh, one life, a new entity that neither person was before.

The instruction Paul gives to husbands in Ephesians 5 is not primarily about authority — it is about sacrifice. "Love your wives as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her." This is the highest and most demanding standard imaginable: the model is not a respectable man who provides for his family, but a God who died for people who had turned away from him. Self-giving, not self-serving, is the orientation.

Paul calls this a "great mystery" — the joining of two lives into one is a picture of something larger: Christ's union with his people. We study this not to feel the weight of an impossibly high standard, but to understand the vision. This is what we're aiming at, preparing for, and growing toward. And it is good.
