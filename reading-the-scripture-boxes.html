How This Site Argues

Reading the Scripture Boxes

Throughout the teaching pages you will find collapsible boxes headed 📖 The Scripture behind this. They exist for one reason: so that nothing here has to be taken on the charm of a story or the say-so of the author. Open one, and you will see the doctrinal claim stated plainly, the handful of passages it rests on, and the reasoning that connects them — so you can check the ground yourself.

Why the boxes exist

This site makes claims about God, sin, the cross, the church, and the end of all things. Those are not matters of taste, and they should not be believed simply because they are told well. The boxes are an open invitation to test the teaching against Scripture — the same posture the Bereans are commended for, who ‘examined the Scriptures daily to see if these things were so’ (Acts 17:11).

There is a second purpose, quieter but just as important. Error rarely enters the church as an obvious lie. It usually slips in when one generation’s interpretation hardens into the next generation’s unquestioned assumption, and the drift compounds. Forcing every significant claim to show its working — and to say honestly what kind of working it is — is a guard against that slow slide. A claim that can only lean on tradition is labeled as such, so it can never quietly pass itself off as the plain word of God.

Each box is collapsed by default. The prose is meant to carry you; the warrant waits underneath for whenever you want it. You never have to open one — but it is always there if you do.

The six labels

Every box wears one of six labels, telling you at a glance what sort of ground the claim stands on. They run, roughly, from the firmest footing to the most openly held-with-an-open-hand. The distinctions matter: a doctrine the Bible states is on different ground than one the church has inferred, and both differ from a question on which faithful Christians still disagree. Naming the difference is not weakness; it is honesty.

Direct

The passage (or passages) state or plainly entail the claim, with little inferential distance. You do not have to combine texts cleverly to reach it — the words carry it in their plain sense. Example: that we are saved by grace, not by works.

Entailed

The claim follows by “good and necessary consequence” — it is not stated in a single verse, but follows rigorously when Scripture is compared with Scripture. The inferential work is shown rather than hidden. Example: that the church exists as both a visible and an invisible body.

Direct / Entailed

Reserved for a few cornerstone passages on which so much doctrine, history, and hope rest that the case for each reading is set out side by side — what Scripture states directly, and what is reached by synthesis. Example: the atonement; the Trinity; the two natures of Christ.

Interpretation

The claim rests substantially on how the church has read Scripture — a tradition’s formulation or a pastoral framework — rather than on a direct chain from the text. It is flagged openly, with the source named, so it is never mistaken for a bare reading of the Bible. Example: the three classic “marks of a true church.”

Contested

A genuine, in-bounds disagreement among orthodox Christians who love the same Lord and read the same Scripture. Here the site states its own position and shows the strongest case on both sides — mapping the terrain rather than handing down a verdict. Example: the Calvinist and Arminian accounts of grace.

Historical

The warrant rests on evidence outside the text — manuscripts, archaeology, the historian’s reasoning — not on Scripture itself. Scripture may warrant the posture (that this is testimony meant to be checked), but the specific conclusions stand on historical scholarship, and the label says so. Example: the manuscript evidence for the New Testament’s reliability.

How to use them well

Read the page first; the box is there if a claim makes you ask ‘says who?’ Notice the label before the verses — it tells you how much weight the claim is asking you to put on it. Look up the passages in your own Bible; the references are exact. And where a box says Contested or Interpretation, take it in that spirit — held firmly, perhaps, but with room for a brother to see it differently. The aim of the whole system is not to win an argument but to let you see the ground clearly enough to stand on it yourself.

A note on translations: the teaching pages quote the ESV unless otherwise marked; the devotional readings more often use the NLT for warmth. Unfamiliar terms in the boxes — entailed, imputation, propitiation, monergism — are gathered and defined in the Glossary.
