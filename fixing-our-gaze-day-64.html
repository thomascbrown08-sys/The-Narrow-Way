Day 64

The Betrayal

Opening Prayer

Lord, you walked toward the torches. You did not hide. You asked who they were looking for and, when they named you, you told them plainly who you were — and they fell to the ground. Even in the moment of your arrest, you were the one with authority in the garden. Nothing was happening to you that was not, at the same time, being chosen by you. Let that sovereignty and that surrender hold together in us today. Amen.

"Then Jesus, knowing all that would happen to him, came forward and said to them, 'Whom do you seek?' They answered him, 'Jesus of Nazareth.' Jesus said to them, 'I am he.' Judas, who betrayed him, was standing with them. When Jesus said to them, 'I am he,' they drew back and fell to the ground." John 18:4–6, ESV

The torches. The soldiers. The officers from the chief priests and Pharisees. Judas at the front, the man who knows the garden and knows the face. And Jesus does not wait for them to find him in the dark. He walks out to meet them.

John's detail — knowing all that would happen to him, came forward — is the whole theology of the arrest in a single phrase. He is not overtaken by events. He has been telling his disciples for months what was coming (Matthew 16:21; 17:22–23; 20:18–19). He has prayed in this garden and resolved in this garden what had to be resolved. And now he walks out of the darkness to the torches, not as a man who has run out of options, but as the good shepherd who lays down his life of his own accord (John 10:18).

"Whom do you seek?" The question is not a request for information. He knows whom they seek. He asks because he intends to present himself — to step into the space between his disciples and the armed cohort and take the arrest for himself. And when they say "Jesus of Nazareth," his answer is the divine name we encountered on Day 8: egō eimi — I am he. Or simply: I AM.

And they fell to the ground. A cohort of Roman soldiers — perhaps six hundred men — and the officers of the temple guard, all drawn back and falling at the sound of two words from an unarmed man. There is no natural explanation for this. The text does not offer one. It records what happened: the I AM spoke his name and an armed company fell. The power that was about to be handed over demonstrated, for one moment, what it was. He was not arrested because he could not resist. He was arrested because he chose not to.

Then he does the thing that makes this arrest completely unlike any other arrest in history: he asks again. "Whom do you seek?" And when they say his name again, he answers again — and this time adds: "If you seek me, let these men go" (18:8). He is not merely surrendering. He is negotiating the terms of the surrender to protect his own. John notes that this was to fulfill his earlier word that he would lose none of those the Father had given him (John 17:12; 18:9). Even in the act of being arrested, he is keeping the sheep. Even now he is standing between the wolf and the flock.

Judas. His name appears once in this passage — standing with them. He has led the arrest party here. He will kiss Jesus and hand him over (Matthew 26:49). Jesus had washed his feet a few hours earlier. Jesus had broken bread with him and handed it to him (John 13:26–27). He had watched Judas leave the upper room and said, quietly, "What you are going to do, do quickly" (John 13:27). He knew. He fed him anyway. He loved him, in the way that love is most costly — when it knows what the beloved is doing and does not stop loving while refusing to stop the consequence.

The garden is over. He allows himself to be bound. The one who commands unclean spirits with a word, who stilled the storm with a sentence, who raised Lazarus with a shout — allows his hands to be tied. And he walks toward Jerusalem as the Lamb who has been led there by his own willing feet.

For reflection

  • "Knowing all that would happen to him, came forward." He walked into his own arrest. What does the voluntary nature of his surrender — the fact that nothing was happening to him that was not also being chosen by him — mean for how you understand the cross? Does it change what it costs you to receive what he gave?
  • They fell at his words — a demonstration that the arrest was proceeding by his permission, not despite his power. And then he let it continue. What does this restraint — choosing not to use the power he clearly had — tell you about what love looked like at that moment?
  • "Let these men go" — even in being arrested, he was protecting his own. Is there a situation in your life where you have felt unprotected, exposed, handed over to something difficult? What does it mean that the one who went to the cross said "let these men go" — that his going was in part so that his people need not?

Going Deeper (optional)

The detail that the soldiers and officers "drew back and fell to the ground" when Jesus said "I am he" (John 18:6) is unique to John's Gospel and has been variously interpreted. Some read it as a purely natural phenomenon — people stumbling in surprise or being pushed back by the crowd. Others read it as a supernatural manifestation of divine power breaking briefly through, consistent with the pattern in John's Gospel of divine glory being disclosed at strategic moments (the wedding at Cana, the Transfiguration, the raising of Lazarus). Given John's theological deliberateness — his repeated use of the egō eimi formula with its divine-name resonances — the latter reading is more consistent with his narrative intent.

What John is presenting is not a contradiction between Christ's power and his arrest but a deliberate, visible demonstration of their coexistence: he has the power not to be arrested; he chooses to be arrested anyway. This is the theological heart of what Philippians 2:8 describes as obedience "unto death" — not the obedience of someone who had no other option, but the obedience of the one who had every option and chose the costly one. The Reformed tradition has always insisted on this voluntary dimension of the atonement: Christ was not a victim of circumstances, not overwhelmed by forces beyond his control, not sacrificed by a God who needed a sufficient victim. He laid down his life. No one took it. And the falling soldiers in the garden are John's way of making sure we understand that clearly before the passion proceeds.
