Day 10

Light vs. Voice: How the Spirit Guides

Now we have received not the spirit of the world, but the Spirit who is from God, that we might understand the things freely given us by God. And we impart this in words not taught by human wisdom but taught by the Spirit, interpreting spiritual truths to those who are spiritual. 1 Corinthians 2:12–13 (ESV)
You gave your good Spirit to instruct them and did not withhold your manna from their mouth and gave them water for their thirst. Nehemiah 9:20 (ESV)

The Reformed tradition makes an important distinction that is worth understanding clearly: the difference between the Spirit illuminating Scripture and the Spirit revealing new content.

Illumination is the Spirit's work of helping believers understand what is already written. When you read a passage and it suddenly opens up — when you see what it means in a way you hadn't before, when it lands with weight and clarity — that is the Spirit at work. This is the Spirit's ordinary, promised ministry to every believer. It is not spectacular, but it is real and profound.

Revelation is the communication of new propositional content — new information about God or his will that goes beyond what Scripture already contains. Reformed theology holds that this kind of revelation closed with the apostolic age. The canon is complete.

This distinction matters practically. When you feel a strong impression about a decision, or a sense of peace about a direction, or a deep conviction that you should do something — that experience is real. But the question to ask is: is this the Spirit helping me understand and apply what Scripture already teaches? Or am I treating it as new revelation that stands alongside or above Scripture? The first is expected and healthy. The second is where errors begin.

Put It Into Practice

The next time you have a strong inner impression about a decision, pause and ask: can I connect this to something Scripture already teaches? Is this illumination of something already revealed, or am I treating it as new revelation?

For reflection

Have you ever had an experience you would describe as the Spirit guiding you? How did you recognize it as such? What's the practical difference between treating an inner impression as illumination versus revelation? Does that distinction change anything about how you would act? What dangers arise when believers routinely treat their impressions as direct revelation from God?
