Day 9

The Spirit Is Not Retired

When the Spirit of truth comes, he will guide you into all the truth, for he will not speak on his own authority, but whatever he hears he will speak, and he will declare to you the things that are to come. He will glorify me, for he will take what is mine and declare it to you. John 16:13–15 (ESV)
Likewise the Spirit helps us in our weakness. For we do not know what to pray for as we ought, but the Spirit himself intercedes for us with groanings too deep for words. And he who searches hearts knows what is the mind of the Spirit, because the Spirit intercedes for the saints according to the will of God. Romans 8:26–27 (ESV)

Before we think carefully about the Spirit's gifts and their boundaries, we need to establish something clearly: the Holy Spirit is not retired. He is not a past-tense reality. He is alive, active, and powerfully at work in the lives of believers right now.

Jesus promises that the Spirit will guide believers into all truth. Paul describes the Spirit interceding for us with a depth of prayer we cannot even articulate ourselves. These are not descriptions of someone who has stepped back from active involvement. The Spirit is present and working.

This matters for discernment because it rules out a lazy cessationism — the attitude that treats all claimed spiritual experience as suspect simply because we're not living in the book of Acts. That posture mistakes caution for wisdom. The Spirit is present. The question is not whether the Spirit is active, but how he works and what criteria we apply to claimed experiences of him.

Jesus' description in John 16 is itself a discernment tool: the Spirit glorifies Christ. He takes what belongs to Christ and makes it known. Any claimed spiritual experience that shifts focus away from Jesus — toward the experience itself, toward the experiencer, toward a leader or movement — fails this most basic test.

Put It Into Practice

Spend five minutes before reading Scripture today in quiet prayer, asking the Spirit to illuminate what you read. Not seeking a new message — simply asking for help understanding the text already given. Notice what happens.

For reflection

What's your current working picture of how the Holy Spirit operates in your everyday life? Where did that picture come from? Jesus says the Spirit glorifies him. What does it look like when claimed spiritual experience glorifies Christ? What does it look like when it doesn't? What's the difference between a cessationist posture and healthy caution about spiritual experience?
