Mount Caution

Mount Caution

The peak where the shepherds show the pilgrims those who strayed — apostasy, presumption, and the perseverance of the saints, walked between two ditches.

"My sheep hear my voice, and I know them, and they follow me... and no one will snatch them out of my hand." John 10:27–28 (ESV)

Among the heights, the shepherds lead the pilgrims to a hill called Caution and bid them look. In the distance, among the tombs at the foot of the mountains, men are stumbling about blindly — once pilgrims like Christian and Hopeful, who strayed from the way, were taken by Giant Despair, blinded, and left to wander among the graves. It is a hard sight, and the shepherds do not soften it. They show it precisely so the pilgrims will take warning. This is the sober peak of the high country: the question of whether a true pilgrim can be finally lost, and what to do with the Scripture that warns of it.

It must be walked carefully, because the warning is easily misread in two opposite and equally damaging directions — and a great deal of needless wreckage, in real lives, comes from missing the narrow path between them.

The two ditchesPresumption on one side, despair on the other

On one side runs the ditch of presumption — treating grace as a license, assuming that because one prayed a prayer or walked an aisle long ago, nothing now matters, however one lives. The warnings of Mount Caution are aimed straight at that complacency. They are real warnings, given to real people, and the New Testament does not deliver them with a wink. Take care, brothers, lest there be in any of you an evil, unbelieving heart, leading you to fall away from the living God. The person who has grown casual about sin, who treats the warnings as if they could not possibly apply to them, is standing nearer the edge than they think.

On the other side runs the ditch of despair — the tender, anxious believer who reads those same warnings, looks at their own coldness and failure, and concludes they must already be among the wandering blind. To that soul the shepherds say something entirely different, and it is the point most often missed: the very fear of having fallen is itself strong evidence that you have not. The truly hardened do not lie awake grieved by their hardness. The presence of the grief is the presence of the life. The warnings were never meant to drive a struggling pilgrim to despair — they were meant to keep a casual one awake.

In plain terms

The Bible's warnings about falling away are serious and real — aimed at anyone who's grown careless. But they are given to keep us walking, not to leave the anxious believer forever terrified that they're already lost.

If you're afraid you've drifted too far, that fear is usually the evidence you haven't. The hardened heart doesn't worry about being hardened.

How the warnings workThe means God uses to keep his own

How can both be true — that genuine believers are kept to the end, and that the warnings against falling are urgent and real? The Reformed answer is that the warnings are not in tension with God's keeping; they are part of how he keeps. God preserves his people not by making their effort irrelevant, but precisely through means — through Scripture's warnings, through the stirring of a troubled conscience, through the fellowship that calls a drifting soul back. The sheep are kept because the Shepherd keeps them; and one of the instruments in his hand is the very warning that makes a true sheep tremble and turn around. To heed the warning is not to doubt your security. It is to experience the security working — for a heart that responds to the warning is a heart still alive to its Shepherd's voice.

This is why the doctrine the Reformed tradition calls the perseverance of the saints has a quiet steel in it: those who are genuinely Christ's may fall, may sin grievously, may pass through seasons of severe doubt — but they cannot finally fall away, because the thing that holds them is not the strength of their grip on Christ but the strength of his grip on them. I give them eternal life, and they will never perish, and no one will snatch them out of my hand. The pilgrims among the tombs are a warning, not a forecast; the path runs safely past them, and it is walked by staying close to the One who holds the sheep.

The warnings are part of how God keeps his people — and a heart that trembles at them is a heart still alive to its Shepherd's voice.

A fair wordWhere sincere Christians differ

Honesty requires one more thing on this peak. Not every faithful Christian reads the warning passages the same way. This site stands, openly, in the Reformed tradition, and so reads them as the means by which God preserves a people who cannot finally be lost. But there is a long and serious tradition — the Arminian and Wesleyan stream — that reads the same passages as describing a genuine possibility of apostasy, holding that a true believer can, through persistent and willful unbelief, fall from grace. That is not a careless reading or a denial of the gospel; it is a different, careful conclusion drawn from the same texts by people who love the same Lord, and it is taken up fairly in the annex on Calvinism and Arminianism just off these heights. What both traditions hold in common is larger than where they differ: that no one is kept by their own strength, that assurance rests finally on Christ, and that the warnings are to be heeded rather than explained away.

So the shepherds bring the pilgrims back from the edge — not to leave them frightened, but to leave them watchful, and to send them on toward the One who keeps his sheep. The road does not stay on the heights. Just below lies the most deceptively pleasant country of the whole journey, where the danger is not a cliff at all, but an air that makes a weary pilgrim want to lie down and sleep.
