Day 2

Two Ways to Get It Wrong

Judge not, that you be not judged. For with the judgment you pronounce you will be judged, and with the measure you use it will be measured to you. Matthew 7:1–2 (ESV)
I know your works, your toil and your patient endurance, and how you cannot bear with those who are evil, but have tested those who call themselves apostles and are not, and found them to be false. I know you are enduring patiently and bearing up for my name's sake, and you have not grown weary. But I have this against you, that you have abandoned the love you had at first. Revelation 2:2–4 (ESV)

There are two easy ways to fail at discernment — and both feel virtuous from the inside.

The first failure is being undiscerning: accepting everything that sounds spiritual, agreeing with whoever spoke last, and treating any questioning of a teacher as uncharitableness. This person wants to be loving, but mistakes credulity for love. The church in Thyatira (Revelation 2:18–20) fell into this — tolerating a false prophet because correcting her felt unkind.

The second failure is being hyper-suspicious: treating every new idea as a threat, every unfamiliar practice as compromise, and every question as evidence of weak faith. The church in Ephesus (Revelation 2:2–4) actually did the right thing — they tested false apostles and found them out. But they lost their love in the process. Their discernment became a way to keep people at arm's length rather than a way to protect them.

Notice what this means: you can be doctrinally correct and spiritually cold. Orthodoxy without love is a kind of failure too. The goal is a discernment rooted in love for God and care for people — one that is rigorous because it takes truth seriously, and warm because it takes people seriously.

Put It Into Practice

Think of a time you fell into one of these two ditches — either too accepting or too suspicious. What was the cost? What would healthy discernment have looked like in that situation?

For reflection

Which failure mode do you think is more common in your church or community — too much credulity or too much suspicion? Which tempts you personally more? Why do you think that is? How does love function as a motive for discernment rather than an obstacle to it?
