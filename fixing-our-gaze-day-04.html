Day 4

Firstborn Over All Creation

Opening Prayer

Sovereign Lord, we confess that we have made Christ too small. We have domesticated him, reduced him to a life coach or a moral example or a private comfort. Open our eyes today to the Christ of Colossians — the one before whom every power in the cosmos must bow. Restore to us the sheer largeness of your Son. And in that largeness, let us find not terror but rest. Amen.

"He is the image of the invisible God, the firstborn of all creation. For by him all things were created, in heaven and on earth, visible and invisible, whether thrones or dominions or rulers or authorities — all things were created through him and for him. And he is before all things, and in him all things hold together." Colossians 1:15–17, ESV

There are passages in the New Testament that read like poetry because they are poetry — ancient hymns of worship that the early church sang before the ink was dry on the rest of Scripture. Colossians 1:15–20 is one of these. Paul may be quoting a baptismal hymn already circulating among the churches; more likely, the Spirit is moving him into elevated, measured prose to speak of a Subject who cannot be adequately addressed in ordinary language. Either way, what we have is not a dry Christological thesis — it is an act of worship.

And it begins with a title that must be understood carefully: the firstborn of all creation. This phrase has confused people for two thousand years, and caused real damage when misread. Does "firstborn" mean Christ was the first to be created — the earliest of God's creatures, as Arius claimed? The answer, resoundingly, is no — and Paul makes it self-defeating for anyone who reads past the first clause. He immediately explains: "For by him all things were created." One cannot be part of "all things" while simultaneously being the one through whom all things were made. The logic is sealed. He stands outside the created order as its Author.

"Firstborn" (prōtotokos) is a title of sovereignty and supremacy, not of sequence. Psalm 89:27 makes this clear: God calls David "my firstborn, the highest of the kings of the earth" — not because David was chronologically the first king, but because he holds the position of preeminent heir. Christ is the firstborn over creation, not within it. He is supreme above all that exists, the heir of the cosmos, the one to whom everything ultimately belongs.

The sweep of what follows is staggering. All things in heaven and on earth — visible and invisible — were created through him and for him. That second prepositional phrase deserves to stop us completely. Not merely through him, as if he were a neutral instrument the Father employed. For him. Christ is not only creation's agent; he is creation's purpose. The universe was made to be his. Every mountain and galaxy, every soul and civilization, every creature that has drawn breath since the first morning — all of it was made for Jesus. N.T. Wright observes that in Paul's vision, history is not a collection of random events drifting toward entropy, but a story with a name at its center and a destination already determined.

And then: "In him all things hold together." This is not past tense — it is present and continuous. Right now, at this moment, the coherence of all reality is a function of Christ's active, ongoing sustaining. The laws of physics are not self-sustaining abstractions; they are expressions of the One who holds everything in his word. Every time a bond between two atoms remains intact, it is because Christ is still speaking. The universe does not maintain itself. It is maintained — by him, moment by moment, without ceasing.

This vision of Christ is almost too large for the mind to hold. And perhaps that is precisely the point. The Colossians were being tempted by a philosophy that placed Christ as one spiritual power among many — a rung on a cosmic ladder, significant but not supreme. Paul's response is not to argue; it is to behold. He sets before them a portrait of Christ so vast that no other figure can stand in the same frame. Before all things. Through all things. Sustaining all things. Heir of all things.

The practical question that presses through all of this is one that C.S. Lewis often circled back to: Is Jesus the kind of thing a person can be truly satisfied with, or is he merely one good thing among others? Colossians says: he is not one good thing among others. He is the One for whom all other good things were made. To receive him is not to add something to a life already complete; it is to find the One in whom the whole of life finally coheres.

For reflection

  • Paul says all things were created "for him" — Christ is not only creation's agent but its purpose and goal. What does it mean for your daily life to be oriented around Christ as its telos rather than treating him as one resource among many?
  • "In him all things hold together" — this is happening right now, without interruption. Where in your life do you most need this cohering, sustaining power? How might knowing this change the way you approach that area?
  • The Colossians were reducing Christ to one spiritual power among others. In your own experience, what has tempted you to make Christ smaller than he is? What does this passage push back against in you?

Going Deeper (optional)

The phrase prōtotokos pasēs ktiseōs — "firstborn of all creation" — was the precise flashpoint of the Arian controversy in the fourth century. Arius read it as establishing Christ's creaturely status: the first and greatest being to emerge from God's creative act. The Nicene theologians, led by Athanasius, recognized that this reading collapses under its own logic: if all things were made through Christ, then Christ cannot be included in "all things." Paul's grammar itself forecloses the Arian reading.

But beyond the historical controversy, something practically enormous is at stake. If Christ is merely within creation — its most exalted creature — then his lordship has a ceiling. There are things, forces, and powers above him. His authority is derived and limited. But if he is the one outside and above creation, through whom it was made and in whom it is sustained, then his lordship has no ceiling. Bringing every corner of your life under his authority is not a constraint — it is return. It is giving each thing back to the One for whom it was made in the first place. The Puritan Richard Baxter put it this way: "Christ and his benefits are sufficient for you; receive him and you shall want for nothing that is truly good." That sufficiency rests entirely on his supremacy. A merely great Jesus cannot be enough. The cosmic Christ of Colossians can.
