The Athanasian Creed

The Athanasian Creed

The full panoply forged in the Athanasian Creed — the Trinity and the incarnation stated with relentless, complete precision.

"...contend for the faith that was once for all delivered to the saints." Jude 3 (ESV)

The last weapon in the armory is the full panoply — the complete suit of armor, every piece present and fitted together, nothing left off. The Athanasian Creed (named for the great defender of Nicaea, though written after his death) does not tell a story or soothe; it drills. It states the Trinity and the incarnation with such unbroken, relentless precision that it leaves no crack anywhere for error to slip through. It is meant to be confessed slowly, almost like a drumbeat.

The Athanasian Creed · Quicunque vult, c. 5th–6th century

Whosoever will be saved, before all things it is necessary that he hold the catholic faith. Which faith, except every one do keep whole and undefiled, without doubt he shall perish everlastingly.

And the catholic faith is this: That we worship one God in Trinity, and Trinity in Unity; neither confounding the persons, nor dividing the substance. For there is one person of the Father, another of the Son, and another of the Holy Spirit. But the Godhead of the Father, of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit, is all one: the glory equal, the majesty coeternal.

Such as the Father is, such is the Son, and such is the Holy Spirit. The Father uncreated, the Son uncreated, and the Holy Spirit uncreated. The Father incomprehensible, the Son incomprehensible, and the Holy Spirit incomprehensible. The Father eternal, the Son eternal, and the Holy Spirit eternal. And yet they are not three eternals but one eternal. As also there are not three uncreated, nor three incomprehensibles, but one uncreated and one incomprehensible. So likewise the Father is almighty, the Son almighty, and the Holy Spirit almighty; and yet they are not three almighties, but one almighty.

So the Father is God, the Son is God, and the Holy Spirit is God; and yet they are not three Gods, but one God. So likewise the Father is Lord, the Son Lord, and the Holy Spirit Lord; and yet not three Lords, but one Lord. For like as we are compelled by the Christian verity to acknowledge every person by himself to be God and Lord, so are we forbidden by the catholic religion to say: there are three Gods or three Lords.

The Father is made of none, neither created nor begotten. The Son is of the Father alone; not made nor created, but begotten. The Holy Spirit is of the Father and of the Son; neither made, nor created, nor begotten, but proceeding. So there is one Father, not three Fathers; one Son, not three Sons; one Holy Spirit, not three Holy Spirits. And in this Trinity none is afore, or after another; none is greater, or less than another; but the whole three persons are coeternal together and coequal. So that in all things, as aforesaid, the Unity in Trinity and the Trinity in Unity is to be worshipped. He therefore that will be saved must thus think of the Trinity.

Furthermore it is necessary to everlasting salvation that he also believe faithfully the incarnation of our Lord Jesus Christ. For the right faith is that we believe and confess that our Lord Jesus Christ, the Son of God, is God and man; God, of the substance of the Father, begotten before the worlds; and man of the substance of his mother, born in the world … Who, although he is God and man, yet he is not two, but one Christ; one, not by conversion of the Godhead into flesh, but by taking of the manhood into God; one altogether; not by confusion of substance, but by unity of person. For as the reasonable soul and flesh is one man, so God and man is one Christ;

who suffered for our salvation, descended into hell, rose again the third day from the dead; ascended into heaven, sits at the right hand of the Father, God Almighty; from whence he shall come to judge the living and the dead. At whose coming all men shall rise again with their bodies; and shall give account of their own works. And they that have done good shall go into life everlasting; and they that have done evil into everlasting fire.

This is the catholic faith, which except a man believe faithfully and firmly, he cannot be saved.

What it guardsThe whole, with nothing loose

Where the Nicene sword has one decisive edge and the Chalcedonian breastplate guards one center, this creed armors everything at once. It walks the narrow ridge of the Trinity with exacting care — worshiping one God in three persons, "neither confounding the persons, nor dividing the substance" — refusing the ditch on either side: the error that melts the three persons into one (so the Father just becomes the Son in a different mode), and the error that splits the one God into three. One being; three persons; coequal, coeternal. Then it turns to the incarnation and does the same, holding the two natures of Christ together with the same unbending exactness. It is the armor for a pilgrim who wants no soft spot left anywhere in his confession.

In plain terms

This is the thorough one. Where the others guard a single truth, the Athanasian Creed spells out the Trinity and the person of Christ in full, closing every loophole — which is why it reads more like a precise legal document than a song.

The hard linesThe "damnatory clauses"

Two lines trouble many readers and should be faced honestly rather than skipped: the opening and closing warnings that whoever does not hold this faith "shall perish everlastingly." Several things matter here. First, the faith being made necessary is not an advanced theology exam — it is the gospel's own center, the Trinity and the incarnation, the very identity of the God who saves; to deny these is to deny the gospel itself. Second, the clauses describe what the catholic faith is; they are not a license to pronounce sentence on particular people, which Scripture reserves to God alone. And third, for the tender soul who fears they cannot yet grasp every line: this creed, like the others, was written for people only beginning in the faith. It describes the truth that saving faith lays hold of — it does not add intellectual mastery as a new requirement to be earned. The understanding is meant to grow inside the confessing, not to be finished before it.

One more line"They that have done good... they that have done evil"

The creed says that at the last day "they that have done good shall go into life everlasting; and they that have done evil into everlasting fire." This is the language of Jesus himself, who said nearly the same. It is not a quiet contradiction of grace, smuggling salvation-by-works in at the end. Scripture everywhere teaches that we are justified by faith alone — and that a living faith always bears fruit, so that the final judgment can point to that fruit as the visible evidence of who belongs to Christ. The "good" in view is not a wage that earns heaven; it is the harvest of a faith already united to the Savior. Judgment according to works is not the same as salvation on the basis of them.

Where the others guard one truth each, this one armors the whole — and leaves no soft spot anywhere in the confession.

With this, the armory is complete. The pilgrim has been fitted with the shield he was baptized into, the sword of the Son's full deity, the breastplate of the two natures, and the whole panoply that leaves no part of the confession exposed. These are not museum pieces behind glass; they are working armor, issued because a fight is coming — and they are exactly what lets a pilgrim recognize a counterfeit when he meets one on the road ahead, in the high country, on the slopes of Mount Error.
