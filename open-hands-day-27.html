Day 27

When a Relationship Is the Wound

It is not an enemy who taunts me — I could bear that. It is not my foes who so arrogantly insult me — I could have hidden from them. Instead, it is you — my equal, my companion and close friend. What good fellowship we once enjoyed as we walked together to the house of God. Ephesians 4:31–32 (NLT) Get rid of all bitterness, rage, anger, harsh words, and slander, as well as all types of evil behavior. Instead, be kind to each other, tenderhearted, forgiving one another, just as God through Christ has forgiven you. Colossians 3:13 (NLT) Make allowance for each other's faults, and forgive anyone who offends you. Remember, the Lord forgave you, so you must forgive others. Psalm 55:12–14 (NLT)

David wrote Psalm 55 about a close friend who had turned against him. The raw honesty of those three verses is striking: "it is not an enemy who taunts me — I could bear that." Betrayal from an enemy is painful. Betrayal from a trusted companion is something else entirely. The person knew you. The person was inside your trust. That specificity is its own category of hurt.

Ephesians 4:31 gives us an inventory of what we do when we hold onto relational wounds: bitterness, rage, anger, harsh words, slander. These are not individual isolated sins — they are connected. Bitterness is the root; the others are what it produces over time. If you have ever felt the exhaustion of carrying something against someone for a long time, Ephesians 4:31 is naming what that costs you.

Verse 32 doesn't minimize what happened. It pivots to the one motivation that is actually strong enough to override the natural human response to wound: "just as God through Christ has forgiven you." The standard is not "forgive because it will make you feel better" (though it often does eventually). The standard is: you have been forgiven something immeasurable. That reality is meant to change how you hold what others have done to you.

Colossians 3:13 adds one more word worth sitting with: "make allowance for each other's faults." The word implies a kind of deliberate spaciousness — choosing to create room for the reality that people will fail you, and deciding in advance not to be destroyed by it.

For reflection

Looking at the list in Ephesians 4:31 — bitterness, rage, anger, harsh words, slander — which of these do you most recognize in yourself when someone has hurt you?
