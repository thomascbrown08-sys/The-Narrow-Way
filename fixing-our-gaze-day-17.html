Day 17

Simeon's Song

Opening Prayer

Lord, Simeon had been waiting his whole life to see what we so often take for granted. He held in his arms the one the prophets had strained to see from a distance, and it was enough — enough to die in peace. We confess that we have sometimes held the same reality with careless hands, too familiar with the name to be astonished by the Person. Grant us something of Simeon's wonder. Let us not take for granted what angels longed to look into. Amen.

"Simeon was there. He took the child in his arms and praised God, saying, 'Sovereign Lord, now let your servant die in peace, as you promised. I have seen your salvation, which you have prepared for all people. He is a light to reveal God to the nations, and he is the glory of your people Israel!'… Then Simeon blessed them, and he said to Mary, the baby's mother, 'This child is destined to cause many in Israel to fall, but he will be a joy to many others… And a sword will pierce your very soul.'" Luke 2:28–32, 34–35, NLT

Luke gives us two portraits of aged faithfulness in his nativity narrative: Simeon and the prophet Anna. Both have been waiting. Both have spent their years in the discipline of holy anticipation, expecting something they could only see by faith. Today we linger with Simeon — this righteous, devout man, bent by decades of waiting, on whom the Holy Spirit rested in an unusual way and who had received an unusual promise: he would not die before he had seen the Lord's Christ.

And then, on an ordinary day — in the ordinary traffic of the temple, among the ordinary crowd of worshipers — Mary and Joseph arrive to fulfill the rite of purification, carrying the required offering of two turtledoves. Simeon sees them. He moves through the crowd. He takes the infant in his arms.

Pause here. Hold this image. An old man, at the end of his years, holding an infant who is the eternal Son of God. Flesh and eternity touching in the most ordinary human form available: a child being carried. Simeon has been waiting for this his entire life — not abstractly, not as a theological aspiration, but personally, with the specific urgency of a man who knows his own time is running short. And now the waiting is over. He holds what he has been waiting for.

What he says has become one of the most ancient prayers of the Christian church, sung at Compline — the last office of the day, the prayer before sleep — for two thousand years: Nunc Dimittis. "Now you dismiss your servant in peace." He is not asking for more. He is not requesting an extension. He looks at what he holds and says: this is enough. I have seen your salvation. My life is complete.

The scope of what he sees in that infant staggers the setting. He is standing in the Jerusalem temple, in the heart of Israel, holding a Jewish child of eight days — and he speaks of all peoples, of the nations, of the ends of the earth. "A light to reveal God to the nations, and the glory of your people Israel." He is drawing on Isaiah's Servant Songs (Isaiah 42:6; 49:6), the vision of a coming one who would carry God's salvation not only to Israel but to every people under heaven. Simeon sees this universal scope — and he sees it in eight pounds of sleeping infant.

Then the shadow falls. He blesses the young family and turns to Mary with words that do not belong to the celebration: "This child is destined to cause many in Israel to fall, but he will be a joy to many others… And a sword will pierce your very soul." The song of peace ends with a sword. Even here, at the very threshold of his life, the cross is already present — not as an interruption of the story but as its destination. Peter Kreeft once observed that every scene in the Gospels is lit from behind by the cross and from above by the resurrection. Simeon simply says it aloud, at the beginning, so it cannot be forgotten later.

This is the shape of the salvation that has arrived: it will not be comfortable. It will not leave everyone where they were. It will divide and it will heal, it will wound and it will restore — and the same sword that pierces Mary's soul will ultimately be the wound through which the nations find life. The one Simeon holds is the most welcome and the most unsettling presence in the history of the world.

He still is. And still, for all who hold him — truly hold him, not as an idea but as Lord — he is enough. More than enough. A whole life can be summed up and completed in the encounter with him. Now let your servant depart in peace.

For reflection

  • Simeon says "I have seen your salvation" — and considers his life complete. What would it mean for you to hold this conviction: that to have seen and received Christ is the defining, sufficient thing — the thing that makes a life whole?
  • The shadow of the cross appears even in the manger scene, before Jesus has spoken or done anything in public. What does it tell you about the kind of salvation Christ brings that it requires going through suffering rather than around it — both his and ours?
  • Simeon's song — the Nunc Dimittis — has been sung at the close of day for two thousand years. What is it about the content of his song that makes it feel appropriate as a way to end the day? What would it mean to close your own day with those words?

Going Deeper (optional)

Simeon's declaration that the child will be "a light to reveal God to the nations" (Luke 2:32) is the first explicit announcement in Luke's Gospel of the universal scope of Christ's mission. Luke, writing for a Gentile audience, is careful to establish this from the very opening of his narrative: the salvation of God is prepared "for all people" (v.31). This universal scope runs through Luke-Acts as a major theological thread — from Simeon's song through the commission of Acts 1:8 to Pentecost's gathering of every nation, to Paul's mission to the ends of the earth.

Theologically, this universality does not undermine Israel's particular place in redemptive history but fulfills it. Israel was always called to be a light-bearing people (Genesis 12:3; Isaiah 49:6) — a kingdom of priests through whom blessing would flow to all nations (Exodus 19:6). The tragedy of John 1:11 ("his own people did not receive him") is not the end of the story but its turning point. The promise to Abraham — "in you all the families of the earth shall be blessed" (Genesis 12:3) — does not arrive through Israel's general faithfulness but through the faithfulness of the one faithful Israelite, the son of Abraham and son of David, held in Simeon's arms. Everything God had promised to and through Israel finds its fulfillment in this child. Simeon sees it all — and releases his grip on life with a peace that the watching world could not comprehend.
