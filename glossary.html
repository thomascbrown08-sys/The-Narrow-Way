Old Words, Plainly Said

Glossary

The road uses some old and precise words — partly because they carry meanings ordinary words cannot, and partly because learning them is itself part of coming to know the faith. None of them is meant to keep anyone out. Here they are in plain English, for any capable reader meeting them for the first time.

The Scripture-box labels

Direct
A claim the Bible states or plainly entails, with little need for inference. On this site, the firmest of the six Scripture-box labels.
Entailed
A claim reached by “good and necessary consequence” (see below) — not stated in one verse, but following rigorously when Scripture is compared with Scripture.
Good and necessary consequence
A phrase from the Westminster Confession for truths that Scripture does not state word-for-word but that follow unavoidably from what it does say. The doctrine of the Trinity is the classic example.
Interpretation (as a label)
On this site, a claim that rests mainly on how the church has read Scripture — a tradition’s formulation or a pastoral framework — rather than on a direct chain from the text. Flagged openly so it is not mistaken for the plain word of God.
Contested (as a label)
A genuine, in-bounds disagreement among orthodox Christians. On these questions the site maps the strongest case on each side rather than handing down a verdict.
Historical (as a label)
A claim whose warrant rests on evidence outside the Bible — manuscripts, archaeology, the historian’s reasoning — rather than on Scripture itself.

The faith — grace, salvation, and the Christian life

Aseity
God’s self-existence: he depends on nothing and needs nothing outside himself. He created out of generosity, not loneliness or lack.
Assurance
A believer’s settled confidence of belonging to God. This site insists it rests not on the strength of our feelings but on the finished work of Christ and the seal of the Spirit.
Atonement
What the cross accomplished — the reconciling of sinners to God through the death of Christ in their place.
Fiducia
Latin for trust. The decisive element of saving faith: not merely agreeing the gospel is true, but entrusting oneself to the One it points to. (The older teachers spoke of notitia — knowing the content; assensus — agreeing it is true; and fiducia — trusting personally.)
Fear of the LORD
Not cringing dread but reverent awe before a holy God — love’s truest form, and, Scripture says, the beginning of wisdom.
Heart
In the Bible the “heart” (Hebrew lev; Greek kardia) is not the seat of the emotions as opposed to the head — it is the control center of the whole inner person: where thinking, willing, desiring, and deciding all happen together. Scripture assigns to the heart the very work we tend to give the mind: people reason “in their hearts” (Mark 2:6–8), decide in the heart (2 Corinthians 9:7), and believe with the heart and are justified (Romans 10:9–10). It is the wellspring of all of life (Proverbs 4:23), the true self that God alone sees beneath the surface (1 Samuel 16:7) — which is why the gospel’s promise is a new heart, not merely new feelings (Ezekiel 36:26). Emotions are included, but the heart is closer to mind, will, and deepest loves combined. So “trust in your heart” or “believe in your heart” means far more than working up a feeling: it is the turning of your whole self — convictions, loves, and will together — toward God.
Imputation
To credit something to someone’s account. In justification, Christ’s righteousness is credited to the believer — a standing received as a gift, not earned. Contrast infusion (below).
Infusion
The view (held in Roman Catholic theology) that righteousness is gradually poured into and worked out within a person, rather than credited to them at once. The Reformation held instead to imputation.
Justification
God’s legal declaration that a sinner is righteous in his sight — not because they have become good, but because Christ’s righteousness is credited to them, received through faith.
Monergism
The view that salvation is the work of God alone (Greek mono, one + ergon, work) — that even faith is his gift, not a contribution we add. Contrast synergism, the view that God’s grace and the free human response work together. This is near the heart of the Calvinist/Arminian conversation.
Original sin
The teaching that human nature is corrupted at the root by the fall — not merely that we imitate bad examples, but that we inherit a bent away from God and cannot turn back by unaided effort.
Penal substitution
The understanding that at the cross Christ bore the penalty for sin in the place of sinners, so that God could be at once perfectly just (the debt truly paid) and the justifier of those who trust him.
Perseverance of the saints
The teaching that those genuinely born again will be kept by God to the end — not by the strength of their grip on him, but by the strength of his grip on them.
Propitiation
The turning away of God’s righteous wrath against sin through the sacrifice of Christ — not a reluctant God appeased, but God himself providing the means of peace.
Reconciliation
The restoring of a broken relationship. The gospel’s claim is that God has reconciled enemies to himself through the cross.
Regeneration
The new birth — the Spirit’s work of making spiritually dead people alive, so that they can see and turn to God at all.
Repentance
A turning. This site stresses its direction: a turning toward God (the words behind it, Hebrew shuv and Greek metanoia, carry that sense), from which the turning away from sin then flows.
Sanctification
The lifelong process by which a believer is gradually made more like Christ — growth that is the fruit of grace, not the price of it.
Sola fide / sola gratia / sola Scriptura
Three of the Reformation’s “solas”: that we are justified by faith alone, by grace alone, on the authority of Scripture alone.

The creeds — God, Christ, and the Trinity

Homoousios
Greek for “of one substance/being.” The decisive word of the Nicene Creed, affirming that the Son is of the very same being as the Father — truly God, not a lesser or created copy.
Incarnation
God the Son taking on human nature — “the Word became flesh.” Fully God and fully man in one person.
Trinity
The teaching that God is one Being in three coequal, coeternal persons — Father, Son, and Holy Spirit — neither blending the persons nor dividing the one God.
Two natures
The truth, defined at Chalcedon, that Jesus Christ is one person possessing two complete natures, divine and human, “without confusion, without change, without division, without separation.”
Filioque
Latin for “and the Son.” A disputed clause about whether the Spirit proceeds from the Father alone or “from the Father and the Son” — a historic difference between Eastern and Western churches.

The counterfeits — old errors and their names

Arianism
The early error that the Son was the greatest created being, but still created — not eternally God. Answered by the Nicene Creed.
Pelagianism
The error that human beings can choose good and please God by their own effort, without grace being strictly necessary. Answered by Augustine.
Gnosticism
A family of early errors holding that the material world is evil or inferior and that salvation comes through secret knowledge for an elite — against the goodness of creation, the bodily resurrection, and the public gospel.
Antinomianism
The error that, because we are saved by grace, the moral law no longer matters — treating grace as a license. One of the two ditches this site warns against (the other being legalism).
Legalism
The error of treating rule-keeping as the basis of one’s standing with God — trying to earn what can only be received. The “town of Morality” on the road.
