Day 7

The Danger of Familiarity

The LORD of Heaven's Armies says to the priests: "A son honors his father, and a servant respects his master. If I am your father and master, where are the honor and respect I deserve? You have shown contempt for my name! But you ask, 'How have we shown contempt for your name?' You have shown contempt by offering defiled sacrifices on my altar. Then you ask, 'How have we defiled the sacrifices?' You defile them by saying the altar of the LORD deserves no respect. When you give blind animals as sacrifices, isn't that wrong? And isn't it wrong to offer animals that are crippled and diseased? Try giving gifts like that to your governor, and see how pleased he is!" Malachi 1:6–8 (NLT)

The priests in Malachi's day had not abandoned worship. They were still bringing offerings, still maintaining the temple routine, still showing up. But something had quietly slipped — they had begun bringing the animals no one else wanted. The blind ones. The crippled ones. The diseased ones that weren't worth keeping.

God's response is devastating in its simplicity: try giving that to your governor and see how it goes. You wouldn't dare. You'd bring your best. But here, in the house of the living God, you've learned to make do with leftovers.

This is what familiarity does to worship when we aren't watching. It doesn't usually happen with a decision. It happens gradually — one distracted Sunday, one rushed quiet time, one prayer that's really just a list, until the altar is full of offerings we wouldn't give anyone else. We have learned to go through the motions, and the motions have learned to go on without us.

Malachi's God is not angry because the offerings are small. He is grieved because they reveal a heart that has stopped taking him seriously. The size of what we bring to God reflects what we actually think of him. And what we think of him shapes everything about how we live.

From Church History — The Reformation of Worship — Recovering Seriousness

One of the driving concerns of the 16th-century Reformers was that Roman Catholic worship had become cluttered with forms and routines that had lost their meaning — rote prayers in a language people didn't speak, rituals performed as magical acts, the focus shifted from God to the performance. Calvin's instinct was to strip worship back to its biblical essentials: the Word read and preached, the sacraments rightly administered, sincere prayer in the language of the people. The goal was not austerity for its own sake but engagement — worship that the congregation actually participated in with heart and mind, not worship done on their behalf while they watched.

Put It Into Practice

This week, before a time of prayer or worship, pause for sixty seconds and ask yourself: what am I actually about to bring? Is it my best, or is it a leftover? You don't have to perform. You just have to show up honestly.

For reflection

In what area of your worship life have you slipped into routine without realizing it — prayer, Sunday attendance, giving, private Bible reading? Why do you think familiarity tends to reduce reverence rather than increase it? What would reverse that tendency? What would it look like practically to bring your best to God this week — not perfectly, but intentionally?
