Day 27

The Formative Power of Song

Let the message about Christ, in all its richness, fill your lives. Teach and counsel each other with all the wisdom he gives. Sing psalms and hymns and spiritual songs to God with thankful hearts. Colossians 3:16 (NLT)
Sing psalms and hymns and spiritual songs among yourselves, and make music to the Lord in your hearts. And give thanks for everything to God the Father in the name of our Lord Jesus Christ. Ephesians 5:19–20 (NLT)

Paul's instruction to sing is not primarily about music. It is about formation. He says to let the message about Christ fill your lives — and then immediately: sing. The music is a vessel for the message. And music forms us in ways that plain prose cannot.

We remember what we sing. Long after we have forgotten a sermon, a melody with words attached to it stays. The theological convictions we carry into old age are often the ones we learned in song. There is something about the combination of melody, rhythm, and text that bypasses our ordinary resistance and deposits truth at a deeper level.

This means the songs we sing in worship matter enormously. Not just their style or their musical quality, but their content. A worship diet of songs that are primarily about our experience of God — how we feel, what we long for — will form us differently than a diet that is primarily about who God is and what he has done. Both have a place. The balance matters.

Paul says to make music to the Lord in your hearts — suggesting this is not only corporate but individual, not only Sunday but all week. The person who carries worship songs into their Tuesday afternoon is practicing something the text commends. Singing is formation that we can take with us.

Put It Into Practice

Pay attention this week to what you're singing — in church, in the car, in your head. Ask: what theology am I rehearsing? What picture of God am I forming? If you find the songs are mostly about you, find one that is mostly about him and learn it.

For reflection

What songs have most shaped your understanding of God over the years? What did they teach you? How do you evaluate the content of worship music — in your church or in your personal listening? What is one hymn or song that carries more theology than you typically think about? What would it mean to actually believe what it says?
