Day 2

Holy, Holy, Holy

It was in the year King Uzziah died that I saw the Lord. He was sitting on a lofty throne, and the train of his robe filled the Temple. Attending him were mighty seraphim, each having six wings. With two wings they covered their faces, with two they covered their feet, and with two they flew. They were calling out to each other, "Holy, holy, holy is the LORD of Heaven's Armies! The whole earth is filled with his glory!" Their voices shook the Temple to its foundations, and the entire building was filled with smoke. Then I said, "It's all over! I am doomed, for I am a sinful man." Isaiah 6:1–5 (NLT)

The word "holy" in Hebrew means set apart, distinct, other. When the seraphim cry "holy, holy, holy" — a threefold repetition that Hebrew uses to express the superlative, the ultimate — they are declaring that God is not simply good, or very good, or even supremely good. He is in a category so far beyond every other thing that ordinary language strains to contain it.

Notice what the seraphim do with their wings. Two cover their faces — even they do not look directly at God. Two cover their feet, in a posture of humility. Only two are used for flight, for service. These are the mightiest beings in creation, and they are undone by proximity to God.

Isaiah's response is the most honest thing in the passage: It's all over. I am doomed. This is not an overreaction. This is the only sane response to standing in the presence of perfect holiness when you are not holy. The distance between what God is and what we are is not a small gap we can close with effort. It is an infinite chasm.

And yet — Isaiah was not destroyed. What came next was a coal from the altar, a touch, and the words: your guilt is removed, your sins are forgiven. The encounter that begins with undoing ends with restoration. This is the shape of genuine worship: we are seen clearly, and we are not destroyed. That is grace.

From Church History — The Sanctus — Two Thousand Years of Holy, Holy, Holy

The threefold "Holy, holy, holy" of Isaiah 6 has been sung in Christian worship since the earliest centuries — appearing in the Didache (a first or second century worship manual), in the liturgies of Chrysostom and Basil, and eventually in virtually every tradition's Sunday service as the Sanctus. When a congregation sings these words today, they are joining a chorus that has been rising, without interruption, for two millennia — and before that, in Isaiah's vision itself, for all of eternity. Worship roots us in something far larger than this Sunday morning.

Put It Into Practice

Read Isaiah 6:1–8 slowly, all the way through. Pause at verse 5 — Isaiah's cry of undoing — and let it be yours too. Don't rush to the coal. Sit in the honest weight of the distance between you and God before you receive the grace that closes it.

For reflection

When did you last feel genuinely small before God — not guilty, not afraid, but simply overwhelmed by how great he is? What brought that on? Isaiah's undoing was the first step toward his commissioning. What might God need to undo in you before he can send you? The seraphim covered their faces in God's presence. How does your posture — physical and internal — during worship reflect this kind of reverence?
