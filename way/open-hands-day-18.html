Day 18

Character Over Chemistry

Who can find a virtuous and capable wife? She is more precious than rubies.

...Charm is deceptive, and beauty does not last; but a woman who fears the LORD will be greatly praised. Ruth 3:11 (NLT) "Now don't worry, my daughter. I will do for you what is right, for everyone in town knows you are a virtuous woman." Proverbs 12:4 (NLT) A worthy wife is a crown for her husband, but a disgraceful woman is like cancer in his bones. Proverbs 31:10, 30 (NLT)

Proverbs 31 is a long poem describing a woman of remarkable capability — industrious, generous, wise, strong. Many people feel tired just reading it. But notice what verse 30 says after all of that: "charm is deceptive, and beauty does not last." After twenty verses cataloguing extraordinary gifts, the poem lands on this: what actually endures is the fear of the Lord.

The Hebrew phrase translated "virtuous and capable" or "woman of noble character" is eshet chayil — literally "woman of valor" or "woman of strength." It's a warrior's word applied to a person's character. When Boaz describes Ruth as a "virtuous woman" in Ruth 3:11, he uses a masculine form of the same word — someone of substance, integrity, and proven faithfulness under pressure. That's what he sees. That's what he values.

Proverbs 12:4's metaphor is as sharp as it is uncomfortable: the right marriage is a crown — it lifts, honors, and strengthens. The wrong one is like decay in the bones — slow, invisible, and ultimately devastating. Chemistry can produce both. Character is the better predictor.

A practical note: this applies in both directions. The question isn't only "is this person godly enough for me?" It's also: "Am I becoming the kind of person a godly person would want to build a life with?" Becoming and discerning happen together.

For reflection

If you were evaluated by the same standard you apply to potential partners — faithfulness, character, fear of the Lord — how do you think you'd do?
