Day 13

The Creeds Are Your Friends

Beloved, although I was very eager to write to you about our common salvation, I found it necessary to write appealing to you to contend for the faith that was once for all delivered to the saints. Jude 3 (ESV)
For I delivered to you as of first importance what I also received: that Christ died for our sins in accordance with the Scriptures, that he was buried, that he was raised on the third day in accordance with the Scriptures, and that he appeared to Cephas, then to the twelve. 1 Corinthians 15:3–5 (ESV)

The historic creeds — the Apostles' Creed, the Nicene Creed — are not Scripture. They carry no independent authority. But they are among the most useful tools a discerning person has access to, and they are tragically underused in many churches today.

Think of them as the church's corporate memory — the distillation of centuries of careful thought about what Scripture actually teaches about God, Christ, and salvation. When a new teaching departs from them, it isn't departing from a committee's opinions. It is departing from conclusions that the global church tested against Scripture across many centuries and many cultures.

Jude's phrase 'once for all delivered to the saints' matters. There is a core. It is not indefinitely negotiable. Each generation is called to receive it, understand it, and pass it on — not to reinvent it to suit their preferences. The creeds help us remember what that core is.

Notice that Paul, in 1 Corinthians 15, is already doing something creedal: 'I delivered to you what I also received.' There is content to be transmitted. It has a shape. The creeds simply make that shape explicit and memorable.

Put It Into Practice

Read the Apostles' Creed slowly today — not as a recitation but as a reading. Notice which phrases you believe firmly, which you've never thought about carefully, and which feel unfamiliar. That awareness is itself a form of discernment.

For reflection

Do you tend to trust or distrust the historic church's doctrinal conclusions? What has shaped that posture? What is the difference between the creeds having authority and Scripture having authority? Why does that distinction matter? How might regularly reciting a creed function as a discernment practice in your own spiritual life?
