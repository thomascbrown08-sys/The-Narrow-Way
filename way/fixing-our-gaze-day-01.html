Day 1

The Word Made Flesh

Opening Prayer

Gracious Father, we come to you as those who have not yet seen what we most need to see. Open the eyes of our hearts — not merely our minds — to behold the glory of your Son. We confess that familiarity is the great enemy of wonder, and we have heard these words so many times that we have nearly stopped hearing them at all. Strip away our numbness. Let what is infinite and astonishing land on us with its full weight. We ask this in the name of the very One we seek to behold. Amen.

"And the Word became flesh and dwelt among us, and we have seen his glory, glory as of the only Son from the Father, full of grace and truth." John 1:14, ESV

In the beginning was the Word. Before time had a name, before the first galaxy wheeled through the darkness, before any creature drew breath or spoke a syllable — the Word was. And the Word was with God. And the Word was God.

Then John drops the sentence that should stop the world: The Word became flesh.

Not "appeared as flesh." Not "seemed to inhabit flesh." Became. The eternal second person of the Trinity — the one through whom every star was flung into place, the one in whom all wisdom and knowledge are hidden — took to himself a human nature and entered the very world he had spoken into being. The one who had never known limitation accepted limitation. The one who had never known hunger, cold, or exhaustion began to know all three.

The Greek word behind "dwelt among us" is eskēnōsen — he tabernacled. John reaches back deliberately to the great drama of Exodus, when the glory of God descended on the tent of meeting so thickly that even Moses could not enter (Exodus 40:34–35). That presence — the Shekinah, the visible weight of divine glory — had once resided in linen and acacia wood. Now it moved into the neighborhood. He pitched his tent in our streets. He came to be near.

C.H. Spurgeon once asked his congregation to consider what it cost eternity to enter time. "You cannot fathom it," he said, "but you must attempt it. Kneel before this mystery." The infinite made finite. The immortal taking on mortality. The One who dwelt in unapproachable light now blinking in the Bethlehem darkness, crying with the lungs he had just borrowed from humanity. There is no adequate analogy for this. The closest we might come is to imagine an author stepping bodily into his own novel — except that this author did not merely visit; he became a character, subject to every constraint of the world he had written, while remaining, in some unfathomable sense, its author still.

And what did those who stood closest to him see? "We have seen his glory." John uses the language of eyewitness testimony — not of spiritual impression or mystical sensation, but of sight. He means: we watched him. We ate with him. We heard him teach in the open air and saw his face when he wept. We pressed close when he healed and stepped back when the ground seemed to shake with what he said. And in all of it — in the carpenter's son from Galilee, in the Rabbi who touched lepers and ate with tax collectors — we saw glory. Not in spite of the flesh, but through it.

This is the peculiar genius of the Incarnation. God did not remain abstractly distant, a philosophical first cause or a cosmic architect whose presence could only be felt in trembling and mystery. He drew near in a way that could be examined, questioned, touched. As John would later write, this was about "that which we have heard, which we have seen with our eyes, which we looked upon and have touched with our hands" (1 John 1:1). The eternal was given a face.

N.T. Wright observes that the whole sweep of Israel's story had been building toward this moment. God had been saying, through patriarchs and prophets, through temple and sacrifice, through law and psalm: Here is what I am like. Here is what I require. Here is what I am moving toward. But in Jesus, God stopped pointing and simply showed up. The signpost became the destination.

And he came "full of grace and truth." Not grace at the expense of truth — not a God so fond of us that our sin barely registers. Not truth at the expense of grace — not a God so holy that there is no mercy to be found in his presence. Both, held without loss. In Jesus, what we most deserve and what we most need arrived simultaneously, in the same Person.

What does it mean to behold this? John's first readers would have felt the full weight of that verb — to see, to look hard at something real. And that is precisely what this devotional invites: not the accumulation of information about Jesus, but the act of looking. Of lingering over a face that is unlike any other face in the history of the world. Begin here. Begin with the staggering announcement: God became a man. The Word was made flesh. And everything — your salvation, your understanding of God, your hope for the future, your very identity — everything hangs on that four-letter word: became.

For reflection

  • John says the disciples "have seen his glory" — in ordinary flesh, in the midst of ordinary life. What has your own experience of encountering Christ been like, and what might it mean to see him more clearly?
  • Why do you think it mattered that God came near in a real body and in actual history, rather than communicating through visions or impressions alone? What is gained by the specificity of the Incarnation?
  • "Full of grace and truth" — most of us lean more naturally toward one than the other in our own lives and relationships. Which do you tend to emphasize? What might it look like to hold both together the way Christ does?

Going Deeper (optional)

The Incarnation presses on every article of Christian theology. At the Council of Chalcedon in A.D. 451, the church declared that Jesus Christ is one Person in two natures — fully divine and fully human — without confusion, change, division, or separation. Each of those four negatives protects something precious. Without confusion: his divinity did not dissolve into his humanity, nor his humanity into his divinity. Without change: becoming human did not alter or diminish who God is eternally. Without division: he is not two persons — one heavenly and one earthly — sharing a body. Without separation: even now, the risen and ascended Christ retains his human nature; he is our glorified human Brother before the Father forever (Romans 8:34; Hebrews 7:25).

The practical consequences are immense. If Christ is truly human, then he truly understands your suffering, your hunger, your grief, your temptation — from the inside, not the outside (Hebrews 4:15). He is not a sympathetic observer from a safe distance. If he is truly God, then what he accomplished in that flesh — his perfect obedience, his atoning sacrifice, his resurrection — carries infinite weight. An Incarnation that is less than total on either side gives us a Savior who is less than adequate. The full measure of your need demands the full measure of both his natures. Nothing less will do.
