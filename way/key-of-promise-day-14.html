Day 14

Your Body Will Rise

But in fact, Christ has been raised from the dead. He is the first of a great harvest of all who have died.

So when our dying bodies have been transformed into bodies that will never die, this Scripture will be fulfilled: “Death is swallowed up in victory. O death, where is your victory? O death, where is your sting?”

But thank God! He gives us victory over sin and death through our Lord Jesus Christ. 1 Corinthians 15:20–22, 54–57 (NLT)

For thirteen days we have stayed mostly inside the dungeon, holding promises up against the dark. Now, in these last two days, the promises lift our eyes past the dungeon walls entirely — because the dark, however long, is not where the story ends. And the first thing to see on the far horizon is an empty tomb.

“Christ has been raised from the dead. He is the first of a great harvest of all who have died.” That word — first, the firstfruits — is doing enormous work. The first sheaf of a harvest is not the exception; it is the guarantee and the sample. It means the rest of the field is coming, and coming like that. Christ's resurrection is not merely a wonderful thing that happened to him. It is the promise of what will happen to you, in your own body, made the same kind of pledge as a first ripe stalk promising the whole harvest.

This matters in Doubting Castle because the giant's deepest threat, under all the others, is death itself — the sense that the darkness will simply win, that exhaustion or despair or the grave will have the last word. Paul stands in front of that final enemy and does something audacious: he taunts it. “O death, where is your victory? O death, where is your sting?” He speaks to the last enemy not as a victim but as a survivor at a defeated foe's grave, because the outcome is already decided. Death has been “swallowed up in victory” — not merely survived, but consumed, undone, reversed.

And notice the promise is not that you escape your body, drifting off as a disembodied soul. It is that your “dying body” will be “transformed into a body that will never die.” The Christian hope is not less physical than this life but more — the same you, raised, healed, glorified, deathless. Whatever the dungeon has done to your body and mind, it is not the final state. There is a resurrection coming, and it is as sure as Christ's own.

So lift your eyes tonight. The darkness is real, but it is not the end. Because he rose, you will rise. The last enemy has already lost, and the harvest he guaranteed is coming — with your name in it.

Pray the Promise

Lift your eyes past the present darkness and pray the promise as a settled future: “Because Christ rose, I will rise. My body, my mind, all of this — transformed, healed, deathless. Death is not my end. The harvest is coming, and I am in it.”

Let the taunt become your own prayer of defiance against the dark: “O death, where is your sting? You have already lost.”

For reflection

How does the certainty of your own bodily resurrection — guaranteed by Christ as the firstfruits — change the way the present darkness looks? What does it mean that death is already a defeated enemy?
