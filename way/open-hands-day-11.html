Day 11

Generosity Is Not Optional

Give freely and become more wealthy; be stingy and lose everything. The generous will prosper; those who refresh others will themselves be refreshed. 2 Corinthians 9:6–8 (NLT) Remember this — a farmer who plants only a few seeds will get a small crop. But the one who plants generously will get a generous crop. You must each decide in your heart how much to give. And don't give reluctantly or in response to pressure. "For God loves a person who gives cheerfully." Luke 21:2–4 (NLT) While he was there, he noticed a poor widow putting in two small coins. "I tell you the truth," Jesus said, "this poor widow has given more than all the rest of them. For they have given a tiny part of their surplus, but she, poor as she is, has given everything she has." Proverbs 11:24–25 (NLT)

The word Paul uses for "cheerful" giver is the Greek hilaros — from which we get the word hilarious. There's a kind of joyful, almost laughing abandon in genuine generosity. It's the opposite of what reluctant giving looks like: the careful calculation, the weighing of costs, the slight resentment as the money leaves the hand.

The principle in Proverbs 11:24 strikes most of us as counterintuitive: give freely and become more wealthy; be stingy and lose everything. This isn't prosperity gospel — it isn't a promise that God will financially reward every donation. It's a wisdom observation about two ways of relating to money. The person who hoards becomes ruled by what they're protecting. The person who gives freely has already demonstrated that money isn't their master. That freedom tends to produce more, not less.

The widow in Luke 21 provides the standard of proportionality. Jesus doesn't commend the size of her gift; he commends the proportion. She gave everything she had. The rich gave large amounts that were still small fractions of their total. The question Jesus is implicitly asking isn't "how much do you give?" but "how much do you keep?"

For reflection

Is your giving from the top of what you receive, or from what's left after everything else is covered?
