Day 6

Work Is Good

Take a lesson from the ants, you lazybones. Learn from their ways and become wise! Though they have no prince or governor or ruler to make them work, they labor hard all summer, gathering food for the winter. But you, lazybones, how long will you sleep? When will you wake up? A little extra sleep, a little more slumber, a little folding of the hands to rest — then poverty will pounce on you like a bandit; scarcity will attack you like an armed robber. 2 Thessalonians 3:10–12 (NLT) Even while we were with you, we gave you this command: 'Those unwilling to work will not get to eat.' Yet we hear that some of you are living idle lives, refusing to work and meddling in other people's business. We command such people and urge them in the name of the Lord Jesus Christ to settle down and work to earn their own living. Proverbs 14:23 (NLT) Work brings profit, but mere talk leads to poverty! Proverbs 6:6–11 (NLT)

Here's something the culture around us often forgets: work was part of God's design for human life before anything went wrong. In Genesis 2, before the Fall, God placed the man in the garden "to tend and watch over it" (Genesis 2:15). Work is not a curse; it is a calling. The curse made work painful and difficult — but the work itself was already there, and good.

Proverbs is not flattering about laziness. The ant is held up as a model — no supervisor, no enforcement, no external motivation required. Just the wisdom to see that summer doesn't last forever. The language about poverty "pouncing like a bandit" is vivid and intentional: negligence has consequences, and they often arrive suddenly.

Paul's instruction in 2 Thessalonians sounds harsh if you take it out of context. He was addressing a specific situation: some believers had stopped working because they expected Jesus to return immediately. His response is pastoral but firm — settle down, do your work, earn your own way. Dependence that could be avoided is not a virtue.

The principle in Proverbs 14:23 is simple enough to memorize: work brings profit, talk brings poverty. Planning is good. Talking about plans is not the same as acting on them.

For reflection

Is your work — paid or otherwise — something you approach as a calling, or something to minimize?
