Day 1

A God Who Cannot Be Tamed

When the LORD saw Moses coming to take a closer look, God called to him from the middle of the bush, "Moses! Moses!"

"Here I am!" Moses replied.

"Do not come any closer," the LORD warned. "Take off your sandals, for you are standing on holy ground." Then he said, "I am the God of your father — the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob." When Moses heard this, he covered his face because he was afraid to look at God. Exodus 3:4–6 (NLT)

Moses was not looking for God that day. He was doing his job, tending sheep in the wilderness. And then — a bush on fire that somehow wasn't consumed. He turned aside to look. And everything changed.

The first thing God said was not a greeting. It was a boundary: do not come any closer. Take off your sandals. You are on holy ground. Before a single promise was made or a single command given, God established something fundamental: he is not approachable on our own terms. There is a difference between us and him that cannot be wished away or ignored.

Moses covered his face. This was not timidity or weakness. It was the most appropriate response in the history of the world. He was standing before the Living God — the God who made everything that exists, the God before whom time itself bows. Of course he covered his face.

We live in an era that has largely lost this. We want a God who is accessible, comfortable, on our level. And there is truth there — God does draw near in Christ, and that nearness is one of the most stunning facts in the universe. But nearness does not mean sameness. The burning bush is still burning. The sandals still need to come off. And the worship that forgets this is missing something essential.

Put It Into Practice

Find a few minutes alone and read Exodus 3:1–15 slowly. Imagine yourself as Moses. Before you ask God for anything today, simply sit with the fact of who he is. Let the silence be a kind of removed sandal.

For reflection

When you think about God, what is your default mental image of him? Is he primarily warm and accessible, or primarily holy and transcendent — or somehow both? What does it reveal about your heart when the God of the burning bush feels more threatening than inviting? What would it change about your daily life if you genuinely believed you were always on holy ground?
