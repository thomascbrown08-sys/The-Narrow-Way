Day 28

Practices That Build the Habit

But solid food is for the mature, for those who have their powers of discernment trained by constant practice to distinguish good from evil. Hebrews 5:14 (ESV)
How can a young man keep his way pure? By guarding it according to your word. With my whole heart I seek you; let me not wander from your commandments! I have stored up your word in my heart, that I might not sin against you. Psalm 119:9–11 (ESV)

Hebrews says discernment is trained by constant practice. Not by a single course, a prayer experience, or a moment of insight. By habituated, repeated engagement with the things that form it. This is what the Christian tradition calls formation.

Slow, attentive Scripture reading is the foundation. Not reading for quantity or information, but reading to encounter. Reading the same passage multiple days in a row. Asking what it says about God before asking what it says about you. Letting it sit.

Prayer as desire formation — not prayer as a mechanism for receiving impressions, but prayer as the regular practice of re-orienting your desires toward God and his will. The Lord's Prayer is a model: it shapes what we want before we ask for anything specific.

Self-examination — the Puritan tradition practiced regular, searching review of their motives, affections, and patterns. The Lord's Supper was designed as a corporate self-examination rhythm. Journal-keeping, regular confession to a trusted friend, and deliberate review of one's interior life all serve this function.

Reading in church history builds pattern recognition. When you have read about Montanism, the Arian crisis, the Reformation, and the German church failure, you begin to recognize the shapes of error when they appear in new clothes.

Put It Into Practice

Choose one practice from today's reading to add to your week — just one. Small and consistent beats large and sporadic. Write it down, tell someone, and do it.

For reflection

What practice has most formed your discernment so far in your Christian life? What has it produced in you? What is one practice of formation you have consistently neglected? What would it cost you to begin? How does community accountability make individual formation more effective rather than less?
