Day 10

Undone and Restored

Then I said, "It's all over! I am doomed, for I am a sinful man. I have filthy lips, and I live among a people with filthy lips. Yet I have seen the King, the LORD of Heaven's Armies." Then one of the seraphim flew to me with a burning coal he had taken from the altar with a pair of tongs. He touched my lips with it and said, "See, this coal has touched your lips. Now your guilt is removed, and your sins are forgiven." Then I heard the Lord asking, "Whom should I send as a messenger to this people? Who will go for us?" I said, "Here I am. Send me." Isaiah 6:5–8 (NLT)

We left Isaiah on Day 2 at the worst moment: undone, crying out that it was all over. Today we stay long enough to see what happens next.

A seraph flies toward him with a burning coal from the altar. This is not a comfort — it is a burning coal pressed to the lips. And yet the words that follow are the most beautiful in the passage: your guilt is removed, your sins are forgiven. The instrument of cleansing was not gentle. But the result was complete.

And then something remarkable happens. The man who moments ago was certain he was doomed now hears a question — whom shall I send? — and raises his hand. Here I am. Send me. The encounter that began with undoing has ended with availability. The vision of God's holiness, far from destroying Isaiah, has made him useful.

This is the full arc of genuine worship. We come as we are — flawed, distracted, carrying whatever we carry — and we encounter the one who is holy. We are seen clearly, and we are not destroyed. We are cleansed, and we are commissioned. This is not a story about becoming worthy. It is a story about what happens when the holy God meets us in our unworthiness and refuses to leave us there.

Put It Into Practice

Think of something in your life that makes you feel unworthy to draw near to God — some failure, some pattern, some distance. Bring it into the light of Isaiah 6. The coal still burns. The forgiveness is still complete. What do you need to receive today?

For reflection

Have you ever experienced a moment of genuine spiritual undoing — a time when you saw yourself clearly in God's light? What happened after? Isaiah's cleansing was painful before it was freeing. What in your life might God need to touch painfully before it becomes freedom? After his encounter, Isaiah was available — "send me." How does genuine worship prepare us for mission rather than pulling us away from it?
