Day 14

When the Bible Doesn't Say

One person esteems one day as better than another, while another esteems all days alike. Each one should be fully convinced in his own mind. The one who observes the day, observes it in honor of the Lord. The one who eats, eats in honor of the Lord, since he gives thanks to God, while the one who abstains, abstains in honor of the Lord and gives thanks to God. Romans 14:5–6 (ESV)
'All things are lawful,' but not all things are helpful. 'All things are lawful,' but not all things build up. Let no one seek his own good, but the good of his neighbor. 1 Corinthians 10:23–24 (ESV)

Much of life falls into a space the Reformers called the adiaphora — 'things indifferent' — where Scripture doesn't give a direct command or prohibition. What job to take. What medical treatment to pursue. How to spend your leisure time. These decisions still require discernment, but it works differently here.

Paul's treatment of food sacrificed to idols and the observance of days gives us the framework. In these disputed areas, the goal is not finding the single right answer for everyone. It's being 'fully convinced in your own mind' — having thought it through carefully, in the fear of God, with a formed conscience.

First Corinthians 10 adds the neighbor principle: even when something is lawful, the question 'does this build up my neighbor?' belongs in the discernment. Liberty is real, but it isn't self-contained. Our decisions ripple outward.

The key question in ethical discernment isn't only 'is this permitted?' but 'does this honor God, love my neighbor, and accord with the character that Scripture is forming in me?' That broader frame makes discernment richer and more honest than a simple rules-lookup.

Put It Into Practice

Identify one decision you're currently facing that Scripture doesn't directly address. Write down three principles from Scripture that are relevant to it. How do they shape your thinking?

For reflection

How do you typically make decisions in areas where Scripture is silent? Who or what has most shaped your ethical reasoning? What's the difference between using your liberty wisely and using it selfishly? How does keeping your neighbor's good in view change the ethical questions you ask?
