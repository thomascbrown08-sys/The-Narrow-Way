The Porter's Lodge

Where Watchful the porter meets the pilgrim at the gate, and asks who goes there.

"How lovely is your dwelling place, O LORD of hosts!... a day in your courts is better than a thousand elsewhere." Psalm 84:1, 10 (ESV)

No one simply wanders into Palace Beautiful. At the gate stands a porter — Bunyan names him Watchful — who meets each pilgrim, asks who they are and where they are bound, and only then opens the house. The lodge at the gate is where a pilgrim decides whether this is a house worth entering at all. And for many who come to this road carrying wounds from a previous house, that is exactly the right question, asked at exactly the right place.

So this room is the practical one: how to find a healthy church. Let us be honest at the threshold, the way the porter is. The church you are looking for does not exist — not the perfect one, the flawless community of fully sanctified people who always love well and never wound. That church will not exist until Christ returns. The real question is not "how do I find the perfect church?" but "how do I find a community genuinely trying to be what the New Testament describes, and honest when it fails?" That church does exist, and it is worth looking for carefully.

In plain terms

Don't look for a flawless church — there isn't one, and a church that claims to be flawless is showing you a warning sign, not a virtue. Look for an honest one.

If a previous church was the wound, you are allowed to be careful, and to take your time at the gate. Carefulness is not faithlessness.

What you are looking for, the Reformers named plainly — the marks of a true church: the Word of God truly preached, the sacraments rightly administered, and discipline lovingly exercised. Where the gospel is genuinely proclaimed, where baptism and the Lord's Supper are honored as Christ gave them, and where holiness is taken seriously without crushing people, there is a true church, however small.

And there are warning signs worth taking seriously, because they are how good people get hurt: a church built around one unaccountable personality; a culture where questions are treated as disloyalty; secrecy about money or power; leadership that demands and shames rather than feeds and tends. A healthy church will not be perfect — manage that expectation gently, in yourself and in others — but it will be safe in the way a good home is safe: a place where the bruised reed is not broken.

You are not looking for a flawless church. You are looking for an honest one.

We will lay out the fuller, practical guidance in its own page — the questions to ask, the signs to read, what to do if you are coming in wounded, and how to manage your own expectations. For now: the porter is not your enemy. He is keeping a house worth entering, and he is glad you have come to the gate.
