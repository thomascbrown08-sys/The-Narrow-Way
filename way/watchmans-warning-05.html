Study 5

Respectable Sins

The sins that no one confronts because everyone shares them

"Now the works of the flesh are evident: sexual immorality, impurity, sensuality, idolatry, sorcery, enmity, strife, jealousy, fits of anger, rivalries, dissensions, divisions, envy, drunkenness, orgies, and things like these." Galatians 5:19–21 (ESV)

Notice that Paul's list in Galatians 5 does not organize sins by severity. Jealousy is in the same list as sorcery. Envy stands next to sexual immorality. "Fits of anger" shares a sentence with idolatry. Scripture does not maintain the polite ranking system we have constructed for it.

The Ranking System We Have Built

In most Christian communities — and in most Christian hearts — there is an unofficial but well-understood hierarchy of sins. At the top are the ones we talk about: serious sexual sin, addiction, violence, apostasy. These are the sins that produce crisis, that get people removed from leadership, that we are genuinely alarmed by. We know these are serious. We have sermons about them.

But there is a lower tier — a large and populous tier — of sins that we have collectively agreed not to alarm ourselves too much about. We worry about them in the abstract. We acknowledge them when asked. But we do not really fight them, because fighting them would require confronting ourselves and one another in ways that would be genuinely uncomfortable for everyone in the room. And since everyone in the room shares most of them, there is an implicit social contract: we will not press too hard here.

Jerry Bridges, in his book Respectable Sins, named this problem with devastating clarity. He listed what he found when he looked honestly at the sins that evangelical Christians tolerate most readily: anxiety and worry. Discontentment and ingratitude. Impatience and irritability. Judgmentalism and self-righteousness. Envy, jealousy, and covetousness. Gossip, slander, and the more socially acceptable forms of contempt. Gluttony. Pride — in its subtler forms.

None of these is on the "dramatic sins" list. None of them is likely to get someone removed from a church role. But every one of them is in the Bible's taxonomy of what grieves the Spirit, what damages relationships, and what, left unchecked, quietly hollows out a soul.

Why We Have Given Them a Pass

The normalization of respectable sins happens through a reliable mechanism: universality as excuse. Because these sins are so common — because nearly everyone in the room is managing anxiety, snapping at family members, nursing a low-level judgmentalism toward certain types of people, scrolling through someone else's life with quiet envy — we have quietly reclassified them. Not as sins, exactly. As human nature. As things to manage rather than repent of. As the water we all swim in.

Paul does not grant this reclassification. Galatians 5:21 concludes the list with a warning: "those who do such things will not inherit the kingdom of God." This is not a verse that is comfortable to sit with, and we will handle what it does and does not mean more carefully in later studies. But for now, notice what it refuses to do: it does not sort the list by severity and draw a line below which God stops caring. Envy is on the same list as those things.

A Closer Look at the Usual Suspects

Matthew 6:25–34 contains Jesus's extended teaching on anxiety. It ends with the famous words: "Therefore do not be anxious about tomorrow, for tomorrow will be anxious for itself. Sufficient for the day is its own trouble" (Matthew 6:34). Most Christians know this passage. Most Christians worry anyway, and most have made a quiet peace with that gap.

The passage is not primarily a therapeutic technique for reducing stress. It is a theological statement: anxiety, in its chronic form, is a failure of trust in the Father who knows what we need and has committed to provide it. That does not make all anxiety sin — not all anxiety is willful, and there is a difference between the acute anxiety of genuine crisis and the habitual, low-grade worry that has simply become a way of life. But Bridges' point is well-taken: most of us have never really grappled with our anxiety as something God wants us to actually bring to him in faith, rather than simply manage.

Irritability is perhaps the most socially tolerated sin in Christian homes. We are patient in public. We are impatient with the people we love most — our spouses, our children, our parents — because with them we have dropped the performance and what is underneath comes out. The child who is slow, the spouse who asks the wrong question at the wrong moment, the driver who is going three miles per hour below the speed limit: these are where our actual character appears.

Proverbs 14:29 says: "Whoever is slow to anger has great understanding, but he who has a hasty temper exalts folly." Proverbs does not make exceptions for traffic.

Gossip, in its most common form, does not feel like gossip. It feels like care — "I'm worried about them, have you heard what's been going on?" It feels like the kind of honesty that real friendship requires: "I think I understand why they are the way they are." It feels like the responsible thing, spiritually: "We need to pray for them — let me tell you what's happening." These are the things we say because we want to connect, to be trusted, to help. The desire underneath is not always bad. But accuracy does not determine whether sharing is right, and neither does the warmth of the feeling that prompted it. The question is: does this person have a legitimate need to know this? And: am I sharing it in a way that builds this person up in the eyes of my listener, or tears them down?

Judgmentalism is gossip's cousin — the habit of making confident, negative assessments of other people's character from a position of comfortable superiority. It is not the same as discernment, though it uses discernment's language. Discernment is humble and provisional. Judgmentalism is settled and satisfied.

Social media has made envy endemic in a way that no previous generation has experienced. The carefully curated lives of others — their vacations, their accomplishments, their marriages, their children — appear in an unbroken stream. Envy needs no invitation; it arrives unbidden and settles quietly. A slight tightening when a friend's good news arrives. A mild pleasure when something goes wrong for someone whose success made you feel small. We rarely call this envy. But Galatians 5:21 does.

Why This Matters

It matters for the same reason everything in this study matters: sin is not primarily a problem of degree. It is a problem of allegiance. Every respectable sin, however modest on our cultural scale, is a small act of trusting something other than God to meet a need that only God can actually meet. Anxiety is trusting our own worry over God's care. Irritability is trusting our own comfort over other people's dignity. Envy is trusting our own instinct about what constitutes a good life over God's actual provision. Gossip is trusting our own desire to be interesting over our neighbor's reputation.

None of this is dramatic. All of it is real. And all of it, by the Spirit's power, can be fought — not because we generate the strength to defeat it ourselves, but because the same risen Christ who freed us from sin's penalty is actively freeing us from its dominion.

The Ferguson Lens

Respectable sins are the natural habitat of both ditches, but the residents look different. The legalist tends to see others' respectable sins very clearly — judgmentalism is practically a spiritual gift in the legalist ditch — while managing their own through the language of self-awareness. I know I can be impatient, but at least I'm working on it. The antinomian tends to absorb respectable sins into their theology: anxiety becomes "struggling with trust," gossip becomes "processing in community," chronic irritability becomes "I'm just being authentic." Both are keeping the sins without the label.

Christ, Our Anchor

It is easy to think of Christ's atoning work as covering the dramatic sins — the ones we are genuinely ashamed of, the ones that would scandalize a congregation. But the active obedience of Jesus, which is the other half of the gospel — his perfect life lived in our place — covered the respectable sins too. Every moment of irritability you have managed to avoid naming as sin, every envy you have justified, every anxious sleepless night: these were part of what the Father laid upon him.

Isaiah 53:6 says, "All we like sheep have gone astray; we have turned — every one — to his own way; and the LORD has laid on him the iniquity of us all." The word "all" does not sort by severity. And this truth — that Christ bore the full weight of the respectable sins too — is not a reason to minimize them further. It is a reason to take them more seriously: they were costly enough to require the cross. And it is a reason to fight them with hope: the same grace that covered them is the grace that, by the Spirit, is slowly defeating them.

For reflection & discussion

  • Without naming anyone else, which of the "usual suspects" in this study — anxiety, irritability, judgmentalism, gossip, envy — do you find most present in your own life? What have you told yourself about it?
  • The study describes an "implicit social contract" in Christian communities about which sins we don't press on. Have you experienced this? What happens when someone breaks the contract and names something honestly?
  • Galatians 5:21 places envy in the same list as serious sexual sin and then warns about inheriting the kingdom. How do you handle a verse like that — do you feel its weight, or do you find ways to soften it? Why?
  • The study reframes respectable sins as "small acts of trusting something other than God." Does that framing change how you feel about them? What does your anxiety or irritability say you are trusting?
  • Is there a respectable sin you have made peace with because everyone around you has made peace with it too? What would it mean to stop making that peace?
