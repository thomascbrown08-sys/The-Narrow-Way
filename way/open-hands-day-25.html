Day 25

Forgiveness and Reconciliation Are Not the Same

Never pay back evil with more evil. Do things in such a way that everyone can see you are honorable. Do all that you can to live in peace with everyone.

Dear friends, never take revenge. Leave that to the righteous anger of God. For the Scriptures say, "I will take revenge; I will pay them back," says the LORD.

Instead, "If your enemies are hungry, feed them. If they are thirsty, give them something to drink. In doing this, you will heap burning coals of shame on their heads."

Don't let evil overcome you, but overcome evil with good. Luke 17:3–4 (NLT) "If another believer sins, rebuke that person; then if there is repentance, forgive." Romans 12:17–21 (NLT)

Yesterday we defined forgiveness as releasing a claim — canceling a debt in your own heart. Today we need to draw a line that is often blurred in a way that causes real harm.

Forgiveness is one-sided. It is something that happens in your heart. You can forgive someone who is dead, who has never apologized, who doesn't know you've been hurt. You can forgive someone who is completely unrepentant. Forgiveness doesn't require anything from the other person.

Reconciliation is two-sided. It requires two parties. Notice Luke 17:3: "if there is repentance, forgive." Repentance is named as the condition for reconciliation — not for forgiveness, but for the restoration of relationship. You can fully forgive someone and still not be in restored relationship with them if they have not repented and changed behavior.

Romans 12:18 contains one of the most honest phrases in the New Testament: "If possible, so far as it depends on you, live peaceably with all." Notice the conditions: "if possible" and "so far as it depends on you." Paul is realistic. Sometimes reconciliation is not possible — the other party may be unrepentant, unsafe, or absent. Your responsibility is what depends on you.

This distinction matters enormously for people who have experienced abuse, betrayal, or serious harm. You are not required to re-enter a relationship with someone who has harmed you in order to forgive them. Forgiveness releases you from bitterness. Reconciliation is a separate question, with different conditions. You can be free while still maintaining appropriate distance.

For reflection

Are you currently confusing forgiveness and reconciliation — either demanding more of yourself than God does, or avoiding forgiveness because you fear it means you have to restore a relationship that isn't safe?
