Fixing Our Gaze

A 90-day devotional whose one purpose is to behold the person of Jesus Christ — the companion for the whole journey, returning the pilgrim again and again to the steady gaze that finishes the race.

"...let us run with endurance the race that is set before us, looking to Jesus, the founder and perfecter of our faith." Hebrews 12:1–2 (ESV)

Every other study on this site belongs to a place — a landmark, a room, a peak. This one belongs to the whole road. Fixing Our Gaze is the companion meant to walk beside a pilgrim through every country of the journey, from the City of Destruction to the gates of the Celestial City, because it does the one thing that every stretch of the road requires equally: it looks at Jesus. The writer of Hebrews gave the image — let us run with endurance the race set before us, fixing our eyes on Jesus — and named the secret of finishing: not staring at the road, not measuring our own progress, but keeping our eyes on the one who runs ahead and waits at the end.

It is a ninety-day journey, the longest of all the companions, and its purpose is the simplest. Not to accumulate more doctrines about Christ, though doctrine has its rightful and necessary place; not to marshal more arguments on his behalf, though the road has rooms for those too. Its single, repeated aim is to behold the Person himself — to look, day after day, at who Jesus is, until the looking begins to change the one who looks.

In plain terms

This is a 90-day devotional whose one purpose is to look at Jesus — not just to learn facts about him, but to gaze at the Person, day by day, the way you'd come to know a face you love. It's the companion for the whole journey, not any single stop.

Why the gaze, and not merely the study

There is a kind of knowing that information cannot give. You can know every fact about a person and still not know them; and you can be changed by beholding someone in a way that no list of facts about them could ever accomplish. The Christian life, at its center, is not finally the mastery of a system but the beholding of a Person — and the New Testament makes the startling promise that beholding him is itself transforming: that as we look, with unveiled faces, at the glory of the Lord, we are changed into the same image, from one degree of glory to the next. We become like what we look at. This is why the gaze is not a beginner's exercise to be outgrown but the lifelong heart of the thing. The arguments clear the way and the doctrines guard the truth — but it is the looking that remakes the soul.

A companion for every countryThe fixed point on the horizon

Because it belongs to the whole journey, this companion is built to walk every stretch of it. In the City of Destruction, it is the face that first draws a captive's eyes upward. In the valleys of Humiliation and the Shadow, it is the fixed point a pilgrim holds to when the ground gives way. On the Enchanted Ground, it is the gaze that keeps a drowsy soul awake. In Beulah, it is the One whose nearness makes the air sweet. A runner who watches his own feet stumbles; a runner who watches the finish line runs. Ninety days is not long to spend learning to keep one's eyes lifted to the one fixed and faithful point on the whole horizon — the face turned toward us, that we, slowly, learn to turn toward in return.

We become like what we look at. The arguments clear the way and the doctrines guard the truth — but it is the looking that remakes the soul.

So this companion has no single home on the map, because its home is the road itself. Wherever you are on the journey — just set out from the City, toiling up the Hill, resting in the Palace, trembling with wonder in Beulah — the counsel is the same, and it is the counsel that carries a pilgrim all the way home: keep your eyes on Jesus. Everything else on this entire site exists to return you, in the end, to that one steady gaze.
