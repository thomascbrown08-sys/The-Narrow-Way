Day 84

He Is Able to Save Completely

Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, the letter to the Hebrews was written to people who were tempted to give up — to turn back from Christ toward something that seemed more familiar, less costly, less demanding of total commitment. The answer given them was you: you are the better covenant, the better priest, the better sacrifice. You are able to save completely those who come to God through you. Let that ability be the anchor of our faith today. Amen.

"Therefore he is able to save completely those who come to God through him, because he always lives to intercede for them." Hebrews 7:25, NIV

We returned to Hebrews 7:25 on Day 79 in a different translation to explore his permanent priesthood. Today we dwell in it again — in the NIV's "save completely" — because the completeness deserves its own day. Not partially. Not provisionally. Not "save to a reasonable extent given the circumstances." Completely. The Greek eis to panteles — which we also met in the more literal "to the uttermost" — means all the way through, to the furthest possible end, with nothing left unaddressed.

The letter to the Hebrews is addressed to people on the edge of giving up. Jewish Christians who have paid a real cost for their faith — public contempt, loss of social standing, perhaps imprisonment — and who are being tempted to return to the familiar institutions of Judaism. The argument the writer makes is not primarily an argument about their courage or endurance. It is an argument about the adequacy of Christ. He is better. His priesthood is better. His sacrifice is better. His covenant is better. And the practical conclusion is: hold on, because the one you are holding on to is fully able to bring what he has started to its complete conclusion.

He is able to save completely. There is a question that haunts the edges of sincere Christian faith — the question that fear asks in the middle of the night: is he able to save someone like me? Not humanity in general, not the respectable sinner who needs mild adjustment, but me — with this history, this pattern of failure, this besetting sin that has not yet yielded, this accumulation of years of inconsistency? The verse does not address categories. It addresses the condition of coming: those who come to God through him. The condition is not moral rectitude. It is coming — through him, to God, in the posture of one who has no other path and no other advocate.

The ground of the completeness is stated again: because he always lives to intercede. The permanence of his priesthood is the mechanism of the completeness of the salvation. If there were a time when his intercession ceased — if the high priest retired, if the advocate stopped presenting the case, if the one who always lives to intercede were somehow no longer always living — then the completeness would be conditional. But the permanent life grounds the complete salvation. He will not stop interceding before the salvation is complete. He will not step down from the priesthood before the last sheep is safely gathered. He is able because he always lives — and he always lives because he is the eternal Son, risen and ascended and interceding, whose life has no terminus.

C.H. Spurgeon preached on this text and asked his congregation: can you find a sinner so fallen, so long-wandering, so deeply scarred by their own choices, that this verse would not apply to them? Not to those who refuse to come. The condition is real: they must come. But for any who come — however haltingly, however late, however uncertain of their welcome — he is able to save them completely. The completely is not a ceiling he might bump against with more difficult cases. It is a description of the nature of the salvation he provides: total, terminal, thorough, reaching every corner of the need and leaving nothing unaddressed.

Come, then. Come through him. He is able.

For reflection

  • "He is able to save completely those who come." The condition is coming, not qualifying. Is there a part of yourself — a sin, a history, a pattern — that you have treated as outside the reach of complete salvation, as a case too difficult for him? What would it mean to bring that specific thing to the one who is able to save it completely?
  • The letter to the Hebrews was written to people tempted to give up. What is the specific argument the writer makes against giving up, and how does it apply to whatever you find hardest to persist in? Is the difficulty in your faith a reason to go back, or a reason to go further in?
  • "Completely" — nothing unaddressed, all the way through to the final end. What area of your life do you most need to receive as covered by the completeness of his saving work, rather than as an ongoing project whose outcome is uncertain?

Going Deeper (optional)

The word sōzein — to save — in Hebrews 7:25 encompasses more than the moment of initial justification. In the New Testament, salvation operates in three tenses: we have been saved (justification, the past completed act), we are being saved (sanctification, the present ongoing process), and we will be saved (glorification, the future completed state). The "completely" of Hebrews 7:25 reaches across all three: the same Christ who justifies the sinner also sanctifies the believer and will glorify the saint. He is not merely the initiator of salvation who then leaves the rest to the believer's own effort. He is the completer — the author and perfecter of faith (Hebrews 12:2), who finishes what he begins.

The Hebrews argument for Christ's superiority runs through chapters 1–10 and reaches its climax in the comparison between the Levitical priesthood (whose repeated sacrifices demonstrated that sin's problem was not solved) and Christ's single, sufficient, permanent sacrifice (which solved it once for all). The repeated sacrifices of the old covenant were not evidence of God's half-heartedness; they were the honest acknowledgment that no animal sacrifice could address the root of the problem. Christ's one sacrifice can — and does — address it completely, because he offered himself (Hebrews 9:14), and the infinite value of the offering is proportionate to the infinite depth of the need. Complete salvation requires a complete Savior. The letter's argument is simply: he is that Savior. And because he always lives, the salvation he provides never runs short.
