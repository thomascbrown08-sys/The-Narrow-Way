The Tradition Trap

The Tradition Trap

Habit granted the authority that belongs to Scripture alone.

The tactic. Institutional habit is treated as if it carried the authority of Scripture — ‘we have always done it this way’ standing in for ‘the Bible requires it.’

How it looksThe shape of the move

Someone proposes a reasonable change, with a case from Scripture or pastoral wisdom. The reply comes not as a counter-argument from Scripture but as an argument from history: we have always done it this way. The weight being appealed to is not a biblical command but accumulated habit — the sheer gravity of the past functioning as though it were a theological argument against the present.

The virtue it twistsWhy good people fall for it

The instinct is not worthless; in fact it is wise. Traditions exist for reasons, and humility honors what earlier generations learned at a cost we may not see. Chesterton’s fence stands: do not tear down what you do not yet understand. The trap is not respecting tradition — it is the failure to distinguish what history requires from what Scripture requires, and letting the first borrow the authority of the second.

A scene — how it might run

Imagine proposing a small change to how the church does something — with a thoughtful reason — and being met not with ‘here is why Scripture would caution against that’ but with a slightly wounded ‘this is how we’ve always done it.’ The conversation ends there, as if that settled the matter biblically. But it hasn’t; it has only revealed that a human habit has quietly been granted divine weight. Press the point and you’re ‘disrespecting’ the church; accept it and a good and faithful idea dies for no reason Scripture would recognize.

What it costsThe damage it does

It is exactly the move Jesus confronted in Mark 7: he did not deny that the tradition of handwashing existed — he denied its authority, saying, ‘you leave the commandment of God and hold to the tradition of men.’ The cost is that a human habit, granted the weight of God’s command, ends up silencing an actual good — and the church slowly mistakes its customs for its convictions.

Disarming itThe peacemaker’s move, up close

Separate the two kinds of weight, without embarrassment. The move is to ask, plainly and without disrespect: ‘Can we locate this in Scripture specifically — is it a biblical requirement, or a practice that has served us well? Both deserve respect, but different kinds of respect.’ That single distinction defuses most of the trap, because it does not attack the tradition; it simply asks the tradition to say honestly which kind of thing it is.

Then honor the tradition properly by asking what it was for — the goal it was protecting — and whether the proposed change might serve that very goal better. In yourself, the discipline is to notice when your defense of a practice has run out of Scripture and is reaching for ‘we’ve always’ — that is the moment to admit you are defending a custom, which may be wise but cannot be binding the way a command is. Respect the fence; just be honest about whether God built it or we did.

📖 The Scripture behind this Directwhat’s this?›

The claim: Human tradition, however venerable, does not carry the authority of God’s Word; Jesus rebuked treating the commandments of men as though they were the commandment of God.

  • Mark 7:8 (ESV) — “You leave the commandment of God and hold to the tradition of men.”Jesus draws the exact line: God’s commandment is one thing, human tradition another.
  • Colossians 2:8 (ESV) — “See to it that no one takes you captive by philosophy … according to human tradition … and not according to Christ.”Human tradition can take the church captive when it displaces Christ’s authority.
  • 2 Timothy 3:16–17 (ESV) — “All Scripture is breathed out by God and profitable … that the man of God may be complete.”Scripture, not custom, is the God-breathed and sufficient rule.
The reasoning (direct)Marked direct because Jesus states the distinction in plain words: to ‘hold to the tradition of men’ while leaving ‘the commandment of God’ is to confuse two different authorities (Mark 7:8). Paul warns against captivity to ‘human tradition … not according to Christ’ (Colossians 2:8), and grounds the rule in God-breathed Scripture (2 Timothy 3:16–17). The disarming move — asking whether a practice is a biblical requirement or an honored custom — simply applies the line Jesus himself drew, refusing to let habit borrow the authority that belongs to Scripture alone.

The distinction between God's command and human tradition is stated directly by Jesus; honoring a tradition while denying it Scripture's authority follows from it.
