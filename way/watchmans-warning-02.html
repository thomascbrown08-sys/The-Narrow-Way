Study 2

The Two Ditches

Legalism, antinomianism, and what lies underneath both

"What shall we say then? Are we to continue in sin that grace may abound? By no means! How can we who died to sin still live in it?" Romans 6:1–2 (ESV)
"Come to me, all who labor and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest. Take my yoke upon you, and learn from me, for I am gentle and lowly in heart, and you will find rest for your souls." Matthew 11:28–29 (ESV)

This study introduces a diagnostic tool that will appear throughout everything that follows. Consider it a pair of lenses — held up not to accuse but to help us see ourselves more clearly, so we can understand the patterns beneath the surface of our spiritual lives.

A Road With Two Ditches

Imagine a road through the middle of Christian experience. The road runs between two ditches. The ditches have names: legalism on the left, antinomianism on the right. Both look, from a distance, like they might be roads. Both are, in fact, places where people get stuck and sometimes badly hurt.

These two errors are ancient. They have been the church's recurring temptations in every generation. They appear not only as theological positions but as moods, habits, and patterns of life that most of us fall into without ever consciously choosing them. Understanding them — really understanding them, not just learning the vocabulary — is one of the most practically useful things a Christian can do.

A Controversy in Old Scotland

In the early eighteenth century, a controversy erupted in the Church of Scotland that would eventually yield one of the most helpful frameworks for diagnosing these errors. A small book called The Marrow of Modern Divinity — originally written in the seventeenth century — was republished and began circulating among Scottish ministers. A group of pastors, later called the "Marrow Men," saw in it a valuable corrective to something they thought was quietly going wrong in their church.

What was going wrong? The church's official leadership had become suspicious that the emphasis on free grace — the gospel offer held out freely to all people — was too dangerous. If you told everyone that salvation was freely available without conditions, wouldn't people just abuse it? Wouldn't they hear "free grace" and think they could live however they liked? So the church began adding subtle conditions: you must show signs of worthiness; you must prepare yourself; you must bring a certain quality of repentance before you can come to Christ. This was not called legalism. It was called responsible teaching. But the Marrow Men recognized it as a distortion of the gospel.

Sinclair Ferguson, a Scottish theologian, returned to this controversy in his remarkable book The Whole Christ and drew from it a set of observations that apply far beyond eighteenth-century Scotland. His central insight is worth sitting with carefully, because it runs against our natural assumptions.

The Surprising Diagnosis

Here is what Ferguson observed: legalism and antinomianism are not actually opposites. They share the same root.

We tend to think of them as polar extremes — one error adding rules, the other throwing them off. But Ferguson points to something deeper. Both errors, he argues, arise from the same underlying problem: a distorted, untrusting view of God. Neither the legalist nor the antinomian has truly come to know and trust God as he has revealed himself in the gospel.

Let's look at each ditch closely.

The legalist is someone whose relationship with God is fundamentally shaped by performance. This does not usually mean they believe their works literally earn salvation — most evangelical Christians would reject that idea if stated bluntly. But it shows up in more subtle ways. The legalist feels closer to God when the morning's devotional went well, and further away when it didn't. They bring a tally sheet to prayer, even if they don't know it. They read the promises of God and add a quiet condition: for people who are doing better than I am.

The driving question of legalism is: Am I good enough? And the answer is always either an anxious "maybe" or a proud "yes" — neither of which is the restful freedom the gospel actually offers.

Legalism is not really about being too serious about holiness. It is about a misplaced seriousness — trying to earn or maintain something that has already been given. It treats the law as the basis of one's standing with God rather than as the guide for someone whose standing is already secure in Christ.

The antinomian has, at some level, heard the word "grace" and drawn the wrong conclusion: that it means no standards. Anti-nomos means literally "against the law." The antinomian may be able to quote Romans 6:14 ("for you are not under law but under grace") but has heard it as the law no longer matters rather than as what it actually means: the law is no longer the basis of your standing before God.

In practice, antinomianism often shows up not as a stated position but as a lifestyle habit. "I already asked forgiveness for this." "God knows my heart." "Nobody's perfect." "I'm under grace, not law." These phrases, used as a reason not to fight sin, are antinomianism — not a doctrine, but a pattern of life that has domesticated grace until it no longer has any power to challenge anything.

Antinomianism is not really about taking grace too seriously. It is about taking a shrunken version of grace — grace as mere forgiveness, stripped of its transforming power — and using that diminished grace as a ceiling below which no challenge can reach.

The Shared Root

Here is Ferguson's penetrating observation: both errors misunderstand the same thing. The legalist looks at God and sees primarily a demanding standard to be met. The antinomian looks at God and sees primarily a permissive pardon to be exploited. Both have failed to see God as he actually is — a Father who both requires everything and has provided everything in his Son, who both commands and enables, who both calls us to holiness and supplies the power to walk in it.

In short: both errors fail to trust God. The legalist doesn't trust that God's love is real without performance. The antinomian doesn't trust that God's love is serious — that it actually cares what you become. Neither has arrived at the place where they can rest in the gospel and, from that rest, genuinely want to obey.

This is why the solution to both problems is not "try to find the middle." The solution is more of Christ — a deeper, fuller, more confident knowledge of the God who justifies the ungodly and then, by his Spirit, begins to change them from the inside out.

Why This Matters for Everything That Follows

You will encounter these two patterns repeatedly in this study. Pride tends to wear the legalist costume in church and the antinomian costume at home. Self-deception serves both masters. Respectable sins are the natural habitat of the antinomian and the particular blind spot of the legalist. Spiritual apathy is often the final destination of someone who tried legalism, found it exhausting, and quietly gave up without ever finding the genuine alternative.

When you encounter the Ferguson Lens in later studies — marked with a distinctive heading — it is an invitation to ask: which ditch is this sin or pattern more likely to live in? And what does that tell me about what is actually happening underneath?

The Ferguson Lens — Summary

Legalism and antinomianism are not theological opposites. They share a root: a failure to trust God as he truly is — both free in his grace and serious in his holiness. The answer to both is not a balance between them, but a deeper knowledge of Christ, in whom grace and obedience are perfectly united. We are saved by his obedience, not ours — and that truth, received deeply, is precisely what frees genuine obedience to grow.

Christ, Our Anchor

Jesus is the only human being who never fell into either ditch. He is the one in whom grace and obedience are not in tension but perfectly unified. He obeyed not from fear of punishment or hope of reward, but from love for his Father — the kind of obedience that legalism pretends to have and antinomianism claims not to need.

The gospel offer is this: what Jesus did counts for you. His perfect obedience, his unblemished record, his unswerving trust in the Father — these are credited to your account at the moment you believe (Romans 4:5; 2 Corinthians 5:21). This is the ground that makes legalism unnecessary: your standing does not depend on your obedience. And it is the ground that makes antinomianism impossible: the God who has done this for you is not indifferent to what you do with the life he has redeemed.

The goal of this study is to help you live more fully from that ground — not reaching for a standing you already have, and not squandering the life you have been given.

For reflection & discussion

  • If you had to honestly assess your default tendency, do you lean more toward the legalist ditch or the antinomian one? What does that look like in your daily life — not as a theological position, but as a felt pattern?
  • The study says that legalism and antinomianism both fail because neither truly trusts God as he is. Does that framing surprise you? How does it change your understanding of either error?
  • Can you think of phrases or habits in your own spiritual life that might be functioning as antinomianism without being called that? ("God knows my heart." "Nobody's perfect." And so on.)
  • What would it look like to obey God not out of fear or habit, but from genuine love and trust? Does that feel attainable, or distant? Why?
  • The study says the answer to both ditches is "more of Christ — a deeper, fuller knowledge." What would it take to pursue that this week, practically?
