Day 22

Why Careful Skepticism Is Warranted

For false christs and false prophets will arise and perform great signs and wonders, so as to lead astray, if possible, even the elect. Matthew 24:24 (ESV)
And no wonder, for even Satan disguises himself as an angel of light. 2 Corinthians 11:14 (ESV)

The continuationist case is serious. And sustained, careful skepticism about claimed supernatural gifts is also warranted. Both things are true simultaneously, and holding them together is what the 'skeptical continuationist' position actually requires.

Jesus himself warns that false signs and wonders will be part of the end-times deception — powerful enough to potentially mislead even believers. Paul warns that the enemy is capable of impersonating light itself. These are not theoretical concerns. They are reasons for rigorous standards, not credulity.

The practical pattern of charismatic movements across church history shows significant recurring problems: claimed prophecies that don't come to pass, leaders who claim spiritual authority that becomes a tool for control, emotional manipulation that produces experiences rather than genuine transformation, and a drift toward experience-centered spirituality that gradually displaces Scripture. These are not marginal cases. They are common enough to constitute a pattern worth taking seriously.

The landing point is not cessationism — the gifts may well continue. The landing point is a high bar for verification, a deep commitment to Scripture as the measure of all claimed experience, and a strong preference for the ordinary means of grace (Word, prayer, sacrament, community) as the primary channels through which God reliably works. Be open. Be very careful.

Put It Into Practice

Apply three tests to a claimed supernatural experience you've personally encountered or observed: Does it accord with Scripture? Does it point toward Christ and produce Christlike fruit? Is it submitted to community accountability rather than claimed as private, unchallengeable authority?

For reflection

What's the difference between being open to the Spirit and being credulous? Where is the line, and how do you find it? What recurring patterns in charismatic movements give you pause? What genuine goods do you also observe? How does a strong commitment to Scripture-centered spirituality protect against the problems identified in this lesson — without shutting the door on the Spirit's genuine work?
