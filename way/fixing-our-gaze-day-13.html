Day 13

He Was There in the Beginning

Opening Prayer

Lord, before you spoke the world into being, there was already joy within your life — a delight that preceded creation and did not require it. You were not lonely; you were not incomplete; you needed nothing. And yet you made the world, and made us, and your ancient Wisdom rejoiced in it. Let us receive today the staggering gift of being objects of that joy — anticipated, not accidental; delighted in, not merely tolerated. Amen.

"…then I was beside him, like a master workman, and I was daily his delight, rejoicing before him always, rejoicing in his inhabited world and delighting in the children of man." Proverbs 8:30–31, ESV

In Proverbs 8, Wisdom speaks in first person. This is one of Scripture's most striking literary forms — personification, the giving of voice to an attribute or quality as though it were a person. And what Wisdom says about herself, in this passage, reaches deeper than any other description of God's inner life prior to the New Testament.

"Then I was beside him, like a master workman, and I was daily his delight, rejoicing before him always." Before the world was made. Before any morning had dawned, before any creature had drawn a first breath — there was Wisdom, beside God, as companion and craftsman, delighting and being delighted in. The creation scene in Genesis says what God did. This passage in Proverbs glimpses what it was like — the interior of the act, the joy in which it was done.

The New Testament makes a decisive interpretive move: it identifies this Wisdom, personified in Proverbs, with the person of Jesus Christ. Paul writes that Christ is "the power of God and the wisdom of God" (1 Corinthians 1:24), and that in him "are hidden all the treasures of wisdom and knowledge" (Colossians 2:3). John calls him the Logos — the Word, the rational and creative principle of the universe — "through whom all things were made" (John 1:3). Wisdom in Proverbs is not a literal description of the pre-incarnate Son in the way John 1 is; she is a literary personification pointing forward, a shadow reaching ahead of the substance. But the substance is Christ, and the New Testament makes this explicit. He is the one through whom and for whom creation was made. He is the one who was "beside him, like a master workman."

What arrests us in Proverbs 8, though, is not the craftsmanship but the delight. The passage could have emphasized the technical skill of Wisdom's creative work — and it does, briefly. But it lingers on the joy. Daily his delight. Rejoicing before him always. Rejoicing in his inhabited world and delighting in the children of man. This is God at work, and his work is characterized by happiness. Not laborious effort, not reluctant obligation, not divine duty grimly discharged. Rejoicing. Delighting.

G.K. Chesterton once pressed on this observation in one of the most unusual and luminous passages in all of his writing: "It is possible that God is strong enough to exult in monotony. It is possible that God says every morning, 'Do it again,' to the sun; and every evening, 'Do it again,' to the moon. It may not be automatic necessity that makes all daisies alike; it may be that God makes every daisy separately, but has never got tired of making them. It may be that He has the eternal appetite of infancy; for we have sinned and grown old, and our Father is younger than we."

The children of man are the last thing named in this litany of delight: "delighting in the children of man." Before any human being had done anything to merit it, before the image of God had been marred by the fall, before history had produced a single figure worth noticing — Wisdom delighted in the children of man. You are not, on this account, an accident. You are not a byproduct of creative overflow that God must now manage. You were anticipated. The one who made you was glad to do it.

And this is what Paul means in Ephesians 1:4 when he says we were "chosen in him before the foundation of the world" — not that we were chosen in cold predestination but that we were chosen in love, in the context of this ancient delight. The divine creativity was never impersonal. It has always had a face, a name, a delight. And that face — we now know — is the face of Jesus Christ, in whom all the fullness of wisdom has taken flesh, and in whose company the ancient joy still rejoices.

For reflection

  • Wisdom in Proverbs 8 is "delighting in the children of man" before any of them have done anything to deserve it. What does it mean to you that you were an object of divine delight before you existed — that you are anticipated, not merely accommodated?
  • Paul says in Christ "are hidden all the treasures of wisdom and knowledge" (Colossians 2:3). What does it mean that wisdom is a Person to be known rather than merely a quality to be acquired? How does this change how you pursue understanding and discernment?
  • Chesterton imagined God as "younger than we" — still delighting in each daisy, having never grown tired of making them. Where in the ordinary, repeated fabric of your daily life might God's ancient delight still be active and visible — if you had eyes to see it?

Going Deeper (optional)

A word of careful theological orientation is worth offering here. The relationship between Proverbs 8's Wisdom and the Logos of John 1 has been a matter of careful attention throughout church history. The early Fathers — Justin Martyr, Origen, and Augustine — read Proverbs 8 as a direct description of the pre-incarnate Son. The Reformers were more cautious, recognizing that Proverbs 8 uses the literary device of personification (Lady Wisdom is also contrasted with Lady Folly in Proverbs 7 and 9) and that reading it as a literal personal pre-existence requires importing more than the text itself asserts.

The Reformed position — maintained carefully by Geerhardus Vos, Bavinck, and others — holds that the Wisdom of Proverbs 8 is a personification of God's own creative wisdom, which finds its personal fulfillment in the Son. Paul's declaration that Christ is "the wisdom of God" (1 Corinthians 1:24) is the New Testament key: not that Proverbs 8 directly depicts the pre-incarnate Logos, but that the Wisdom sketched there is made flesh in him. The shadow becomes substance. The poem becomes Person. This reading is more careful exegetically and no less glorious in its implications: when we see Wisdom delighting in creation and in the children of man, we are seeing the quality that Christ fully embodies — a joy in making, in giving, in the flourishing of the ones he has made. That joy has not diminished. It is looking at you still.
