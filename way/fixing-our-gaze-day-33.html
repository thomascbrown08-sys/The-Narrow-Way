Day 33

The Lord's Prayer

Opening Prayer

Father, the disciples asked Jesus to teach them to pray — and the prayer he gave them assumes everything we have been learning to see about who he is. It assumes we can call God Father. It assumes the kingdom is coming. It assumes we are needy, indebted, and in danger. It assumes that God is good enough to address all three. Let this prayer form us today, not merely inform us. Amen.

"Our Father in heaven, hallowed be Your name. Your kingdom come. Your will be done on earth as it is in heaven. Give us this day our daily bread. And forgive us our debts, as we forgive our debtors. And do not lead us into temptation, but deliver us from the evil one." Matthew 6:9–13, NKJV

The prayer Jesus gives his disciples in Matthew 6 is sometimes called the Lord's Prayer, though it is more accurately the disciples' prayer — the one Jesus teaches rather than prays himself (he would not need to ask for forgiveness of debts). Its familiarity can be its greatest enemy: words prayed since childhood tend to flow past the mind without landing. Today, read them slowly. Each phrase is load-bearing.

Our Father in heaven. Before any request is made, the prayer establishes a relationship. Not "O omnipotent Creator" or "sovereign Judge" or even "holy God" — though all of these are true. Father. And not merely Father but our Father — spoken in community, binding those who pray it to one another in a shared family identity. To call God Father is, in the mouth of a disciple, to claim an identity given not by birth but by adoption — the adoption that Paul says was accomplished by the sending of the Son (Galatians 4:4–6). We can say Father only because he has made us children, and he has made us children only by sending the Son, and the Son only by the Spirit who cries Abba in our hearts. The first two words of the prayer contain the entire gospel.

Hallowed be your name. Your kingdom come. Your will be done on earth as it is in heaven. Three petitions, all focused entirely on God before the prayer reaches us. This structure is deliberate and formative. Before we ask for anything we need, we ask for what God deserves — for his name to be treated as holy, for his reign to extend, for his will to saturate the earth the way it saturates heaven. This sequence does not come naturally to us. Our natural prayer begins with our needs and reaches God as an afterthought. Jesus reverses the order, not because God doesn't care about our needs, but because the needs will be addressed rightly only if they are addressed within the frame of who God is and what he is doing.

Give us this day our daily bread. When the prayer finally turns to us, it asks for the most basic thing: enough for today. Not security for the year. Not provision confirmed in advance. Today's bread. This petition is a daily act of trust — a deliberate refusal to grip the future, a repeated acknowledgment that dependence on the Father is not a stage to graduate from but a posture to inhabit forever. The manna in the wilderness fell fresh each morning and could not be stored (Exodus 16:19–20). This prayer is manna-shaped: it trains the soul toward daily reception rather than accumulated security.

Forgive us our debts, as we forgive our debtors. The metaphor is financial: debt. What we owe God that we cannot repay. And the prayer links our asking for forgiveness to our extending of it — not as a condition (as if our forgiving earns God's forgiveness), but as a consequence (those who have received unmeasured grace and then refuse to extend small portions of it to others have not yet understood what they received). The parable of the unmerciful servant in Matthew 18 unpacks exactly this dynamic. The one who has been forgiven ten thousand talents and chokes a fellow servant over a hundred denarii has missed the point of the forgiveness entirely.

Deliver us from the evil one. The prayer ends not with comfort but with battle. There is an adversary. There is danger. There is something to be delivered from. The one who gave his disciples this prayer is the same one who faced the adversary in the wilderness, who came to destroy the works of the evil one (1 John 3:8), who told his disciples he had already overcome the world (John 16:33). The final petition anchors us in that victory — not our own resilience, but his. Deliver us. We cannot deliver ourselves. And he is able.

For reflection

  • The prayer opens with God-focused petitions before reaching human needs — hallowed name, coming kingdom, done will, before daily bread. Does your own prayer life follow this sequence or tend to invert it? What would change in your prayers if you spent the first portion entirely focused on God and his concerns?
  • "Give us this day our daily bread" — enough for today, not security for the year. Where in your life are you most anxious about tomorrow's provision? What would it mean to practice the posture this petition assumes: daily trust rather than accumulated security?
  • The prayer is a corporate prayer — "our Father," "give us," "forgive us." It is not primarily a private transaction between an individual and God but a community's shared address. How does praying this prayer with others — or even praying it with the awareness that others across the world are praying it simultaneously — change its weight?

Going Deeper (optional)

The Lord's Prayer has been called the summary of the whole gospel — containing within its compact structure the core doctrines of God's fatherhood, the kingdom's coming, human dependence and need, the forgiveness of sins, and the cosmic conflict with evil. John Calvin devotes more space to commentary on the Lord's Prayer in the Institutes than to almost any other single passage, because he sees it as the pattern into which all Christian prayer should be shaped. The six petitions he analyzes in terms of two movements: the first three (God-directed) belong to God's glory; the last three (human-directed) belong to our need. And the order is not incidental — it is educative. To pray in this order is to be formed into a person who has learned to seek God's glory before their own comfort.

The petition "forgive us our debts as we forgive our debtors" has generated the most theological discussion, because it appears to make human forgiveness a condition of divine forgiveness. Jesus makes this explicit in Matthew 6:14–15: "For if you forgive others their trespasses, your heavenly Father will also forgive you, but if you do not forgive others their trespasses, neither will your Father forgive your trespasses." The Reformed tradition reads this not as a work that merits forgiveness but as a diagnostic: those who refuse to forgive others have not yet understood or received the forgiveness of God. If you have genuinely received the grace of God — who forgave a debt of ten thousand talents — you cannot permanently withhold a forgiveness of a hundred denarii. The refusal to forgive is evidence that the greater forgiveness has not yet been received as the real and weighty thing it is. The petition is therefore both a request and a mirror: it asks for forgiveness and simultaneously shows us whether we have understood what forgiveness is.
