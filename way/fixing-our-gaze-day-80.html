Day 80

Union with Christ

Opening Prayer

Lord, Paul says "I have been crucified with Christ. It is no longer I who live, but Christ who lives in me." This is not poetry. It is ontology — a description of what is actually true about those who are in you. We confess that we often live as if this were not true, as if the old self still occupied the throne. Let this verse not merely inform us today but reconstitute us. Amen.

"I have been crucified with Christ. It is no longer I who live, but Christ who lives in me. And the life I now live in the flesh I live by faith in the Son of God, who loved me and gave himself for me." Galatians 2:20, ESV

This is possibly the most compressed autobiography in all of Paul's letters. In four clauses, he describes what has happened to him — what has happened to everyone who is in Christ — and what that means for how the present life is lived. It is not primarily a description of Paul's experience (though it is his experience). It is a description of the Christian's ontological status: what is true, structurally and permanently, about the person who has been united to Christ by faith.

I have been crucified with Christ. Paul does not say "Christ died for me" — though he says that in the next clause. He says "I have been crucified with Christ" — which is an identity claim. He did not watch the crucifixion from a safe distance and benefit from it. He was in it. When Christ died, Paul died — the old self, the self constituted by the Adam-identity of sin and separation, was put to death with and in Christ. This is what Romans 6 describes: "Do you not know that all of us who have been baptized into Christ Jesus were baptized into his death?" (Romans 6:3). The union is so complete that what happened to him happened to those who are in him.

It is no longer I who live, but Christ who lives in me. The "I" that no longer lives is not Paul's personality or individuality or embodied existence — he is still clearly present in the next clause, living in the flesh. What is gone is the self that was constituted by its independence from God, the self that placed itself at the center of its own existence and organized everything else around its own comfort and advancement. That self was crucified. And in the space of its absence, something else lives: Christ, by the Spirit, inhabiting the life that was surrendered. This is not mystical absorption — Paul's personality is vivid throughout his letters. But the control center has changed. The engine is different. The motivating love is no longer self-love trying to make room for God; it is Christ's own love, poured into the heart by the Spirit (Romans 5:5), working its way outward.

The life I now live in the flesh I live by faith in the Son of God. The present, physical, embodied life — eating, working, relating, suffering, aging — is lived by faith. Not by sight, not by the visible evidence of the new life, not by feeling the constant presence of Christ in the way one might feel sunshine. By faith: oriented toward what is true and unseen rather than what is comfortable and visible. This is the daily mode of union: not the ecstatic mystical experience that bypasses the ordinary, but the ordinary life lived in the direction of the one who is present within it.

Who loved me and gave himself for me. The verse ends not with a principle or a doctrine but a Person — and the most specific, personal expression of that Person's relationship to Paul: not "who loved humanity" or "who loved the church" but who loved me, who gave himself for me. The universal love of the Lamb (Day 77) is simultaneously particular: it reaches each person in the singularity of their name, in the specificity of their history, with a giving that was not a general contribution to human welfare but a specific death for a specific person. This is the nature of the union: not merger into a mass but personal, named, individual inclusion into the life of the one who knows and loves each one.

Eighty days in. Ten remain. The devotional has been moving toward this: the recognition not merely that Christ is glorious and authoritative and risen and present, but that he is yours — that the crucified and risen and ascended and interceding and returning Christ gave himself for you, and lives in you now, and the life you live, you live by faith in him.

For reflection

  • "I have been crucified with Christ" — the old self constituted by independence from God was put to death with him. Are there areas of your life where the old self is still operating as if it were alive — still at the center, still organizing things around its own comfort and advancement? What would it look like to reckon that self as actually dead (Romans 6:11), in the way Paul does here?
  • "Christ who lives in me" — not "Christ whom I try to imitate" or "Christ whose teachings I attempt to follow," but Christ who is the active, present, living energy within the life. What would it change in your daily moment-by-moment experience to take this literally rather than as a metaphor for moral effort?
  • "Who loved me and gave himself for me." Not for humanity in general, but for me. Is this particular, personal reality — that the Son of God loved you specifically and gave himself for you — something you live from, or something you assent to abstractly? What stands between the doctrine and the daily experience of it?

Going Deeper (optional)

Galatians 2:20 is one of the clearest expressions of what theologians call "mystical union" or "union with Christ" — the foundational reality from which all other aspects of salvation derive. John Murray's description in Redemption Accomplished and Applied is worth returning to: "Union with Christ is really the central truth of the whole doctrine of salvation… Every step of the application of redemption is in the bond of this union… It is not simply that we believe the same truths, or that we share in the same experience of grace, but that we are joined to Christ in the bond of a real and vital union." The union is not merely legal (though it has legal dimensions) or merely moral (though it has moral dimensions) or merely emotional (though it has experiential dimensions). It is ontological — it describes what is actually, really true about the person who is in Christ.

The implication for sanctification is decisive. The Christian does not become more holy by imitating Christ from a distance, by applying Christ's principles to their behavior, or by summoning sufficient willpower to conform to Christ's example. They become more holy as the life of Christ within them — the Spirit's work, grounded in the union — increasingly expresses itself through them. This is why Paul says the fruit of the Spirit is "love, joy, peace…" — fruit is not manufactured; it grows from the vine. The Christ who lives in the believer is the source of the life that looks increasingly like him. Sanctification is not the achievement of a Christ-resemblance from the outside in; it is the outward expression of a Christ-life that is already present from the inside out.
