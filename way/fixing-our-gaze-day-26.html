Day 26

Homeless in His Own World

Opening Prayer

Lord, the man who wanted to follow you was perhaps expecting the privileges of a disciple close to a powerful rabbi. Instead you offered him the truth: the one he wished to follow had no settled home in the world he had made. Give us today an honest reckoning with what it means to follow someone whose life among us was, from beginning to end, the life of a sojourner. Amen.

"And Jesus said to him, 'Foxes have holes, and birds of the air have nests, but the Son of Man has nowhere to lay his head.'" Luke 9:58, ESV

Someone approaches Jesus on the road with evident enthusiasm: "I will follow you wherever you go." It is a generous and apparently sincere offer. Jesus does not receive it with warm encouragement. He offers a corrective — not a discouragement, but an honest picture of what the following actually costs. Foxes have dens. Birds have nests. The Son of Man has no fixed address.

This is not a complaint on Jesus' part. There is no self-pity in the statement. He is simply telling the truth, and the truth is striking: the one who made every den and every nest, who formed every fox and every bird, the one for whom all things were created and in whom all things hold together — has nowhere in his own creation to call his home. He is the landlord living on the street. He is the author who has no reserved seat in his own theater.

The homelessness of Christ is not accidental. It follows, with a kind of theological logic, from who he is and why he came. He came not to inhabit the world in comfort but to redeem it from the inside — to enter its displacement and its lostness so fully that no human experience of homelessness or rejection or marginality could be said to be foreign to him. He has been there. Not as a visitor who kept his return ticket. As someone who gave up the return ticket in order to stay.

There is a pattern in his life that carries this forward: born in a borrowed space, raised in a region of Israel that other Jews looked down on ("Can anything good come out of Nazareth?" — John 1:46), rejected in his hometown synagogue (Luke 4:28–29), with no house of his own during his public ministry, ultimately arrested in a garden that was not his and executed outside the city walls and buried in a tomb that belonged to someone else. He is, from the first moment to the last, a person for whom the world has never had a proper place.

And this identification with the placeless and the displaced is not merely biographical. It shapes how his disciples are to live in the world. "Beloved," Peter writes to scattered Christians across the Roman Empire, "I urge you as sojourners and exiles to abstain from the passions of the flesh" (1 Peter 2:11). He is not merely describing their social situation. He is describing their identity in Christ. Those who belong to the one who had nowhere to lay his head find that they, too, carry a certain homesickness in this world — a sense that no present arrangement is quite the final resting place. Their citizenship is elsewhere (Philippians 3:20). Their city is still to come (Hebrews 13:14).

The reformer John Calvin called this the Christian's peregrinus status — the pilgrim life, lived in the world without being fully of it, sustained by the hope of an inheritance that belongs to those who are heirs of the one who made everything and claimed nothing. The homelessness of Jesus is not just a fact about his earthly biography. It is a permanent aspect of his identification with us. He goes to prepare a place for us (John 14:2–3) — because he understands, from the inside, what it is to be without one.

For reflection

  • Jesus told the eager would-be follower the truth about what following him costs before the man had committed — he did not conceal the difficulty to secure the enrollment. What does this honesty tell you about the kind of relationship Christ invites us into?
  • Peter describes Christians as "sojourners and exiles" — those who are passing through rather than fully at home. Do you experience this sense of not quite belonging in the world? Is that something you resist or something you are learning to inhabit? How does Christ's own homelessness meet you in that feeling?
  • "I go to prepare a place for you" (John 14:2). The one who had nowhere to lay his head is preparing a permanent home for those who follow him. What does it mean to live now from the security of that promise rather than from the anxiety of having no settled place?

Going Deeper (optional)

The concept of the pilgrim life runs as a continuous thread through the whole of Scripture. Abraham "went out, not knowing where he was going… for he was looking forward to the city that has foundations, whose designer and builder is God" (Hebrews 11:8, 10). The whole catalogue of faith in Hebrews 11 describes people who "acknowledged that they were strangers and exiles on the earth" and who "desired a better country, that is, a heavenly one" (Hebrews 11:13, 16). They were not passive about earthly life — they built cities, raised families, governed nations. But they held it all with an open hand, knowing that what they were ultimately seeking could not be found in any present arrangement.

Christ's homelessness on earth is the ultimate expression of this pilgrim identity — and it grounds our own. He is the pioneer of our faith (Hebrews 12:2), the one who went before us not only in suffering and death and resurrection but in the specific experience of living in the world without being fully of it. When his disciples sense the peculiar homesickness of the Christian life — the nagging sense that nothing quite satisfies, that every good thing here is a shadow of something fuller — that experience is not a spiritual deficiency. It is a response to reality. The world as it currently stands is not the final home. And the one who knows that best, and felt it most acutely in his own human experience, is even now preparing the place where the sojourning ends.
