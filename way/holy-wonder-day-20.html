Day 20

When We Make God Small

"You have said, 'What's the use of serving God? What have we gained by obeying his commands or by trying to show the LORD of Heaven's Armies that we are sorry for our sins? From now on we will call the arrogant blessed. Evil people are the winners. They prosper, and when they put God to the test, they go unpunished.'" Malachi 3:14–15 (NLT)
Oh, how great are God's riches and wisdom and knowledge! How impossible it is for us to understand his decisions and his ways! For who can know the LORD's thoughts? Who knows enough to give him advice? Romans 11:33–34 (NLT)

There is a subtle but devastating form of worship distortion that treats God primarily as a means to our ends. We approach him for what he provides — health, success, peace, resolution to our problems — and our measure of whether worship is "working" is whether the outcomes we want are materializing.

The people in Malachi 3 have arrived at this logic's conclusion: if following God doesn't produce visible benefits, why bother? They have reduced God to a transaction partner, and the transaction isn't paying off. This is not cynicism about God, exactly — it is something more subtle: they still believe God exists, they just believe he exists for their benefit.

The Prosperity Gospel makes this logic explicit and systematic — sowing financial seeds, claiming healing by faith, treating the promises of God as contracts we can enforce. But the same logic runs more quietly through ordinary Christian life whenever we find ourselves calculating what God owes us for our devotion.

Paul's doxology in Romans 11 is the corrective. The riches and wisdom and knowledge of God are so vast as to be incomprehensible. His thoughts are not our thoughts. His ways are not our ways. To worship this God is not to manage a transaction. It is to stand before an ocean and be overwhelmed by what you cannot contain.

Put It Into Practice

Read Romans 11:33–36 slowly and aloud. Let it do its work. Then ask honestly: have I been approaching God primarily as a resource for my life? What would worship look like if I came simply for him — not for what he could provide?

For reflection

Where are you most tempted to treat God as a means to your ends rather than as the end himself? What happens to your worship when God doesn't give you what you expected? Does your devotion fluctuate with your circumstances? Romans 11 ends with "all glory to him forever." How does genuine worship reorient us away from ourselves and toward that?
