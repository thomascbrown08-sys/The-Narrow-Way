Day 15

He Will Make All Things New

I heard a loud shout from the throne, saying, “Look, God's home is now among his people! He will live with them, and they will be his people. God himself will be with them. He will wipe every tear from their eyes, and there will be no more death or sorrow or crying or pain. All these things are gone forever.” Revelation 21:3–4 (NLT)

We end where we began. On the first day, the covenant promise was “I will be your God.” Now, at the very end of the Bible and the very end of these days, the same promise returns, completed: “they will be his people. God himself will be with them.” The circle closes. Everything the promise meant at the start, it now means in full and forever. This is where the whole story has been going all along.

Look closely at the picture, because it is the exact opposite of the dungeon. Doubting Castle is a place of distance — God feels far. Here, “God's home is now among his people... God himself will be with them.” Doubting Castle is a place of tears. Here, “he will wipe every tear from their eyes.” And see the tenderness of that: not a tearless place where crying is forbidden, but a God who comes close enough to wipe each tear personally, with his own hand, the way a parent does for a child. The very tears the dungeon wrung out of you will be wiped away by God himself.

Then comes the list, and it is the dungeon's whole inventory, abolished: “no more death or sorrow or crying or pain.” Read it slowly and let each word land, because each one names something you may know far too well. Death — gone. Sorrow — gone. Crying — gone. Pain — gone. “All these things are gone forever.” Not paused. Not eased. Gone, and gone forever. The country that is coming has no Doubting Castle in it, and no Giant Despair, and no dark cell, because it has no death or sorrow or pain for them to feed on.

Remember how Bunyan's story actually ends for Christian and Hopeful. They do escape the giant's dungeon — with the key called Promise, turned while it was still dark — and they go on, past the castle, all the way to the Celestial City, into exactly this: the presence of God, every tear wiped away. The dungeon was real, and it was not the end of their road. It is not the end of yours.

So this is the last and largest thing the promises say to the dark: it does not last. A day is coming — as certain as God's own word — when he will make all things new, when he will live with you and be your God in the fullest sense the covenant ever meant, and when he will wipe the last tear from your eyes with his own hand. Hold the key. Turn it, even in the night. The door opens, and the country on the other side is this.

Pray the Promise

Lift your eyes all the way to the end and pray the promise as longing and hope: “Come, Lord. Make all things new. Wipe every tear from my eyes. Be my God, fully and forever, in the place where there is no more death or sorrow or pain. I am waiting for you.”

Let your prayer be the prayer the whole Bible ends on — the prayer of a people who know the dark is not the last word: Come, Lord Jesus.

For reflection

When you picture the dark finally and forever gone — God himself with you, every tear wiped away by his hand — what rises in you? How might holding that certain future change the way you walk through the present dark?
