Day 51

Rise, Take Up Your Bed

Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, you asked a man who had been ill for thirty-eight years whether he wanted to be healed. The question seems obvious. But it was not. There are ways of being accustomed to our condition — ways our weakness has become our identity — that make the prospect of health more frightening than the familiar misery. Give us honest hearts today, willing to hear the question and to answer it truthfully. Amen.

"When Jesus saw him lying there and knew that he had already been there a long time, he said to him, 'Do you want to be healed?' The sick man answered him, 'Sir, I have no one to help me into the pool when the water is stirred up, and while I am going another steps down before me.' Jesus said to him, 'Get up, take up your bed, and walk.' And at once the man was healed, and he took up his bed and walked." John 5:6–9, ESV

The pool of Bethesda was surrounded by five covered colonnades sheltering a great multitude of the sick — the blind, the lame, the paralyzed (John 5:3). A popular belief held that when an angel stirred the water, the first person to enter the pool would be healed. This man has been waiting for thirty-eight years. He has not, in thirty-eight years, been the first one in.

Jesus singles him out. John tells us something significant: Jesus "knew that he had already been there a long time." The knowledge is not incidental; it shapes the compassion. He is not responding to a cry for help or answering a request that was made. He sees the man, he knows the man's history, and he moves toward him on his own initiative. The seeking pattern holds: he finds before being found.

"Do you want to be healed?" This is the strangest question in the passage, and its strangeness is intentional. Thirty-eight years of waiting would seem to answer the question in advance. But Jesus asks it anyway — not because he doesn't know the answer, but because healing that bypasses the will does not produce what it appears to produce. The question is an invitation to desire, to acknowledge need, to step out of the passive accommodation to an unchanged condition and actively want something different.

The man's response is revealing. He does not say yes. He explains why healing has not happened yet: I have no one to help me, and others always get there first. He is describing the obstacle rather than expressing the desire. Thirty-eight years of failed attempts have perhaps shaped a man who no longer expects anything other than continued failure, who has learned to describe the impossibility of healing rather than to hunger for it. There may be, deep beneath the surface of this man's life, a question not unlike the one Jesus is asking him to ask himself: do I still want this? Or have I made peace with what I am?

Jesus does not wait for a clean yes. He acts from the desire that is there, however buried under accumulated disappointment: Get up, take up your bed, and walk. Three commands in rapid succession — and the man obeys before he has time to doubt the possibility. The healing does not begin with the man entering the pool under his own power. It begins with an authoritative word, and the obedience to the word is both the expression of faith and the mechanism of the healing. He takes up his bed. He walks.

The Sabbath controversy that follows (John 5:10–18) is itself illuminating. The religious authorities see a man carrying his mat on the Sabbath and address the rule violation rather than the miracle. They are looking at the mat and missing the healing. It is the inverse of the Pharisees' pattern throughout the Gospels: the presence of the transforming Christ on the Sabbath triggers a discussion about regulations rather than a response of wonder. Jesus' answer to their accusation is the theological summit of the passage: My Father is working until now, and I am working (John 5:17). He claims to share the Father's Sabbath-transcending work. He is not breaking the Sabbath; he is the Lord of it, doing what the Father has always been doing — giving life, restoring what is broken — now in the specific form of a word spoken to a man who had been waiting for thirty-eight years.

For reflection

  • "Do you want to be healed?" — Jesus' question implies that our desire matters, that passivity or resignation is not the same as trust. Is there an area of your life — a sin, a wound, a pattern — where you have stopped actively wanting to be free and have instead settled into description of the obstacle? What would it mean to answer the question honestly?
  • The man described why healing hadn't happened rather than expressing desire for it. Thirty-eight years of failed waiting can calcify hope. Where in your own life has repeated disappointment reshaped your expectations downward — and what would it mean to bring that calcified hope to the one who asked the question?
  • Jesus healed the man on the Sabbath and was accused of Sabbath-breaking. His answer — "My Father is working, and I am working" — claimed partnership with the Father's continuous work of restoration. What does it tell you about Christ that his activity in people's lives cannot be contained or constrained by the systems and schedules we build around him?

Going Deeper (optional)

Jesus' statement in John 5:17 — "My Father is working until now, and I am working" — provoked the most severe reaction yet from the authorities: "This was why the Jews were seeking all the more to kill him, because not only was he breaking the Sabbath, but he was even calling God his own Father, making himself equal with God" (5:18). The reaction shows that his hearers understood the claim precisely. To say "My Father is working and I am working" is to claim participation in the divine work — a co-laboring with the Creator that no merely human figure could claim without blasphemy. John uses this confrontation to introduce one of the longest Christological discourses in the Gospel (John 5:19–47), in which Jesus systematically expounds his relationship to the Father: the Son does what he sees the Father doing (5:19), the Father has entrusted judgment to the Son (5:22), those who honor the Son honor the Father (5:23), and the Son has life in himself as the Father has life in himself (5:26).

This discourse is the theological ground from which the healing at Bethesda is rightly understood. The man was not healed by a particularly gifted healer or an unusually effective miracle worker. He was healed by the one who shares the Father's life-giving authority — the one who, as John 5:21 declares, "gives life to whom he will." The healing is a demonstration of what the discourse explains: the Son does what the Father does because the Son is in the same relationship to created life as the Father is. He does not heal by permission or delegation; he heals as the source, as the one in whom, as John 1:4 says, "was life, and the life was the light of men." The Bethesda miracle is not a display of power. It is a display of nature.
