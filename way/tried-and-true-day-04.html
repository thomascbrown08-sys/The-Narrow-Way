Day 4

The Problem Inside Us

The heart is deceitful above all things, and desperately sick; who can understand it? Jeremiah 17:9 (ESV)
For I know that nothing good dwells in me, that is, in my flesh. For I have the desire to do what is right, but not the ability to carry it out. For I do not do the good I want, but the evil I do not want is what I keep on doing. Romans 7:18–19 (ESV)

Discernment is hard for reasons outside us — false teachers are clever, error wears the costume of truth. But the deeper problem is inside us. Sin doesn't just make us behave badly. It warps how we perceive. It bends the instrument we use to evaluate everything else.

Theologians call this the noetic effects of sin — the way sin affects the mind and its ability to reason clearly. We are not neutral observers. We tend to see what we want to see, believe what we want to believe, and find reasons to justify what we've already decided. We are very good at this, and very unaware that we're doing it.

Jeremiah's diagnosis is uncomfortable precisely because it includes us. It's tempting to apply his words to others — surely their hearts are deceitful. But the passage doesn't exempt those who read it. Paul's confession in Romans 7 is the same: even someone who genuinely desires what is right finds the desires and the actions at war with each other.

This is not an excuse to give up on discernment. It is a reason to hold our conclusions with some humility, to seek outside counsel, to test ourselves as rigorously as we test others — and to depend on the Spirit who searches all things, including the depths of our own hearts.

Put It Into Practice

Identify one belief you hold that you've never seriously examined. Not to abandon it — simply to notice that you haven't questioned it. What would it look like to test it the way the Bereans tested Paul?

For reflection

Can you think of a time you believed something primarily because you wanted it to be true? What happened when you examined it more closely? What does self-deception feel like from the inside? How do you recognize it after the fact? How does acknowledging the problem inside us change how we approach discernment — both of others and of ourselves?
