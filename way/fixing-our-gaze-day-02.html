Day 2

Before the World Was

Opening Prayer

Eternal Father, we stand at the edge of a mystery that language cannot cross but that faith may glimpse. Your Son prays in this passage not as one requesting something new, but as one reclaiming something ancient — a glory that preceded every sunrise, every heartbeat, every moment of created time. Quiet us now. Let us listen not only to what Jesus says, but to who he is who says it. In his name, amen.

"And now, Father, glorify me in your own presence with the glory that I had with you before the world existed." John 17:5, ESV

The prayer of John 17 is the most intimate ground in all the Gospels. Scholars have called it the High Priestly Prayer — and rightly so — because here Jesus intercedes before his Father on the eve of his death, speaking with a tenderness and authority that no mere prophet could claim. But the verse we pause over today says something even more startling than what he asks for. It is the ground of his asking.

Father, glorify me with the glory that I had with you before the world existed.

Before the world. Before anything. The Jesus who speaks these words is not only the Jesus of Galilee and Jerusalem. He is not simply a great teacher who reached the summit of his influence in the Sermon on the Mount and the upper room. He is the one who existed — in unbroken relationship with the Father — before any molecule was set in motion, before light was summoned out of darkness, before the Spirit hovered over the formless deep. His story does not begin at Bethlehem. Bethlehem is not his beginning; it is his arrival.

A.W. Pink wrote with his characteristic directness: "To lose sight of the preexistence of Christ is to lose sight of his glory." And he was right. If Jesus only began at the manger, he is an extraordinary human being — perhaps the most extraordinary who ever lived. But if he existed before the manger, if he was present and active in the eternal counsels of God before creation's first morning, then Bethlehem itself becomes something almost incomprehensible: not a beginning, but a descent. The eternal Son stooped. The one who had been clothed in unapproachable light took on the swaddling clothes of a borrowed stable.

The Puritans meditated deeply on what they called the pactum salutis — the eternal covenant of redemption — the agreement within the Godhead, before time began, that the Son would come and secure the salvation of a people for the Father's glory. Herman Bavinck described the love of God as being, from all eternity, redemptive in its orientation toward those whom the Father had given to the Son. The cross was not Plan B. It was woven into the eternal counsel of a God who, from before creation's first sunrise, had already determined to be the kind of God who rescues. The glory that Jesus requests in John 17 is the glory that the plan of redemption was always designed to reveal.

What does this preexistence mean for us? First, it means that when Jesus speaks to the Father in John 17, he does not speak as a stranger petitioning for a hearing. He speaks from within an eternal relationship of love and trust so deep, so ancient, so foundational, that even the darkness of Gethsemane and Golgotha will not break it — though it will cost everything to sustain it. The cry from the cross — "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" — is not the severing of that eternal love. It is the most costly expression of it. The Son, who has dwelt at the Father's side since before time began, steps into our forsakenness so that we might never have to bear it.

Second, this preexistence means that when you approach Christ in prayer or worship today, you are approaching someone who is not new to glory. The risen Lord whom Paul encountered on the Damascus road — so blazing in brightness that Paul fell face-down on the dust — is the same Lord who prayed this prayer in the upper room. The same one who, before a single star was ignited, dwelt in the fullness of the Father's presence. The one you approach in this quiet moment is greater than you have yet begun to imagine.

Blaise Pascal, after his famous "night of fire" when God became real to him not as a philosophical principle but as a Person, wrote simply: "the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob — not the God of the philosophers and scholars." When you begin to take seriously that Jesus existed before time, your worship of him takes on a new gravity. You are not worshiping a memory of a good man. You are not relating to an inspiring idea. You are beholding a Person whose story stretches back before your story began — indeed, before Story itself had a first chapter. Let his antiquity enlarge your adoration. He was — and is — and is to come.

For reflection

  • What difference does it make to you that Jesus did not begin at Bethlehem — that his story stretches into an eternity before creation? How does his preexistence change how you think about who he is?
  • John 17:5 shows Jesus praying with confident, intimate authority to the Father — as one who belongs there, and has always belonged there. He is praying for you right now in that same posture (Hebrews 7:25). How might this shape the way you pray?
  • A.W. Pink says that to lose sight of Christ's preexistence is to lose sight of his glory. What glory do you think he had in mind? What is gained when we see Christ as eternally pre-existent rather than merely historically great?

Going Deeper (optional)

The preexistence of the Son is not a theological abstraction — it is structurally necessary for everything Christianity claims. If the Son came into being at the Incarnation, then he is a creature, however exalted, and the worship Christianity directs toward him is a form of idolatry. But if he eternally exists as the second Person of the uncreated Trinity, then his sacrifice on the cross carries infinite weight: the life laid down is an eternal life, freely given, not exhausted by death. This is why Hebrews can say that Christ "offered himself without blemish to God" (Hebrews 9:14) in a way that "put away sin forever" (9:26) — once, without repetition, permanently effective.

Anselm of Canterbury argued in Cur Deus Homo that sin against an infinite God requires an infinite remedy — one that no finite creature could supply. The eternal Son taking on flesh and laying that flesh down in our place is the only offering that could accomplish what our condition demanded. His preexistence is not a theological footnote. It is the load-bearing wall of the gospel. Without it, the cross is a tragedy. With it, the cross is the eternal purpose of an eternal love, arrived at last in time.
