The Missionary & Global Church

The Missionary & Global Church

The last shelf of the records — the Great Awakening, the great century of missions, the modern church's costliest witness and gravest failures, and the faith's explosive growth across the Global South.

"...you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the end of the earth." Acts 1:8 (ESV)

The last shelf carries the story outward and forward — from the cooling embers of the Puritan age to a revival that crossed an ocean, out along the missionary routes of the nineteenth century, and into a twentieth century that was the worst and best of times for the church at once. It ends not in the West, where this story began, but in the Global South, where the faith is now most alive. The records here keep, as always, both the glory and the grief.

1700–1800The fire lit again

By the early eighteenth century the spiritual energy of the Puritan generation had largely dissipated — the churches remained, the forms remained, but the fire was mostly out. Then, within a few years of each other on opposite sides of the Atlantic, it was lit again. George Whitefield may be the greatest preacher in the history of the English-speaking world — certainly the most widely heard before microphones, preaching to open-air crowds of twenty or thirty thousand, crossing the Atlantic thirteen times in the age of sail. Even Benjamin Franklin, no friend of religious enthusiasm, measured how far he could stand and still hear him, and reported it with admiration. His message never varied: you must be born again.

Jonathan Edwards gave the awakening its theological mind. Pastor of a small Massachusetts congregation, he watched genuine revival break out in 1734 and set himself to the careful work of distinguishing true spiritual transformation from its counterfeits — work that produced his Religious Affections, one of the most searching examinations of the inner life ever written. And in England, John Wesley and his brother Charles — who gave the church over six thousand hymns — launched the Methodist movement; Wesley rode a quarter-million miles on horseback and preached forty thousand sermons. He and Whitefield, close friends, parted over predestination — Whitefield Reformed, Wesley Arminian — but shared an absolute conviction that the gospel was for everyone and that the church had no right to stay comfortable while people lived and died without hearing it.

A hard record on the shelfThe blind spot of the awakened

And here the archive keeps one of its hardest records. Whitefield preached that every soul was equally precious before God — and owned slaves, and lobbied successfully to legalize slavery in Georgia, telling himself that the gospel he preached to enslaved people mattered more than the freedom they were denied. He was wrong; the two could not be separated as he imagined, and the American church's long, catastrophic entanglement with the defense of slavery was shaped in part by the precedent that you could preach grace on Sunday and hold people in chains on Monday without resolving the contradiction. Edwards, arguably the most rigorous theological mind the American church has produced, also held human beings as property — not in ignorance, but by choice. To their lasting credit, Wesley was a fierce abolitionist whose last letter, three days before his death, urged William Wilberforce on against what he called "that execrable villainy." The Whitefield–Wesley divide on predestination is the one everyone knows; their divide on slavery mattered more to the millions who lived under it. These were men of profound spiritual insight with a catastrophic moral blind spot their own theology, faithfully applied, should have corrected — which is precisely the warning. The question is never whether we have blind spots, but whether we will be honest enough to suspect that we do.

You could preach grace on Sunday and hold people in chains on Monday. The question is never whether we have blind spots, but whether we are honest enough to suspect we do.

1792 onwardThe great century of missions

William Carey was a cobbler who taught himself Greek, Hebrew, Dutch, and French while mending shoes. In 1792 he preached what may be the most consequential sermon in the history of Protestant missions — expect great things from God; attempt great things for God — and the next year sailed for India, never to return, spending forty years translating Scripture into Bengali, Sanskrit, and dozens of other tongues. Hudson Taylor went further into the interior of China, adopted Chinese dress to the scandal of his colleagues, and founded the China Inland Mission on the principle that there is a living God who has spoken in the Bible, means what he says, and will do all he has promised; he lost his first wife and two children in China and went back. Amy Carmichael went to India at twenty-eight and did not come home for fifty-five years, much of it spent rescuing children from temple prostitution. And Jim Elliot, killed at twenty-eight by the people he had gone to reach in Ecuador, left the line that became his epitaph: he is no fool who gives what he cannot keep to gain what he cannot lose — after which his wife Elisabeth went back to those who had killed him and saw many become Christians.

This era is one of the most remarkable chapters in Christian history, and one of the most complicated. It traveled alongside European colonialism, often shared its assumptions, and sometimes did active damage. The honest account holds both: genuine gospel proclamation that transformed communities, and a cultural imperialism that imposed Western forms alongside the faith. The Christianity now flourishing across sub-Saharan Africa, South Korea, and the Global South is in large part the fruit of this era — a fruit the missionaries themselves could not fully have foreseen.

1900–presentThe worst and best of centuries

In the West, the twentieth century opened with a battle for the faith's very substance. The Fundamentalist–Modernist controversy posed a stark question: could the historic doctrines — the virgin birth, the bodily resurrection, the authority of Scripture, the atonement — survive a culture reshaped by Darwinism and higher criticism? J. Gresham Machen argued with devastating precision in Christianity and Liberalism that liberal theology was not a revised Christianity but a different religion altogether; he founded Westminster Theological Seminary when Princeton capitulated, and was right about the diagnosis — the denominations that embraced liberalism have spent the subsequent century in demographic collapse.

Dietrich Bonhoeffer gave the century its most costly witness. A German Lutheran pastor, he opposed the Nazi regime from its first days, helped found the Confessing Church, ran an illegal underground seminary, and was hanged in a concentration camp three weeks before Germany surrendered. He did not fight an external enemy; he fought his own church — for the German church's capitulation to National Socialism was no fringe affair. The "German Christians" movement hung swastikas in sanctuaries, banned Jewish converts, and tried to purge the Old Testament from their Bibles — ordained ministers in churches that had confessed the Nicene Creed for centuries.

The American church's entanglement with racial segregation is its own long catastrophe: Southern churches supplied the theological justification first for slavery and then for Jim Crow, and when Martin Luther King Jr. wrote from a Birmingham jail in 1963, he was writing not to atheists but to white moderate pastors, indicting a church that had been "a tail-light rather than a headlight" on justice. And the abuse crisis, cutting across Catholic and Protestant churches alike, is not a set of aberrations but the fruit of structures that placed trust in authority without accountability — structures the church's own theology should have prevented, since Reformed ecclesiology insists that all authority is accountable and no one, however gifted, is above correction.

In plain terms

These failures didn't happen to the church from outside. They happened from within, done by people who read the same Bible and confessed the same creeds. The honest conclusion isn't that Christianity is false — it's the one the Bible itself insists on: the gospel is true, and its carriers are broken.

The treasure is kept in clay jars. We trust the treasure, not the jar — and we hold the jar accountable.

The story's leading edgeThe faith goes global

The most important story of the century, though, is not the decline of the West but the explosive growth of Christianity in the Global South. In 1900, roughly four-fifths of the world's Christians lived in Europe and North America; by 2020 the majority lived in Africa, Asia, and Latin America, and the numbers were growing fastest there. The Pentecostal and charismatic movements, dismissed when they emerged, became the fastest-growing expression of Christianity in history — a striking challenge to any assumption that the faith's future belongs to the Western church.

The same era saw a remarkable Reformed renewal in the English-speaking world — the recovery of the confessional Reformed and Puritan tradition through publishers like the Banner of Truth and teachers like J. I. Packer, R. C. Sproul, John Piper, and Tim Keller, preaching that took both the doctrines of grace and the integration of faith and life seriously. That renewal is the very water this curriculum swims in — which means the records on this last shelf run right up to the reader's own feet.

In 1900 most Christians were Western; today most are not. The faith's leading edge has moved south — and the story is still being written.

And there the records, for now, run out — not because the story has ended, but because it has reached you. You stand on the leading edge of a tale that runs unbroken from a borrowed upper room to this present hour: an unlikely, blood-marked, grace-soaked, gloriously persistent thing that no empire could kill and no failure could finally corrupt. Christian read these records and went out from the Palace braver, and so may you. You have joined a very great company — and the road they walked is the road still beneath your feet.
