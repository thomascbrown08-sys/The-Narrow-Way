Day 8

The Fear That Draws Near

You have not come to a physical mountain, to a place of flaming fire, darkness, gloom, and whirlwind, as the Israelites did at Mount Sinai... No, you have come to Mount Zion, to the city of the living God, the heavenly Jerusalem, and to countless thousands of angels in a joyful gathering. You have come to the assembly of God's firstborn children, whose names are written in heaven. You have come to God himself, who is the judge over all things. You have come to the spirits of the righteous ones in heaven who have now been made perfect. You have come to Jesus, the one who mediates the new covenant between God and people. Hebrews 12:18–19, 22–24 (NLT)

The Israelites at Mount Sinai were terrified — and rightly so. The mountain blazed with fire. There was darkness, gloom, whirlwind, the sound of a trumpet, and a voice so overwhelming that the people begged it to stop. They stood at the foot of the mountain and shook. Moses himself said he was terrified.

Hebrews tells us: you have not come to that mountain. You have come to something better. Mount Zion. The heavenly Jerusalem. The joyful gathering of angels. The assembly of those whose names are written in heaven. And to God himself — approached now through Jesus, the mediator of a new covenant.

The fear hasn't disappeared. Later in Hebrews 12, the author still says to worship with holy fear and awe. But the character of the approach has changed. We are not standing at the foot of a mountain we are forbidden to touch. We are coming to a city, an assembly, a family gathering around the throne.

This is the astonishing gift of the gospel: the holy fire is still holy fire, and yet in Christ we may draw near. Not because the distance doesn't exist, but because Jesus has crossed it on our behalf. The filial fear this produces is not a cowed cringing. It is the breathless gratitude of someone who has been let into a room they had no right to enter — and finds they are welcomed as a child.

Put It Into Practice

Read Hebrews 12:18–29 slowly from beginning to end. Let the contrast sink in: Sinai on one side, Zion on the other. Which mountain do you actually live at in your experience of worship? Ask God to help you receive the invitation to Zion.

For reflection

Do you find it easier to think of God as distant and terrifying, or close and welcoming? What has shaped that? How does the image of coming to a "joyful gathering" change how you think about what Sunday worship is supposed to feel like? What does it mean to approach God with "holy fear and awe" while also coming boldly, as Hebrews 4:16 invites?
