Day 28

All of Life as Worship

And whatever you do or say, do it as a representative of the Lord Jesus, giving thanks through him to God the Father. Colossians 3:17 (NLT)
So whether you eat or drink, or whatever you do, do it all for the glory of God. 1 Corinthians 10:31 (NLT)

Whatever you do. Whether you eat or drink. The scope of these instructions is intentionally all-encompassing. There is no category of human activity — work, rest, conversation, eating, creating, sleeping — that falls outside the possibility of being worship.

This does not mean every moment must feel religious. It does not mean we should be mentally praying while driving or spiritualizing our grocery shopping. It means something subtler and more profound: that all of life can be lived as an offering to God, with the awareness that everything we have and do belongs to him and is received from him.

Brother Lawrence was a 17th-century monk who washed dishes in a monastery kitchen and became famous — after his death — for a simple practice: he did his kitchen work in conscious awareness of God's presence. Not with elaborate prayers, just with the sense that he was working before his Father. What he discovered was that this awareness gradually transformed the most mundane work into something holy.

This is the destination of our thirty days — not a technique or a discipline, but an orientation. When the fear of the LORD has done its work, and when genuine worship has taken root, the boundary between sacred and secular begins to dissolve. Monday and Sunday are continuous. The kitchen and the cathedral are the same ground.

From Church History — Brother Lawrence and the Practice of the Presence

Nicholas Herman was born in poverty in 17th-century France and became a lay brother in a Carmelite monastery in Paris, where he worked in the kitchen for most of his life. He had no formal theological education and no significant public ministry. What he developed quietly, over decades, was a practice he called the Practice of the Presence of God — a continuous, simple awareness of God's presence in every moment and every task. After his death, his letters and conversations were collected in a small book that has shaped Christians across denominations for three centuries. His testimony: "The time of business does not with me differ from the time of prayer; and in the noise and clutter of my kitchen, I possess God in as great tranquility as if I were upon my knees."

Put It Into Practice

Choose one ordinary task today — something you do routinely and without much thought. Do it consciously as an act of worship. Not with elaborate spiritual commentary, just with the quiet awareness: this is done before God, for God. Notice what happens to the task.

For reflection

What would change in your daily life if you genuinely believed that washing dishes could be worship? What areas of your life feel most disconnected from your spiritual life? What would it look like to begin offering those to God? Brother Lawrence found that consistency in small moments built something larger over time. What small practice could you begin this week?
