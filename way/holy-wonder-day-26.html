Day 26

Creation Sings First

The heavens proclaim the glory of God. The skies display his craftsmanship. Day after day they continue to speak; night after night they make him known. They speak without a sound or word; their voice is never heard. Yet their message has gone throughout the earth, and their words to all the world. Psalm 19:1–4 (NLT)
For ever since the world was created, people have seen the earth and sky. Through everything God made, they can clearly see his invisible qualities — his eternal power and divine nature. So they have no excuse for not knowing God. Romans 1:20 (NLT)

Before any human being worshiped, creation was already at it. The heavens have been proclaiming the glory of God since the first moment they existed — silently, continuously, without pause. Every sunrise is a sermon. Every galaxy is a doxology. We are not the first to worship; we are joining something that has never stopped.

Paul says in Romans 1 that God's invisible qualities — his eternal power and divine nature — are clearly seen through what he has made. Creation is not just beautiful. It is communicative. It is telling us something specific about the one who made it.

The Psalms are full of creation worship — Psalm 8, Psalm 19, Psalm 104, Psalm 148 — a sustained attention to the world as a theater of God's glory. This is not pantheism, the belief that creation is God. It is the recognition that creation is the work of God's hands and bears the mark of his character.

For our own worship, this means that a walk in the woods, a clear night sky, the sound of rain, the strangeness of a living creature — all of these are available as means of encounter with God. He speaks through what he has made. We are wise to learn to hear him there.

Put It Into Practice

Go outside today — even for ten minutes — specifically to pay attention to creation as a testimony to God. Look slowly at something you usually walk past. Let what you see prompt worship. Say something to God about what you notice.

For reflection

When did creation last genuinely move you toward worship? What happened — or what has kept it from happening? Romans 1 says that what is seen clearly reveals God's eternal power and divine nature. What qualities of God do you most clearly see in the natural world? How might you build more creation-attentiveness into your regular life as a worship practice?
