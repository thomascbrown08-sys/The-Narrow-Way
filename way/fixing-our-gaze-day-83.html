Day 83

Nothing Can Separate Us

Opening Prayer

Lord, Paul makes a list of everything that might threaten our hold on you — death, life, angels, rulers, things present, things to come, powers, height, depth, anything in all creation — and then declares that none of it can separate us from your love. The security is not in our grip on you. It is in yours on us. Let that land today in whatever place we feel most insecure. Amen.

"For I am sure that neither death nor life, nor angels nor rulers, nor things present nor things to come, nor powers, nor height nor depth, nor anything else in all creation, will be able to separate us from the love of God in Christ Jesus our Lord." Romans 8:38–39, ESV

Romans 8 ends with one of the great sustained arguments in all of Scripture — a movement from present suffering through adoption through intercession through divine sovereignty to a final declaration that stands like a wall at the end of the chapter, behind which nothing threatening can pass. Paul has been asking: what can God's elect have against them? What can charge them? What can condemn them? Who can separate them from the love of Christ? And his answers are: nothing, no one, not even these.

He makes a list. It is the kind of list a person makes when they are trying to be exhaustive — when they want to cover every possible category of threat so that no one reading it can find a loophole. Death — the final enemy, the thing that ends every human story. Life — with all its complexity, its temptations, its capacity to distract and diminish and exhaust. Angels — even spiritual authorities in the heavenly places. Rulers — even the most powerful human governments and institutions. Things present — whatever is bearing down right now, today, in the particular shape of your current difficulty. Things to come — whatever you are afraid of, whatever you cannot yet see but already dread. Powers. Height. Depth. And then, with the sweep of a hand that closes off every remaining category: nor anything else in all creation.

The list is not comprehensive because Paul thought of every possible threat and checked them off. It is comprehensive because he is making an ontological claim: there is nothing in creation that has the power to sever the bond between Christ and those who are his. The bond does not depend on the creature's grip. It depends on the Creator's love — and the Creator cannot be overcome by what he has made.

Notice where the love is located: in Christ Jesus our Lord. Paul does not say the love of God in general, or the love of God as an abstract divine attribute. He says the love of God in Christ Jesus our Lord. The love that cannot be broken is the love that was expressed at Calvary, that was confirmed at the empty tomb, that is present in the indwelling Spirit, and that will be perfected at the resurrection. It is not an emotion in the divine mind; it is an act completed in history, a Person dwelling within, and a promise secured by the permanent life of the one who lives to intercede for those he loves.

What are you afraid of today? Name it. Then run it through the list. Death: not able to separate you. Whatever is coming: not able to separate you. The worst thing you can imagine: not able to separate you. This is not denial of the reality of danger and loss and suffering. Romans 8:35 specifically lists tribulation, distress, persecution, famine, nakedness, danger, and the sword as things Paul and those he addresses have faced and will face. The declaration is not that these things will not happen. It is that none of them has the power to sever the bond. You can go through the worst and still come out in the arms of the love that was there before the worst began and will be there after it ends.

We are near the capstone now. Seven days remain. In all of them — in the morning when they begin and the evening when they close — this is the ground you stand on: nothing can separate you from the love of God in Christ Jesus your Lord. Nothing.

For reflection

  • Paul's list tries to cover every category of threat. What is the specific thing — the one you did not see listed but have been quietly holding — that most makes you feel like you could lose your footing with God? Now bring it to this verse. What does Paul say about it?
  • The love is "in Christ Jesus our Lord" — grounded in the historical act of the cross and the living Person of the risen Christ, not merely in the warm generality of divine goodwill. Why does it matter that the love is specifically located in a Person rather than floating as a theological principle? What difference does the particularity make?
  • Paul says "I am sure" — the Greek is pepeismai, a perfect passive, "I have been persuaded and remain persuaded." His certainty is not merely emotional confidence but the result of having been brought to conviction by the evidence and holding that conviction. What would it mean for you to be persuaded — really persuaded, to the depth of your being — that nothing can separate you? What still resists that persuasion?

Going Deeper (optional)

Romans 8:38–39 is the culmination of the most sustained argument for the perseverance of the saints in all of Scripture. The chapter moves systematically through the foundations of that perseverance: the Spirit who intercedes for us (8:26–27), the divine foreknowledge and predestination that grounds our calling and justification and glorification (8:29–30), the logic that the one who did not spare his own Son will freely give us all things (8:32), the finished work of Christ who condemns no one he has justified (8:33–34), and the interceding Christ at the right hand of the Father (8:34). Every potential source of insecurity is systematically addressed before the final declaration arrives.

The Reformed doctrine of the perseverance of the saints — sometimes called "eternal security" in popular usage — is grounded in exactly this structure: not in the believer's own tenacity or faithfulness, but in the character and work and ongoing intercession of the triune God. Westminster Confession 17.1 states: "They, whom God hath accepted in His Beloved, effectually called, and sanctified by His Spirit, can neither totally nor finally fall away from the state of grace, but shall certainly persevere therein to the end, and be eternally saved." The "certainly" is the certainty of Romans 8:38–39: it rests not on the believer's performance but on the God "who did not spare his own Son" and who "always lives to make intercession" for those who draw near through him. The love that cannot be separated from them is the love that went to the cross to secure them. That love has never failed what it fully committed to, and it will not fail now.
