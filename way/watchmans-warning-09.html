Study 9

Presumption and the Hardening Heart

A warning we must not sleep through

"Keep back your servant also from presumptuous sins; let them not have dominion over me! Then I shall be blameless, and innocent of great transgression." Psalm 19:13 (ESV)
"Therefore, as the Holy Spirit says, 'Today, if you hear his voice, do not harden your hearts as in the rebellion, on the day of testing in the wilderness, where your fathers put me to the test and saw my works for forty years. Therefore I was provoked with that generation, and said, "They always go astray in their heart; they have not known my ways." As I swore in my wrath, "They shall not enter my rest."' Take care, brothers, lest there be in any of you an evil, unbelieving heart, leading you to fall away from the living God. But exhort one another every day, as long as it is called 'today,' that none of you may be hardened by the deceitfulness of sin." Hebrews 3:7–13 (ESV)

David's prayer in Psalm 19 moves from wonder at God's creation to gratitude for God's law and finally to a request that most of us skip past: keep me from presumptuous sins. The word presumes something about what these sins are. They are not accidental. They are not sins of ignorance. They are sins committed with full awareness — and David is afraid of them.

What Presumption Is

A presumptuous sin is one committed with the quiet assumption that it will be dealt with. It is the sin that is already planning its own pardon before it has been committed. It is the internal calculation that says: I know this is wrong, and I know there will be forgiveness, and so I will proceed.

This is different from the weakness that falls into sin against its better instincts and then finds forgiveness. The person who falls in weakness and finds forgiveness is exactly where the gospel is designed to meet them. But the person who has worked the forgiveness into the equation before the sin — who has removed the genuine risk from the decision — has done something to themselves that goes beyond the act itself. They have exercised a small, quiet presumption on the patience of God.

Romans 2:4–5 identifies precisely this dynamic: "Do you presume on the riches of his kindness and forbearance and patience, not knowing that God's kindness is meant to lead you to repentance? But because of your hard and impenitent heart you are storing up wrath for yourself on the day of wrath when God's righteous judgment will be revealed." The kindness is real. The forbearance is real. But presuming upon it — using it as a reason to continue sinning rather than as a call to repentance — is not a neutral act. Something accumulates.

The Anatomy of a Hardening Heart

Hebrews 3 uses the generation in the wilderness as a case study in hardening, and the application it draws is immediate and personal: "Take care, brothers, lest there be in any of you an evil, unbelieving heart, leading you to fall away from the living God." The audience is "brothers." The warning is against a hardening that can happen to people in the community of faith.

What makes this passage so important is the phrase "hardened by the deceitfulness of sin." The hardening is not arbitrary. It follows from the deceitfulness of sin — from the process by which sin, repeated and tolerated, distorts the heart's perception. The person whose heart is hardening typically does not experience it as hardening. They experience it as normalizing, as growing more realistic, as becoming less naive about the demands of the Christian life. But the realism is not wisdom. It is, in Hebrews' own words, deception.

How does the hardening progress? Not usually in a single dramatic step. It moves through stages:

First: A sin is committed with some sense of its wrongness. The conscience registers it, if unevenly.

Second: The sin is repeated. The conscience registers it less sharply. The gap between the act and the internal alarm is growing.

Third: The sin is rationalized. The studies we have already done — on self-deception and respectable sins — describe exactly how this process works. The sin begins to be described in terms that are not its own.

Fourth: The rationalization becomes an established position. The conscience, which once protested, has been argued past or simply tired out. What once required explanation requires none.

Fifth: The hearing of the word of God is affected. Passages that should challenge this pattern are now received passively, sorted into a mental category of "yes, but in context," or heard as addressed to someone else.

The text has not changed. The ears have.

The Hebrews passage makes clear that this is not a comfortable resting point. The Word of God is living and active (Hebrews 4:12) and it continues to do its work even as the heart resists it. The conflict does not simply resolve — it intensifies, until a point is reached where the Scripture's description of the Israel who "always go astray in their heart" becomes descriptively accurate: not a temporary failure, but a settled direction.

Why Today Matters

The Hebrews author quotes the Psalm's urgency with full intent: "Today, if you hear his voice, do not harden your hearts." The word today is doing significant work. It is not saying: eventually, get around to this. It is saying: the window is now. The heart that hears the word and responds to it today has something it may not have tomorrow. Not because God withdraws — but because the heart, if it hardens by degrees, may hear the word tomorrow with less capacity to respond than it has today.

This is the trumpet this study is meant to sound. Not a verdict — not "you have already hardened past the point of return." But a question: which direction is your heart moving? Are the things that once alarmed your conscience becoming routine? Are there sins you used to fight that you no longer bother fighting? Are there passages of Scripture that once cut you, and now seem to pass by harmlessly? If so — today is the day to take that seriously.

The Ferguson Lens

Presumption on grace is the antinomian ditch taken to its logical development. What began as "grace means I don't have to fight very hard" matures, over time, into "grace means I can sin with no meaningful consequence." The presumptuous heart has not abandoned the vocabulary of grace — it has enlisted grace in the service of its sin. The legalist's failure is exhaustion; the antinomian's failure, taken to its development, is this: a heart that has become genuinely comfortable with its own corruption, and genuinely indifferent to the God who calls it to turn.

Christ, Our Anchor

Hebrews 3 warns about hardening. Hebrews 7:25 answers it: "Consequently, he is able to save to the uttermost those who draw near to God through him, since he always lives to make intercession for them."

The "uttermost" is not merely a measure of depth. It is a measure of persistence. Jesus saves to the uttermost — to the furthest point, to the very end, without ceasing. And he does it because he "always lives to make intercession." Right now, as these words are read, Christ is before the Father as our advocate — not making a case that needs to be remade constantly, but standing as the completed and perpetually effective sacrifice, the one whose blood speaks better things than the blood of Abel (Hebrews 12:24).

The purpose of the warning in Hebrews 3 is not to tell you that you have gone too far. It is to bring you back before you do. The same letter that contains the most severe warnings in the New Testament also contains Hebrews 7:25. They are not in tension. The warning is the shape that grace takes toward those who are drifting toward the cliff. The anchor is the one who is already, always, standing between you and the fall.

Pastoral Note

This study and the one that follows (Study 10) engage the most serious warnings in this collection. They are not meant to produce despair, and they are not written for those who are already in the pit. They are written as a trumpet — for those who are drifting in the directions these pages describe, with the genuine hope that the trumpet can be heard before the destination is reached. If these studies produce fear rather than honest self-examination, please re-read the pastoral guide, and please talk to your pastor or a mature Christian friend. The goal is repentance and rest — not panic.

For reflection & discussion

  • The study distinguishes between "weakness that falls into sin" and "presumption that plans for it in advance." Can you identify the difference in your own experience? Have you ever caught yourself making the second kind of calculation?
  • Romans 2:4–5 says that presuming on God's kindness stores up wrath. How do you hold together the genuine patience and kindness of God with the seriousness of this warning? Does the patience of God lead you to repentance, or to something else?
  • The five-stage hardening process is described as something that typically feels like "growing more realistic." Have you seen this dynamic in someone else, or recognized it in yourself? What are the early signs that are easiest to miss?
  • The study asks: are there things that once alarmed your conscience that now seem routine? Without needing to be specific with the group, are you willing to sit with that question honestly, alone, with God?
  • Hebrews 7:25 says Christ "always lives to make intercession." How does that truth speak into the fear that presumptuous sin might have placed you beyond reach? What would it mean to bring that intercession into the picture?
