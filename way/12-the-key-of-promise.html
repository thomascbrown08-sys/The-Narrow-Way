The Key of Promise

A devotional on prayer and the promises of God — the key Christian carried all along, turned in the lock of despair even while it was still night.

"...a key in my bosom, called Promise, that will open any lock in Doubting Castle." from The Pilgrim's Progress

It is the most famous turn in all of Bunyan: Christian and Hopeful, locked in the dungeon of Doubting Castle, beaten and starved by Giant Despair, all but resolved to die there — until Christian, half the night gone, startles upright and cries out that he has been a fool. I have a key in my bosom, called Promise, that will, I am persuaded, open any lock in Doubting Castle. They had carried the means of escape the whole time. The door that had held them was openable all along. This devotional is about that key.

The landmark above mapped the dungeon — what doubt and despair are, and why even true pilgrims sometimes land there. This companion is about the way out: the promises of God laid hold of in prayer. For the key was not a feeling that the despair had lifted, and it was not a clever argument that talked the giant down. It was a promise — God's own word, remembered and trusted and turned in the lock while the cell was still dark.

In plain terms

This is a devotional on prayer and the promises of God — specifically as the way out of despair. The "key" is not positive thinking; it is God's spoken promise, taken hold of and prayed back to him even before anything feels different.

Why the key is a promise, and not a mood

Here is the detail that makes all the difference, and that the despairing pilgrim most needs: Christian used the key while it was still night, and while he still felt like a prisoner. The escape did not wait for his feelings to improve. This is the opposite of how despair tells us prayer must work — despair says, wait until you can pray sincerely, wait until the darkness lifts, wait until you feel God is listening. But the key of promise turns in a cold lock. Prayer in despair is not the report of a heart that has already recovered; it is the act by which a faltering heart reaches for something outside itself and holds on. You do not generate the promise. You remember it, and you pray it, and you let it — not your mood — do the work of the lock.

The ring of keysPromises for the dark

And the key is not a single promise but a whole ring of them, given precisely for the cells a pilgrim falls into. There is a promise for the one who feels abandoned — that he will never leave us nor forsake us. A promise for the one buried under guilt — that if we confess, he is faithful and just to forgive. A promise for the one who cannot feel God's nearness — that he is near to the brokenhearted, and close to the crushed in spirit. A promise for the one too weary even to form words — that the Spirit himself intercedes for us with groanings too deep for speech. This companion is a slow walk through such promises: not to admire them, but to teach the hand to find them in the dark, and the mouth to pray them before the feelings have caught up.

Christian turned the key while it was still night, and while he still felt like a prisoner. The promise opens the lock — not the mood.

Giant Despair's dungeon is real, and some who walk this road know its stones by heart. But the door was never as locked as it looked, and the key has been in the bosom all along. This companion is here to help you remember it — and to turn it, even in the dark.
