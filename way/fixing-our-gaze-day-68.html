Day 68

The Darkness and the Curtain

Opening Prayer

Lord, the darkness at noon was not a natural eclipse. The curtain did not tear in a draft. These were the created order responding to what was happening on the cross — the sky going dark over the one who is the light of the world, the barrier between God and humanity torn open by the hands that had made it necessary. We do not want to explain these signs too quickly. Let us simply receive them. Amen.

"Now from the sixth hour there was darkness over all the land until the ninth hour… And Jesus cried out again with a loud voice and yielded up his spirit. And behold, the curtain of the temple was torn in two, from top to bottom. And the earth shook, and the rocks were split." Matthew 27:45, 50–51, ESV

Three hours of darkness. From noon until three in the afternoon, the land is dark. Not the normal darkness of clouds or weather — the Passover fell at full moon, and a solar eclipse is impossible at full moon. This is the darkness that the prophets associated with the Day of the LORD — the moment when God acts in judgment and the ordinary conditions of creation are overridden by something weightier (Amos 8:9–10; Joel 2:31). Golgotha at midday has become a kind of cosmic darkness, as if creation itself is responding to what is happening on the hill outside Jerusalem.

And in the middle of this darkness, the cry that we cannot afford to rush past: Eli, Eli, lema sabachthani? My God, my God, why have you forsaken me? (Matthew 27:46). He is quoting Psalm 22:1. He is not losing his scriptural composure in extremity; he is praying in the only language adequate to what he is experiencing. And what he is experiencing is the experience that the Psalm describes: real, felt abandonment by God. Not the illusion of abandonment. Not the appearance of distance while the Father watches approvingly from behind a screen. The real thing — the actual experience of God-forsakenness — which he was bearing in himself so that every one of his people who ever cried this prayer in their own darkness would be crying it after him, not into a void, but into an experience their Savior has already had and survived.

He is bearing the cup. The wrath that belongs to sinners, the God-forsakenness that is the ultimate consequence of sin — separation from the source of all life and light and joy — is being experienced by the Son of God in our place. This is the deepest suffering of the cross: not the nails, not the thirst, not the humiliation, but this. The eternal Son who has dwelt in the Father's presence from before time began, who said "I and the Father are one," who has known nothing but the Father's love and approval — for these hours, bears what the lost bear. The Father's face is hidden. The light is dark. The Son is alone with our guilt.

And then he dies. And the curtain tears.

The veil of the temple — sixty feet high, four inches thick, embroidered in blue, purple, and scarlet, representing the barrier between the holy and the Most Holy Place — the curtain that kept human beings out of the immediate presence of God on pain of death — tears from top to bottom. Not from the bottom up, as a human hand tearing in anger would do. From the top down. God's hand. The way into the Most Holy Place is open. The barrier has been removed. The access that no human performance could secure and that no sacrifice could permanently purchase has been given, in the death of the one perfect sacrifice, to everyone who comes through the torn veil to find him there.

The earth shakes. The rocks split. The created order registers what theology will take centuries to articulate: something of absolute cosmic significance has happened on this hill. The darkness says it. The torn curtain says it. The trembling ground says it. The one who made all of it died here, and all of it knows.

For reflection

  • "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" — a real cry from a real experience of abandonment, borne in our place. Is there a darkness in your own story where you have cried something similar? What does it mean that Christ went there before you — that he has been inside the experience of God-forsakenness and came out the other side?
  • The curtain tore from top to bottom — God's action, not humanity's. The access to God that the cross provides is entirely his initiative and his gift. What barriers have you erected — of performance, of worthiness, of theological correctness — between yourself and that open access? What does the torn curtain say about those barriers?
  • The darkness, the earthquake, the torn curtain — creation responded to the cross. If you were standing at Golgotha that afternoon, what do you think you would have understood? And what do you understand now, sixty-eight days in, that you would not have understood at Day 1?

Going Deeper (optional)

The cry of dereliction — "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" (Matthew 27:46) — is the most theologically demanding sentence in the entire Gospels, and it has generated more careful theological reflection than perhaps any other utterance of Christ. The question it presses is acute: if the Father and the Son are one in essence (John 10:30), in what sense can the Son be "forsaken" by the Father? The Chalcedonian answer, developed carefully by the Reformed tradition, is that the God-forsakenness is experienced through the human nature of the Son — not a sundering of the eternal Trinity, but the Son bearing, through his human nature, the full experiential weight of what sin produces: separation from God.

J.I. Packer expressed it carefully: the cry is not evidence that the Trinity was divided or that the love between Father and Son was severed. It is evidence of the depth of the Son's identification with sinners. He who knew no sin was "made sin for us" (2 Corinthians 5:21); he who had never experienced a moment of God-forsakenness experienced it for every sinner who would ever live. The Father's love for the Son did not cease at the cross — the resurrection is the proof. But in the hours of the cross, the Son bore what sinners bear, in the place where sinners are, so that sinners who later cry this prayer can know that the one they are crying to has already been inside the darkness they are in. He is not calling from outside the experience. He is calling from within it, having gone through it, having come out on the other side of it alive. This is the deepest comfort of the cross: not that suffering is explained, but that it has been entered, by the one who can do something about it from the inside.
