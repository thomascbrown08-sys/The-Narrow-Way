Day 11

More Than a Song

And so, dear brothers and sisters, I plead with you to give your bodies to God because of all he has done for you. Let them be a living and holy sacrifice — the kind he will find acceptable. This is truly the way to worship him. Don't copy the behavior and customs of this world, but let God transform you into a new person by changing the way you think. Then you will learn to know God's will for you, which is good and pleasing and perfect. Romans 12:1–2 (NLT)

When most of us hear the word worship, we think of music. Of hands raised, voices lifted, songs sung on Sunday morning. And that is real worship — the New Testament is full of it. But Paul's definition goes somewhere much larger.

Present your bodies as a living sacrifice. This is the way to worship. Every meal you eat, every morning you get out of bed, every conversation you have, every dollar you spend — all of it belongs to the category of worship when it is offered to God as an act of surrender and gratitude.

The logic flows from the words that come just before Romans 12: because of all he has done for you. Paul has just spent eleven chapters describing the breathtaking grace of God — the salvation, the adoption, the Spirit, the promise. Worship is the only sane response to understanding what God has actually done. Not worship as performance, but worship as a life reoriented entirely around its source.

The transformation of the mind in verse 2 is both a prerequisite and a product of this kind of worship. You cannot offer yourself as a living sacrifice if your mind is still shaped by the world's values. And as your mind is renewed, your worship deepens. The two grow together in a spiral that — if we let it — keeps going for the rest of our lives.

Put It Into Practice

Pick one ordinary activity today — making breakfast, commuting to work, doing dishes — and consciously offer it to God as you do it. Not with elaborate prayer, just a simple: this is yours. Notice what that awareness does to the activity.

For reflection

What does it mean practically to offer your body as a living sacrifice? What part of your everyday life do you most struggle to see as worship? Paul says transformation of the mind is how we learn to know God's will. What is currently doing the most work of forming your mind — Scripture, culture, work, social media? How does understanding what God has done (Romans 1–11) change the character of the worship you offer in response?
