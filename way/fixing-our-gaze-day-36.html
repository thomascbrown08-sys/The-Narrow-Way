Day 36

The Lost Sheep

Opening Prayer

Lord, this parable turns on a question that seems to have an obvious answer and does not. "Which of you, having a hundred sheep, does not leave the ninety-nine to find the one?" The honest answer, for most of us, is: I might not. The mathematics are against it. But you do not do the mathematics. You go. Let us understand why, and let that understanding reshape how we see our own lostness and your searching. Amen.

"What man of you, having a hundred sheep, if he has lost one of them, does not leave the ninety-nine in the open country, and go after the one that is lost, until he finds it? And when he has found it, he lays it on his shoulders, rejoicing. And when he comes home, he calls together his friends and his neighbors, saying to them, 'Rejoice with me, for I have found my sheep that was lost.' Just so, I tell you, there will be more joy in heaven over one sinner who repents than over ninety-nine righteous persons who need no repentance." Luke 15:4–7, ESV

The three parables in Luke 15 are spoken in response to the same grumble: "This man receives sinners and eats with them." The Pharisees and scribes are the audience — religious professionals who have, over decades of faithful observance, built the kind of scaffolding around the law that they believe keeps holiness intact. And into that carefully maintained world, this wandering rabbi keeps inviting the wrong people to dinner. The parables are Jesus' answer to the grumble.

Which of you, having a hundred sheep, does not leave the ninety-nine to find the one? The question is addressed to people who understand livestock economics. A hundred sheep was a substantial flock — a good shepherd's livelihood. To leave ninety-nine unattended in open country to search for one is, by any practical calculation, a risky proposition. The ninety-nine could scatter, could be taken by predators, could wander while the shepherd is chasing the one. The rational decision is to grieve the loss and protect the majority.

The shepherd in the parable does not make the rational decision. He goes. And he does not go with a resigned sense of duty or a calculated assessment that the one is worth the risk. He goes until he finds it — the phrase is relentless, implying a search that does not consider stopping. And when he finds it, he does not lead it back sternly. He lays it on his shoulders — the posture of a shepherd who knows that a lost and frightened sheep cannot walk steadily home and needs to be carried. And then the celebration: neighbors gathered, rejoicing, the singular found thing treated as the occasion for a party that, to the outside observer, seems entirely out of proportion.

The final verse is the sharpest in the parable, and it is addressed directly at the grumbling scribes and Pharisees: "There will be more joy in heaven over one sinner who repents than over ninety-nine righteous persons who need no repentance." The ninety-nine who need no repentance — this is a category that requires careful handling. Jesus is not suggesting that some people genuinely need no repentance and thus receive less of heaven's attention. He is describing the Pharisees as they see themselves: righteous enough that they do not think the shepherd's frantic search applies to them. The irony is layered. The ones who think they are safely in the fold and need no finding are the ones at greatest risk of the particular lostness of self-sufficiency.

Charles Spurgeon, preaching on this parable, asked his congregation to consider the weight of the phrase "until he finds it." Not until he grows tired. Not until the search seems futile. Not until the cost exceeds the value. Until. He finds it. The search is defined by its terminus, not its difficulty. And the terminus is finding — which means there is no point at which the shepherd decides the sheep is not worth finding. The value of the one sheep is not calculated by comparing it to the ninety-nine. It is established by the fact that the shepherd goes at all.

You are the sheep in this story — or you have been. The specific, individual, irreplaceable creature who went astray not because anyone drove you out but because sheep wander, because the grass somewhere else looked more promising, because you drifted before you knew you were drifting. And the shepherd left the fold to find you. Carried you back on his shoulders, not because you found your way but because he did. This is the shape of the grace that receives sinners and eats with them. It is not polite tolerance. It is the celebrating-that-the-found-has-been-found joy of a shepherd who is not interested in the mathematics.

For reflection

  • "He goes until he finds it" — the search is defined by finding, not by reasonable effort. What does this tell you about the persistence of Christ's seeking? Is there a way you have been treating his search for you as conditional or limited — as something you might exhaust by wandering too far or too long?
  • The "ninety-nine who need no repentance" are a warning as much as a contrast. Is there any way in which you have settled into a posture of spiritual sufficiency — of being already in the fold and therefore past the need for the kind of active, desperate seeking the lost sheep requires?
  • "There will be more joy in heaven over one sinner who repents." Heaven throws a party for the found. What does it do to you to know that your own finding — or your returning, if you have wandered — is an occasion of joy in heaven rather than merely an administrative note in a ledger?

Going Deeper (optional)

The three parables in Luke 15 share a common structure that is theologically significant: in each one, the initiative belongs entirely to the seeker. The shepherd leaves — the sheep does not find its way home. The woman searches — the coin does not roll back to her. The father watches the road — but when the son comes home, the father has been the one holding the posture of waiting and watching all along. There is nothing in these parables to support the idea that the lost, left to themselves, return. They are found, not self-returning. The Reformation's insistence on the initiative of grace in conversion — that regeneration precedes and enables repentance rather than following it as its reward — finds a parabolic expression in the structure of all three stories.

At the same time, each parable ends with repentance — the son "came to himself" and returned; the lost coin and lost sheep prompt celebrations explicitly described as analogous to heaven's joy "over one sinner who repents" (Luke 15:7, 10). The Reformed tradition holds both truths without collapsing either into the other: the initiative is entirely God's (the sheep does not find itself), and the response is genuinely the sinner's (the son arose and came). These are not contradictory — they describe the same event from two different angles. Grace does not override the will; it renews it, so that the will genuinely wants and chooses what grace has made available. The sheep is carried home; but the son, renewed in his mind, walks home on his own two feet while his father runs to meet him. Both are the same grace, differently expressed.
