The Vocabulary Trap

The Vocabulary Trap

One word, two dialects — and each mistakes the other for heresy.

The tactic. Two people use the same word to mean two different things, and each walks away convinced the other holds a dangerous position.

How it looksThe shape of the move

Two believers discuss grace, or authority, or accountability. Both are serious; both believe they are being faithful. And the longer they talk, the more each suspects the other of something subtly heretical — because neither has noticed they are speaking one word in two different theological dialects. When a person formed in a Reformed world says grace, they mean sovereign, unmerited favor that precedes every human response. When a person formed in a Wesleyan world says grace, they also mean something precise — but not the same thing. A conversation that never establishes which definition is running will almost always misdiagnose a different doctrine as a deficient one.

The virtue it twistsWhy good people fall for it

The good thing here is precision — the instinct that words matter, that theology should be exact, that we should not paper over real differences. That instinct is right. The trap springs precisely because both people have it: each is listening carefully, and each is carefully measuring the other’s words against their own dictionary.

A scene — how it might run

Imagine I tell a friend that I’ve been learning to rest in God’s grace rather than striving. I mean: I’ve stopped trying to earn what Christ already secured. My friend, formed elsewhere, hears: he’s gone soft on obedience, treating grace as a license. I hear his concern as legalism; he hears my freedom as license. We are both alarmed, both think we are defending the gospel — and we have not yet disagreed about a single actual thing. We are two people with one word and two dictionaries, frightening each other.

What it costsThe damage it does

The cost is rupture between people who may not actually disagree at all — and it is cruelest with those whose vocabulary was formed in harm. To someone wounded in a high-control church, words like submission or authority do not arrive as neutral terms; they come carrying memory. Press such a word without noticing, and you can re-injure the already-wounded while believing you are merely being precise.

Disarming itThe peacemaker’s move, up close

Define before you debate. The move is to slow down and surface the dictionary: ‘When you say that word — what do you mean by it, here, in this conversation? Not in general; just here.’ Half the time the apparent heresy dissolves the instant the definitions are laid side by side, and you discover you were agreeing in two languages.

And listen for the word that lands harder than the moment warrants. If authority or accountability seems to strike with surprising force, ask gently whether there is history with the term — and then actually listen, without advancing your point a single step, before you say anything more. In yourself, the discipline is humility about your own fluency: the fact that a word has one obvious meaning to you is not evidence that it has the same meaning to the person across the table. Assume a shared dictionary only after you have checked it, not before.

📖 The Scripture behind this Interpretationwhat’s this?›

The claim: Loving your neighbor in conversation includes the patience to understand what they actually mean before judging it — refusing to bear false witness by misreading their words.

  • Proverbs 18:13 (ESV) — “If one gives an answer before he hears, it is his folly and shame.”Answering before truly hearing — the vocabulary trap exactly — is named as folly.
  • James 1:19 (ESV) — “let every person be quick to hear, slow to speak, slow to anger.”The order is hearing first; speaking and indignation come later, if at all.
  • Exodus 20:16 (ESV) — “You shall not bear false witness against your neighbor.”To misrepresent what someone holds — even by misreading their words — is a form of false witness.
The reasoning (flagged as interpretation, not direct derivation)Marked interpretation because the application — ‘define the word before you debate it’ — is a wise practice drawn from Scripture’s commands rather than a rule the text states in those terms. The materials are direct and strong: answering before hearing is folly (Proverbs 18:13); we are to be quick to hear and slow to speak (James 1:19); and to misrepresent a neighbor’s position is a species of false witness (Exodus 20:16). Drawing from these the specific counsel to establish shared definitions before pronouncing someone wrong is a sound application of the texts — but a pastoral synthesis, which is why it is flagged rather than presented as a direct command.

A sound application of Scripture's commands about hearing and truthful speech, but a practice derived from them rather than stated outright.
