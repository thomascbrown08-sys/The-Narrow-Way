Day 70

Why Seek the Living Among the Dead?

Opening Prayer

Lord, the women came to the tomb with spices to anoint a body. They came prepared for death, not life. The stone was already rolled away when they arrived. The body was gone. Two men in dazzling apparel said: why are you looking for the living among the dead? He is not here. He has risen. Let those words ring through every darkened expectation we carry today. He is not among the dead. Amen.

"But on the first day of the week, at early dawn, they went to the tomb, taking the spices they had prepared. And they found the stone rolled away from the tomb, but when they went in they did not find the body of the Lord Jesus. While they were perplexed about this, behold, two men stood by them in dazzling apparel… And they said to them, 'Why do you seek the living among the dead? He is not here, but has risen.'" Luke 24:1–6, ESV

They came at early dawn on the first day of the week — the women who had followed him from Galilee, who had watched the crucifixion from a distance, who had sat opposite the tomb on Friday evening. They come with spices, with the practical purposefulness of people who have grieved and are now doing what grief requires: honoring the dead. They have not come expecting surprise. They have come expecting a sealed tomb and a body that needs anointing.

The stone has already been rolled away. They do not find a body inside. They stand there in their perplexity — aporeō in Greek, a word that means to have no passage forward, to be at a complete loss — and two figures in dazzling clothing speak to them. The question the angels ask is not a rebuke. It is a recalibration: Why are you seeking the living among the dead? The assumption that he belongs among the dead — the assumption the spices and the early morning visit are built on — is wrong. He is not here. He has risen.

Then a word that carries the full weight of biblical promise: Remember how he told you, while he was still in Galilee, that the Son of Man must be delivered into the hands of sinful men and be crucified and on the third day rise (Luke 24:6–7). The resurrection is not a surprise from above, an unexpected divine intervention that reversed what looked like defeat. It is the fulfillment of what he said would happen — the conclusion of the story as he told it from the beginning. The women knew this. They had heard him. They had simply not believed that what he said could actually be true. And now the empty tomb stands as the visible confirmation of a word that was already true before anyone could see the evidence of it.

The resurrection is the pivot of everything. Paul's argument in 1 Corinthians 15 is stark: if Christ has not been raised, our faith is futile, we are still in our sins, and we are of all people most to be pitied (15:17–19). Christianity does not merely claim that Jesus died for sins and that his teaching is valuable and that his example is inspiring. It claims that a specific body was placed in a specific tomb on a specific Friday afternoon, and that three days later that body came out — transformed, glorified, yet bearing the marks of the nails and the spear, recognizably the same body that had walked and eaten and wept and been crucified. Not a ghost. Not a vision. Not a spiritual survival. A resurrection.

The women are instructed to tell the disciples. They run. The disciples, hearing it, do not believe them — their words seem to them "an idle tale" (Luke 24:11). Peter runs to the tomb alone, stoops, and sees the linen cloths lying there, and the face cloth folded separately (John 20:6–7). He goes home marveling. The evidence is accumulating. The stone is rolled away, the tomb is empty, the grave clothes are folded, the angels have spoken. And still it takes time to believe what is too large to fit inside an ordinary expectation.

He is not here. He is risen. The darkness of Good Friday and the silence of Holy Saturday have broken into the light of the first morning of the new creation. The same word that called Lazarus out of his tomb has, from within the tomb, called its own death and found nothing with authority to refuse.

For reflection

  • The women came prepared for death, not life — their whole errand assumed the tomb held a body. Where in your own faith have you come with "spices" — prepared for a closed tomb, expecting limitation, not daring to hope for what the promise actually says? How does the empty tomb press against those expectations?
  • "Why do you seek the living among the dead?" — the question assumes that seeking Christ where he isn't is possible. Are there places you have been looking for him — in the past, in a memory, in a closed system — rather than where the risen Christ actually is? What would it mean to seek the living rather than the dead?
  • The disciples did not believe the women's report at first. The resurrection required witnesses who gradually, through accumulated evidence and encounter, came to believe something their assumptions had made impossible. What has been the pattern of your own growing belief in the resurrection — not the doctrine but the reality? What has made it more real to you?

Going Deeper (optional)

The physical, bodily resurrection of Christ is the non-negotiable center of apostolic Christianity. This is not primarily a metaphysical claim (though it is that); it is a historical claim — the kind that can in principle be verified or falsified by evidence. Paul's argument in 1 Corinthians 15:3–8 explicitly grounds the resurrection in eyewitness testimony: "He appeared to Cephas, then to the twelve. Then he appeared to more than five hundred brothers at one time, most of whom are still alive, though some have fallen asleep." Paul is writing within twenty years of the events, while witnesses are still alive and available to be questioned. This is the structure of historical testimony, not mythological narrative.

The Reformed tradition has consistently maintained the bodily, historical resurrection as the foundation of justification, adoption, and the believer's future hope. Romans 4:25 summarizes: "He was delivered up for our trespasses and raised for our justification." The resurrection is not a separate event from the atonement; it is the Father's declaration that the sacrifice was accepted, the penalty fully paid, the work complete. The empty tomb is the receipt stamped across the account. Because he was raised, those who are in him are justified — their debt settled, their standing secure, their future guaranteed by the living one who holds the keys of death and Hades (Revelation 1:18) and who, having passed through the tomb, will never pass through it again.
