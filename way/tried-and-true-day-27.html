Day 27

What the Church Provides

If I delay, you may know how one ought to behave in the household of God, which is the church of the living God, a pillar and buttress of the truth. 1 Timothy 3:15 (ESV)
And he gave the apostles, the prophets, the evangelists, the shepherds and teachers, to equip the saints for the work of ministry, for building up the body of Christ, until we all attain to the unity of the faith and of the knowledge of the Son of God, to mature manhood, to the measure of the stature of the fullness of Christ, so that we may no longer be children, tossed to and fro by the waves and carried about by every wind of doctrine, by human cunning, by craftiness in deceitful schemes. Ephesians 4:11–14 (ESV)

Paul calls the church 'the pillar and buttress of the truth.' This is a startling claim in an age when we are inclined to locate truth-keeping in individual conscience or private study. The church — the actual gathered community, with its elders, its Word, its sacraments, its discipline — is the structure God has designed to hold up and protect truth in the world.

Ephesians 4 describes the pastoral gifts (apostles, prophets, evangelists, shepherds, teachers) in terms of a specific goal: that the body might no longer be 'tossed to and fro by every wind of doctrine.' Theological instability is what the church's teaching ministry is designed to prevent. When these structures are weak or absent, believers become vulnerable precisely where a community should protect them.

Submission to a local church — its elders, its discipline, its community life — is not an optional add-on for particularly obedient Christians. It is the ordinary context in which discernment is exercised, tested, and corrected. The Lord's Supper, practiced regularly, is a community-level self-examination rhythm. Congregational discipline, practiced carefully, is community-level discernment about the boundaries of fellowship.

This doesn't mean churches are infallible or that elders are immune from error. It means that the God-designed structure for protecting discernment is the community, and abandoning it in favor of private judgment is not a gain but a loss.

Put It Into Practice

Honestly assess your relationship to a local church community. Are you submitted to it — its teaching, its community, its leadership? If your relationship is more consumer than member, what would greater participation look like?

For reflection

What's the difference between healthy submission to church authority and blind obedience? Where are the legitimate limits? What does it mean practically for the church to be 'the pillar and buttress of the truth'? What does that look like in an ordinary congregation? Have you ever been tempted toward the Donatist move — seeking a purer, more faithful community elsewhere rather than working for faithfulness in the one you're in?
