Day 11

He Will Finish What He Started

And I am certain that God, who began the good work within you, will continue his work until it is finally finished on the day when Christ Jesus returns. Philippians 1:6 (NLT)

We come now to the fear that haunts the back half of the journey, once a pilgrim has been on the road long enough to know his own weakness intimately: I am not going to make it. I will drift, or fall, or simply run out, and somewhere short of the end I will be lost. I cannot keep myself. And on that last point, the fear is entirely right. You cannot keep yourself. If your arrival depended on the strength of your grip, you would have every reason to despair.

So read the promise with great care, and see where the strength actually lies. “God, who began the good work within you, will continue his work until it is finally finished.” Every active verb belongs to God. He began it. He continues it. He will finish it. Your perseverance is not, at bottom, a feat of your willpower. It is a project of God's faithfulness. The same God who started the work has taken responsibility to complete it.

This is the doctrine the old writers called the perseverance of the saints, though it might better be called the preservation of the saints — because the emphasis falls not on our persevering but on God's preserving. We do persevere; we do press on; but we press on because we are held, not in order to become held. The continuing is real, and it is the fruit of his keeping, not the condition of it.

Think of how a craftsman regards his own work. No serious craftsman abandons a piece half-finished; the very fact that he began it commits him to its completion. God is not a hobbyist who loses interest. The good work in you is his good work, and his character is staked on finishing it. Your salvation is not a project you are completing for God, liable to be abandoned when your energy fails. It is a project God is completing in you, and he does not leave things half-done.

So when you look ahead and cannot see how you will possibly endure to the end — good. You were never going to endure by the power of your seeing or your feeling or your gritted teeth. You will endure because the One who began the work has promised to finish it, and the date is already set: “the day when Christ Jesus returns.” He will not lose you between now and then.

Pray the Promise

Take your fear of not finishing — of falling away, drifting, running out — and pray the promise back to God: “I cannot keep myself, and I'm afraid I won't last. But you began this work, and you have promised to finish it. I trust your faithfulness, not my grip.”

Rest the weight of your perseverance where it actually rests: on the One who started the work, not on the strength of the one being worked on.

For reflection

Where do you carry the fear that you won't make it to the end? What changes when perseverance is grounded in God's commitment to finish, rather than your ability to hold on?
