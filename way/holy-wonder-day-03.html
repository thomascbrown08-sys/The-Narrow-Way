Day 3

Majesty and Intimacy Together

O LORD, our Lord, your majestic name fills the earth! Your glory is higher than the heavens. When I look at the night sky and see the work of your fingers — the moon and the stars you set in place — what are mere mortals that you should think about them, human beings that you should care for them? Yet you made them only a little lower than God and crowned them with glory and honor. You gave them charge of everything you made, putting all things under their authority. Psalm 8:1, 3–6 (NLT)

David does something remarkable in this psalm. He looks up at the night sky — at the moon and stars, at the sheer scale of what God has made — and he is overwhelmed by smallness. What are mere mortals? The question is not rhetorical despair. It is wonder. Genuine wonder at the fact that the God who made all of this even notices us.

And then the stunning pivot: yet you made them only a little lower than God. The same God whose glory exceeds the heavens has dignified human beings beyond comprehension. These two things — the infinite greatness of God and the astonishing dignity of persons — belong together in biblical worship. You cannot have one without the other.

This is why worship is not self-deprecation. We are not worms groveling before a tyrant. We are beloved, dignified creatures standing before a Father whose love for us is precisely what makes his greatness so staggering. The majesty and the intimacy amplify each other.

The best worship holds both. It does not flatten God into a friend who happens to be nice, nor does it turn him into a distant force too great to care about us. It looks up at the stars and whispers the exact question David asked — and then receives the exact answer David received.

Put It Into Practice

If you can, go outside tonight and look at the sky for five minutes. No phone. No agenda. Just look. Let the scale of what you see do its work. Then read Psalm 8 aloud.

For reflection

Does your worship tend to emphasize God's greatness or God's nearness? What has formed that tendency in you? The psalm moves from the vastness of creation to the dignity of the person. How does it change your sense of your own worth to know that the God of galaxies cares specifically about you? What would it look like in practice for your worship to hold majesty and intimacy together rather than choosing one?
