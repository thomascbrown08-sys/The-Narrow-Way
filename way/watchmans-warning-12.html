Study 12

No Longer Slaves

The hope that is not wishful thinking

"We know that our old self was crucified with him in order that the body of sin might be brought to nothing, so that we would no longer be enslaved to sin. For one who has died has been set free from sin. Now if we have died with Christ, we believe that we will also live with him. We know that Christ, being raised from the dead, will never die again; death no longer has dominion over him. So you also must consider yourselves dead to sin and alive to God in Christ Jesus." Romans 6:6–11 (ESV)
"No temptation has overtaken you that is not common to man. God is faithful, and he will not let you be tempted beyond your ability, but with the temptation he will also provide the way of escape, that you may be able to endure it." 1 Corinthians 10:13 (ESV)
"For freedom Christ has set us free; stand firm therefore, and do not submit again to a yoke of slavery." Galatians 5:1 (ESV)

This is where we end. Not with a summary of the warnings, but with the word that is underneath all of them and has been, from the beginning, their only real purpose: you do not have to stay where you are.

What Has Actually Happened

The Christian life, as the New Testament describes it, is not primarily a project of self-improvement. It is a participation in something that has already happened. Christ died. Christ rose. And the one who is united to Christ by faith has died and risen with him — not as a metaphor, but as a spiritual reality that is as genuinely true as anything in this world.

Paul says we "know" three things in Romans 6. We know that our old self was crucified with Christ (v. 6). We know that Christ being raised will never die again (v. 9). We know — or we are told to consider it so — that we are dead to sin and alive to God (v. 11). The word translated "consider" or "reckon" in verse 11 is the Greek logizesthe — an accounting term. It means: treat this as a settled fact on the ledger. Act from it, not toward it. Do not try to make it true; receive it as already true, and live accordingly.

This is the hope that is not wishful thinking. It is not "I hope one day to be free from sin's dominion." It is "sin's dominion over me has been broken in the death of Christ, and I am called to live from that reality." The freedom is participated, not merely promised. The question is not whether it is available. The question is whether we are living from it.

The Way of Repentance

One of the things this study has been reaching toward, and which this final page wants to name directly, is that the Christian life is not a series of crises followed by recoveries. It is, at its healthiest, a life of continual repentance — not a life in which we are always failing dramatically and always confessing dramatically, but a life in which there is a sustained, daily, ongoing posture of turning.

The Hebrew word for repentance, shub, means to turn — to change direction. The Greek, metanoia, means a change of mind, a reorientation. Acts 20:21 speaks of "repentance toward God and of faith in our Lord Jesus Christ" — repentance is directional, always oriented toward something rather than merely away from something. The most important thing about repentance is not what it is leaving behind. It is where it is going.

This is the rhythm of a healthy Christian life: not a cycle of dramatic failure and desperate recovery, but a steady, daily turning toward God — bringing the sin, the weakness, the drift, the respectable sins and the serious ones, into the light, where the blood of Christ cleanses, and then turning — face toward him, feet moving in the direction of obedience, however imperfectly, by the Spirit's power.

The Promise That Does Not Fail

1 Corinthians 10:13 is not a guarantee that temptation will be easy. It is a guarantee that it will not be irresistible. The God who knows exactly what you face — who is, as Paul says, faithful — has committed himself to providing a way through every temptation, without exception. Not a way around it. A way through it.

This promise has often been misused — deployed as an accusation against those who fall, as if the promise of escape means every fall was preventable and is therefore inexcusable. That is not what the passage says. It says there is a way of escape — not that every person who struggles has found it, or used it, or been given the faith in that moment to take it. But it does say something that matters enormously for the life of daily repentance: the fight is not futile. The resistance is not against an unconquerable force. The Spirit who lives in you is greater than the sin that works against you (1 John 4:4).

What Galatians 5:1 Is Actually Saying

The grammar here is telling. Paul does not say: Christ will set you free if you work hard enough. He says: Christ has set you free. The freedom is the accomplished reality. The imperative — stand firm, do not submit again — follows from it. It is a call to live from what is already true, not to create what does not yet exist.

The yoke of slavery in Galatians 5:1 is the yoke of a life spent trying to earn what has already been given. But it is also, by extension, the yoke of sin — the yoke of patterns of life that have been your master and that, in Christ, no longer have to be. Do not submit again implies that submitting is still possible — that the freedom can be lived below, that the chains can be picked back up. This is not a reason for despair. It is a reason for vigilance. And it is simultaneously a call and a promise: you were made free. Live like it.

A Final Word

You have come through eleven studies of honest, searching material. If you have read carefully, something has been asked of you: a willingness to look without flinching at the ways we hide from God and from ourselves, at the patterns we have normalized, at the warnings we have been sleeping through. That is not a comfortable thing. It is necessary. And it is the gift that the watchman's trumpet was always meant to give.

But the watchman's job ends at the gates. The one who enters the gate, the one who meets you there, is not the watchman. It is the God who swore by his own life that he takes no pleasure in your destruction — who sent his Son not to condemn the world but that the world through him might be saved (John 3:17), who calls out to the drifting and the comfortable and the frightened and the hardening with the same unbearable cry: turn back, turn back from your evil ways, for why will you die?

He is not asking for a perfect record. He is asking for a turned face and an open hand. The rest — the transformation, the growth, the slow and often painfully gradual work of becoming what we were made to be — he has promised to do himself. "And I am sure of this, that he who began a good work in you will bring it to completion at the day of Jesus Christ" (Philippians 1:6).

That is not wishful thinking. It is the word of the God who does not lie, spoken over the life of everyone who has turned their face toward him. The trumpet has sounded. The gate is open. Come.

Christ, Our Anchor

"So if the Son sets you free, you will be free indeed" (John 8:36). This is the last word. Not free in theory. Not free potentially. Free indeed — a freedom that is genuine, substantial, and secured by the one who spoke it.

The resurrection of Jesus is not only a past event. It is a participated reality for those who are in him. The same power that raised Christ from the dead is the power at work in you (Ephesians 1:19–20). It is not a small power. It is not a power that can be defeated by the strength of your sin or the length of your drift or the stubbornness of your heart. It is the power that conquered death itself.

This is the hope that holds. Not the hope of a person who is doing well enough that they dare to hope. The hope of a person who has nothing to bring but their need, and who has found that the God who meets need has already brought everything that need requires. You are no longer slaves. That is what he says. Believe it. And turn.

For reflection & discussion

  • The study says the Christian life is "a participation in something that has already happened." How does it change the way you approach holiness if freedom from sin is already true rather than something you are working toward?
  • The word logizesthe (reckon, consider) in Romans 6:11 means: treat this as a settled fact and live from it. What would it look like today to live from the fact that you are dead to sin and alive to God — rather than hoping to feel that way eventually?
  • Repentance is described here as directional: always toward God rather than merely away from sin. How does that change the texture of repentance for you? Is there a sin or pattern you have been confessing without having yet turned your face toward God?
  • 1 Corinthians 10:13 promises a way of escape from every temptation. When have you actually found and taken that way of escape? What made it possible? And when have you looked away from it — and what was happening in you at that moment?
  • Looking back over this entire study — all twelve pages — what is the one thing that most needs to move from your head to something you are actually doing? Who will you tell, and when?
