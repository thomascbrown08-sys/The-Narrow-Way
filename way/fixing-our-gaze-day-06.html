Day 6

The Voice from Heaven

Opening Prayer

Father, before Jesus preached a single sermon, healed a single wound, or bore a single stripe — you spoke these words over him. Help us understand what it means that he went to the cross not to earn your love but because he already possessed it. Let this reorder everything we think we know about identity, approval, and the kind of love that actually holds. Amen.

"When Jesus was baptized, he went up immediately from the water. The heavens suddenly opened for him, and he saw the Spirit of God descending like a dove and coming to rest on him. And a voice from heaven said: 'This is my beloved Son, with whom I am well-pleased.'" Matthew 3:16–17, CSB

Three sentences. A cracked-open sky, a descending dove, a voice — and everything the Gospel of Matthew is going to say about Jesus Christ has already been said. The rest is unpacking.

Note what has happened in these verses, and what has not. Jesus has not yet preached a word in public. He has not healed anyone. He has not performed a single miracle, issued a single command, or made a single claim about himself. He has done nothing that the watching crowd could cite as grounds for divine approval. He walks into the water as a carpenter from Nazareth, and he walks out of it having heard his Father's voice — before any of it.

This is not incidental. It is the whole shape of the Gospel in miniature. The Father's delight in the Son is not earned by the Son's obedience; it is the foundation from which the Son's obedience flows. Jesus heals the sick and loves the outcast and faces the cross not in order to secure the Father's approval but because he already has it, has always had it, will never lose it. He operates from a position of settled belovedness, not anxious achievement.

The word the Father uses — agapētos, "beloved" — is not a generic term of affection. In its Old Testament background, it carries the weight of a singular, irreplaceable love: the love Abraham had for Isaac, the only son (Genesis 22:2), the one who cannot be replaced and whose loss would be the loss of everything. The Father is not saying I am fond of you. He is saying: you are the one I love most, the one I will give everything for, the one in whom my whole heart rests.

The voice also echoes Isaiah 42:1 — the opening of the Servant Songs: "Behold my servant, whom I uphold, my chosen, in whom my soul delights; I have put my Spirit upon him." Matthew's readers would have heard this resonance immediately. Jesus is the long-awaited Servant of the Lord, the one Isaiah saw coming seven centuries before the Jordan river opened to him. The Servant who does not shout in the streets, who does not break a bruised reed, who brings justice quietly and at immense personal cost. The one the Spirit will rest upon and never depart from (Isaiah 11:2; 61:1).

And what of us? There is a profound and easily-missed grace hidden in this scene. Tim Keller observed that Jesus came not only to die in our place but also to live in our place — to do everything that we were required to do and could not do, so that his righteousness might be credited to our account. This means that the Father's declaration over Jesus at the Jordan — This is my beloved Son, in whom I am well-pleased — is a declaration that now covers all who are in Christ. "You are no longer a slave," Paul writes, "but a son. And if a son, then an heir" (Galatians 4:7). The Spirit who descended on Christ at his baptism is now poured into the hearts of believers, making them cry Abba — Father (Romans 8:15). The voice that spoke at the Jordan speaks now by the Spirit over every one of his redeemed children: This is my beloved.

The practical weight of this cannot be overstated. Most of us live as if the Father's approval is something we are still working toward — as if the posture of our Christian life is striving, performing, hoping that the balance eventually tips in our favor. But the baptism of Jesus inverts this entirely. Christ went to the cross from a position of perfect security, not of anxiety. He served not to be accepted but from acceptance. And by union with him — clothed in his righteousness, hidden in him before the Father — so do we (Romans 8:1; Galatians 3:27). The conditions of his acceptance have been fully met by him on our behalf. What we receive is not our own standing but his. The love that drove him into the water and out of it and ultimately to Golgotha is the same love that, because of all he accomplished, now covers everyone who is found in him — without additional condition, without ceiling, without end.

For reflection

  • The Father's approval came before the work began, not as its reward. How might your life — your prayer, your service, your willingness to fail and try again — look different if you operated from a position of settled belovedness rather than working to earn it?
  • The Spirit descended on Jesus to equip him for public ministry. Acts 10:38 tells us that "God anointed Jesus of Nazareth with the Holy Spirit and with power." What does it tell you that Jesus did what he did not in his own unassisted divine power, but in dependence on the Spirit?
  • If you are in Christ, the Father's delight in the Son now covers you (Romans 8:1; Galatians 4:4–7). Does this feel real to you, or abstract? What would need to change in your thinking for it to become a lived reality?

Going Deeper (optional)

Matthew 3:16–17 is one of the most compressed Trinitarian passages in the entire New Testament. All three Persons of the Godhead are distinctly present and active in a single moment: the Son in the water, the Spirit descending, the Father speaking. This is not incidental — it reflects the eternal inner life of God, what theologians call the immanent Trinity: the love the Father expresses for the Son at the Jordan is not a new thing introduced by the Incarnation. It is the eternal love shared within the Godhead from before time, now publicly disclosed within time.

This matters enormously for understanding what the cross will mean. When Jesus goes to Calvary, he does not go as a reluctant victim abandoned by a distant deity. He goes as the beloved Son, in full agreement with the Father and the Spirit, executing the plan of redemption that their eternal love has always purposed. The cry of dereliction — "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" — is not the Father stopping to love the Son. It is the Son bearing, in our place, the experience of God-forsakenness that we deserved, so that we might never have to bear it. The voice at the baptism is not withdrawn at the cross; it is what makes the cross possible. The Trinity does not fracture at Golgotha. It sacrifices together — at infinite cost to all three Persons — for the sake of the ones they have loved from before the world began.
