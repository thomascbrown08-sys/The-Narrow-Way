Day 65

Tried and Condemned

Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, Pilate asked you if you were the King of the Jews and you answered him — and then fell silent before all the accusations that followed. Pilate was amazed. He knew something was wrong with this trial. He tried four times to release you. And yet you said nothing to save yourself, because saving yourself was not what you had come to do. Let your silence speak to us today with greater weight than many words. Amen.

"And Pilate asked him, 'Are you the King of the Jews?' And he answered him, 'You have said so.' And the chief priests accused him of many things. And Pilate again asked him, 'Have you no answer to make? See how many charges they bring against you.' But Jesus made no further answer, so that Pilate was amazed." Mark 15:2–5, ESV

The trials — the Sanhedrin, then Pilate, then Herod, then back to Pilate — are a procession of injustice so blatant that the participants themselves know it. The Sanhedrin violates its own legal standards: night proceedings were illegal, testimony must agree and these witnesses cannot keep their stories straight, conviction on the same day as the hearing was forbidden. Pilate declares three times that he finds no guilt in this man (John 18:38; 19:4; 19:6). Herod finds him amusing but not dangerous. And yet the machinery moves forward, driven by a political calculation that the religious leaders make explicitly: "It is better for you that one man should die for the people" (John 11:50).

Before Pilate, Jesus answers the one question that matters — Are you the King of the Jews? — and then says almost nothing more. The chief priests pile accusation on accusation. Pilate turns to him again: have you no answer? See how many charges. But he stands there in silence. And Mark records the detail with a word that we do not often associate with Pilate: he was amazed.

The silence is not helplessness. He who taught with authority, who answered every challenge and entrapment the Pharisees laid before him with devastating clarity, who dismantled the lawyers' trick questions and stilled the crowds with his presence — he has no answer here not because no answer exists but because an answer would interfere with what is happening. He has come to be condemned. An adequate defense would acquit him. Acquittal is not why he is here.

Isaiah had seen this silence centuries before: He was oppressed, and he was afflicted, yet he opened not his mouth; like a lamb that is led to the slaughter, and like a sheep that before its shearers is silent, so he opened not his mouth (Isaiah 53:7). The silence of the Servant is not passivity. It is the deliberate choosing of the role that was appointed — the role of the one who bears in silence rather than defends in eloquence. Every false accusation that went unanswered was, by his choice, allowed to stand — not because it was true, but because the one who bore it was already carrying something heavier than the false testimony.

Pilate's four attempts to release him (John 18:39; 19:4; 19:6; 19:12) are among the most agonizing moments in the trial narratives. He knows. His wife has sent a message: Have nothing to do with that righteous man (Matthew 27:19). His own judicial instinct tells him the charges are politically motivated. And he asks Jesus one last time, privately: "Where are you from?" (John 19:9). Jesus does not answer. And Pilate, in his frustration, says: "Don't you know that I have the power to release you and the power to crucify you?" And Jesus answers this one, gently: "You would have no authority over me at all unless it had been given you from above" (John 19:11).

Even before the judge who is about to condemn him, he reminds the judge who the real authority is. Then he falls silent again, and the sentence is given, and it is wrong, and it will turn out to be the most right thing that has ever happened.

For reflection

  • He remained silent before false accusations — not because they were unanswerable, but because answering them would have prevented what he had come to accomplish. Is there a situation in your life where the Spirit might be calling you to a similar silence — not the silence of helplessness but the silence of choosing not to defend yourself when defense would cost something more important?
  • Pilate knew the verdict was wrong and gave it anyway. The political calculation overrode the moral one. Where in your own life have you known the right thing and done the convenient thing instead? What does Pilate's choice — and the way history has remembered it — tell you about the long-term cost of that kind of compromise?
  • "You would have no authority over me unless it had been given you from above." Even in his condemnation, Jesus asserts the sovereignty of the Father over the entire proceeding. What does it mean that the most unjust verdict in human history was simultaneously within the Father's sovereign purpose? How do you hold injustice and sovereignty together?

Going Deeper (optional)

The trial and condemnation of Jesus is one of the most theologically compressed passages in the Gospels, because it sets in relief the paradox at the heart of the atonement: the perfectly just one condemned by an unjust process, the guiltless one bearing the verdict meant for the guilty, the one who holds all authority submitting to an authority that is exercising it corruptly. Peter's sermon at Pentecost captures this paradox with striking clarity: "This Jesus, delivered up according to the definite plan and foreknowledge of God, you crucified and killed by the hands of lawless men" (Acts 2:23). Two things are simultaneously true: this was the definite plan of God, and lawless men did it. The sovereignty of God does not eliminate the moral responsibility of the agents; it encompasses it.

The Reformed tradition's doctrine of divine providence — that God governs all things including the sinful acts of free agents — finds nowhere more demanding a test case than Calvary. The Pilates and Caiaphases of that day are not puppets acting out a mechanical script; they are moral agents making real choices for which they are genuinely responsible (Acts 2:23 speaks of their guilt even while affirming God's foreknowledge). And yet the event they bring about is the very event that accomplishes the salvation of the world. This is not a comfortable paradox to sit with — and it was not meant to be. It is the form that the deepest mystery of providence takes when it becomes flesh and is condemned to death by the ones it came to save.
