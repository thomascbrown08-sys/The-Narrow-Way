Day 26

You Can't Do This Alone

Where there is no guidance, a people falls, but in an abundance of counselors there is safety. Proverbs 11:14 (ESV)
And let us consider how to stir up one another to love and good works, not neglecting to meet together, as is the habit of some, but encouraging one another, and all the more as you see the Day drawing near. Hebrews 10:24–25 (ESV)

Individual discernment has real limits. We have seen throughout this study that we are prone to self-deception, culturally shaped in ways we don't notice, and susceptible to errors that flatter us. The corrective God provides for these limitations is not primarily a technique. It is community.

Proverbs' wisdom about counselors is practical rather than romantic. It doesn't promise that community will always agree with you. It promises that safety lies in submitting your judgment to the scrutiny of others rather than relying on it alone. A decision that seems obviously right to you but that multiple wise people in your life question — that pattern should give you serious pause.

Hebrews frames the gathering of believers in terms of mutual accountability and encouragement — stirring up one another, not just being stirred. This is active and reciprocal. The community of faith is not simply a resource you draw on. It is a body you participate in, whose health partly depends on your active contribution.

Lone-wolf Christianity — the person who reads widely, forms strong views, but submits those views to no one — is both unbiblical and spiritually dangerous. The most confident people are often the most in need of this correction. Strong private conviction without community accountability is a vulnerability, not a strength.

Put It Into Practice

Is there someone in your life who has genuine permission to challenge what you believe and how you're living? If not, what is one step you could take this week toward that kind of relationship?

For reflection

Why does individualism feel safer than community accountability? What are we actually protecting when we resist it? Who in your life currently serves the function of a wise counselor? What makes them trustworthy for that role? What does it look like to be an active contributor to the discernment health of a community, rather than just a receiver?
