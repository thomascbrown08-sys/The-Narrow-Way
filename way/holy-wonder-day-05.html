Day 5

The One Thing

The one thing I ask of the LORD — the thing I seek most — is to live in the house of the LORD all the days of my life, delighting in the LORD's perfections and meditating in his Temple. Psalm 27:4 (NLT)
Seek the Kingdom of God above all else, and live righteously, and he will give you everything you need. Matthew 6:33 (NLT)

David is writing from a position of real danger. Enemies surround him. His life is under threat. And the one thing he asks for — the thing he calls the thing he seeks most — is not rescue, not victory, not justice. It is to behold the beauty of the LORD.

This is either the strangest priority in the history of human desire, or it is the sanest. David has encountered the living God — has stood in his presence, been carried through dark valleys, tasted his goodness — and what he discovered is that there is nothing in the universe as worth wanting as God himself. Everything else is a derivative good. God is the source.

The word the NLT translates as "delighting in" carries the sense of gazing with wonder — the way you look at something so beautiful you can't quite stop looking. David's worship was not duty. It was longing. He wanted God the way a thirsty person wants water.

This is what healthy worship feels like at its deepest — not an obligation met but a desire satisfied. And the strange gift of studying the fear of God and the life of worship is that these thirty days are not primarily about improving your technique. They are about recovering — or perhaps discovering for the first time — that the God before whom we stand is so beautiful that beholding him is its own reward.

Put It Into Practice

Write down your honest answer to this question: what is the one thing you seek most in life right now? Don't write what you think you should say. Be honest. Then sit with Psalm 27:4 and ask God to slowly make it true of you.

For reflection

David's one thing is the presence of God. What is yours, honestly? What does that reveal about where your heart is pointed? Is there a difference between wanting to worship and wanting God himself? What might that difference feel like? What would it cost you — practically and emotionally — if God became your one thing the way he was David's?
