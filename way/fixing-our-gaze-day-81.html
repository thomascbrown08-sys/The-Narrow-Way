Day 81

Christ in You, the Hope of Glory

Opening Prayer

Lord, Paul calls the mystery hidden for ages and generations "Christ in you, the hope of glory." Not Christ above us, not Christ before us, not Christ at a theological distance we are working toward — Christ in you. The hope of glory is not a far-off aspiration; it is a present indwelling Person. Let that present reality matter to us today. Amen.

"To them God chose to make known how great among the Gentiles are the riches of the glory of this mystery, which is Christ in you, the hope of glory." Colossians 1:27, ESV

Paul has been describing a mystery — a word he uses for something once hidden in God's purposes and now disclosed. The mystery of the ages, the thing that prophets and kings and angels longed to understand, the hidden plan of God that the whole of the Old Testament was reaching toward without being able to name — Paul names it in five words: Christ in you, the hope of glory.

Not "Christ for you" — though he is that. Not "Christ with you" — though he is that too. In you. The indwelling is the mystery. That the eternal Son who made the cosmos and inhabits eternity would take up residence in the interior of a human being — not merely visiting, not merely influencing from outside, but making his home inside the life of the person who receives him — this is the disclosure that no one fully anticipated. The temple was always a symbol pointing toward this: not a building of stone but a living person, indwelt by the fullness of the God who dwelt in the cloud of glory over the ark. That is what you are. The old temple is obsolete because the true temple has arrived — in you, by the Spirit, where Christ himself has come to dwell.

And this indwelling is the hope of glory. Not hope in the sense of uncertain wishing — not "perhaps glory, if things go well." Hope in the New Testament sense of confident expectation: a future reality guaranteed by its present down payment. The Spirit in you now is the firstfruits of the glory that is coming (Romans 8:23). Christ in you now is the guarantee of the transformation that will be complete at the resurrection. The presence of the indwelling Lord is not the entire inheritance; it is the engagement ring, the title deed, the signed contract that makes the future certain in advance.

What is the glory that is hoped for? Romans 8:18 speaks of it as the glory to be revealed to us — a disclosure that will surpass every present suffering by an incalculable margin. 2 Corinthians 3:18 describes it as the transformation into Christ's own image from one degree of glory to another. 1 John 3:2 says simply: "when he appears, we shall be like him, for we shall see him as he is." The consummation of what Christ in you has begun is to become fully what you already are in him — the image of God, restored and glorified, reflecting the face of the one who indwells you with a clarity and completeness that the present life, with its fog and its failures and its partial sight, cannot yet achieve.

Jonathan Edwards described the Christian life as a river whose source is the Holy Spirit within the believer, flowing from Christ in you outward — through love, through prayer, through service, through the witness of a changed life — and ultimately flowing into the ocean of God's own life at the consummation. The direction is always outward from the indwelling. The mystery does not stay hidden inside. Christ in you presses toward expression, toward the world, toward the neighbor, toward the glory that will one day be visible in what it is now only producing quietly from within.

Nine days remain. We are nearly at the capstone. These final days are the days of dwelling — not rushing toward the end but inhabiting what has been given. Christ is in you. The hope of glory is present, active, certain, and working its way outward from the inside. This is not doctrine for contemplation. It is a Person to be received, daily, as the ground and the direction of everything you are and do and hope for.

For reflection

  • "Christ in you" — not "Christ whom you are working toward" or "Christ whom you aspire to resemble." Present tense, indicative mood, dwelling. Does this feel like your daily experience of faith, or more like a theological truth you affirm but rarely inhabit? What is the gap, and what might help close it?
  • The hope of glory is guaranteed by its down payment — the Spirit now is the firstfruits of the glory coming. Where in your life do you most see what might be "firstfruits" — places where the indwelling Christ is already producing something that points toward what will be complete? What does that give you confidence about?
  • "We shall be like him, for we shall see him as he is." The final transformation is tied to the final seeing — beholding produces becoming. How has eighty-one days of fixing your gaze on Christ already begun to produce something in you? What do you see in yourself now that you did not see before you began?

Going Deeper (optional)

The phrase "Christ in you, the hope of glory" stands at the intersection of two of Paul's most foundational theological convictions: the indwelling of the Spirit as the present reality of salvation, and the future glory as its consummation. The Reformers, following Augustine, emphasized that the Christian life is fundamentally a life of progressive transformation — not a static possession of grace but a dynamic movement from what we are toward what we will be, powered by the Spirit who already dwells within. Calvin spoke of the Christian life as a pilgrimage of mortification and vivification: the old self dying daily, the new self rising daily, in the direction of the image of Christ.

The phrase also resolves a question that often troubles new believers: if I have Christ in me, why do I still struggle with sin? The answer the New Testament gives is not "you don't really have him yet" or "perhaps you never did." It is "the firstfruits are real; the full harvest is not yet." The indwelling Christ is the guarantee and the agent of the transformation that is underway — but underway is not complete. Sanctification is real growth in real time, and real growth takes time. The seed is genuinely present; the tree is not yet fully grown. This tension — between the certainty of what Christ in you is producing and the incompleteness of what is yet visible — is not a failure of the promise. It is the shape of hope: present, real, active, and not yet finished.
