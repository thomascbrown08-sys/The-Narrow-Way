Day 20

What the Bible Actually Says About Gifts

Now there are varieties of gifts, but the same Spirit; and there are varieties of service, but the same Lord; and there are varieties of activities, but it is the same God who empowers them all in everyone. To each is given the manifestation of the Spirit for the common good. 1 Corinthians 12:4–7 (ESV)
Having gifts that differ according to the grace given to us, let us use them: if prophecy, in proportion to our faith; if service, in our serving; the one who teaches, in his teaching; the one who exhorts, in his exhortation; the one who contributes, in generosity; the one who leads, with zeal; the one who does acts of mercy, with cheerfulness. Romans 12:6–8 (ESV)

The Bible clearly and consistently teaches that the Holy Spirit distributes gifts to believers for the building up of the church. This is not controversial. The Spirit is generous, sovereign in his distribution, and purposeful — the gifts are given for the common good, not for individual benefit or display.

What the Bible actually says about gifts is considerably less tidy than either the enthusiast or the skeptic often acknowledges. The gift lists in 1 Corinthians 12, Romans 12, and Ephesians 4 overlap but are not identical. They include the obviously miraculous (healing, tongues, miracles), the obviously ordinary (serving, giving, mercy), and things that sit somewhere in between (prophecy, teaching, exhortation).

The debate between cessationists (who believe certain gifts ceased with the apostolic age) and continuationists (who believe all gifts continue) is a real debate among serious, Bible-believing Christians. It deserves more than a bumper-sticker answer in either direction.

The goal of the next three days is to give that debate a fair hearing — looking honestly at the biblical evidence for continuationism, then at the genuine grounds for sustained skepticism, and then landing on the posture of a thoughtful, discerning believer who takes Scripture seriously from both directions.

Put It Into Practice

Read the three gift lists side by side: 1 Corinthians 12:8–10, Romans 12:6–8, and Ephesians 4:11. Notice what overlaps and what differs. Notice which gifts feel familiar and which feel foreign to your experience. Sit with the diversity.

For reflection

What gifts do you believe you have? How did you come to recognize them? Has the church confirmed your sense? What assumptions do you bring into this topic? Are they formed by Scripture, tradition, experience, or reaction? Why does Paul say the gifts are given 'for the common good' rather than for the individual? What difference does that make?
