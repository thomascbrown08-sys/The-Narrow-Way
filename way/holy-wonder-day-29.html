Day 29

The People We Worship With

If one part suffers, all the parts suffer with it, and if one part is honored, all the parts are glad. All of you together are Christ's body, and each of you is a part of it. 1 Corinthians 12:26–27 (NLT)
And the King will say, 'I tell you the truth, when you did it to one of the least of these my brothers and sisters, you were doing it to me!' Matthew 25:40 (NLT)

C.S. Lewis wrote in "The Weight of Glory" that there are no ordinary people. Every person you have ever met will live forever — either in a glory that makes them dazzling beyond imagining, or in a ruin beyond what we can comprehend. The dullest, most uninteresting person you sit next to in church is an immortal soul. He meant that the people around you deserve a reverence only slightly less than you give to God.

This reframes corporate worship entirely. It is not a gathering of individuals who happen to be in the same room doing the same thing. It is the assembled body of Christ — each person a member, each necessary, each carrying something no one else carries. The man whose singing is off-key is an eternal soul made in the image of God. The woman whose theology you find annoying is Christ's sister.

Jesus' words in Matthew 25 push this further: what you do to the least of these, you do to him. The person beside you in the pew is not just your fellow worshiper. They are, in some mysterious way, the presence of Christ to you — and you to them.

We often go to church hoping to encounter God while managing to avoid too much contact with the people. But the people are part of what God is doing in worship. The one thing you cannot worship well while doing entirely alone is loving the body.

Put It Into Practice

This Sunday, choose one person in your church community — someone you don't know well, or someone you've found difficult — and pray for them before the service. During the service, be aware of them as an immortal soul in the presence of God alongside you.

For reflection

Do you find it easy or difficult to think of the people around you in church as essential to your worship? What shapes your experience of corporate worship most? Lewis says your neighbor is the holiest object presented to your senses apart from the sacrament. What would it change in how you treat people if you actually believed that? Where has the body of Christ been the presence of Christ to you? Who has served that role?
