Day 16

The Table as Worship

For I pass on to you what I received from the Lord himself. On the night when he was betrayed, the Lord Jesus took some bread and gave thanks to God for it. Then he broke it in pieces and said, "This is my body, which is given for you. Do this in remembrance of me." In the same way, he took the cup of wine after supper, saying, "This cup is the new covenant between God and his blood. Do this in remembrance of me as often as you drink it." For every time you eat this bread and drink this cup, you are announcing the Lord's death until he comes again. 1 Corinthians 11:23–26 (NLT)

The Lord's Supper is one of the most concentrated acts of worship available to us, and one of the most underappreciated. In a few minutes of bread and cup, we do something extraordinary: we remember backward, we receive in the present, and we announce forward.

We remember: this body was broken. This blood was poured out. Everything we have — forgiveness, adoption, access to God, the promise of resurrection — cost something. The Table will not let us forget the cost. Every time we take it, we are rehearsing the heart of the gospel.

We receive: the early church consistently understood the Lord's Supper as a genuine encounter with Christ — not a memorial of someone absent, but a meeting with someone present. We come to the Table hungry, and we find there the one who called himself the bread of life.

We announce: Paul says this proclaims the Lord's death until he comes again. The Lord's Supper is an eschatological act — it reaches forward to the wedding supper of the Lamb, the great feast at the end of all things. Every time we take it, we are rehearsing a meal we will one day take in his physical presence.

From Church History — The Frequency Debate and What It Reveals

John Calvin wanted the Lord's Supper celebrated every Sunday in Geneva. The city council refused, worried the people would treat it as routine. They compromised on quarterly observance. Calvin later called this compromise one of the great failures of the Reformation. His instinct was sound: the Table feeds the soul in ways nothing else quite does. Churches that observe it rarely often report that members simply forget its weight and significance. The frequency debate is ultimately a debate about what we think the Table actually does — and whether we trust it to do it.

Put It Into Practice

The next time you take the Lord's Supper, go slowly. As you take the bread, name something Christ's body accomplished for you. As you take the cup, name something the blood of the covenant has secured. Let the symbols do their work.

For reflection

How do you typically experience the Lord's Supper — as a meaningful encounter or a familiar routine? What would help you receive it more fully? What does it mean to you that every time you take the Lord's Supper you are "announcing" something? Who is the announcement for? If the Table is a genuine meeting with Christ, what would you want to bring to that meeting this week?
