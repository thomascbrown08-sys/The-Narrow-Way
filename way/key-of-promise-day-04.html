Day 4

I Will Remember Their Sins No More

“But this is the new covenant I will make with the people of Israel after those days,” says the Lord. “I will put my instructions deep within them, and I will write them on their hearts. I will be their God, and they will be my people. And they will not need to teach their neighbors, nor will they need to teach their relatives, saying, ‘You should know the Lord.’ For everyone, from the least to the greatest, will know me already,” says the Lord. “And I will forgive their wickedness, and I will never again remember their sins.” Jeremiah 31:33–34 (NLT)

Jeremiah spoke this promise to people at the absolute bottom. Jerusalem was falling. The temple would burn. They were being marched into exile, and every catastrophe could be read as the deserved end of a covenant they had broken a thousand times. To those people, at that moment, God promised something brand new: not a patched-up old arrangement, but a new covenant — and at its heart, a stunning clause: “I will never again remember their sins.”

We must be careful what we think that means. It does not mean God's memory fails — the God who knows all things does not misplace facts. It is covenant language, the language of a court and a king. To “remember sins no more” is to refuse to count them, to decline forever to bring them up as a charge. It is a legal not-counting, a sworn decision never to hold your wickedness against you. Your sins are not forgotten in the sense of slipping his mind. They are forgotten in the far stronger sense that he has bound himself never to raise them again.

The writer of Hebrews tells us how God can righteously make such a promise: this new covenant is sealed “in his blood.” The not-counting is not leniency; it is purchased. God can refuse to bring up your sin because the sin was already brought up — at the cross, and answered there in full. The promise made to exiles is the promise kept in Christ.

Consider what this dismantles in Doubting Castle. The giant's dungeon is essentially a courtroom that never adjourns, where your old sins are read out on an endless loop. But God has closed the court. He has entered a verdict and sworn never to reopen the case. When an old sin comes back to accuse you — one you confessed years ago, one you thought was settled — you are not hearing the voice of God. God has promised not to remember it. You are hearing the voice of the accuser, reading from a file the Judge has already sealed.

There is a freedom here that grace alone can give. If God has stopped counting your sins, you are permitted to stop counting them too — not by pretending they never happened, but by refusing to pick up what God has laid down forever.

Pray the Promise

Think of one sin you have already confessed but still carry — one you keep re-counting. Pray the promise over it: “You have sworn to remember this no more. You sealed that promise in Christ's blood. I will stop holding what you have let go.”

When it returns to accuse you again, answer it not with your own defense but with God's oath: he remembers it no more.

For reflection

Which forgiven sin do you keep re-counting against yourself? If God has bound himself never to bring it up, whose voice are you listening to when it returns?
