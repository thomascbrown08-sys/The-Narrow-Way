Day 11

Testing What We're Taught

Beloved, do not believe every spirit, but test the spirits to see whether they are from God, for many false prophets have gone out into the world. By this you know the Spirit of God: every spirit that confesses that Jesus Christ has come in the flesh is from God, and every spirit that does not confess Jesus is not from God. 1 John 4:1–3 (ESV)
Beware of false prophets, who come to you in sheep's clothing but inwardly are ravenous wolves. You will recognize them by their fruits. Matthew 7:15–16 (ESV)

'Test the spirits' is a command, not a suggestion. John doesn't say consider testing or test if you feel uncertain. He says do not believe every spirit — the default posture is testing, not automatic acceptance.

John gives a doctrinal criterion: does the teaching confess that Jesus Christ has come in the flesh? This is not arbitrary. It goes to the heart of the gospel — the incarnation, the real humanity of Jesus, the fact that God actually entered history and took on a human body. Anything that blurs or denies this is, John says, not from God.

Jesus adds a practical criterion: fruit. Not the immediate popularity of a teacher, or the impressiveness of their credentials, or the size of their platform. Fruit — what does this teaching produce in people's lives over time? Does it make people more loving, more humble, more Christ-like? Or does it produce pride, dependency, or drift from Scripture?

Together, these give us a practical framework for evaluating a teacher or a teaching: Does it rightly handle who Jesus is? Does it accord with apostolic teaching as recorded in Scripture? And what does it produce in people who follow it? Three questions. Most dangerous teaching fails at least one.

Put It Into Practice

Think of your primary source of spiritual teaching — a pastor, author, podcast host, or mentor. Apply these three questions honestly: Does their teaching rightly represent Jesus? Does it accord with Scripture? What fruit do you observe in people shaped by it?

For reflection

What would make you question a teacher you currently trust? Is there anything that would be disqualifying for you? Why does John begin with a doctrinal test rather than a character test? What does that tell us about the relationship between doctrine and discernment? How do you distinguish between fruit that is genuinely spiritual and fruit that is merely cultural success?
