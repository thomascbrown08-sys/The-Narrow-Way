Holy Wonder · Section 2: What Does “Fear” Actually Mean?Day 6 of 30
Day 6

Two Kinds of Fear

Such love has no fear, because perfect love expels all fear. If we are afraid, it is for fear of punishment, and this shows that we have not fully experienced his perfect love. 1 John 4:18 (NLT)
Since we are receiving a Kingdom that is unshakable, let us be thankful and please God by worshiping him with holy fear and awe. For our God is a devouring fire. Hebrews 12:28–29 (NLT)

Here is a question the Bible seems to answer two different ways: Should we fear God, or not? First John says perfect love drives out fear. Hebrews says worship him with holy fear and awe. Which is it?

The answer is that there are two kinds of fear, and only one of them has been expelled. Theologians have called them servile fear and filial fear. Servile fear is the slave's dread of an angry master — the fear that God is watching you, waiting to punish you, ready to condemn. This is the fear that 1 John says love expels. If you are in Christ, there is no condemnation (Romans 8:1). That fear has no place.

But filial fear — the child's deep, reverent awe of a beloved and holy Father — is not expelled by love. It is deepened by it. The closer you draw to God, the more you understand who he is, the more this kind of fear grows. It is the trembling of genuine encounter, not the cringing of threatened punishment. It draws you toward God, not away from him.

Hebrews says our God is a devouring fire. That has not changed in the New Covenant. What has changed is that we approach that fire not as enemies who deserve to be consumed, but as children who have been brought near in Christ. The fire is still fire. And the worship it produces in us is not casual. It is holy wonder.

Put It Into Practice

Think about your own relationship with God. Which kind of fear is more present in you right now — the servile fear that makes you want to hide, or the filial awe that makes you want to draw near? Talk to God honestly about what you find.

For reflection

Where did your picture of God come from — your upbringing, a specific experience, Scripture, something else? How has that shaped which kind of fear is more natural to you? What would it look like for love and reverent awe to exist in you at the same time, feeding each other rather than competing? First John says servile fear shows we haven't fully experienced God's perfect love. What do you think would help you experience that love more fully?
