Day 54

No One Ever Spoke Like This Man

Opening Prayer

Lord, the officers sent to arrest you came back empty-handed, undone by your words. They had a task and they forgot it. They had an order and they could not carry it out, because something in what you said broke through every professional obligation and left them simply standing there, unable to explain what had happened to them. We pray for something like that today. Not information — encounter. Amen.

"The officers then came to the chief priests and Pharisees, who said to them, 'Why did you not bring him?' The officers answered, 'No one ever spoke like this man!'" John 7:45–46, ESV

The Pharisees had sent officers — temple police — to arrest Jesus. It was not a casual assignment. These were professional enforcers of public order in the temple precincts, experienced with crowds and confrontations, familiar with religious controversies and the particular volatility of festival gatherings. They went with a mandate. They came back without the prisoner. When asked to explain themselves, they had only seven words: No one ever spoke like this man.

These men were not disciples. They were not sympathizers. They were not people who had been prepared by months of following Jesus to receive his words with favorable bias. They were sent by his opponents, tasked with his arrest, professionally obligated to carry out their orders. And when they stood in the crowd and listened to him, something happened that their professional identity could not survive intact. They heard something that broke through every armor of official indifference and left them standing empty-handed before the very authorities who sent them.

John 7 is set during the Feast of Tabernacles — Sukkot — at a point in Jesus' ministry when the controversies about his identity have reached a new intensity. Jesus has been teaching in the temple courts, and the crowds are divided (7:40–43): some say "This is really the Prophet"; others say "This is the Christ"; others raise objections about geography. The atmosphere is charged. And into this charged atmosphere, the officers stand and hear him — and are rendered incapable of their assigned task.

What does it mean that no one ever spoke like this man? It cannot be merely rhetorical brilliance — the ancient world had great orators, and the officers would have heard many teachers in the temple precincts. It is not the eloquence alone. It is the authority behind the eloquence: the sense, which we have been exploring since Day 46, that the words have a source behind them that no human tradition can explain or fully account for. There is a weight to what he says that does not derive from his training or his citations or his reputation. It derives from who he is. And even hostile professional officers, sent to silence him, could feel it.

C.H. Spurgeon preached on this text and made the observation that the only response adequate to this verdict is the same one — that having heard Christ speak in the Scripture, through the Spirit, we come away saying something similar. Not that he speaks the most interesting things or the most useful things or the most culturally sophisticated things. That no one else has ever spoken like this, because no one else is what he is. His words have a texture to them that the words of every other teacher, however great, do not share. They press differently. They stay differently. They do something to the one who truly hears them that no merely human teacher's words can do.

The Pharisees' response to the officers' report is contemptuous: "Have you also been deceived?" (7:47). They assume that being moved by Jesus' words is a form of intellectual failure — gullibility, susceptibility, the kind of thing that happens to ordinary unlearned people. The officers' experience is dismissed as weakness rather than received as testimony. And in that dismissal, the Pharisees demonstrate precisely what Jesus has been saying about the condition of the hardened heart: that expertise and learning can function as an insulation against the very thing they are supposed to lead toward. The officers went to arrest him and came back undone. The Pharisees interrogated the officers and felt nothing. The officers' failure is, from one angle, the most instructive moment in the chapter.

For reflection

  • The officers came to do a task and were undone by what they heard. Has there been a moment when a word of Christ — from Scripture, from a sermon, from a passage you had read before — broke through your ordinary resistance and simply stopped you? What was it, and what did it do to you?
  • The Pharisees dismissed the officers' response as being "deceived." They treated being moved by Christ's words as a form of intellectual failure rather than a form of appropriate response. Is there a way that sophistication, familiarity, or theological learning functions in your own life as an insulation against genuine encounter with what he says?
  • "No one ever spoke like this man" — said by people who were not trying to be impressed. What would it mean to come to Christ's words today not with the goal of extracting useful content but with the openness of the officers: simply to stand and hear, and to see what happens?

Going Deeper (optional)

The officers' verdict in John 7:46 is one of several testimonies to the unique character of Jesus' speech scattered throughout the Gospels — the centurion at the cross who says "Surely this man was the Son of God" (Mark 15:39), the disciples on the road to Emmaus whose hearts "burned within them" while he spoke (Luke 24:32), and the Sermon on the Mount's crowd who were "astonished at his teaching, for he was teaching them as one who had authority" (Matthew 7:28–29). These are not the responses of people who were predisposed to be impressed. They are the responses of observers who encountered something they could not categorize and reported it honestly.

The Reformed tradition's doctrine of the internal testimony of the Holy Spirit (the testimonium Spiritus Sancti internum, developed most fully by Calvin in the Institutes, 1.7–8) is relevant here: the Scripture does not establish its own authority primarily through external arguments and proofs, though these are not without value. It establishes it primarily through the internal witness of the Spirit, who works in the reader's heart to produce the recognition that this is the word of God — that what is heard here is different in kind from what is heard elsewhere. The officers' experience in John 7 is, in a sense, a pre-Pentecost version of this same testimony: the Spirit, through the voice of the Son, produced in hostile listeners a recognition they could not resist and could not explain except by the only words adequate to it. No one ever spoke like this man. This testimony, confirmed by the Spirit in the hearts of believers across twenty centuries, remains the strongest answer to the question of why the words of Jesus carry the weight they carry.
