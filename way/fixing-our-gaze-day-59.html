Day 59

All Authority Is Given to Me

Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, you give the Great Commission not from a throne room but from a hillside in Galilee, to a handful of disciples who still have doubts (Matthew 28:17). And the ground of the entire commission is not their competence or their courage but your authority — already given, already complete, heaven and earth already yours. Let that indicative precede every imperative in our lives. Amen.

"And Jesus came and said to them, 'All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me. Go therefore and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, teaching them to observe all that I have commanded you. And behold, I am with you always, to the end of the age.'" Matthew 28:18–20, ESV

Matthew's Gospel ends where it began. The opening genealogy presented Jesus as the son of Abraham, through whom all the nations of the earth would be blessed (Genesis 12:3), and the son of David, whose kingdom would have no end (2 Samuel 7:16). Now, at the close, the risen Christ stands on a Galilean hillside and unfolds the implication: the kingdom has come, the authority has been given, and the commission to carry the blessing to all nations is issued from the ground of that authority.

All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me. The first thing to notice is the word all. Not some authority, not authority in the spiritual domain while earthly authority remains with Caesar, not authority in principle subject to practical limitations. All authority. Heaven and earth together, without reservation or remainder. The risen Christ is not a spiritual power operating within a domain carved out by permission from larger forces. He is the Lord of all that is, and the resurrection is the public declaration of that lordship, the Father's vindication of the Son's life and death and the establishment of his universal reign.

The second thing to notice is that this is spoken to people who have doubts. Matthew explicitly says "when they saw him they worshiped him, but some doubted" (28:17). The commission does not wait for the doubts to resolve. It is given to doubting worshipers — people simultaneously bowing and uncertain, which is probably the most honest description of faith in this life that the New Testament offers. He does not require certainty before he sends. He sends into the uncertainty, accompanied by his own certain presence.

Therefore go. The therefore connects the command to the authority. The mission is not grounded in the disciples' confidence or their gifts or the likely receptivity of the nations. It is grounded entirely in the authority that has already been given. Go because he has the authority to back up what you proclaim. Make disciples because he has the power to save those you reach. Baptize in the Trinitarian name because the triune God who commissioned the mission will be present in it. Teach because his commands are true and his word will not return void (Isaiah 55:11).

The commission is simultaneously global and personal. All nations — the Greek panta ta ethnē, all the people groups of the earth. Not the individuals who happen to approach the church but the systematic, intentional reaching of every people group under heaven. And yet the commission is carried out by ordinary people with doubts, one conversation at a time, one baptism at a time, teaching the same words to the next generation that were taught to the last. The vast scope and the intimate simplicity are not in tension; they are the same commission at different scales.

And then the closing word — the word that Matthew ends his whole Gospel on: I am with you always, to the end of the age. This is the Emmanuel promise from Matthew 1:23 — "God with us" — now given its fullest possible expression. Not "I was with you" and not "I will be with you at the end." Always. Every moment of the commission, every step of obedience, every failure, every recovery, every day from this Galilean hillside to the last day of the present age. He does not send them out and watch from a distance. He goes with them, in the authority that is already his, to the end of everything that can end.

For reflection

  • "All authority has been given to me — therefore go." The mission rests on his authority, not your confidence. Where do you find yourself waiting for more confidence, more preparation, or more certainty before acting on what he has clearly commissioned? What would it mean to go from the authority rather than from your own readiness?
  • The commission was given to people who still had doubts. What does it mean to you that Jesus does not require doubt-free faith before commissioning? How does the presence of doubt in faith change the way you think about your own qualifications for the work he gives you?
  • "I am with you always, to the end of the age." This is Emmanuel — God with us — at its fullest. In your daily life, do you live with an awareness of this presence? What would change — in your courage, your prayer, your engagement with others — if the reality of his constant presence became more vivid to you?

Going Deeper (optional)

The Great Commission's Trinitarian formula — "baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit" — is the most explicit Trinitarian statement in the Synoptic Gospels. The singular "name" (not "names") indicates that Father, Son, and Spirit share one divine name — one identity, one being — while remaining three distinct Persons. This formula was used in Christian baptism from the very earliest period and became the standard expression of Trinitarian faith in both liturgy and theology.

The scope of "all authority in heaven and on earth" echoes and fulfills Daniel 7:13–14, which we explored in Day 12: the Son of Man receiving from the Ancient of Days "dominion and glory and a kingdom, that all peoples, nations, and languages should serve him." The resurrection and ascension are the fulfillment of that vision: the Son of Man has now been presented before the Father, has received all authority, and commissions his disciples to carry the announcement of his universal reign to all peoples. The mission of the church from Matthew 28 to the end of the age is therefore not a venture into uncertain territory, hoping that Christ might have enough authority to support what the church is doing. It is the announcement, by authorized ambassadors, of a reign that has already been established — the calling of all peoples to receive and submit to the one who already holds all authority, whether or not they yet know it. The church does not create Christ's kingdom by its mission; it announces and invites people into a kingdom that the risen Christ already possesses completely.
