Day 40

New Wine, New Wineskins

Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, you are not a renovation of what came before. You are not a patch on an old garment or new wine in an old container. You are the new thing that fulfills and surpasses everything that preceded you — the substance that the shadows were reaching toward. Give us the flexibility, the humility, and the wonder to receive you as you actually are, rather than insisting you fit inside categories we have already arranged for you. Amen.

"And no one puts new wine into old wineskins. For the wine would burst the wineskins, and the wine and the skins would both be lost. New wine calls for new wineskins." Mark 2:22, NLT

The question that occasions this saying is about fasting. The Pharisees' disciples fast; John's disciples fast; Jesus' disciples do not. Why not? The implicit accusation is that Jesus' movement lacks the appropriate austerity — that his feasting with sinners and his disciples' failure to fast represent a laxity, a looseness, a failure to take the religious life seriously enough.

Jesus answers with two images — a patch on a garment, and wine in a wineskin — that say the same thing in different forms: what he is bringing cannot be contained within the old structures. Not because the old structures were bad, but because the new thing is of a different order entirely. The old wineskin is not the enemy; it served its purpose. But new wine — fermenting, expanding, alive — will burst a dried and brittle container. The container must match the wine.

The "new wine" Jesus is describing is himself and the kingdom he is inaugurating. He is not a reformed version of the Judaism of his day, any more than he was merely a new installment in the line of the prophets. He is the one to whom the prophets pointed — which means that when he arrives, the pointing structures have reached their terminus. The sacrificial system was never meant to be permanent; it was always a sign pointing forward to the one who would offer the one sufficient sacrifice. The temple was never meant to be God's permanent dwelling; it was the place where God's presence resided until the one who is himself the temple arrived (John 2:21). The law was a tutor leading to Christ (Galatians 3:24) — not an end in itself, not the final word, but the instrument that prepared its own pupils for graduation into something larger.

The Pharisees' mistake was not their love for the old forms. It was their treatment of the forms as the substance — their inability to see that the wineskin was not the wine. They had become so committed to the container that they could not receive the thing the container was designed to hold. When the wine arrived, they could not open their hands to receive it because both hands were already occupied with defending the skin.

But this image also carries a challenge directed forward — toward us. The tendency to treat religious forms as if they were the substance they contain is not unique to first-century Pharisees. Every generation produces its own version of the old wineskin — the familiar liturgy, the comfortable theology, the inherited practice — that risks becoming more important than the reality it was meant to carry. The question is not whether forms and practices have value (they do, often enormously); it is whether they are transparent to the one they are meant to mediate, or whether they have become opaque — ends in themselves rather than windows.

The wine is always new because Christ is always more than we have yet understood. Forty days into this devotional, you have seen more facets of the diamond than you had perhaps seen before. And there are fifty more. And beyond these ninety days, there is a whole life of looking, and beyond that, an eternity of increasing sight. He does not become more familiar in the bad sense — he becomes more familiar in the best sense: the deeper you go, the more there is. The wine has not yet finished fermenting. New wineskins, always. The capacity for wonder must grow to receive the reality that keeps revealing itself as larger than any container we have brought.

For reflection

  • The Pharisees' problem was treating the container as the substance — the wineskin as if it were the wine. Is there a religious form, practice, or tradition in your own life that has become more important to you than the reality it was meant to carry? How would you know?
  • Jesus says the new wine of the kingdom cannot be poured into old, brittle structures — something must give way. What structures of thinking, tradition, or expectation in your own understanding of Christ may need to be replaced with new wineskins — more flexible, more capacious — to receive what he is actually offering?
  • We are forty days in. The wine has not stopped revealing itself as larger than any container. What is the most unexpected thing you have seen about Christ in these forty days — the facet that has most surprised you, or that has most expanded your sense of who he is?

Going Deeper (optional)

The new wine / new wineskins saying is part of Mark's presentation of Jesus as the inaugurator of an eschatological newness — a new era in the history of God's dealings with his people that both fulfills and transcends the old covenant order. The theological term for the relationship between the two covenants is "typology": the people, events, and institutions of the old covenant were types — patterns and prefigurations — of the realities that Christ would bring. The Passover lamb was a type of Christ; the temple was a type of Christ; the Davidic king was a type of Christ. None of these types is destroyed by the arrival of the reality; they are fulfilled — filled full of their ultimate meaning — in the one they were always pointing toward.

The Reformed tradition, following the Westminster Standards' teaching on the unity of the covenant of grace across both Testaments, insists that the discontinuity between old and new covenants is real but not total. It is the discontinuity of fulfillment: the forms change because the substance has arrived, but the substance they carried across the centuries is the same substance — grace, through faith, in Christ. What changes is not the gospel but its clarity. The shadows give way to the light; the types give way to the antitype; the wineskin gives way to the wine. And the wine that has arrived is the same wine that was promised in the garden, to Abraham, through Moses, in the psalms, by the prophets — the same grace, now fully visible, fully poured out, for all who have wineskins new enough and flexible enough to receive it.
