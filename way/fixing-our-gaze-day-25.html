Day 25

The Son of Man

Opening Prayer

Lord, James and John wanted seats of honor beside you in the kingdom. They were thinking about rank, about glory, about the positions that greatness confers. And you answered their ambition not with rebuke but with a revelation: the Son of Man came not to be served but to serve, and to give his life. Let that inversion work its way through every hierarchy we have quietly constructed in our own hearts. Amen.

"For even the Son of Man came not to be served but to serve others and to give his life as a ransom for many." Mark 10:45, NLT

Mark 10:45 is, in a single sentence, a complete theology of the Incarnation, the ministry, and the cross. It answers three questions simultaneously: Why did he come? What did he do while he was here? And what did it cost him? He came to serve. He served by giving. He gave his life as a ransom. The sentence descends from the general to the specific to the most specific: from service as a disposition, to gift as its expression, to ransom as the definition of the gift.

The context is a dispute among the disciples about greatness — triggered, with painful irony, by a request from the mother of James and John for her sons to sit at Jesus' right and left in the kingdom (Matthew 20:20–21). The other disciples are indignant, Mark tells us, which probably means they are not morally offended but simply competitive. They all want the same thing. And Jesus gathers them and tells them that among the nations, greatness means lordship over others — but that in his kingdom, the direction runs the other way. "Whoever would be great among you must be your servant, and whoever would be first among you must be slave of all." And then the ground: For even the Son of Man came not to be served but to serve.

The for is crucial. He is not merely offering them an ethical principle. He is pointing to himself as the reason the principle is true and the power by which it becomes possible. His service is not a model to imitate by sheer willpower; it is a gravity field that reshapes everything around it. You do not become a servant by deciding to be less ambitious. You become a servant by being captured by the example of the one who had every right to be served — and chose to serve instead.

The word ransom carries the weight of the slave market in first-century usage. A ransom is the price paid to secure the release of someone who is held. It presupposes that the one being ransomed cannot secure their own release — they are not merely inconvenienced or temporarily constrained; they are held, and only an external payment can free them. And the ransom Jesus provides is not money or goods or the currency of any ordinary transaction. It is his life. He gives what he has to give, which is everything, to secure the release of those who have nothing with which to secure their own.

John Stott spent much of his writing life pressing on what "ransom" means and what it tells us about the nature of the atonement. A ransom paid to whom? Some have asked this too literally. The point is not a commercial transaction with a specific recipient but the logic of substitution: a life given in the place of other lives, a death accepted so that others need not die it. He paid what we owed by dying what we deserved, so that we might receive what only he had earned. The Son of Man who could have arrived in glory and demanded tribute, arrived in a manger and ended on a cross — as the price of your freedom.

Sanguis eius — his blood — was the first-century church's shorthand for this whole transaction: the Son of Man's life, given for ours. And what is remarkable about the way Jesus frames it here is that he does not present the cross as something done to him against his will. He states it as the purpose for which he came: came not to be served but to serve, and to give his life. The ransom is not a tragedy he was unable to prevent. It is the mission he deliberately undertook, from a position of absolute freedom, for the sake of the many who had no way out.

For reflection

  • Jesus grounds his call to servant leadership in his own example: "for even the Son of Man came not to be served." Where in your relationships, work, or community do you find the pull toward being served rather than serving — toward recognition rather than contribution? What would it look like to let his example be the gravitational field that reorients that pull?
  • The word "ransom" assumes you were held and could not free yourself. How honest are you about the depth of your own captivity — to sin, to fear, to self, to the approval of others? And what does it mean to be ransomed from those particular prisons?
  • "To give his life as a ransom for many" — he came specifically to do this, not reluctantly but purposefully. How does knowing the cross was his mission rather than his misfortune change how you understand what he did there?

Going Deeper (optional)

Mark 10:45 is often called the "ransom saying" and stands as one of the clearest statements of substitutionary atonement in the Synoptic Gospels. The Greek word lytron — ransom — was used in the first-century world for the price paid to release a slave or a prisoner of war. When Jesus says he gives his life as a ransom "for many" (anti pollōn in Greek), the preposition anti carries a specifically substitutionary force: in the place of, instead of, as an exchange for. He is not saying his death benefits many in a general way; he is saying it takes the place of what the many owe.

This is the textual anchor for what Reformed theology calls penal substitutionary atonement — the teaching that Christ bore in his own person the penalty that our sin deserved, in our place, as our substitute, so that justice is satisfied and the guilty go free. The Reformers did not invent this doctrine; they recovered and clarified what Scripture had been saying since Isaiah 53. Anselm had developed the substitutionary logic in his Cur Deus Homo centuries earlier; the Reformers anchored it more precisely in the specific category of penal penalty rather than merely honor-satisfaction. The ransom is not paid to the devil (as some patristic writers proposed). It is the Son bearing, in his perfectly obedient human life and God-forsaken death, the penalty that the holiness of God required for our sin. The freedom it purchases is not negotiated freedom; it is purchased freedom, secured at the price of the ransom-giver's life — and offered, freely, to all who receive it.
