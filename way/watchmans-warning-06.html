Study 6

The Slow Drift

How we drift without ever deciding to

"Therefore we must pay much closer attention to what we have heard, lest we drift away from it." Hebrews 2:1 (ESV)
"I know your works: you are neither cold nor hot. Would that you were either cold or hot! So, because you are lukewarm, and neither hot nor cold, I will spit you out of my mouth." Revelation 3:15–16 (ESV)

Drift is passive. You do not drift by making a decision. You drift by failing to make one — by not paddling, by letting go, by allowing the current to do what currents do when you stop resisting them.

The Grammar of Drifting

Notice the grammar of Hebrews 2:1. The author does not say, "lest we walk away." He says, "lest we drift away." Drift is the slow, often imperceptible movement of a boat that has stopped being steered. It requires no act of will. It requires only inattention.

This is the study most people in a group like this will find most personally descriptive — not because dramatic fall is rare, but because quiet drift is almost universal. The person who has walked away from the faith in a single conscious act of rejection is someone we can see clearly and point to. The person who has drifted over five years of busyness, mild discouragement, accumulated small compromises, and slowly cooling affections? That is much harder to see — especially from the inside.

The ancient church had a word for the spiritual condition that most often produces this drift: acedia. The desert fathers of the early Christian centuries described it as the "noonday demon" — a torpor, a flatness, a loss of the will to care. Acedia is not simply laziness, though it looks like that from the outside. It is the loss of spiritual appetite. The person in acedia still knows the right things, still attends the right services, may still go through the motions of devotion — but the fire is out, and they are no longer sure whether they want it relit.

How Drift Happens

Drift rarely has a single cause. It is usually the accumulated effect of several things working together over time. Here are the most common contributors.

When we fall into the same sin repeatedly — when we confess the same thing for the tenth time, resolve once more to do better, and then find ourselves in exactly the same place three weeks later — something in us begins to give up. Not dramatically. Not with an announcement. Just a quiet lowering of expectation, a slow withdrawal of the energy we had once invested in the fight. This is just how I am. This is as good as it gets. Why keep trying?

This kind of discouragement is particularly dangerous because it mimics humility. It looks like realistic self-assessment. It feels like accepting what is true. But it is not humility — it is despair wearing humility's clothes. Genuine humility says, I cannot do this on my own, so I am more dependent on God than ever. Despair says, I cannot do this, so I will stop expecting it.

We live in a culture that has elevated busyness to a virtue and made silence uncomfortable. The disciplines of the Christian life — unhurried prayer, slow reading of Scripture, honest self-examination, real community — are not compatible with a pace of life that leaves no margin. They require time that most of us feel we do not have. And so they get managed around rather than maintained. They shrink. And as they shrink, so does the attentiveness they were producing.

The insidious thing about busyness is that it is almost always explicable. We are not busy because we chose entertainment over prayer. We are busy because of the children, the job, the aging parents, the legitimate responsibilities of a life lived in the world. The cause is not bad; the effect on spiritual attentiveness is real. Busyness does not have to be sinful to be spiritually destructive.

Every time we make a decision we know is questionable and find it easier to live with than we expected, the threshold shifts slightly. What made us uncomfortable last year makes us comfortable this year. The sin that required rationalization six months ago requires none now. We have adapted.

This is the hardening process working in slow motion. It does not produce the dramatic hardness of a heart that is openly defiant against God. It produces the quiet hardness of a conscience that has been gradually, painlessly, over years, stopped registering certain categories of sin as sin at all.

Signs of Drift

These are not infallible diagnostics, and some of them can describe healthy seasons of rest rather than drift. But as a pattern, over time, they are worth taking seriously.

Prayer feels mechanical. You say the words. Nothing seems to be happening. You continue because you feel you should, but the sense of actual communication — of speaking to someone who hears — has faded.

Scripture seems dry. Reading it produces no particular response. It is information rather than encounter. You know what the passage says before you finish reading it.

Church attendance has become obligation. You go because you are supposed to go. The singing feels performative. The sermon slides past. You are physically present but spiritually elsewhere.

Sin no longer alarms you. Things that would once have produced a sharp awareness of guilt now produce very little. The conscience has been recalibrated by exposure.

You are mostly bored by God. Not angry with him. Not running from him. Just — not particularly interested. The relationship, such as it is, is formal and functional.

These signs matter because they describe not a loss of salvation but a loss of vitality — the difference between being alive and being awake. And the message of Hebrews, and of the letter to Laodicea in Revelation 3, is that the answer to lukewarmness is not a shrug. It is a trumpet: pay much closer attention. Be zealous and repent (Revelation 3:19).

The Ferguson Lens

Drift tends to be the final destination of failed legalism. A person who has tried hard to earn God's favor, who has found the effort exhausting and unrewarding, who has never discovered the restful obedience that flows from grace rather than striving for it — that person often ends up in the antinomian ditch not out of theological conviction but out of spiritual exhaustion. They didn't plan to stop caring. They just ran out of the kind of energy that was fueling their religion, and they never found the other kind. The cure is not more effort. It is a wholly different understanding of what is being asked of them and why.

Christ, Our Anchor

Isaiah 42:3 contains one of the most tender descriptions of Jesus in the entire Old Testament: "A bruised reed he will not break, and a faintly burning wick he will not quench." The context is the servant of the Lord — the one Matthew 12:20 explicitly identifies as Jesus — and the bruised reed and the smoldering wick are his particular concern. Not the strong and flourishing, but the barely-there.

To the person who feels themselves to be a smoldering wick — still technically alight but not really burning, barely producing light, wondering whether it is worth it — that verse is addressed with the full weight of prophecy. He does not snuff out the smoldering wick. He tends it. He cups his hands around the ember.

Jesus told the story of the shepherd who leaves the ninety-nine to find the one who has strayed (Luke 15:4–6). The sheep that has drifted is not the sheep he has given up on. It is the sheep he goes looking for. You have not drifted beyond his reach. The question is whether you are willing to be found.

For reflection & discussion

  • The study distinguishes between walking away from the faith and drifting. Which do you think is more common? Which is more dangerous, and why?
  • Of the three contributors to drift described — discouragement after failure, busyness, and small compromises — which do you find most active in your own life right now?
  • The study says that giving up on the fight against a recurring sin can look like humility but is actually despair. Have you experienced that? What is the difference in how they feel from the inside?
  • Look at the "signs of drift" list. Without oversharing, does any of them describe your experience right now, or a season you can clearly remember? What did it feel like to come out of it — or are you still in it?
  • Isaiah 42:3 says Jesus will not snuff out the smoldering wick. Does that feel true to you right now — or does it feel like something you believe in theory but can't quite grasp? What would it mean to actually believe it?
