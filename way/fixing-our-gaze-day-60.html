Day 60

The Bread of Life

Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, the crowd had just been fed with loaves and fish and came back the next day wanting more. You told them the food they were seeking perishes. And then you offered them yourself — bread that gives life to the world, living bread come down from heaven, eating which a person will never hunger again. We confess that we return too often for the bread that perishes. Give us hunger for the living bread. Amen.

"Jesus said to them, 'I am the bread of life; whoever comes to me shall not hunger, and whoever believes in me shall never thirst… I am the bread of life… I am the living bread that came down from heaven. If anyone eats of this bread, he will live forever. And the bread that I will give for the life of the world is my flesh.'" John 6:35, 48, 51, ESV

The day after the feeding of the five thousand, the crowd finds Jesus on the other side of the lake. They have come back for more bread. He tells them plainly: you are looking for me not because you saw signs, but because you ate your fill (John 6:26). They want the food that satisfies hunger for a day. He offers them something that satisfies hunger forever. And the crowd, by the end of the discourse, has largely walked away.

The I AM declarations in John's Gospel — which we have been encountering across these weeks — each address a specific human need by identifying Jesus as its answer. The bread of life addresses the deepest human hunger: not the body's need for carbohydrates but the soul's need for the sustenance that only God can provide. Israel in the wilderness had been fed with manna — bread from heaven, given daily, sufficient for each day, unable to be stored (Exodus 16). Jesus does not deny the miracle. He interprets it: the manna was a sign pointing forward to something greater, a daily gift that prefigured the one who would himself be the gift, the one about whom the gift was always speaking.

I am the bread of life; whoever comes to me shall not hunger, and whoever believes in me shall never thirst. The promise is total and final: the hunger and thirst that are the deepest marks of the soul's unmet need — the restlessness that Augustine described, the incompleteness that no created thing can fill — find their end in him. Not in believing correct things about him but in coming to him, in eating him, in the intimate reception of the person rather than the doctrine. The metaphor is deliberately visceral. You don't eat food from a respectful distance. You take it into yourself. It becomes you.

And then the hardest sentence in the discourse: the bread that I will give for the life of the world is my flesh. The crowd disputes and is offended (6:52–60). Many disciples leave (6:66). They do not like the implication — and the implication is exactly what they think it is: his body, given in death, is the source of the life he is offering. The bread of life is not an abstract spiritual supply. It is a crucified body and shed blood, received by faith, through which the life that was poured out becomes the life of those who eat and drink.

This is the Lord's Supper in its earliest theological context, and it is also simply the gospel: the life that was sacrificed is the life that is given; the death that was suffered is the source of the nourishment; the one who is eaten is the one who was broken. You cannot have the living bread without the broken body. The hunger that is permanently satisfied is satisfied by the one who was hungry on Calvary so that the disciples need never hunger again.

We stand at the threshold of Section V. For sixty days we have seen the eternal Son, followed the servant through Galilee, listened to the words of the Teacher, and watched the authority of the King unfold. Tomorrow we turn toward the cross — the place toward which everything we have seen has been moving. The bread of life is given for the life of the world. We are about to watch how it is given.

For reflection

  • The crowd returned the day after the miraculous feeding, seeking more bread — more of what satisfied yesterday's hunger. Jesus offered them himself — the hunger that is permanently, finally addressed. What are the "breads that perish" you most commonly return to for satisfaction? And what would it look like to redirect that hunger toward the bread of life?
  • "Whoever comes to me shall not hunger" — the promise is to coming, not to doctrinal adherence. The metaphor of eating suggests intimate reception: taking in, being nourished by, becoming what you have consumed. How does this differ from intellectual belief in Christ? Are you eating — receiving him in the way food is received — or maintaining a respectful distance?
  • We stand at the threshold of the cross. Looking back across sixty days at everything you have seen of Christ — his eternal glory, his earthly ministry, his teaching, his authority — what is the single thing you most want to carry into the next thirty days, as we follow him toward Golgotha and out the other side?

Going Deeper (optional)

John 6:51 — "the bread that I will give for the life of the world is my flesh" — is one of the most eucharistically weighted verses in the New Testament and has been the subject of intense interpretive debate across the history of the church. The Roman Catholic and Eastern Orthodox traditions have read it as strongly supporting the real bodily presence of Christ in the eucharistic elements. The mainstream Reformed tradition has read it as primarily a discourse about saving faith — coming to Christ, believing in Christ, receiving Christ — with the eucharist as the sacramental sign and seal of that reception but not the primary referent of the discourse.

What is not in dispute across these traditions is the theological core: the life Jesus offers is inseparable from the death by which it is given. The bread of life is the broken body. The living water is the blood poured out. Calvin's language is characteristic: Christ offers himself to us in the eucharist not "naked" (as doctrine alone) but "clothed in the promise" — present not just as a theological truth to be affirmed but as a person to be received, a nourishment to be taken in, a life to be lived from. The point of the bread-of-life discourse is not primarily sacramental mechanics; it is the absolute centrality of Christ himself as the substance of salvation. Not the benefits of Christ detached from his person — not forgiveness-as-a-transaction or life-as-a-product. Christ. His flesh. His blood. Himself, given, for the life of the world. You cannot have what he gives without having him.
