Day 15

The Stranger in Our Midst

Opening Prayer

Lord, the world was made through him, and the world did not know him. He came to his own, and his own people did not receive him. Let that land on us in its full strangeness and sorrow. And then let verse 12 land on us in its full grace — for we who were once among those who did not receive him, if we have received him, it is only because you opened our eyes. Thanks be to God. Amen.

"He was in the world, and the world was made through him, yet the world did not know him. He came to his own, and his own people did not receive him. But to all who did receive him, who believed in his name, he gave the right to become children of God." John 1:10–12, ESV

John constructs, in three verses, one of the most devastating contrasts in all of Scripture. The logic of verse 10 is almost unbearable when you follow it carefully: he was in the world — the world was made through him — yet the world did not know him. The Creator arrived in his own creation and went unrecognized. The Author walked into his own novel and the characters did not recognize the voice.

"He came to his own." The word in Greek is idios — his own things, his own property, his own domain. The world is not foreign territory to him; it is his home. He is not a visitor. He is the landlord, come to his own estate. And "his own people did not receive him" — even Israel, the people shaped over fifteen centuries by God's own revelation specifically to know and anticipate the one who was coming, largely missed him when he arrived. The very Scripture they read, the very temple in which they worshiped, the very covenant they carried — all of it had been pointing at the one now standing in front of them.

This is not merely a historical observation about first-century Jews, and we misread it badly if we receive it as such. It is a diagnosis of something about the human condition in general. The fall has not merely made us morally deficient; it has made us constitutionally unable to perceive the truth about God when it appears before us without the Spirit's work. We do not see correctly. "The light shines in the darkness," John has already said, "and the darkness has not overcome it" (John 1:5) — but neither, left to itself, does the darkness comprehend it.

And yet: "But to all who did receive him." The contrast between verse 11 and verse 12 is, in compressed form, the entire gospel. There are some who receive him. However unexpected, however undeserving, however improbable — some do. And to these, he gives something that staggers the imagination: the right — exousia, the authority, the legal standing, the privilege — to be called children of God. Not admirers. Not students. Not servants. Children. With all the intimacy and inheritance that word entails. The one who was rejected is, in the very act of being rejected by the many, receiving the few as his own family.

Verse 13 tells us how this receiving happens, and it settles a question that is sometimes left uncomfortably open: "who were born, not of blood nor of the will of the flesh nor of the will of man, but of God." Those who receive him, receive him because they have first been born again — renewed from above, given new eyes — by God's own initiative. The new birth is God's act before it is our act. This is not a denial of the reality of human response; verse 12 is clear that receiving and believing are genuinely our acts, genuinely ours. But verse 13 answers the prior question: what makes those acts possible? What opens eyes that are constitutionally shut? God does. The light shines; the Spirit opens the eyes; and those eyes, now open, behold what has been there all along.

Augustine wrote it most memorably: "Our heart is restless, O Lord, until it rests in Thee." The rejection of Christ in John 1 is not a mystery separate from our own story — it is the story of every human heart before grace. The stranger in our midst is strange to us not because he is far away but because we are, by nature, blind to him. And verse 12 is the story of what grace does: it makes the stranger known, and makes the children of God out of those who were children of darkness. If you are reading this, and you have received him — marvel at how that came to be. It was not, at its root, your searching that found him. It was his grace that opened your eyes.

Tomorrow we turn to the Incarnation from a new angle — not from eternity looking down, but from time looking up at the one who entered it. For fifteen days we have stood outside of history and beheld the Son in his eternal glory. Now he comes in, and we follow him in.

For reflection

  • "He was in the world, and the world did not know him." Where in your own life — before or after coming to faith — have you been blind to what was right in front of you? What opened your eyes, and what did it feel like when the recognition came?
  • Verse 12 gives those who receive him "the right to become children of God." Children — not employees, not subjects, not admirers. What does that word, in its full weight, mean for how you relate to God — and how you see yourself?
  • Verse 13 insists the new birth is God's work, not human initiative alone. How does this change the way you think about your own coming to faith? And how does it shape the way you pray for those you love who have not yet received him?

Going Deeper (optional)

John 1:12–13 is one of the key texts in the Reformed tradition's account of the sovereignty of grace in conversion. The sequence is theologically significant: receiving Christ and believing in his name (v.12) is grounded in being born of God (v.13) — not the other way around. This order — regeneration preceding and enabling faith, rather than faith preceding and causing regeneration — is the claim underlying the Reformed doctrine of effectual calling (Westminster Confession of Faith, chapter 10): the Spirit's work in the heart is what makes genuine reception of Christ possible, rather than an unaided human decision being the initiating cause.

John Calvin commented on this passage that God "begins his good work in us before we have any thought of him." This is not a denial of the reality and genuineness of human response. John 1:12 is clear that receiving and believing are real human acts. But the question is what makes those acts possible — what opens eyes that are, by nature, constitutionally shut to the light. Verse 13 answers: God does. This is why the reception of Christ is always, at its root, an occasion for gratitude rather than self-congratulation. Those who receive him are not the spiritually perceptive who managed to see what others missed. They are the graciously opened — the ones whom God was pleased to give eyes. The glory belongs entirely to the Giver of sight.
