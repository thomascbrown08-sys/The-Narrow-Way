Day 7

His Face Shone Like the Sun

Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, on the mountain you did not acquire a glory you had previously lacked. You revealed a glory that was always there, hidden beneath ordinary humanity, available to those with eyes to see. We confess that we see only the ordinary — the surface, the familiar, the manageable. Grant us, by your Spirit, eyes trained to see what is eternally true rather than only what is temporarily visible. Amen.

"Six days later Jesus took Peter and the two brothers, James and John, and led them up a high mountain to be alone. As the men watched, Jesus' appearance was transformed so that his face shone like the sun, and his clothes became as white as light… a bright cloud overshadowed them, and a voice from the cloud said, 'This is my dearly loved Son, who brings me great joy. Listen to him.' The disciples were terrified and fell face down on the ground. But Jesus came over and touched them. 'Get up,' he said. 'Don't be afraid.'" Matthew 17:1–2, 5–7, NLT

There is a detail that Matthew slips past us almost too quickly: Jesus' appearance "was transformed." The Greek word is metemorphōthē — from which we get "metamorphosis." But the transformation here is not the creation of something new. It is the uncovering of something always present. The glory that blazed from his face and turned his garments to white light was not bestowed on him that afternoon on the mountain. It was revealed. The veil was lifted, briefly, on what had always been there, just beneath the ordinary surface of the carpenter from Nazareth.

Peter and James and John had walked with Jesus for months by now. They had heard him teach and watched him heal and eaten with him and argued over who was greatest in his kingdom. They thought they knew who he was. And then the mountain happened.

When Isaiah stood before the heavenly throne in his vision, he saw the Lord seated in glory, the train of his robe filling the temple, and the seraphim crying "Holy, holy, holy" — and his immediate response was: "Woe is me! I am undone" (Isaiah 6:5). When John on Patmos saw the risen, glorified Christ, he "fell at his feet as though dead" (Revelation 1:17). The disciples on the mountain fall face-down on the ground in terror. This is what unfiltered divine glory does to human beings. We are not designed to absorb it unmediated. We have been so accustomed to the domesticated Jesus — the one we address casually, the one we fit into our schedules — that we have forgotten what he is.

Moses and Elijah appear alongside Jesus on the mountain — the Law and the Prophets, the two great pillars of Israel's revelation. Their appearance is not a parallel honor; it is a completion. Everything Moses received and recorded and commanded, everything the prophets saw and spoke and suffered to deliver, has been moving toward this moment. They are not equals with Jesus; they are witnesses to him. And the Father's voice, which had spoken at the baptism, speaks again — but this time with an addition, a final imperative that surpasses even the declaration of sonship: "Listen to him."

Three words that compress the entire trajectory of redemptive history into a command. Not "listen to Moses" — though the law has its proper and glorious place. Not "listen to Elijah" — though the prophets spoke truly. Listen to him. The cloud descends, and when it lifts, Moses and Elijah are gone, and there is no one but Jesus only. This is the Father's definitive statement about the relationship between the old covenant revelation and the new: both pointed here. Both find their completion here. And now the one to whom they pointed stands alone and speaks with final authority.

Peter's instinct in the middle of this is quietly revealing: "Lord, it is good for us to be here. If you wish, I will put up three shelters — one for you, one for Moses, and one for Elijah" (Matthew 17:4). He wants to build something — to extend the moment, give it a container, make a dwelling out of a revelation. It is a very human impulse: when we encounter something transcendent, we want to institutionalize it, manage it, keep it in a place we can return to. The cloud interrupts him mid-sentence. Some things cannot be housed. Some glories will not be administered.

But notice — and this is the detail that should stay with you longest — what Jesus does after the disciples fall in terror. He comes over and touches them. "Get up. Don't be afraid." The same hands that just blazed with uncreated light reach down and make contact with the trembling ones who cannot look up. The one who is too glorious to behold is also the one who stoops. The glory and the tenderness are not in tension; they are aspects of the same Person. This is who you are approaching when you come to him in prayer: the one whose face shone like the sun, who nonetheless reaches for you in your fear and says, simply, rise.

For reflection

  • Peter wanted to build shelters — to stay in the moment, contain the glory, make a habitation out of a revelation. When have you found yourself trying to manage or institutionalize an experience of God rather than simply receiving it and being changed by it?
  • "Listen to him" — this is the Father's final directive from the cloud, the one imperative that overrides all others. What area of your life most needs to come under the specific, particular authority of Christ's voice right now?
  • Jesus touches the terrified disciples and says "Don't be afraid." What does this pattern — glory and tenderness coexisting in the same Person — tell you about the nature of who Christ is and how he meets us?

Going Deeper (optional)

The Transfiguration is often read primarily as a Christological event, but it is simultaneously an eschatological one — a brief flash of what is coming at the end of all things. Peter, writing decades later, refers to it as the moment when he and the others "were eyewitnesses of his majesty" (2 Peter 1:16), and he grounds his confidence in Christ's return on what he saw that day. The glory that blazed on the mountain is the glory that will one day fill all things (Ephesians 1:23; Revelation 21:23). The Transfiguration is the future briefly visible in the present.

For the Reformed tradition, the appearance of Moses and Elijah alongside Christ confirms the profound unity of the two Testaments. They appear not despite their historical roles but because of them: the law given through Moses and the prophetic witness borne by Elijah have not been abolished by Christ — they have been completed in him (Matthew 5:17). He is the one to whom the whole of Scripture points, the integrating center around which all of God's revelation rotates. The Father's voice — silencing Moses, dismissing Elijah, and directing all attention to his Son — traces this trajectory in a single sentence. Everything before Christ was preparation. Everything after him is response. He is the hinge on which all of history turns, and the Transfiguration is the moment when the hinge briefly becomes visible to human eyes.
