Day 21

He Touched the Leper

Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, you did not have to touch him. A word would have been enough — you healed at a distance on other occasions, and you could have done so here. The touch was not medically necessary. Which means it was something else entirely: a choice, a statement, a gift of contact to someone who had not been touched in years. Let us understand today what that touch means, and what it reveals about the kind of God you are. Amen.

"And a leper came to him, imploring him, and kneeling said to him, 'If you will, you can make me clean.' Moved with pity, he stretched out his hand and touched him and said to him, 'I will; be clean.' And immediately the leprosy left him, and he was made clean." Mark 1:40–42, ESV

Under Levitical law, a person with leprosy was required to live outside the camp, to wear torn clothes, to leave their hair disheveled, and to cry "Unclean! Unclean!" whenever anyone approached (Leviticus 13:45–46). This was not cruelty but public health, given the understanding of the time — and it was also a ritual statement: this person's condition placed them outside the boundary of covenant community, outside the reach of temple worship, outside the network of normal human touch. The leper in Mark 1 has likely been living this way for years.

He comes to Jesus "imploring him, and kneeling." The posture is deliberate. He is not demanding; he is begging, with his whole body. He is not sure whether Jesus will do this for him. And the words he chooses are among the most carefully phrased in the Gospels: "If you will, you can make me clean." Not "if you are able" — he has no doubt about Christ's power. His uncertainty is entirely about Christ's desire. Will you? Is someone like me worth your attention, your power, your willingness?

Mark's answer is immediate and completely unambiguous: moved with pity, he stretched out his hand and touched him. The word translated "pity" — splagchnistheis — is the strongest word in Greek for gut-level compassion, an emotional response that originates in the viscera, the deep interior of the body. Jesus is not mildly sympathetic. He is moved to his center. And out of that center, the hand extends.

Consider what this touch means in context. By the logic of Levitical law, touching an unclean person transmitted uncleanness. The holy priest who touched the impure became impure. The clean who contacted the unclean became contaminated. Every religious instinct in first-century Judaism said: do not touch. Keep the boundary. Maintain the separation. And Jesus reaches across it.

But the transmission runs in the opposite direction. The leper does not make Jesus unclean; Jesus makes the leper clean. Holiness, in this encounter, is the stronger force. The life in Christ does not shrink from contamination because it cannot be contaminated — it can only contaminate, in the best sense, spreading its purity outward into whatever it contacts. This is the logic of the Incarnation itself: God did not become impure by taking on human flesh; he made human flesh capable of glorification. The same logic holds in every healing, every shared meal, every touch offered to those the world has pushed to the margins.

Then he speaks: "I will; be clean." The answer to the man's uncertain question — if you will — is given directly, without hesitation, without qualification. I will. There is no deliberation here, no weighing of the man's merit. The willingness is immediate and total. And then the command, which is simultaneously a declaration: be clean. Not "I will heal you" but "be clean" — as though the new reality is created by the word itself, which in this case it is.

Whatever has kept you from Christ — whatever uncleanness, whatever sense of being too far outside the boundary, whatever suspicion that you might be the one exception to his willingness — the leper's story is the answer. He did not ask whether the man deserved it. He did not take inventory of the man's compliance with religious obligation. He looked at him, was moved in his center, and reached across every boundary the world had drawn between them. The hand that touched the leper is the same hand that is extended now.

For reflection

  • The leper's question was not about Christ's power but his willingness: "If you will, you can make me clean." Is there something in your life about which you have no doubt of God's ability but genuine uncertainty about his willingness? How does this story speak to that?
  • Jesus touched the leper before he spoke the healing word — the touch was not medically necessary. What does it say about Christ's compassion that contact itself was part of the gift, not just the healing outcome?
  • Christ's holiness was not fragile but aggressive — it moved toward contamination and transformed it rather than retreating to protect itself. Where in your own life or relationships might this pattern — entering rather than avoiding — be the more Christlike response?

Going Deeper (optional)

The healing of the leper is not only a compassion story — it is a priestly story. Under the Mosaic law, the only one who could declare a former leper clean was a priest (Leviticus 14:2–9). The process involved examination, sacrifice, and a formal declaration of restoration to community. Jesus does not follow this procedure; he precedes it. He makes the man clean first, then sends him to the priest for the official confirmation that the law requires (Mark 1:44). He is acting with an authority that the Levitical priesthood could never claim: not the authority to declare clean what God has already cleansed, but the authority to cleanse directly, on his own word.

This is consistent with his pattern throughout Mark's Gospel: he forgives sins on his own authority (Mark 2:5–7), commands the Sabbath (Mark 2:28), and casts out demons without mediated ritual. The religious authorities recognize this authority and are disturbed by it — rightly, given their framework. What they cannot account for is the possibility that the one standing before them is himself the source from which all priestly authority derives. He is not circumventing the law; he is the one the law was always pointing to. And the leper, walking to the priest with a clean body that nothing but a divine word had touched, is a living announcement of who that word came from.
