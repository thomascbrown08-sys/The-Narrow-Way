The Armory

The Armory: The Creeds

The church's hard-won summaries of the faith — the armor laid up in the house of rest for every pilgrim's road.

"Put on the whole armor of God, that you may be able to stand against the schemes of the devil." Ephesians 6:11 (ESV)

Before Christian left Palace Beautiful and went down toward the valley, the family of the house led him into one more room. It was the armory, and there they fitted him out for the road ahead — with armor and weapons kept ready for pilgrims, so that he would not face what was coming unprepared. Bunyan lingers on the detail because he means it: you do not walk the dangerous country ahead in whatever you happened to be wearing. You go armed.

The creeds are that armor. We took up the oldest of them — the Apostles' Creed — back in the house itself, as the baptismal confession every pilgrim is handed at the door. The three gathered here go deeper, and each one was forged in a specific battle, hammered out by people fighting to protect something the gospel could not afford to lose. To understand them is to be fitted with armor for the schemes waiting just down the road.

First, what a creed isArmor, not a rival to Scripture

A word before the texts, because creeds are easily misunderstood in two opposite directions. They are not a second Bible, set up alongside Scripture as an independent authority — and they are not optional human opinions a Christian may take or leave. They are something more useful than either: Scripture's own load-bearing truths, gathered into a compact form you can carry into a fight. Their authority is real but entirely derivative. A creed binds the conscience only because, and only insofar as, it faithfully says what Scripture says. Scripture is the rule that norms; the creed is the rule that has been normed by it. The church did not vote these truths into being. It recognized what was already there, and wrote it down where it could be reached in the dark.

The first battleNicaea: is the Son truly God?

In the early fourth century a gifted teacher named Arius pressed a tidy, reasonable-sounding claim: that the Son, however exalted, was the first and highest of God's creatures — made, not eternal. There was, Arius said, a time when the Son was not. It sounded humble; it kept God's oneness clean. And it was fatal. A created Christ cannot reconcile us to God, for only God can do that — and to worship a creature, however glorious, is idolatry. The whole gospel hung on the question.

The council that met at Nicaea answered with one Greek word the Arians could not bring themselves to sign: homoousios — "of one Being with the Father." Not like God, not God's finest product, but God from God, true God from true God. The expanded creed that followed has become the most widely shared confession in all of Christendom, said across Catholic, Orthodox, and Protestant churches alike:

The Nicene Creed · 325 / 381 AD

We believe in one God, the Father Almighty, maker of heaven and earth, of all things visible and invisible.

We believe in one Lord, Jesus Christ, the only-begotten Son of God, born of the Father before all ages, God from God, Light from Light, true God from true God, begotten, not made, of one Being with the Father. Through him all things were made. For us and for our salvation he came down from heaven; he became incarnate by the Holy Spirit and the virgin Mary, and was made man. For our sake he was crucified under Pontius Pilate; he suffered death and was buried. On the third day he rose again in accordance with the Scriptures. He ascended into heaven and is seated at the right hand of the Father. He will come again with glory to judge the living and the dead, and his kingdom will have no end.

We believe in the Holy Spirit, the Lord, the giver of life, who proceeds from the Father and the Son, who with the Father and the Son is worshiped and glorified, who has spoken through the prophets.

We believe in one holy catholic and apostolic church. We acknowledge one baptism for the forgiveness of sins. We look for the resurrection of the dead, and the life of the world to come. Amen.

A note on one disputed line. Where this creed says the Spirit "proceeds from the Father and the Son," the words and the Son — in Latin, the filioque — were added later by the Western church and are not in the original Eastern text, which reads simply "from the Father." The Eastern churches never accepted the addition, and it remains a real point of difference between East and West to this day. This site stands in the Western Reformed tradition and confesses the fuller form, while recognizing the Eastern concern as a serious one: a desire to guard the Father as the single source within the Trinity. It is a family disagreement among those who together confess the Son is fully God — which is the thing Nicaea was fighting for.

What the creed guards is therefore everything: if the Son is fully God, then the rescue at the Cross was God himself coming to get us, and the worship the church gives to Jesus is not idolatry but simple truth.

The second battleChalcedon: how is he also a man?

Granting that Jesus is fully God, a harder question pressed in: then how can he also be genuinely human? Two errors pushed from opposite sides. One blurred the natures together into a kind of third thing — neither quite God nor quite man. The other split him down the middle into two persons sharing a body. The Definition produced at Chalcedon answered not by explaining how the union works — the church did not claim to know that — but by fencing the mystery on every side:

The Chalcedonian Definition · 451 AD (condensed)

Following the holy fathers, we all with one voice confess our Lord Jesus Christ to be one and the same Son, the same perfect in Godhead and the same perfect in manhood, truly God and truly man … of one substance with the Father as regards his Godhead, and the same of one substance with us as regards his manhood … acknowledged in two natures, without confusion, without change, without division, without separation; the difference of the natures being in no way abolished because of the union, but rather the characteristic property of each nature being preserved, and concurring into one Person and one hypostasis.

Those four "withouts" are the famous fences. Without confusion and without change: the two natures are not blended, and neither loses what belongs to it — the divine does not become mortal, the human does not become all-knowing. Without division and without separation: yet he is not two beings loosely paired, but one Person, genuinely and permanently. Fully God, fully man, one Christ.

This is not hair-splitting; it is the gospel's hinge. The one who saves us had to be fully God, or he could not save — and fully one of us, or he could not stand in our place. Lose either nature and the rescue falls apart. Chalcedon keeps both nailed down.

The thirdThe Athanasian Creed: the whole of it, with nothing left loose

The last and most relentless of the three does not tell a story or soothe; it drills. Named for the great defender of Nicaea (though written after his death), it states the Trinity and the incarnation with such unbroken precision that it leaves no crack anywhere for error to slip through. It is meant to be confessed slowly, almost like a drumbeat:

The Athanasian Creed · Quicunque vult, c. 5th–6th century

Whosoever will be saved, before all things it is necessary that he hold the catholic faith. Which faith, except every one do keep whole and undefiled, without doubt he shall perish everlastingly.

And the catholic faith is this: That we worship one God in Trinity, and Trinity in Unity; neither confounding the persons, nor dividing the substance. For there is one person of the Father, another of the Son, and another of the Holy Spirit. But the Godhead of the Father, of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit, is all one: the glory equal, the majesty coeternal.

Such as the Father is, such is the Son, and such is the Holy Spirit. The Father uncreated, the Son uncreated, and the Holy Spirit uncreated. The Father incomprehensible, the Son incomprehensible, and the Holy Spirit incomprehensible. The Father eternal, the Son eternal, and the Holy Spirit eternal. And yet they are not three eternals but one eternal. As also there are not three uncreated, nor three incomprehensibles, but one uncreated and one incomprehensible. So likewise the Father is almighty, the Son almighty, and the Holy Spirit almighty; and yet they are not three almighties, but one almighty.

So the Father is God, the Son is God, and the Holy Spirit is God; and yet they are not three Gods, but one God. So likewise the Father is Lord, the Son Lord, and the Holy Spirit Lord; and yet not three Lords, but one Lord. For like as we are compelled by the Christian verity to acknowledge every person by himself to be God and Lord, so are we forbidden by the catholic religion to say: there are three Gods or three Lords.

The Father is made of none, neither created nor begotten. The Son is of the Father alone; not made nor created, but begotten. The Holy Spirit is of the Father and of the Son; neither made, nor created, nor begotten, but proceeding. So there is one Father, not three Fathers; one Son, not three Sons; one Holy Spirit, not three Holy Spirits. And in this Trinity none is afore, or after another; none is greater, or less than another; but the whole three persons are coeternal together and coequal. So that in all things, as aforesaid, the Unity in Trinity and the Trinity in Unity is to be worshipped. He therefore that will be saved must thus think of the Trinity.

Furthermore it is necessary to everlasting salvation that he also believe faithfully the incarnation of our Lord Jesus Christ. For the right faith is that we believe and confess that our Lord Jesus Christ, the Son of God, is God and man; God, of the substance of the Father, begotten before the worlds; and man of the substance of his mother, born in the world … Who, although he is God and man, yet he is not two, but one Christ; one, not by conversion of the Godhead into flesh, but by taking of the manhood into God; one altogether; not by confusion of substance, but by unity of person. For as the reasonable soul and flesh is one man, so God and man is one Christ;

who suffered for our salvation, descended into hell, rose again the third day from the dead; ascended into heaven, sits at the right hand of the Father, God Almighty; from whence he shall come to judge the living and the dead. At whose coming all men shall rise again with their bodies; and shall give account of their own works. And they that have done good shall go into life everlasting; and they that have done evil into everlasting fire.

This is the catholic faith, which except a man believe faithfully and firmly, he cannot be saved.

Two lines trouble many readers, and they should be faced honestly rather than skipped: the opening and closing "damnatory clauses," which warn that whoever does not hold this faith "shall perish everlastingly." Several things are worth saying plainly. First, the faith being made necessary here is not an advanced theology exam — it is the gospel's own center, the Trinity and the incarnation, the very identity of the God who saves. To deny these is to deny the gospel itself. Second, the clauses describe what the catholic faith is; they are not a license to pronounce sentence on particular people, which Scripture reserves to God alone. And third, for those who fear they cannot honestly confess what they do not yet fully grasp: this creed, like the others, was written for people only beginning in the faith. It describes the truth that saving faith lays hold of — it does not add intellectual mastery as a new requirement to be earned. The understanding is meant to grow inside the confessing, not to be finished before it.

One further line deserves a word. The creed says that at the last day "they that have done good shall go into life everlasting; and they that have done evil into everlasting fire." This is the language of Jesus himself, who said the same in nearly the same words. It is not a quiet contradiction of grace, smuggling salvation-by-works in at the end. Scripture everywhere teaches that we are justified by faith alone — and that a living faith always bears fruit, so that the final judgment can speak of that fruit as the visible evidence of who belongs to Christ. The "good" the creed has in view is not a wage that earns heaven; it is the harvest of a faith already united to the Savior. Judgment according to works is not the same as salvation on the basis of them.

Why it is hereArmed for the road

These are not museum pieces, kept behind glass for the curious. Bunyan's armory was a working room, and the armor was issued because a fight was coming. So it is here: knowing what these creeds guard is exactly what lets a pilgrim recognize a counterfeit when it appears further along — for the old errors, as the Delectable Mountains will show, never stop coming back in new clothes. To confess that the Son is fully God, that he is one Person in two natures, that the three are one God — this is to be dressed for the country ahead, where the adversary's first weapon has always been a lie about who God is.

The creeds are not a second Bible. They are the first Bible's deepest truths, gathered where you can reach them in the dark.
