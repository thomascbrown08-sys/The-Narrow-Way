Day 28

Receiving Forgiveness

But if we confess our sins to him, he is faithful and just to forgive us our sins and to cleanse us from all wickedness. Psalm 103:10–12 (NLT) He does not punish us for all our sins; he does not deal harshly with us, as we deserve. For his unfailing love toward those who fear him is as great as the height of the heavens above the earth. He has removed our sins as far from us as the east is from the west. 1 John 1:9 (NLT)

We have spent several days thinking about extending forgiveness to others. But there is a foundation beneath all of it that we haven't addressed directly: receiving forgiveness yourself.

Notice the word "just" in 1 John 1:9. God forgives because he is faithful — and just. Not because the sins were trivial or because he's decided to overlook them. He forgives because Christ bore the penalty. Justice has been satisfied at the cross. When God forgives, it is not a soft waiving of the rules — it is the announcement that the debt has been paid in full, by someone else, on your behalf.

Psalm 103:12 gives one of Scripture's most beautiful images of forgiveness: "as far as the east is from the west." North and south are directions that actually meet — at the poles. East and west don't. There is no pole where east becomes west. The psalmist chose those directions deliberately: the distance is absolute. Your sins have been moved to a location that cannot be reached.

The unmerciful servant in Matthew 18 was immediately, completely forgiven an impossible debt — and went out and demanded repayment of a trivial one. The tragedy of his story is that the forgiveness he received didn't change him. He didn't let it in.

The whole arc of our six days on forgiveness culminates here: forgiveness flows out from a heart that has genuinely received it. If you find it nearly impossible to forgive others, the question worth sitting with is whether you have actually let God's forgiveness of you land.

The man who is forgiven much loves much. He does not love much who does not know that he has been forgiven much. — Charles Spurgeon

For reflection

Is there something you haven't allowed yourself to be forgiven for — something you keep picking back up? What would it mean to let Psalm 103:12 be true for you, specifically?
