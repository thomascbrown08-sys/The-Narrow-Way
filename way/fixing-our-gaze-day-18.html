Day 18

The Boy in the Temple

Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, your first recorded words in any Gospel are a question about your Father's house — spoken not from a throne but from a twelve-year-old's place among scholars who did not yet know who they were speaking with. Help us understand that the same compulsion that drove you then — the must, the necessity of being about the Father's business — drove you all the way to the cross and still drives your intercession for us now. Amen.

"After three days they found him in the temple, sitting among the teachers, listening to them and asking them questions. And all who heard him were amazed at his understanding and his answers… And he said to them, 'Why were you looking for me? Did you not know that I must be in my Father's house?'… And Jesus increased in wisdom and in stature and in favor with God and man." Luke 2:46–49, 52, ESV

Luke is the only Gospel writer who gives us a window into the years between the nativity and Jesus' public ministry. He could have chosen any moment from those hidden decades — and the single scene he preserves is this one: a twelve-year-old boy in Jerusalem at Passover, left behind when the caravan departs, found three days later not lost and frightened but seated among the teachers of Israel, listening and asking questions, his understanding astonishing everyone who hears him.

The question Mary asks is entirely human and entirely understandable: "Child, why have you treated us so? Behold, your father and I have been searching for you in great distress." Three days. Every parent knows this fear — the particular, cold panic of not knowing where your child is. And Jesus' response is the first sentence he speaks in any of the four Gospels: "Why were you looking for me? Did you not know that I must be in my Father's house?"

The word must — in Greek, dei — is not mild. It is the word of divine necessity, of vocation received and unshakeable. Jesus uses it throughout the Gospels at the most defining moments: the Son of Man must suffer many things (Mark 8:31); he must be lifted up (John 3:14); I must preach the good news of the kingdom of God to the other towns as well (Luke 4:43). At twelve years old, before any public action has been taken, the must is already working in him. Something has him and will not let him go. It is not compulsion from outside; it is compulsion from within — the love of the Father, which he cannot not follow.

And notice: "my Father's house." Not the house of Joseph, the carpenter. Not his earthly father's workshop in Nazareth. The temple. The house of the God who is his Father in a way that goes deeper than biology, deeper than covenant, deeper than anything Israel has yet language to describe. At twelve, before he has explained anything to anyone, Jesus already knows who he is in relation to the Father — and the knowing is not a secret he is keeping from Mary; it is a knowing he assumes she must share.

Mary does not share it, apparently. "And they did not understand the saying that he spoke to them." This is a mercy and a mystery both. Mary, who has kept so many things in her heart — the angel's announcement, the shepherds' wonder, Simeon's words, Anna's praise — cannot yet fully comprehend the child she has been given. Some realities are too large for even the most faithful heart to hold all at once.

And then Luke adds a verse that must be held alongside the whole of what we have seen in the first two sections of this devotional: "And Jesus increased in wisdom and in stature and in favor with God and man." He grew. He learned. He developed. The one who upholds the universe by the word of his power also, at some point in his childhood, learned the Torah from a scroll. The eternal Son who is "the same yesterday and today and forever" (Hebrews 13:8) went through seasons, stages, and the ordinary accumulation of a human life. His humanity was not a costume worn over unchanged divinity. It was genuine, experienced from the inside, entered fully and without reservation.

Hebrews 5:8 says he "learned obedience through what he suffered." Not that he learned what obedience is — the eternal Son knows what obedience is. But he learned it as a body learns it: through practice, through cost, through the long patience of a hidden life in Nazareth before the public life in Galilee. The years between twelve and thirty are not wasted or merely preparatory. They are part of what he offered — the full span of a human life, lived in obedience to the Father, from first breath to last.

For reflection

  • Jesus' first recorded words express a sense of unshakeable compulsion — "I must be in my Father's house." What is the deepest compulsion of your own life — what you cannot not be drawn toward? And how does it relate to his?
  • "He increased in wisdom and in stature and in favor with God and man" — Jesus genuinely grew and developed as a human being over time. Why does it matter to you, personally, that his humanity was not a costume but a real experience from infancy onward?
  • Mary "treasured up all these things in her heart" (v.51). What does it mean to treasure something about Christ — to hold it carefully rather than pass through it quickly and move on? Is there something from these eighteen days that you want to hold more carefully?

Going Deeper (optional)

Luke 2:52 — "Jesus increased in wisdom and in stature and in favor with God and man" — is one of the most theologically demanding verses in the Gospels. How can the omniscient Son of God increase in wisdom? How can the one in whom the fullness of the Godhead dwells bodily (Colossians 2:9) grow? The Chalcedonian solution affirms that the two natures — divine and human — are united in one Person without confusion or mixture. Christ's divine nature did not grow; it is by definition perfect and complete. But his human nature genuinely developed. He grew up.

What this means practically is that the eternal Son, in taking on humanity, accepted the real limitations of human development and experience. He learned the Torah from a scroll. He experienced time sequentially. He grew in understanding the way children grow — through observation, instruction, and the accumulation of experience. This is not a diminishment of his deity but a measure of the depth of his condescension: he did not live his human life with the omniscience of his divine nature on constant conscious display. He was not pretending to be a child. He was a child — fully, genuinely, for those thirty hidden years — without ceasing to be the Son. The technical term for this in Reformed Christology, following Chalcedon, is the communicatio idiomatum — the communication of attributes between the two natures in the one Person. The Person who grew is the divine-human Person; the growing itself belongs to the human nature. The mystery is real. The humanity is real. And both matter for our salvation.
