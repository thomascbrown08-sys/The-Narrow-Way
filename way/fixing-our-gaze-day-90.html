Day 90

Alpha and Omega

Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus — Alpha and Omega, the first and the last, the beginning and the end. We have spent ninety days fixing our gaze on you, and we arrive at the end of this devotional knowing that we have barely begun. You were before everything we have read. You will remain after everything we have written. There is no point of origin that precedes you and no horizon that you do not already occupy. We finish with full hands and a deeper sense of how much more there is to see. Amen.

"'Behold, I am coming soon, bringing my recompense with me, to repay each one for what he has done. I am the Alpha and the Omega, the first and the last, the beginning and the end.'" Revelation 22:12–13, ESV

We began with John 1:1: In the beginning was the Word. We end with the Word himself speaking from the last pages of Scripture: I am the Alpha and the Omega, the first and the last, the beginning and the end. The arc is complete. Or rather — the arc has arrived at the point where its fullness is declared, and we discover that the fullness extends in both directions beyond anything we can see.

Alpha is the first letter of the Greek alphabet. Omega is the last. Between them lies every letter, every word, every sentence, every meaning that the language can express. To say "I am the Alpha and the Omega" is to say: I am the vocabulary from which all reality is constructed. I am the first sound and the last sound and everything that can be said between them. Before the first word was spoken, I was. After the last word has been said, I remain. Every story that has ever been told was told within the story that I am telling, and my story has no beginning that anything precedes and no ending that anything follows.

He says it three ways in one verse, as if one expression is not sufficient: Alpha and Omega. First and last. Beginning and end. Three overlapping declarations of the same infinite reality, pressed against each other until the weight of the claim is felt. He is not claiming to be present at both ends of human history while the middle belongs to others. He is claiming that the entirety of what has ever happened or ever will happen occurs within him — not as a container he merely occupies, but as the one whose nature is the source and the goal and the sustaining energy of every moment in between.

Now bring everything you have seen across these ninety days to bear on this title. The eternal Son, delighting before the creation of the world (Day 13). The Word making the flesh he would take on (Day 1). The Lamb of God, whose death was determined before the foundation of the world (Revelation 13:8). The one who stilled the storm (Day 47), who raised Lazarus (Day 52), who bore the cup of wrath (Day 63), who said it is finished (Day 67), who appeared to Mary by name (Day 71), who cooks breakfast on the shore for exhausted disciples (Day 74), who always lives to intercede (Day 79), who will wipe every tear (Day 89). All of this — every facet we have examined across three months of sustained attention — is one face. One Person. The Alpha and the Omega.

The ninety days have not exhausted him. They have not even begun to exhaust him. Thomas Aquinas, near the end of his life, had a mystical experience that led him to say of the millions of words he had written about God: "All that I have written seems like straw to me." He did not mean his theology was worthless. He meant that what he had written was straw in comparison to the reality — that every formulation of the truth about God is infinitely exceeded by the truth itself. The same is true of these ninety days. What has been written here is true. It points at what is real. And the real thing is incomparably larger than any pointing at it.

The invitation of this devotional has been: look. Look at Jesus Christ. Look longer and more carefully and more honestly than you have looked before. Let the looking do what looking does — let it produce longing, let it reshape desire, let it displace lesser loves by presenting one that is incomparably greater. The mechanism, described on Day 85, is this: beholding produces transformation, degree by degree, by the Spirit, toward the image of the one beheld. And the beholding is not finished. It will not be finished in this life. It will reach its consummation on the day described in 1 John 3:2: when he appears, we shall see him as he is. Face to face. Unveiled. The full sight that the present life gives us only in fragments and mirrors.

Until that day, the devotional is not the end. It is the beginning of a longer practice, the deepening of a capacity for seeing that will serve you for the rest of your life. Keep reading the Gospels as if seeing him for the first time. Keep bringing his face to mind in prayer. Keep finding in him the answer to every longing that other things have promised to satisfy and failed. Keep looking at the wounds, as Thomas did, until the only words adequate to what you see are: My Lord and my God.

He is the Alpha and the Omega. He was before you were born. He will be when everything you know has passed away. He is the first word your story was written with and the last word it will end with. He loved you before you knew his name. He is interceding for you at this moment. He is coming for you. And he is worth — infinitely, inexhaustibly, incomparably worth — every day of looking, and every day to come.

Fix your gaze. It will take eternity to see everything that is there.

For reflection

  • Alpha and Omega — first and last — beginning and end. Everything that has ever existed has existed within his story, not beside it. How does this claim — this total, comprehensive, all-encompassing Lordship — land for you today, at the end of ninety days of looking at him? Is he larger to you now than he was on Day 1? What has most enlarged him?
  • The ninety days are complete, but the beholding is not. What will you do tomorrow to continue fixing your gaze on Jesus Christ — not as a religious obligation to maintain, but as the ongoing, increasing, never-completed practice of looking at the one who is worth seeing for eternity? What is the next step?
  • On Day 1 we asked what it means to behold. Ninety days later, what does beholding mean to you now — not as a definition but as a lived experience? What has it produced in you? What do you carry away from this devotional that you did not carry in?

Going Deeper (optional)

The title "Alpha and Omega" appears three times in Revelation — in 1:8, where it is applied to "the Lord God… who is and who was and who is to come, the Almighty"; in 21:6, where God seated on the throne says "I am the Alpha and the Omega, the beginning and the end"; and here in 22:13, where the one who is speaking — and who in 22:16 identifies himself as "Jesus" — takes the title. The repetition of a divine title for both "the Lord God" and "Jesus" in the same book is a Christological statement of the highest order. Revelation does not merely claim that Jesus is very important or uniquely exalted. It applies the titles of YHWH directly to him — identifying him as one with the God of Israel in a way that echoes John 10:30 ("I and the Father are one") and Thomas's confession ("My Lord and my God").

The practical theology of "Alpha and Omega" is a theology of sufficiency. Because he is the beginning, there is no need that predates him or that he has not anticipated. Because he is the end, there is no destination that he is not already occupying. Because he is everything between — the whole alphabet of reality — there is no experience, no suffering, no joy, no longing, no loss that falls outside the range of what he encompasses. "In him all things hold together" (Colossians 1:17) is the Pauline expression of the same reality: the coherence of everything is his active, continuous, loving work. To fix your gaze on him is not to narrow your vision — it is to find the one thing that is large enough to hold everything else without losing any of it. He is the Alpha and the Omega. He is enough. He is more than enough. He is, to borrow the language of Day 37, the pearl of great price — and the merchant who found him and sold everything for him discovered that what he had given up was straw, and what he had found was everything. Fix your gaze. He is worth it. He always will be.
