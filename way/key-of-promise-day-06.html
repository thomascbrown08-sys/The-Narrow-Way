Day 6

Confess and Be Cleansed

But if we confess our sins to him, he is faithful and just to forgive us our sins and to cleanse us from all wickedness. 1 John 1:9 (NLT)

Here is one short verse that has carried more burdened consciences out of the dark than almost any other, and its power is hidden in two words we tend to read past: faithful and just.

We would expect the verse to say God is merciful to forgive, or kind to forgive — and he is. But John reaches for stronger words. He says God is faithful and just to forgive. Faithful: he is keeping a promise he made, so your forgiveness is not a mood that might pass but a commitment he is bound to honor. Just: he is doing what is right, because the penalty has already been paid in Christ, so to forgive the confessing sinner is not for God to bend the rules — it is for him to honor the payment his Son already made. Your forgiveness is not God being soft. It is God being faithful to his word and just to his Son.

That reframes confession entirely. In the dungeon, confession curdles into something dark: an endless self-accusation, a groveling that tries to feel bad enough, long enough, to earn its way back. But that is not what this verse describes. To confess is simply to agree with God about your sin — to stop hiding it, name it truly, and turn back toward him. It is the turning of the key, not the paying of a fine. The payment is already made.

And see how total the cleansing is: “forgive us our sins and cleanse us from all wickedness.” Not the sins you managed to articulate well. Not the ones you felt sufficiently sorry for. All wickedness — including the sin tangled up in your very repentance, the mixed motives in your confession itself. You do not have to confess perfectly to be cleansed completely. You have to confess honestly, to a God who is faithful and just.

So confession is not the despairing soul's punishment. It is its release. It is not how you feel bad enough to be readmitted. It is how you agree with God, turn around, and receive what he has already bound himself to give.

Pray the Promise

Name your sin to God plainly — not with a flood of self-condemnation, but honestly, the way you would name a true thing. Then, instead of waiting to feel forgiven, pray the promise and receive it: “You are faithful and just to forgive me, because Christ has paid. I receive your cleansing now.”

Resist the urge to keep groveling after you have confessed. The promise is not waiting for you to feel worse. It is waiting for you to take it.

For reflection

Does confession feel to you more like turning back to a faithful Father, or like trying to feel sorry enough to earn re-entry? What would change if forgiveness were something God is bound to give the confessing heart?
