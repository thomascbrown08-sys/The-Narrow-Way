Study 1

The Watchman's Call

Why this warning, and why now?

"Son of man, speak to your people and say to them, If I bring the sword upon a land, and the people of the land take a man from among them, and make him their watchman, and if he sees the sword coming upon the land and blows the trumpet and warns the people, then if anyone who hears the sound of the trumpet does not take warning, and the sword comes and takes him away, his blood shall be upon his own head." Ezekiel 33:2–4 (ESV)

There is an ancient image behind this study. A city on a hill. A man posted on the wall. The enemy moving in the dark valley below. The watchman's job is not to fight the war — it is to blow the trumpet before anyone else can see what he sees.

Ezekiel 33 is God's own commentary on why he keeps sending warnings to people who do not want to hear them. He uses the image of the watchman to explain something about his own character: he warns because he must, and because the alternative — watching people walk toward destruction in silence — is unbearable to him.

Read what he says in verse 11, and read it carefully, because this sentence is the foundation of everything that follows in this study: "As I live, declares the Lord God, I have no pleasure in the death of the wicked, but that the wicked turn from his way and live. Turn back, turn back from your evil ways, for why will you die?"

That question — why will you die? — is not a taunt. It is a plea. It is the cry of someone who can see what the one being addressed cannot, or will not, see. The watchman does not blow the trumpet to enjoy the alarm. He blows it because the sword is real, the danger is real, and the people in the city are people he loves.

The Uncomfortable Truth About Warnings

We live in a time and a church culture that is understandably suspicious of warnings. Too many warnings have come wrapped in manipulation, or been weaponized by spiritual abuse, or landed without love and left only wounds. We have good reasons to be careful about the rhetoric of fear in Christian community.

But the response to misused warnings is not the silence of a watchman who has decided warnings are too dangerous. The response is warnings given in love — with accuracy, with care, without manipulation, grounded in Scripture, and attached to genuine hope. That is what we are attempting here.

The Bible is not a comfortable book. It contains terrifying passages alongside the most tender ones ever written. It holds together, in the same breath, the gentleness of a shepherd who carries lambs in his arms (Isaiah 40:11) and the urgency of a God who says to the comfortable and the drifting, "Today, if you hear his voice, do not harden your hearts" (Hebrews 3:15). To strip out the warnings is not to make the Bible more loving. It is to make it less true.

Who This Study Is For

This study is written for people who already know the basic shape of the Christian story — that God is holy, that we are not, that Jesus lived and died and rose in our place, and that this is the ground of our standing before God. We are not going to rehearse those foundations in every study. We assume you know them, at least in your head.

What this study is asking is a different, harder question: How much of what you know in your head has actually taken root in your life? Between what we believe and how we live, there is often a gap we have learned to step over without looking down. This study is about looking down.

It is written for the person who has sat in church long enough to know all the right answers — and who suspects, sometimes late at night, that knowing the answers and being changed by them are not the same thing. It is written for those who have developed a comfortable relationship with sins they have decided are inevitable, and for those who have been striving so hard in their own strength that they have forgotten whose strength they are supposed to be relying on. It is written for the drifter and the striver, the complacent and the desperate, the one who suspects they have never really changed and the one who suspects they have already gone too far.

In other words: it is written for all of us.

A Study With Tears

In September 1741, Jonathan Edwards preached a sermon called "Sinners in the Hands of an Angry God." It is almost certainly the most famous sermon in American history, and it is most often remembered for its imagery of danger and judgment. What is less often remembered is how it was preached. Edwards held his manuscript near and read steadily, without dramatic gestures. He did not perform alarm. By many accounts, people wept — not because Edwards was frightening them, but because they could sense that he was telling the truth, and that he was telling it because he cared.

That is what we reach for here. These studies are not meant to manipulate. They are not designed to produce a particular emotional response by turning up the rhetorical pressure. They are meant to be honest — to say what Scripture says, about sin, about hardening, about the seriousness of God's warnings — and to say it plainly, because anything less would be a betrayal of the people in the room.

And they are meant to end — every one of them — with hope. Not the thin hope of a cheerful afterthought stapled onto a warning, but the deep and grounded hope of a God who says turn back because turning back is genuinely possible, and who sent his own Son to make it so.

What the Watchman Cannot Do

There is one more thing the Ezekiel passage makes clear. The watchman blows the trumpet. What happens next is not his to control. If the people hear and do not turn, Ezekiel says plainly, "his blood shall be upon his own head" (33:4). The watchman has done his job. The blood is not on his hands.

That is both a sobering and a liberating word for anyone who loves someone who seems not to be hearing. You can sound the trumpet. You cannot make anyone listen. And God, who knows every heart, holds every person accountable for what they have heard and not heeded — and for what they have heard and turned toward in genuine faith.

He does not ask you to convict. He asks you to be honest. That is what we are attempting, together, in the studies that follow.

Christ, Our Anchor

The Ezekiel watchman sees the sword coming and blows the trumpet. But Jesus, the ultimate Watchman, did something the human watchman could never do: he did not merely warn about the sword. He stepped in front of it. He bore the judgment that our unwillingness to hear had been accumulating.

The prophet speaks from outside the danger. Christ speaks from within it — not from the safety of the wall but from the cross, having absorbed everything the sword could do. Every warning in this study, when heard rightly, is heard against that backdrop: the God who warns you is the same God who has already paid the price of your not listening. His warnings are not the words of a prosecutor building a case. They are the words of a Savior who has already won the acquittal and is pleading with you to live like it.

Isaiah put it this way: "The LORD has laid on him the iniquity of us all" (Isaiah 53:6). The watchman became the wall. That is the miracle beneath every warning that follows.

For reflection & discussion

  • When you hear the word "warning" in a spiritual or religious context, what is your first instinct? Where does that instinct come from?
  • God says in Ezekiel 33:11 that he takes "no pleasure in the death of the wicked." How does that change the tone in which you hear the warnings in this study? Does it change it?
  • The study suggests that between what we believe and how we live, there is often a gap we have learned to step over without looking down. Without naming specifics, does that description resonate with you? What does that gap feel like from the inside?
  • Edwards wept when he preached about judgment. What do you think it means to warn someone while also genuinely loving them? What does that look like in practice?
  • The watchman can blow the trumpet but cannot make anyone listen. How do you sit with that truth — either as someone who loves another person who seems not to be hearing, or as someone who wonders if you yourself have been tuning it out?
