Day 18

Intensity Is Not Evidence

And he said, 'Go out and stand on the mount before the LORD.' And behold, the LORD passed by, and a great and strong wind tore the mountains and broke in pieces the rocks before the LORD, but the LORD was not in the wind. And after the wind an earthquake, but the LORD was not in the earthquake. And after the earthquake a fire, but the LORD was not in the fire. And after the fire the sound of a low whisper. And when Elijah heard it, he wrapped his face in his cloak and went out and stood at the entrance of the cave. 1 Kings 19:11–13 (ESV)
Not everyone who says to me, 'Lord, Lord,' will enter the kingdom of heaven, but the one who does the will of my Father who is in heaven. On that day many will say to me, 'Lord, Lord, did we not prophesy in your name, and cast out demons in your name, and do many mighty works in your name?' And then will I declare to them, 'I never knew you; depart from me, you workers of lawlessness.' Matthew 7:21–23 (ESV)

Elijah has just experienced fire from heaven on Mount Carmel (1 Kings 18). He has seen dramatic, unmistakable divine power. And now God speaks to him — not in wind, earthquake, or fire, but in a low whisper. The dramatic register is deliberately absent. The presence of God is real. The intensity is not.

This is a significant corrective to the expectation that genuine encounters with God are always marked by emotional intensity, physical manifestations, or overwhelming sensory experience. Sometimes they are. But the absence of intensity is not the absence of God.

Matthew 7 is sobering in a different direction. People will claim dramatic spiritual experiences — prophecy, exorcism, miracles — and Jesus will say he never knew them. Not that the experiences didn't happen. That they didn't indicate a saving relationship. Experience, even apparently miraculous experience, is not self-interpreting. It does not prove its own source.

The question is never primarily was this experience real and intense? but does this experience point toward Christ, produce obedience to the Father, and bear the fruit of the Spirit? A whisper that leads to genuine obedience is worth more than a dramatic experience that leads nowhere.

Put It Into Practice

Read 1 Kings 19:11–13 slowly. Then sit in silence for five minutes. Practice being present to the possibility that God may be speaking in quiet, undramatic ways in your current season. Notice what comes.

For reflection

Has an intense emotional or spiritual experience ever led you somewhere you later questioned? What was the fruit of it over time? What expectations do you carry about what a genuine encounter with God should feel like? Where did those expectations come from? How does Jesus' warning in Matthew 7:21–23 reshape how you evaluate spiritual experience?
