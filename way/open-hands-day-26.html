Day 26

When Money Is the Wound

"If you are sued in court and your shirt is taken from you, give your coat, too." Proverbs 17:14 (NLT) Starting a quarrel is like opening a floodgate, so stop before a dispute breaks out. Matthew 18:15 (NLT) "If another believer sins against you, go privately and point out the offense. If the other person listens and confesses it, you have won that person back." Matthew 5:40 (NLT)

Financial betrayal is a distinctive kind of wound. Being cheated, stolen from, or defrauded by someone you trusted combines the harm of material loss with the deeper pain of relational betrayal. And because money represents real choices and real needs, the injury is hard to dismiss as minor.

Matthew 5:40 is often misread as endorsing passive surrender. But in its original context it was actually a subversive act of dignity. Roman law allowed a soldier to demand a person carry his pack one mile. Jesus says carry it two. If someone takes your shirt in court, give your coat as well — publicly, voluntarily. The act exposes the injustice rather than hiding it, and it demonstrates that the other person cannot take your dignity along with your coat. This is not doormat behavior; it is creative, dignified, unretaliatory response.

Proverbs 17:14 counsels a kind of pre-emptive wisdom: before the dispute becomes a flood, count the cost. Litigation is expensive — financially, emotionally, relationally. Sometimes absorbing a financial loss preserves something more valuable. Sometimes pursuing justice is necessary and right. Wisdom discerns the difference.

And Matthew 18:15 still applies: when possible, address the wrong directly and personally before escalating. Many financial disputes between people who know each other could be resolved with one honest conversation that never happens.
