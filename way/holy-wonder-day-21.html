Day 21

When Feeling Becomes the Thing

"I hate all your show and pretense — the hypocrisy of your religious festivals and solemn assemblies. I will not accept your burnt offerings and grain offerings. I won't even notice all your choice peace offerings. Away with your noisy hymns of praise! I will not listen to the music of your harps. Instead, I want to see a mighty flood of justice, an endless river of righteous living." Amos 5:21–24 (NLT)
But don't just listen to God's word. You must do what it says. Otherwise, you are only fooling yourselves. James 1:22 (NLT)

Amos is delivering one of the most unsettling passages in the Old Testament to a people who thought their worship was going well. The festivals were being held. The offerings were being made. The hymns were being sung. And God says: I hate it. I won't even notice.

Why? Because the worship was happening in complete disconnection from how they were living. They were exploiting the poor, perverting justice, living in luxury while the vulnerable suffered — and then coming to the temple on schedule and lifting their hands.

Worship that never makes it into the rest of life is not worship. It is a performance. When the emotional experience of Sunday becomes the point — when we come to feel something, and the feeling is sufficient — we have made the worship experience itself into an idol.

James makes this concrete: if you hear the Word and don't do it, you have fooled yourself. The measure of genuine worship is not how we felt in the service. It is whether something is different on Monday. Does the encounter with the holy God actually change anything? Does the love celebrated in song produce justice in action? The answer to those questions is what God is looking for.

Put It Into Practice

Think back to the last time you worshiped — privately or corporately. What changed in how you treated people this week? If nothing changed, that is worth sitting with. Not for condemnation, but for honesty.

For reflection

Has emotional worship experience ever functioned as a substitute for actually changing how you live? What did that look like? God says through Amos that he wants a flood of justice rather than noisy praise. How does that reframe what the "output" of genuine worship should be? What would it look like for your Sunday worship to be more visibly connected to your Monday life?
