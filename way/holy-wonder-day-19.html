Day 19

An Audience of One

Watch out! Don't do your good deeds publicly, to be admired by others, for you will lose the reward from your Father in heaven... When you pray, don't be like the hypocrites who love to pray publicly on street corners and in the synagogues where everyone can see them. I tell you the truth, that is all the reward they will ever get. But when you pray, go away by yourself, shut the door behind you, and pray to your Father in private. Then your Father, who sees everything, will reward you. Matthew 6:1, 5–6 (NLT)

Jesus doesn't say the hypocrites aren't praying. They are praying — loudly, publicly, impressively. What they're doing wrong is the audience. They are performing for the people around them, and they get exactly what they're seeking: human admiration. There is nothing left over from God, because God was never the one they were talking to.

Performance is one of the subtlest and most damaging distortions of worship. It can happen in a church service, in a small group prayer, in the language we use to describe our spiritual life to others. We begin shaping what we say and how we say it based on how it will land rather than what is true. And when we do, we lose the thing worship is actually for.

Jesus' solution is strikingly physical: go to a room. Shut the door. Create the conditions in which there is genuinely only one audience. What is left when all the watching eyes are removed is what you actually bring to God. That remainder — honest, unperformed, perhaps smaller than what you'd say in public — is what he is after.

This is not an argument against corporate worship. Jesus himself worshiped publicly and regularly. It is an argument about motive — about remembering, in every act of worship public or private, who you are doing this for.

From Church History — Desert Fathers — The Flight from Audience

In the 3rd and 4th centuries, thousands of Christians fled the cities of Egypt and Syria for the desert. Their motivations were complex, but one thread was clear: they were fleeing the performance of public religion, which had grown comfortable and socially acceptable after Constantine. They wanted to find out what was left when stripped of audience. What they discovered — recorded in the Sayings of the Desert Fathers — was both humbling and formative: that without an audience, the self's remaining attachments became painfully visible. The desert became a school of honest self-knowledge and genuine prayer.

Put It Into Practice

Find fifteen minutes alone today — genuinely alone, with no phone, no chance of being seen. Pray. Notice what changes when there is no one watching. Notice what you bring when performance is impossible.

For reflection

Where in your worship life are you most tempted to perform — for others or perhaps for yourself? What do you think you would pray about if you knew with certainty that no one would ever know what you said? How does the knowledge that God "sees in secret" (Matthew 6:6) change how you approach private worship?
