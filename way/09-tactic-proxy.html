The Wounded Proxy War

An argument carrying a wound it was never built to resolve.

The tactic. A real theological argument is carrying a wound it was never built to carry — and no amount of careful theology will resolve it, because the argument is not the thing.

How it looksThe shape of the move

The argument is genuine — church discipline, elder authority, how the leadership handled an abuse — and the person can cite Scripture for their position. But something is off. The intensity runs higher than the question alone explains, and no amount of careful, charitable theological engagement reaches a resting point. The reason is that the person is not, finally, trying to win the argument; they are trying to be heard. Often a wound is speaking through the only language the church ever made available to it: doctrine. The position is shaped by the wound — but that does not make it wrong. The intensity is exactly proportionate to the question plus the unprocessed pain beneath it.

The virtue it twistsWhy good people fall for it

What is real and good here is conviction joined to courage — the willingness to raise a hard question about how power was used, and not to let it go. That is often exactly what a healthy church needs. The tragedy is that the very intensity which signals the wound also makes the person easy to dismiss as a malcontent.

A scene — how it might run

Suppose someone keeps pressing, in every members’ meeting, on the precise limits of elder authority — with real proof-texts, and a heat that never cools no matter how thoroughly the elders answer the question. It would be easy to file them as a troublemaker. But the heat is not really about polity. Years ago, authority was used to crush them, and ‘the limits of elder authority’ is the only sanctioned vocabulary their pain has ever been allowed to wear. Answer the argument forever and you will never touch the thing that is actually crying out.

What it costsThe damage it does

The adversary’s work here is to keep both dimensions from ever being addressed at once — so the argument grinds on while the wound stays invisible, and the person never receives what they actually need: to be believed about what was done to them. This is the same care the Slough of Despond asked for, surfacing again now inside a disagreement.

Disarming itThe peacemaker’s move, up close

Safety before tactics — and never lead with ‘this is really about your wound.’ That sentence, however true, lands as a dismissal: it tells the person their argument can be ignored because they are damaged. Do the opposite. Validate the substance first and for real: ‘What you’re raising is a genuine question, and it deserves a genuine answer’ — and then give one. Only once the argument has been honored is there room to make space for the person beneath it: ‘Can I also ask how you are in all this?’

The order is everything. The wound has to be seen before the argument can rest, but the person has to be respected before the wound can be named — and they should usually name it themselves. If it is you carrying the heat, the gift is to notice it: if a doctrinal question will not release you, and your intensity outruns the issue, it is worth asking what the question is standing in for — and whether what you most need is not to win the point but to be believed by someone safe.

📖 The Scripture behind this Directwhat’s this?›

The claim: God draws near to the wounded, and his people are called to weep with those who weep — so a hurting person inside a disagreement is to be cared for, not merely out-argued.

  • Psalm 34:18 (ESV) — “The LORD is near to the brokenhearted and saves the crushed in spirit.”God’s own posture toward the wounded is nearness — the pattern for his people.
  • Romans 12:15 (ESV) — “Rejoice with those who rejoice, weep with those who weep.”The commanded response to another’s pain is to grieve alongside, not to debate it away.
  • 1 Thessalonians 5:14 (ESV) — “admonish the idle, encourage the fainthearted, help the weak, be patient with them all.”Different conditions need different responses — the fainthearted need encouragement, not correction.
The reasoning (direct)Marked direct because Scripture states the posture plainly. God is ‘near to the brokenhearted’ (Psalm 34:18); his people are commanded to ‘weep with those who weep’ (Romans 12:15); and Paul directs that the fainthearted be encouraged and the weak helped, not lumped under correction (1 Thessalonians 5:14). These directly warrant the disarming move: when a wound is speaking through an argument, the faithful response is to care for the person while honoring the question — because Scripture tells us how to treat the brokenhearted, and it is not with a sharper rebuttal.

The call to care for the wounded is stated directly; recognizing a wound beneath an argument is the discernment the tactic asks of us.
