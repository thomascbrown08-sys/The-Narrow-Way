Day 63

Gethsemane

Opening Prayer

Lord, in Gethsemane you fell on your face and asked whether the cup could pass. This was not theater. This was a genuine human agony in the face of what was coming. We come to this garden with reverence and without hurry. Whatever the cup contained — and we will try today to look honestly at what it contained — you drank it willingly. Not because it was easy. Because you loved us more than you loved avoiding it. Amen.

"Then he said to them, 'My soul is very sorrowful, even to death; remain here, and watch with me.' And going a little farther he fell on his face and prayed, saying, 'My Father, if it be possible, let this cup pass from me; nevertheless, not as I will, but as you will.'" Matthew 26:38–39, ESV

He has sung the Passover hymn with his disciples and walked out into the night (Matthew 26:30). The garden on the slope of the Mount of Olives — a place he had come to often, a place Judas knew (John 18:2) — receives him for the last time. He takes Peter and the two sons of Zebedee aside, deeper into the garden. And then Matthew, who was not among this inner group and who received this account from those who were, records what happened next with a directness that is almost unbearable: he began to be sorrowful and troubled.

My soul is very sorrowful, even to death. The language is extreme. The Greek word — perilypos — means encompassed by grief, surrounded by it on all sides, with no direction to look that is not grief. The qualification even to death suggests not merely intense sorrow but a sorrow of such magnitude that it threatens the limit of what a human person can endure. Luke adds the physiological detail that most translators render as sweating drops of blood — thromboi haimatos — a condition associated in ancient medicine with extreme psychological distress (Luke 22:44). His body is carrying the weight of what his mind is facing.

He falls on his face. This is not a composed kneeling. It is prostration — the posture of someone overwhelmed. And he prays: My Father, if it be possible, let this cup pass from me. The cup in the prophetic tradition of the Old Testament — particularly in Isaiah, Jeremiah, and Revelation — is the cup of God's wrath, the concentrated judgment that falls on those who bear the weight of divine displeasure against sin (Isaiah 51:17; Jeremiah 25:15–16). This is what Jesus is looking at in Gethsemane. Not merely physical suffering — he has known physical suffering, and the cross will bring more. What he is facing, and from which every fiber of his sinless humanity recoils, is the cup of divine wrath poured out on sin — the full weight of the Father's holy judgment against everything that has ever been wrong, borne in his own person, in the place of those who deserved it.

He asks if there is another way. Three times (Matthew 26:44). The repeated asking is not weak faith; it is the honest prayer of a person who truly does not want to drink what is in front of them, who brings the full weight of the desire not to drink to the Father's feet, and who, having brought it fully, submits it: not as I will, but as you will. The submission is not resignation or defeat. It is the highest act of love possible — the willing acceptance of the most costly obedience, not because the cost does not register, but because the love that drives the acceptance is greater than the pain of what the acceptance costs.

His disciples are asleep. Three times he comes back to them; three times they are sleeping. They cannot watch one hour. He returns to the prayer alone, and alone he settles what must be settled: the cup will not pass. He will drink it. And when the torches appear at the garden gate, he walks toward them — not away. The agony was real. The will was set. The love held.

For reflection

  • He asked three times whether the cup could pass — and each time submitted his will to the Father's. This is not the prayer of someone who has made peace with suffering before they pray; it is the prayer of someone who brings their genuine resistance and then releases it. How does this shape the way you pray in the face of something you genuinely do not want?
  • The cup he faced was not primarily physical suffering but the wrath of God poured out on sin — the thing his sinless nature recoiled from most absolutely. What does his willingness to drink it — not because it was easy but because love required it — say to you about what the cross cost him and what your salvation is worth to him?
  • "Not as I will, but as you will" — the prayer of complete submission spoken in the middle of complete anguish. Is there a cup in your own life — something God's will seems to be leading you toward that your will desperately resists? What would it mean to bring the full weight of your resistance to the Father and then release it the way he did?

Going Deeper (optional)

The Gethsemane prayer presses on one of the most careful questions in Christology: what does it mean for the Son to ask the Father whether something is possible, and then to submit his will to the Father's? If the Son and the Father are one in essence (John 10:30), how can there be a conflict of wills to be resolved? The Chalcedonian answer, developed most precisely by Maximus the Confessor in the seventh century and affirmed by the Third Council of Constantinople (680–681 AD), is that Christ has two wills corresponding to his two natures: a divine will (shared with the Father and the Spirit, always in perfect harmony with the Father's purpose) and a human will (which, in Gethsemane, genuinely recoils from the cross). The prayer of "not my will but yours" is the human will submitting itself to the divine will — not the Son opposing the Father, but the human nature of the Son exercising the genuine freedom of a fully human will and choosing, in that freedom, obedience.

This is not a peripheral theological point. If Christ does not have a genuine human will, then his obedience in Gethsemane is not genuine human obedience — and if it is not genuine human obedience, it cannot be credited to human beings who need human obedience credited to them. The active obedience of Christ (Day 16) culminates in Gethsemane: the most costly act of human obedience in history, freely chosen, with full awareness of the cost, in the power of the Spirit, for the sake of those whose disobedience it would reverse. He was obedient to the point of death (Philippians 2:8). And the obedience began in a garden, on his face, at midnight, alone.
