Mount Caution

Mount Caution

The peak where the shepherds show the pilgrims men who strayed, and what became of them.

"Take care, brothers, lest there be in any of you an evil, unbelieving heart, leading you to fall away from the living God." Hebrews 3:12 (ESV)

Among the heights, the shepherds lead the pilgrims to a hill called Caution, and bid them look. In the distance, among the tombs at the foot of the mountains, men are stumbling about blindly among old graves. The shepherds explain: these were once pilgrims, like Christian and Hopeful, who strayed from the way — and were taken by Giant Despair, blinded, and left to wander among the tombs. It is a hard sight, and the shepherds do not soften it. They show it precisely so the pilgrims will take warning.

This is the sober room of the high country: the warning of the fallen. Scripture does not flinch from it either — it speaks plainly of those who were once enlightened, who tasted the goodness of the word, and who then fell away. The road would not be honest if it pretended this peak were not here. But it must be walked carefully, because the warning is easily misread in two opposite and equally damaging directions.

In plain terms

The Bible takes falling away seriously — seriously enough to warn us sharply about it. But the warnings are given to keep us walking, not to leave us perpetually terrified that we might already be lost.

If you are afraid you have fallen too far, that very fear is usually evidence you have not. The truly hardened do not lie awake worrying about it.

On one side is the error of presumption — treating grace as a license, assuming that because one once prayed a prayer or walked an aisle, nothing now matters. The warnings of Mount Caution are aimed straight at that complacency: keep walking, stay awake, do not stray from the way. On the other side is the error of despair — the tender, anxious believer who reads these warnings and concludes they must already be among the wandering blind. To them the shepherds say something different: the perseverance of the saints is real, and the God who began a good work will complete it. The warnings are the very means God uses to keep his people on the path — and heeding them is itself a mark that you are still his.

The warnings are given to keep us walking — not to leave us forever afraid we are already lost.

We will treat the harder questions in their own pages — what the warning passages mean, how perseverance and assurance hold together, the sins that harden, and the difference between the believer's stumble and the apostate's departure. For now, take the shepherds' point: the cliff is real, the path runs safely past it, and the way to honor the warning is simply to keep walking, awake, in the company of the Shepherd who keeps his sheep.
