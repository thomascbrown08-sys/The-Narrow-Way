Science & Faith

Science & Faith

Science and faith — the two books, why the conflict narrative is bad history, the fine-tuning argument, and an honest map of the origins positions faithful Christians hold.

"The heavens declare the glory of God, and the sky above proclaims his handiwork." Psalm 19:1 (ESV)

The second annex takes up a quarrel that many pilgrims arrive already believing they have lost: the supposed war between science and Christian faith. Science advances, the story goes, and faith retreats; science is for grown-ups, faith for those who have not grown up yet. Before the argument even begins, that frame deserves examining — because it smuggles in an assumption that most careful thinkers, including a great many scientists, do not actually share.

The real questionDifferent questions, not competing answers

Science, rigorously understood, is a method: a way of investigating the natural world through observation, hypothesis, and testing. It is extraordinarily good at what it does. It is also, by design, silent on a whole range of questions that cannot be reduced to empirical measurement — why is there something rather than nothing? what grounds the reliability of reason itself? what makes anything morally obligatory? is there a personal God? These lie outside its jurisdiction by method, the way a tape measure is simply the wrong instrument for judging a painting. Using the right tool for the right job is not anti-science; it is good thinking.

So the question Christianity addresses is not "how did the universe begin?" but "why does anything exist at all, and does it have a purpose?" — and those are different questions, not rival answers to the same one. A complete account of the Big Bang's mechanism does not, in principle, exclude the claim that a personal God initiated and sustains it.

In plain terms

Picture a kettle boiling. You can explain it completely through physics — heat energy, molecules, a phase change to steam. That explanation is true and complete as far as it goes. But it does not answer "why is the kettle on?" — which needs a different kind of answer: someone put it on to make tea.

Both answers are true; neither cancels the other. Science tells you the mechanism; faith addresses the agent and the purpose behind it. You need both, and one does not make the other false.

📖 The Scripture behind this Interpretation›

The claim: Science and Christian faith are not rival answers to the same question but address different questions — science the mechanism of the natural world, faith the agent and purpose behind it — so a complete scientific account does not exclude God.

  • Genesis 1:1 (ESV) — “In the beginning, God created the heavens and the earth.”Scripture’s claim is about the agent and purpose of creation — the ‘why,’ not the lab mechanism.
  • Colossians 1:16–17 (ESV) — “In him all things hold together.”God is named as the one who sustains the order science describes — not a rival to it.
  • Psalm 19:1 (ESV) — “The heavens declare the glory of God, and the sky above proclaims his handiwork.”The natural order itself points beyond mechanism to its Maker.
The reasoning (flagged as interpretation, not direct derivation)Marked interpretation because the ‘two questions, not competing answers’ framework is a philosophical distinction — deeply consonant with Scripture, but not a doctrine the text states in those terms. What Scripture does supply is its own kind of claim: Genesis 1:1 and Colossians 1:16–17 speak of the agent and purpose behind creation and its ongoing sustenance, and Psalm 19 says the created order points to its Maker — none of which is a claim about the mechanism that science investigates. Drawing from this the principle that science (the ‘how’) and faith (the ‘why’ and ‘who’) occupy different jurisdictions — the kettle’s physics and the reason the kettle is on — is a sound and widely held synthesis, but a framework brought to the texts rather than stated by them. We flag it as interpretation so its philosophical character is clear. (How directly to read Genesis 1 — the age of the earth, the days — is itself a secondary matter on which orthodox believers differ.)

The conflict mythA war that history does not support

The popular narrative of science versus religion is itself bad history. The founders of modern science — Kepler, Newton, Boyle, Faraday, Maxwell — were overwhelmingly believers who understood their work as thinking God's thoughts after him, and who expected the universe to be orderly and intelligible because it was the work of a rational Creator. That expectation was not an obstacle to science; it was one of its seedbeds. The idea that faith and science have been locked in perpetual warfare is a relatively modern story, popularized in the nineteenth century and quietly abandoned by most serious historians of science since — though it lives on in popular slogans.

A universe fine-tunedThe argument worth knowing

One genuine development is worth knowing by name. Through the twentieth century, physicists discovered what is called the fine-tuning of the universe: the fundamental constants — the cosmological constant, the ratio of forces, the mass of the electron, the strength of the nuclear forces — are set within extraordinarily narrow ranges, such that altering any of them even slightly yields a universe with no structure, no chemistry, and no possibility of life. Even physicists who are not believers have found the precision arresting; one wrote that "the impression of design is overwhelming." This is not a proof the physics itself can offer — the question of why the constants are what they are lies outside physics — but it is an observation from physics that demands some philosophical response.

The responses come to essentially three: that the constants are this way by sheer chance (which asks us to accept a staggeringly improbable state of affairs as brute fact); that a multiverse of countless universes with varying constants makes ours a mere selection effect (scientifically intriguing, but presently unfalsifiable and carrying its own baggage); or that the universe was designed with these constants by an intelligence able to set them. None of this proves theism. What it does show is that dismissing design as obviously primitive takes far more philosophical work than the confident slogans admit.

On originsWhere faithful Christians differ — and what holds

And here the annex must, true to its purpose, simply map. On the question of how God created — the age of the earth, the mechanism of life's development, how to read the days of Genesis 1 — sincere, Bible-believing Christians have reached genuinely different conclusions. Young-earth creationism, old-earth creationism, and evolutionary creationism each number serious, faithful believers among their advocates, and this site does not make any one of them a test of orthodoxy. What all faithful readings hold in common is the thing that actually matters and is actually confessed in the creeds: that God — not chance, not an impersonal process — is the Creator of all that is; that creation is purposeful and good; that human beings are made, uniquely, in his image and are not cosmic accidents; and that the heavens declare his glory. The mechanism is disputed among friends. The Maker is not.

The mechanism of creation is disputed among friends. The Maker is not. The heavens declare the glory of God — however he framed them.

So the supposed war turns out, on inspection, to be a boundary dispute that dissolves once each discipline is asked only the questions it is fitted to answer. Science reads one of God's books — the book of his works; Scripture is the other — the book of his words; and the same God stands behind both. The two annexes done, the path rejoins the road, which now descends from the bright clarity of the heights into the most subtly dangerous country of the whole journey.
