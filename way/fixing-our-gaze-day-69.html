Day 69

Buried in a Borrowed Tomb

Opening Prayer

Lord, on Holy Saturday the disciples did not know what we know. The tomb was sealed and guarded and apparently final. There is something we need to learn from sitting in that not-yet-knowing — from the space between the cross and the empty tomb where faith has no visible evidence to lean on and must hold on to what was promised before it was fulfilled. Help us stay in this day before we move to the next. Amen.

"When it was evening, there came a rich man from Arimathea, named Joseph, who also was a disciple of Jesus. He went to Pilate and asked for the body of Jesus. Then Pilate ordered it to be given to him. And Joseph took the body and wrapped it in a clean linen shroud and laid it in his own new tomb, which he had cut in the rock. And he rolled a great stone to the entrance of the tomb and went away." Matthew 27:57–60, ESV

The body must be taken down before sundown — the Sabbath begins at sunset, and the law forbids leaving a body on a cross through the Sabbath (Deuteronomy 21:22–23; John 19:31). Someone must do the practical, dangerous work of approaching Pilate, requesting custody of the body of a condemned man. Most of the disciples have fled. The women are watching from a distance. And two members of the Sanhedrin — the very council that condemned him — step forward: Joseph of Arimathea and Nicodemus, both described as secret disciples, both now acting publicly for the first time and at considerable personal and political risk.

Joseph asks Pilate for the body. Pilate, surprised that Jesus is already dead (Mark 15:44), confirms it with the centurion and grants permission. Joseph wraps the body in clean linen. Nicodemus brings a mixture of myrrh and aloes — a hundred pounds of spices (John 19:39), an extravagance that echoes the woman's ointment at Bethany, a last anointing for burial from men who loved him and had not yet found the courage to say so publicly until there was nothing left to say it over.

The tomb is Joseph's own, new and unused — another borrowed space, like the manger and the upper room and the borrowed donkey. He has never owned a home in the world he made. He is laid in the tomb of another man. Isaiah saw even this coming: they made his grave with the wicked and with a rich man in his death (Isaiah 53:9) — buried in the tomb of a wealthy man, just as the prophet said. The details of his death, written six centuries before they happened, are fulfilled with a precision that his enemies cannot explain and his disciples will, in time, begin to understand.

A great stone is rolled to the entrance. The guards are posted the next day (Matthew 27:62–66), at the insistence of the chief priests who remember his prediction of resurrection and want to make sure no one can claim the body was stolen. They seal the tomb and set the watch. They have done, in their anxiety, exactly what will later make the resurrection undeniable: they have made it publicly verifiable that the tomb was sealed, guarded, and opened by no human hand.

Holy Saturday. The day between. The disciples do not know what we know. For them, this is simply: it is over. The one they followed is dead and in a tomb and the stone is against the door. Peter has denied him. Judas has hanged himself. The women sit opposite the sepulchre (Matthew 27:61) — not yet leaving, not yet believing, not yet anything except present at the site of what has been lost.

We should not move too quickly to Easter. There is a theology of Holy Saturday that is necessary for honest faith: the capacity to be in the not-yet, the in-between, the space where the promises have been made and the evidence is not yet visible. Every person who has prayed and not yet seen the answer, who has believed and not yet felt the presence, who has held on to what Christ said and found themselves looking at a sealed stone — they live in some version of Holy Saturday. The tomb is real. The stone is real. And the word that preceded it is also real. Sunday is coming. But it is not yet Sunday.

For reflection

  • Joseph and Nicodemus acted publicly at the moment of greatest apparent defeat — when there was nothing to gain and everything to lose by associating with Jesus. What does their courage at this particular moment say about the nature of genuine discipleship? And what does it take for secret faith to become public?
  • The disciples sat with the sealed tomb before they knew the resurrection was coming. Is there an area of your life that is currently in Holy Saturday — where you are holding on to a promise that has not yet been fulfilled, where the stone is in place and the evidence is silent? What does this day teach about faith in the absence of visible confirmation?
  • "He had no place to lay his head" (Day 26), and at his death he had no tomb of his own either — buried in borrowed ground. What does this consistent pattern of homelessness and borrowing tell you about how he valued the things the world treats as essential? And how does it shape the way you hold the material provisions of your own life?

Going Deeper (optional)

The burial of Jesus is theologically significant in ways that are sometimes overlooked. Paul places it as an essential element of the gospel summary in 1 Corinthians 15:3–4: "Christ died for our sins in accordance with the Scriptures, and was buried, and was raised on the third day in accordance with the Scriptures." The burial is not incidental; it is the confirmation of the death — the body placed in the ground, the death physically verified and publicly witnessed. This matters because the resurrection that follows is a bodily resurrection: the same body that was placed in the tomb comes out of it. The resurrection is not the survival of a spiritual essence or the beginning of a spiritual legacy; it is the physical body of Jesus of Nazareth, transformed and glorified, walking out of the rock tomb where Joseph of Arimathea laid it.

The burial also establishes the full extent of Christ's identification with human mortality. The Apostles' Creed includes "he descended to the dead" (or "descended into hell" in some versions) — referring, in the Reformed reading, not to Christ suffering in hell after the cross (the suffering was complete on the cross, as "it is finished" confirms) but to the reality that he truly died and entered the state of the dead. He did not merely appear to die. He was dead, buried, sealed, guarded. The resurrection overcame death from the inside, not by bypassing it. The Son of God went all the way down — into the tomb, into the full experience of human death — and then came all the way back, having beaten death not by avoiding it but by entering it, exhausting it, and leaving it permanently empty.
