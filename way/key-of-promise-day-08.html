Day 8

Come to Me, All Who Are Weary

Then Jesus said, “Come to me, all of you who are weary and carry heavy burdens, and I will give you rest. Take my yoke upon you. Let me teach you, because I am humble and gentle at heart, and you will find rest for your souls. For my yoke is easy to bear, and the burden I give you is light.” Matthew 11:28–30 (NLT)

Until now in these days we have mostly heard God speak in promises about himself. Here, suddenly, it is the voice of Jesus, speaking in the first person, directly into the exhaustion that fills a place like Doubting Castle — and the exhaustion is real. The dungeon is tiring in a way that ordinary trouble is not. It is the weariness of carrying guilt that will not lift, of fighting thoughts that will not quiet, of praying prayers that seem to fall to the floor. By the time many pilgrims reach the giant's cell, they are not so much afraid as tired.

And to the tired, Jesus does not say, “Try harder.” He does not say, “Muster more faith, feel more sorrow, fix yourself, and then come.” He says the gentlest two words in all of Scripture: Come to me. Not come to a program. Not come to a higher standard. Come to a Person. And the rest he offers is not first a technique or a discipline — it is himself. “I will give you rest.”

He even tells us why it is safe to come: “I am humble and gentle at heart.” This is the one place in the Gospels where Jesus describes his own heart, and of all the things he could have named, he names gentleness. The One you are afraid to approach in your weariness describes himself, precisely, as gentle toward the weary. The giant has been telling you Christ is exasperated with you, fed up, ready to give up on someone so weak. Jesus says: you have misread my heart. I am gentle. Come.

There is a yoke, yes — he does not pretend the Christian life has no weight. But notice it is his yoke, and a yoke is built for two. It is the difference between dragging a burden alone and being harnessed alongside one who carries the real weight. “My yoke is easy; my burden is light” — not because there is nothing to carry, but because you no longer carry it by yourself.

So the simplest, truest thing a weary soul can do tonight is also the thing despair makes hardest: just come. Not come impressively. Not come having recovered. Just come, with the weariness itself, to the One whose heart is gentle.

Pray the Promise

Pray the simplest prayer there is — the prayer of just coming. Don't try to fix your weariness first or work up better feelings. Pray: “I am weary and I am carrying more than I can hold. You said: come. So I am coming, just as I am. Give me yourself, and your rest.”

If you have no other words tonight, let that be the whole of your prayer: I am coming to you.

For reflection

What weariness are you carrying that you've assumed you must resolve before coming to Christ? What does it mean that he describes his own heart as gentle toward exactly such tiredness?
