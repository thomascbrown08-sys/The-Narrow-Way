Day 21

The Case for Continuationism

Pursue love, and earnestly desire the spiritual gifts, especially that you may prophesy. 1 Corinthians 14:1 (ESV)
And in the last days it shall be, God declares, that I will pour out my Spirit on all flesh, and your sons and your daughters shall prophesy, and your young men shall see visions, and your old men shall dream dreams; even on my male servants and female servants in those days I will pour out my Spirit, and they shall prophesy. Acts 2:17–18 (ESV)

The continuationist argument deserves a fair hearing, and here it is. The case is substantial, and honest discernment requires taking it seriously rather than dismissing it.

First: there is no explicit biblical text that says the sign gifts ceased at the close of the apostolic age. Cessationism is an inference — a reasonable one to some, but an inference nonetheless. Those who hold it should acknowledge they are reading a conclusion into texts that don't quite say it directly.

Second: Acts 2:17 frames the Spirit's outpouring as a 'last days' reality. The New Testament consistently treats the 'last days' as having begun at Pentecost and continuing until Christ's return. If the gifts were poured out for the 'last days,' the case for their cessation before those days end requires careful argument.

Third: Paul's instruction in 1 Corinthians 14 is prescriptive, not merely descriptive. He tells the church to earnestly desire spiritual gifts, especially prophecy, and gives instructions for how to exercise them orderly. He is not describing a temporary situation; he is regulating an ongoing practice.

A thoughtful cessationist has responses to each of these. But the point here is simply this: the continuationist reading of Scripture is serious and honest. It is not naive credulity. Taking it seriously is what responsible discernment requires before drawing conclusions.

Put It Into Practice

Read 1 Corinthians 14:1–5 and ask: what would it look like to take this passage at face value, with no prior assumption about whether these gifts continue or not? What does Paul seem to expect?

For reflection

What would you need to believe for continuationism to be true? Does the Bible present any obstacles to that? Have you previously dismissed continuationism without seriously engaging its biblical arguments? Or embraced it without examining the counterarguments? What is the difference between being open to the ongoing work of the Spirit and being uncritically credulous about claimed gifts?
