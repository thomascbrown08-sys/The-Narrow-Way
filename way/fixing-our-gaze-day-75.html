Day 75

The Ascension

Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, the disciples watched you ascend and were still staring into the sky when the angels asked them why they were looking up. You had told them what to do with the waiting: go to Jerusalem, wait for the Spirit, and then bear witness to the ends of the earth. The ascension is not absence — it is enthronement. Help us live today from that reality rather than gazing at the vacancy where you were. Amen.

"And when he had said these things, as they were looking on, he was lifted up, and a cloud took him out of their sight. And while they were gazing into heaven as he went, behold, two men stood by them in white robes, and said, 'Men of Galilee, why do you stand looking into heaven? This Jesus, who was taken up from you into heaven, will come in the same way as you saw him go into heaven.'" Acts 1:9–11, ESV

Forty days after the resurrection, on the Mount of Olives, in the sight of his disciples, Jesus is lifted up and a cloud takes him from their sight. The cloud is not meteorological — it is the shekinah cloud of God's glory, the cloud that led Israel through the wilderness, the cloud that filled the temple at its dedication, the cloud that overshadowed the disciples at the Transfiguration. He goes into the cloud — into the fullness of the Father's presence — and does not return. Not yet.

The disciples stand gazing into heaven. Two figures in white — angels, as at the tomb — interrupt the gazing with a question: Men of Galilee, why do you stand looking into heaven? The question is not a rebuke. It is a redirection. The gazing is natural, even appropriate — they have just watched their Lord ascend, and the eyes go where the heart is. But standing and gazing is not what they have been commissioned to do. He has given them instructions (Acts 1:4–8): wait in Jerusalem for the promised Spirit, and when the Spirit comes, be his witnesses in Jerusalem and Judea and Samaria and to the ends of the earth. The ascension is not the end of the story; it is the start of a new chapter, and the chapter begins not with gazing but with going.

The ascension is consistently misread as a form of absence — Jesus leaving, the disciples left behind, the church on its own until he returns. It is the opposite. The ascension is the fulfillment of Daniel 7:13–14 (Day 12), the moment when the Son of Man is presented before the Ancient of Days and receives the universal dominion. He has not withdrawn from the world. He has taken the throne. He is more present to every corner of creation from the right hand of the Father than he could have been in the flesh, confined by geography and the limitations of a physical body, present to one location at a time. Now he is Lord of all, reigning over all, present with his people everywhere and always — which is precisely what he promised: I am with you always, to the end of the age (Matthew 28:20).

His humanity ascended with him. This is a detail that theology tends to pass over too quickly. The one who sits at the right hand of the Father is not the divine Son shedding the human nature at the end of his earthly mission. He is the God-man — the human body that was crucified, buried, and raised now glorified and enthroned. There is a human body in heaven. The mediator between God and humanity is still human (1 Timothy 2:5). The great high priest who intercedes for us (Hebrews 7:25) is the same one who wept at Lazarus's tomb and asked whether the cup could pass. He has not stopped being what he became. He is, forever, our brother before the Father.

He will come in the same way as they saw him go — the angels say it plainly. The ascension promises the return. The one who rose into the cloud will descend from the cloud (Matthew 24:30; Revelation 1:7). The story is not complete. Section VI begins tomorrow and carries us through the fifteen days from the ascension to the end — through the throne and the intercession and the union and the hope and the love — to the capstone on Day 90. He is not absent. He is coming. And in the meantime, there is work to do. The disciples stopped gazing and went to Jerusalem. The pattern holds.

For reflection

  • "Why do you stand gazing into heaven?" The disciples were redirected from looking up to going forward. Is there a way you are currently standing and gazing — waiting for a different kind of presence, a more direct confirmation, a more visible sign — when the Spirit and the commission are already given? What would going forward look like for you right now?
  • His humanity ascended — there is a human body at the right hand of the Father. What does it mean for your prayers, your suffering, your daily embodied life to know that the one interceding for you is still human — that he knows the weight of a body from the inside and carries that knowledge into his intercession?
  • He will come in the same way he went. The ascension is a promise of return embedded in the departure. How does living with the expectation of his return — not as an escape from the present but as a horizon that gives the present its meaning — change the way you go about the work he has given you?

Going Deeper (optional)

The ascension of Christ is one of the most theologically freighted and least-preached events in the Christian calendar. It establishes three things simultaneously: the completion of his earthly mission, the beginning of his heavenly session and reign, and the promise of his return. The Reformed tradition has consistently insisted that the ascension is not a retreat but an advancement — Christ goes to the Father not to withdraw his presence but to exercise his Lordship in a mode no longer limited by the physical constraints of incarnate life in one place.

Calvin's treatment of the ascension in the Institutes (2.16.14) is characteristic: the ascension does not remove Christ from his people but intensifies his presence. The Spirit poured out at Pentecost is the mode of Christ's post-ascension presence — not a substitute for him but the means by which he is more fully present to more people than his bodily presence in Galilee could have allowed. This is the logic of John 16:7: "It is to your advantage that I go away, for if I do not go away, the Helper will not come to you." The ascension enables Pentecost; Pentecost enables the universal presence that the incarnate ministry could not have achieved geographically. He ascended so that the cloud of his glory might fill not one temple in Jerusalem but every heart in which the Spirit dwells. The ascension is, in this sense, the beginning of the fullest expression of Emmanuel — God with us — not its end.
