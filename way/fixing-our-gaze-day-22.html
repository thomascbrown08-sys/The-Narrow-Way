Day 22

The One Who Wept

Opening Prayer

Lord, you knew what you were about to do. You knew that within minutes Lazarus would be standing alive at the mouth of the tomb. And yet you wept. Let us not rush past this too quickly. Something is being revealed here about who you are and what it costs you to be present to our suffering — something we dare not flatten into a mere display of solidarity. Help us receive it fully. Amen.

"When Jesus saw her weeping, and the Jews who had come with her also weeping, he was deeply moved in his spirit and greatly troubled. And he said, 'Where have you laid him?' They said to him, 'Lord, come and see.' Jesus wept. So the Jews said, 'See how he loved him!'" John 11:33–36, CSB

The shortest verse in the Bible is also, when you understand it fully, one of the most theologically rich. "Jesus wept." Two words. The verb John uses — edakrusen — refers to the silent tears that fall down a face, not the convulsive sobbing of grief uncontrolled. The same word used for the tears of a person who loves and is watching loss.

What makes this remarkable — and what should prevent us from reading it too quickly — is what Jesus knows at this moment. He has delayed coming deliberately (John 11:6). He has told his disciples plainly that Lazarus's sickness will not end in death (11:4). He has told Martha, moments before, that he himself is the resurrection and the life (11:25). He knows what is about to happen. And he weeps anyway.

This is not performance. It is not strategic display. It is the genuine emotional response of a Person who is fully present to human suffering and is moved by it — even while holding simultaneously the knowledge of what he is about to do about it. The grief of those around him is real, and he receives it as real. The loss of Lazarus is real, and he enters it rather than standing above it with divine detachment. The knowledge that resurrection is coming does not cancel the weight of the moment; it coexists with it.

The crowd reads the tears correctly: "See how he loved him." What they see in his tears is not weakness or confusion but love — the love of someone who is genuinely affected by what affects the ones he cares for. This is a God who does not watch human grief from a composed and untroubled distance. He enters it. He sits in it. He lets it move him.

The word translated "deeply moved" earlier in the passage — embrimaomai — is actually even stronger than that translation suggests. It carries a sense of indignation as well as sorrow — a kind of righteous anger at what death has done, at the wreckage grief makes of the people he loves. He is not only sad; he is, in some sense, furious. Death is an intruder. Grief is not the way the world was supposed to be. And the tears are the expression of both the sorrow and the anger, held together, by someone who has both the feeling and the power to do something about it.

Dietrich Bonhoeffer, writing from prison near the end of his life, reflected on what it means to have a God who suffers with us — a God who does not call us away from our sorrow by announcing that it doesn't ultimately matter, but who enters it, taking it seriously, weeping over it while simultaneously moving to address it. He called this "the suffering of God" and saw it not as a weakness in the divine character but as the highest expression of love: love that is strong enough to be present to suffering rather than avoiding it.

When you carry grief to Jesus — when you bring the loss, the diagnosis, the broken relationship, the unanswered prayer, the death that has left a hole in your world — you bring it to the one who wept. Not the one who will patiently explain why grief is philosophically misguided. Not the one who will immediately move past your sorrow to the silver lining. The one who arrived at the tomb and let his tears fall in full view of those who were watching. He has been where you are. He has let it matter. And in him, as in Lazarus's story, the last word is not the weeping.

For reflection

  • Jesus wept even though he knew the resurrection was moments away. What does this tell you about how God regards your present suffering — does the certainty of future restoration cancel the validity of present grief, in his eyes?
  • The word behind "deeply moved" carries indignation as well as sorrow — a kind of anger at death and grief as intruders into the world as it was meant to be. How does knowing that Jesus shares your anger at what brokenness does to people change the way you pray about things that are genuinely, legitimately wrong?
  • Is there a grief you are carrying right now — something you have not yet brought fully to Christ because you were not sure he wanted to receive it? What would it mean to bring it to the one who wept at the tomb of his friend?

Going Deeper (optional)

The tears of Jesus at Lazarus's tomb raise one of the oldest and most carefully handled questions in Christology: how do we understand divine impassibility — the classical doctrine that God does not "suffer" emotions in the way creatures do — alongside the genuine emotional responses of the incarnate Son? The Reformed tradition, following the Chalcedonian framework, has generally held that God in his divine nature does not suffer change or emotional disturbance (Acts 14:15; Malachi 3:6); but the person of the Son, having taken on human nature, genuinely experiences emotion through that nature — fully, really, and without pretense.

This means that what we see at Lazarus's tomb is not God "pretending" to grieve for our benefit, nor a crack in divine impassibility. It is the eternal Son experiencing, through his genuine human nature, the grief and anger and love that belong to the full range of human emotional life — as he committed to do when he took that nature to himself. Herman Bavinck expressed it well: in the Incarnation, the Son did not merely put on humanity as a garment; he made it his own. And so the tears are genuinely his. They are the tears of the Person who is both the resurrection and the life — and who nonetheless weeps. Both are true. Neither cancels the other. And together they give us a Savior who is neither distant from our sorrow nor overwhelmed by it, but present to it with both the fullness of human feeling and the certainty of divine power.
