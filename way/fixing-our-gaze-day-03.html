Day 3

The Radiance of His Glory

Opening Prayer

Lord God, you are a speaking God. From the first breath of creation to the final word of Scripture, you have refused to remain silent. And in your Son, you have spoken your last and fullest word. Grant us ears trained not merely to receive information but to receive the Person you are presenting. Let the radiance of your glory in the face of Jesus Christ break through every fog of familiarity. Amen.

"Long ago, at many times and in many ways, God spoke to our fathers by the prophets, but in these last days he has spoken to us by his Son, whom he appointed the heir of all things, through whom also he created the world. He is the radiance of the glory of God and the exact imprint of his nature, and he upholds the universe by the word of his power." Hebrews 1:1–3, ESV

The writer of Hebrews opens without ceremony. No greeting, no personal introduction, no warmup. He begins with God: "Long ago… God spoke." And then, with barely a breath: "but in these last days he has spoken to us by his Son." In that single word — but — an entire history pivots.

The prophets were real. Their words were true. Moses spoke from a burning bush; Isaiah stood before the heavenly throne and heard the seraphim; Elijah heard the still, small voice after the fire and the earthquake. God was genuinely communicating through these men, and the writer of Hebrews does not minimize what they received. But all of those words were partial — pieces of a longer sentence whose subject had not yet appeared. They were like hearing music played softly in another room: you catch something of the melody, but not its full resolution, not the face of the one who is singing.

Jesus is the full sentence. Not merely the loudest word God has ever spoken, but the clearest and the last. The original Greek is even more striking: God has spoken to us "in Son" — as if the Son himself is simultaneously the medium, the message, and the speaker. He is not a channel through which divine content flows; he is the content itself, clothed in flesh.

Then the author does something remarkable: he piles up descriptions of this Son in rapid succession, as though no single phrase is adequate, as though language itself must strain at every seam. He is the heir of all things — all creation, past, present, and to come, belongs to him as rightful inheritance. He is the one through whom the world was created — the divine artisan, glimpsed in Proverbs 8 as wisdom personified, delighting at the Father's side as the world took shape. And he upholds the universe by the word of his power — which means that at this very moment, as you read this page, the gravitational coherence of every atom in existence is a function of his active, continuous sustaining. Creation is not a machine he wound up and left running. It is a word he is still speaking.

And then: "He is the radiance of the glory of God and the exact imprint of his nature."

Consider these words with care. Radiance — apaugasma in the Greek — is the light that streams outward from a blazing source. You cannot separate the sun from its radiance; they are not two things but one thing extended. When the light of the sun reaches your face, the sun itself has reached you — not a copy of it, not a representation of it, but the actual sun, arriving in the form of its own outshining. This is the relationship between the Father and the Son: where the Son is, the Father's glory has genuinely arrived.

The exact imprint — charaktēr — is the technical word for an engraved seal pressed into wax. When the seal is pressed, the impression that rises from the wax is an exact replica of the engraved image: every feature, every contour, perfectly reproduced. The Son is the imprint of the Father's very substance — his nature, his being, precisely and completely expressed in personal form.

John Calvin commented that in Christ we have "not a shadow or a sketch of God, but the fullness of deity dwelling bodily." The person who looks at Jesus and says "I wish I knew what God was truly like" has looked directly past the answer they are seeking. Jesus said it himself, with a patience that conceals a kind of ache: "Whoever has seen me has seen the Father" (John 14:9). Not approximately seen. Not partially seen. Seen.

John Stott wrote that Christians are those who have, in seeing Christ, seen enough of God to rest their souls on what they have seen. That is the invitation here. Not to look past Jesus toward some purer or more abstract divinity behind him, but to look at him — attentively, repeatedly, with increasing wonder — and to discover that in looking at him, you are already looking at God. Every page of the Gospels is simultaneously a page of theology. What Christ is like is what God is like. And this changes everything about how you read the Gospels, how you pray, and how you speak of the God who has refused, in his mercy, to remain unknown.

For reflection

  • Hebrews says God spoke "at many times and in many ways" before sending his Son — a long preparation before the fullest Word arrived. What might this pattern suggest about the way God works in your own life and story?
  • If Jesus is the "exact imprint" of the Father's nature, what does looking carefully at Jesus' life and character teach you about what God is actually like? Is there anything in the Gospels that surprises you about God?
  • Christ upholds the universe "by the word of his power" — right now, continuously, without ceasing. How does this picture of the present, active Christ affect the way you think about prayer, or about trusting him in circumstances that feel out of control?

Going Deeper (optional)

The language of Hebrews 1:3 is among the most theologically dense in the New Testament, and the early church spent enormous energy unpacking it rightly. The words apaugasma (radiance) and charaktēr (exact imprint) together drove the Nicene theologians to insist that the Son is "of the same substance" (homoousios) as the Father — not merely "of similar substance," as Arius proposed. This distinction, fiercely debated in the fourth century, may sound like metaphysical hair-splitting. But the practical stakes are immense.

If the Son is merely similar to the Father — a near-copy, an exalted creature who most closely approximates the divine — then what we see in Jesus is an approximation of God, and we must look past him to find the real thing. But if he is of the same substance — if he is the radiance, the exact imprint — then in seeing Jesus we have truly seen. The character of Christ on display in the Gospels — his compassion for the ashamed, his tenderness toward the broken, his fury at religious hypocrisy, his sorrow at the hardness of human hearts — is not a human approximation of the divine character. It is the divine character, embodied. This is what Nicaea protects: the certainty that when you look at Jesus, you are not looking toward God — you are looking at him.
