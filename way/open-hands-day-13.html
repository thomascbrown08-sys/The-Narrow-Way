Day 13

Seek First the Kingdom

"No one can serve two masters. For you will hate one and love the other; you will be devoted to one and despise the other. You cannot serve God and be enslaved to money.

That is why I tell you not to worry about everyday life — whether you have enough food and drink, or enough clothes to wear. Isn't life more than food, and your body more than clothing? Look at the birds. They don't plant or harvest or store food in barns, for your heavenly Father feeds them. And aren't you far more valuable to him than they are? Can all your worries add a single moment to your life?

And why worry about your clothing? Look at the lilies of the field and how they grow. They don't work or make their clothing, yet Solomon in all his glory was not dressed as beautifully as they are. And if God cares so wonderfully for wildflowers that are here today and thrown into the fire tomorrow, he will certainly care for you. Why do you have so little faith?

So don't worry about these things, saying, 'What will we eat? What will we drink? What will we wear?' These things dominate the thoughts of unbelievers, but your heavenly Father already knows all your needs. Seek the Kingdom of God above all else, and live righteously, and he will give you everything you need." Matthew 6:24–33 (NLT)

This is the climax of everything Jesus has said about money in the Sermon on the Mount. And he frames the whole thing as a question of masters.

"You cannot serve God and be enslaved to money." He doesn't say it's difficult, or that it takes great discipline. He says it is structurally impossible. Two masters with different agendas require different things from you. Eventually you will serve one by betraying the other. The only question is which one you've already chosen.

The "don't worry" section that follows isn't an instruction to be naive about money or to stop planning. The birds still forage; the lilies still grow. Jesus isn't commending passivity — he's diagnosing where anxiety actually lives. Financial worry isn't really about whether you'll have enough to eat. It's about whether God can be trusted. That's the question underneath the question.

"Seek first." These two words carry the weight of the whole passage. Jesus doesn't say seek only the kingdom, or that practical needs don't matter. He says: get the order right. When the kingdom is first, everything else finds its proper place. When the kingdom is second — after security, after comfort, after enough — it never really arrives.

Eight days of wisdom about money ends here, with a posture: hands open, eyes up, seeking first.

For reflection

When financial anxiety shows up, what does your first move tend to be? What does Jesus say it should be?
