Day 23

What Forgiveness Is Not

"If another believer sins against you, go privately and point out the offense. If the other person listens and confesses it, you have won that person back. But if you are unsuccessful, take one or two others with you and go back again, so that everything you say may be confirmed by two or three witnesses. If the person still refuses to listen, take your case to the church. Then if he or she won't accept the church's verdict, treat that person as a pagan or a corrupt tax collector." Luke 17:3–4 (NLT) "So watch yourselves! If another believer sins, rebuke that person; then if there is repentance, forgive. Even if that person wrongs you seven times a day and each time turns again and asks forgiveness, you must forgive." Matthew 18:15–17 (NLT)

Before we can understand what forgiveness is, we need to clear away what it isn't — because misconceptions here cause serious harm. They either make forgiveness feel like an impossible demand, or they reduce it to something meaningless. Today is for clearing the ground.

Forgiveness is not pretending the wrong didn't happen. Matthew 18:15–17 makes this plain: Jesus instructs believers to name the offense, go to the person, and address it directly. Forgiveness does not require pretending nothing occurred.

Forgiveness is not saying the action was acceptable. To forgive a wrong is to release it — but releasing it does not mean approving it. You can fully forgive someone while still naming what they did as genuinely wrong.

Forgiveness is not the same as restored trust. Trust is earned through consistent behavior over time. Forgiveness can happen immediately; trust must be rebuilt. These operate on different timelines.

Forgiveness is not the same as reconciliation. This is perhaps the most important distinction, and we will explore it more fully on Day 25. Forgiveness is something you do in your own heart. Reconciliation requires two people — the one who was wronged and the one who caused the wrong — and it depends on the other person's repentance and change of behavior.

Forgiveness is not primarily a feeling. It is an act of the will — a choice to release a claim. Feelings follow, sometimes slowly.

Forgiveness is not a single event you complete and finish. Luke 17:4 — "seven times a day" — suggests it is a repeated practice, not a one-time transaction.

For reflection

Which of these misconceptions have you believed? How has it shaped how you've handled a specific situation?
