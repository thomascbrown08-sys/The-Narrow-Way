Day 10

The Light of the Face of God

Opening Prayer

O God, who spoke light into existence with a word — speak again. Into the darkness of our familiarity, our distraction, our small and cluttered vision of your Son. You have not hidden yourself. The glory of God has a face. Give us eyes today not merely to acknowledge this but to see it, and in seeing it, to be changed by what we behold. Amen.

"For God, who said, 'Let light shine out of darkness,' has shone in our hearts to give the light of the knowledge of the glory of God in the face of Jesus Christ." 2 Corinthians 4:6, ESV

Paul is not a writer who wastes words. Every phrase in this sentence is load-bearing. And the sentence he builds is one of the most quietly explosive in all of his letters — a verse that contains, in compressed form, the entire logic of creation, revelation, and transformation.

He begins with the God of Genesis 1: the one who "said, 'Let light shine out of darkness.'" This is the first creative act — divine speech breaking into the void, ordering what was formless, illuminating what was dark. God did not coax light out of the darkness. He commanded it, and it came. The darkness had no power to resist. In a single sentence, Paul is standing at the beginning of all things.

Then the pivot: this same God has shone in our hearts. The creative act of Genesis 1 and the illuminating act of conversion belong to the same category. The God who spoke photons into existence is the God who speaks understanding into a darkened human heart. Becoming a Christian is not primarily a decision a person makes; it is a light that is given — poured into the heart by the same God who poured light into the cosmos. Paul knows this intimately. He was not reasoning his way toward Jesus on the Damascus road. He was traveling in the opposite direction, with papers authorizing persecution — when light broke through that was not his own, and a voice spoke, and everything changed (Acts 9:3–6). The initiative was entirely God's.

But notice what the light illuminates: not an abstract truth, not a theological system, not a set of religious obligations. The light shines to give "the knowledge of the glory of God in the face of Jesus Christ." The face. The most specific, particular, intimate thing one person can show another. You can describe a person's qualities in abstract terms indefinitely without ever knowing them. But a face — a face is where encounter happens. It is where recognition lives. Paul says the glory of God has been given a face, and that face is the face of Jesus Christ.

We have spent nine days circling this truth from different angles: the Word made flesh, the preexistent Son, the radiance of God's glory, the cosmic firstborn, the Lamb, the beloved at his baptism, the transfigured One blazing on the mountain, the eternal I AM, the one overflowing with grace and truth. Each of those meditations was a facet of the same jewel — a different angle of incidence on the same light. And Paul now gives us the governing principle behind all of it: the reason to keep looking, the reason that sustained attention to the person of Jesus Christ is not merely a devotional habit but a transforming act.

The verse just before this one — 2 Corinthians 3:18 — makes the mechanism explicit: "And we all, with unveiled face, beholding the glory of the Lord, are being transformed into the same image from one degree of glory to another." Beholding Christ is not one spiritual discipline among others that might be swapped for an equivalent. It is the primary instrument the Spirit uses to reshape the human soul. You become, gradually, what you gaze at most. A heart that gazes long and often at wealth begins to take its shape from wealth. A heart that gazes at approval bends toward the contours of what others want. And a heart that gazes at the face of Jesus Christ — really looks, repeatedly, with increasing attention to what is actually there — begins, slowly and imperfectly and by the Spirit's work alone, to reflect something of what it sees.

This is why these ninety days exist. Not to produce information, though understanding deepens. Not to instill discipline, though practice forms character. But because the Puritans were right when they said that the heart cannot be conquered by the law's demands alone — it can only be transformed by a love stronger than what it has been loving. Thomas Chalmers called it "the expulsive power of a new affection": the heart does not empty itself by effort; it empties when it finds something worth more than what it has been holding. And there is nothing — nothing — worth more than the face of Jesus Christ.

Look, then. Keep looking. There is always more to see.

For reflection

  • Paul says God has shone in our hearts — not primarily into our intellects or our behavior, but our hearts. What does this suggest about where genuine transformation begins? And how does the heart's illumination eventually show up in the intellect and the life?
  • Second Corinthians 3:18 says we are "being transformed into the same image" as we behold Christ's glory. How is this different — fundamentally and practically — from trying to change yourself through willpower, resolution, or disciplined effort?
  • You are ten days into this devotional. What — if anything — have you already begun to see more clearly? And what do you hope these remaining eighty days of fixing your gaze on Jesus Christ might do in you?

Going Deeper (optional)

Second Corinthians 4:6 is the theological charter of this entire devotional. Its logic runs as follows: the same creative power that brought order and light to the formless darkness of Genesis 1 is at work in conversion — shining the knowledge of God's glory into hearts that were, by nature, opaque to it. The "light" Paul has in mind is not emotional warmth or subjective inspiration but the specific, objective knowledge of God as he is revealed in Jesus Christ. It is propositional (it has content: the knowledge of the glory of God) and it is personal (that glory has an address: the face of Jesus Christ).

This verse anchors something crucial for the Christian life: beholding Christ is not merely a spiritual discipline among others — it is the primary mechanism by which the Spirit transforms us. The Reformed tradition speaks of "the means of grace" — the Word, prayer, sacraments, the gathered church — as the ordinary channels through which God works his sanctifying power. But underlying all of them is this: the Spirit uses the revelation of Christ to reshape the heart from the inside out. What the law cannot accomplish by command, the sight of Christ accomplishes by attraction. Thomas Chalmers saw this clearly: "the heart vacates its previous occupation" not when it is emptied by force, but when it finds something more glorious than what it has been clinging to. To see Christ — really see him, in his grace and truth and glory and condescension and power and tenderness — is to find the only thing that can finally compete with every lesser love that has held the heart captive. This is what the next eighty days are for.
