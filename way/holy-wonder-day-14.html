Day 14

Lament Is Worship Too

My God, my God, why have you abandoned me? Why are you so far away when I groan for help? Every day I cry out to you, my God, but you do not answer. Every night I lift my voice, but I find no relief. For he has not ignored or belittled the suffering of the needy. He has not turned his back on them, but has listened to their cries for help. Psalm 22:1–2, 24 (NLT)

These are the words Jesus cried from the cross. He borrowed David's lament and made it his own in the hour of greatest darkness — and in doing so, he sanctified something we desperately need to recover: the cry of dereliction as an act of worship.

Lament is not the absence of faith. It is faith speaking its pain. Notice that Psalm 22 begins with "My God" — even in the cry of abandonment, David is still addressing God, still holding on to the relationship even when it feels like it has evaporated. That is worship under extreme conditions.

Much of contemporary Christian culture has lost this register. We push toward resolution too quickly — toward the praise chorus after the hard verse, toward the silver lining, toward "but God." And sometimes those things are exactly right. But sometimes the most faithful thing we can do is stay in the lament a little longer, like Jesus did — not because we've given up, but because we're honest.

The last section of Psalm 22 does arrive at praise. But it earns that praise by going through the darkness honestly. The worship that emerges from genuine lament is not the same as the worship that skips over suffering. It is deeper, more honest, and more costly — which is perhaps why God treasures it.

Put It Into Practice

If you are in a season of pain or confusion, write your lament to God today — not a polished prayer, just honest words. If you're in a season of peace, read Psalm 22 slowly and let it prepare you for the seasons of lament that will come.

For reflection

Have you experienced lament as a form of worship? Or has your picture of worship been mostly positive and celebratory? Why do you think God included so many psalms of lament in Scripture? What does that tell us about what he wants from us? Jesus cried Psalm 22 from the cross. How does that change how you read it — and how you think about your own suffering?
