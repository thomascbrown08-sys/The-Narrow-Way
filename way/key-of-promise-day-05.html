Day 5

The Spirit of Sonship: Abba

So you have not received a spirit that makes you fearful slaves. Instead, you received God's Spirit when he adopted you as his own children. Now we call him, “Abba, Father.” For his Spirit joins with our spirit to affirm that we are God's children.

And because we are his children, God has sent the Spirit of his Son into our hearts, prompting us to call out, “Abba, Father.” Romans 8:15–16; Galatians 4:6 (NLT)

We have heard that God acquits us and that he refuses to count our sins. But a person could believe all that and still feel like a stranger who has been let off — pardoned, perhaps, but standing awkwardly at the door, not really belonging inside. Despair loves that posture. It will concede, on a good day, that you are forgiven, and then add the quiet sting: but you are not really wanted. A servant at best. Tolerated, not loved.

Against that, hear what God has actually done. He did not only drop the charges. He adopted you. He brought you into the family, gave you his own name, and — most tender of all — “sent the Spirit of his Son into our hearts,” the very Spirit who taught Jesus to pray, so that the word now rising in you toward God is the word a child uses: Abba. Not “Master.” Not “Your Honor.” Father.

Sit with how far that goes beyond mere pardon. A judge can acquit a defendant and never see him again. What judge takes the acquitted prisoner home, sets him at the family table, and puts his own Spirit within him so the former criminal calls him Father? This is not the language of the courtroom anymore. It is the language of the home. You have not been merely cleared. You have been brought in.

And notice who does the affirming. “His Spirit joins with our spirit to affirm that we are God's children.” On the days your own spirit cannot affirm it — when you feel least like a child, most like an intruder — there is a second witness, the Spirit of God himself, testifying within you to a sonship you did not feel. Your assurance does not finally rest on your ability to feel like God's child. It rests on the Spirit's witness and the Father's adoption decree.

So when you pray from the dungeon and feel like a servant pleading at the gate, the Spirit within you is forming a different word. Let him form it. The relationship you are praying from is not master-and-slave. It is Father-and-child, and the deed of adoption has already been signed.

Pray the Promise

However much you feel like a servant or a stranger tonight, address God deliberately as your Father. Begin your prayer with the word the Spirit is prompting: “Abba, Father.”

If the word feels presumptuous, that is exactly why to pray it — you are not claiming a status you earned; you are using the name a child of God is given the right to use.

For reflection

Do you relate to God more like a fearful servant or a beloved child? Where does that show up in how you pray — or in why you avoid praying?
