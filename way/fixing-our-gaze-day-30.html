Day 30

He Who Humbled Himself

Opening Prayer

Lord, Paul gives us here, in a handful of verses, the whole arc from the eternal throne to the servant's basin to the criminal's cross — and then back, through the grave, to the name above every name. Let us not read it quickly. Let its movement take us through the same descent and the same exaltation — not merely as theology but as an act of worship. We bow before the one who first bowed for us. Amen.

"Have this mind among yourselves, which is yours in Christ Jesus, who, though he was in the form of God, did not count equality with God a thing to be grasped, but emptied himself, by taking the form of a servant, being born in the likeness of men. And being found in human form, he humbled himself by becoming obedient to the point of death, even death on a cross. Therefore God has highly exalted him and bestowed on him the name that is above every name." Philippians 2:5–9, ESV

Fifteen days ago we entered Section II: The Servant Who Came. We have followed him through the manger and the temple and the wilderness and the crowded roads of Galilee. We have watched him touch the leper, weep at the tomb, eat with the excluded, seek and find the lost. Today, before we turn to his words and then to his final hours, we pause with this ancient poem — this hymn that Paul places inside a letter to a church in conflict, of all things, as if to say: here is what love, in its most essential form, looks like. Now have this mind among yourselves.

The descent in these verses is described in deliberate steps. He was in the form of God — not merely divine-like, not elevated far above the human, but in the very form, the morphē, the essential reality of God. Equality with the Father was not a title he held provisionally or on loan. It was his. And then: he did not count it a thing to be grasped. He could have held it, displayed it, leveraged it. He chose not to. Not because equality with God was not his — but because his love for us was greater than his attachment to the prerogatives of his position.

He emptied himself. The Greek is ekenōsen — he poured himself out. Not of his divine nature, which cannot be emptied — but of the status, the glory, the right to be treated as what he was. He became what he was not: a servant. He was born as what he had always been infinitely above: a human being. And then the descent continues one step further than any of us might have predicted: he humbled himself by becoming obedient to the point of death, even death on a cross. The cross is the bottom of the descent. There was further to fall than the manger. Further still than a homeless itinerant rabbi. The furthest point, the lowest rung of Roman society's cruelty, is where he stopped falling.

And then the turn — sudden, total, cosmic: Therefore God has highly exalted him. The therefore is doing everything. It is because of the descent, not despite it, that the exaltation comes. The cross is not a detour on the road to glory; it is the road. The path down is the path up. This is the shape of the kingdom inverted from every other kingdom in the history of the world: the way to the highest name is through the lowest act, freely chosen, for the sake of those who could not ascend on their own.

Paul does not present this as merely a story to admire. He presents it as a pattern to inhabit: have this mind among yourselves, which is yours in Christ Jesus. The descent of Christ is not only the ground of our salvation; it is the template for our common life. The Philippian church is in conflict — people are competing for position, insisting on their own interests, fracturing the community with self-assertion. And Paul's answer to all of it is not a management technique or a conflict-resolution framework. It is a Person: look at what he did. Look at where he went. Look at what he poured out and what he gave up. Now go and do likewise — not by your own strength but by the mind that is "yours in Christ Jesus," the same disposition that drove his descent, now available to those who are in him.

We are thirty days in. We have seen his glory from eternity, and we have followed him through the dust of Galilee. We have watched him heal and weep and eat and refuse to be what anyone expected. And in all of it, this is the underlying shape: he humbled himself. Everything he did, from the manger to the last supper, was the expression of a love too large to stay at a distance, too committed to stop at inconvenience, too deep to be turned back by rejection. The one who is above all things came below all things — for you.

For reflection

  • Paul presents the Incarnation as the governing pattern for life in community — "have this mind among yourselves." Where in your relationships or community is there a conflict, a competition, or a self-assertion that this pattern most directly addresses? What would it look like to apply it?
  • The descent of Christ is the road to exaltation, not a detour from it. How does this inversion of the world's logic — that the way up is down — challenge the way you think about your own ambitions, status, or need for recognition?
  • You are thirty days into this devotional. Looking back at what you have seen of Christ — from his eternal glory to the dusty roads of his earthly ministry — what one thing has most changed the way you see him? And what do you want to see more clearly in the days ahead?

Going Deeper (optional)

Philippians 2:5–11 is one of the most studied passages in the New Testament and the locus classicus of kenotic Christology. The key exegetical question centers on the phrase "emptied himself" (ekenōsen heauton): of what did the Son empty himself? Two broad positions have been taken in the history of theology. The first (kenotic Christology in the more radical sense) holds that the Son divested himself of divine attributes — omniscience, omnipotence, or omnipresence — in order to become genuinely human. The second (the Chalcedonian and Reformed position) holds that the Son retained all divine attributes in his divine nature, but voluntarily relinquished the full exercise of those attributes and the external manifestation of divine glory, taking instead the form of a servant.

The Reformed tradition has consistently maintained the second position, grounded in Chalcedon's insistence that the two natures remain unimpaired and without mixture. What the Son "empties" is not his divine nature (which is immutable) but the external prerogatives of divine status: he gave up the independent exercise of divine glory, accepted the limitations of human creatureliness, and submitted to the conditions of a human life — including death. This reading preserves both the full divinity of the Son (he did not become less than God) and the genuine humanity of his life (he did not live it above the conditions he accepted). The exaltation in verse 9 then makes full sense: God does not restore something the Son lost; he publicly vindicates and enthrones the one whose humility has been absolute and whose obedience has been perfect. The name above every name is given as the Father's declaration that the Son's descent was the right path — and that the one who went lowest is the one who belongs highest.
