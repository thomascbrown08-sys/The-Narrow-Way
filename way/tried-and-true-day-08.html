Day 8

When Someone Claims a Word from God

And if you say in your heart, 'How may we know the word that the LORD has not spoken?' — when a prophet speaks in the name of the LORD, if the word does not come to pass or come true, that is a word that the LORD has not spoken; the prophet has spoken it presumptuously. Deuteronomy 18:21–22 (ESV)
But even if we or an angel from heaven should preach to you a gospel contrary to the one we preached to you, let him be accursed. As we said before, so now I say again: If anyone is preaching to you a gospel contrary to the one you received, let him be accursed. Galatians 1:8–9 (ESV)

Claims of direct revelation — 'God told me,' 'I had a vision,' 'the Lord showed me' — are not new. They appear throughout church history, and they appear constantly in contemporary Christian life. The question isn't whether such things ever happen. The question is: how do we test them?

Deuteronomy provides the most basic test: does it come true? A prophecy that fails is not from God. This is a stricter standard than we often apply. We frequently give teachers a pass when their confident predictions don't materialize, reasoning that they must have misheard. Deuteronomy doesn't offer that grace.

Paul's standard in Galatians is stricter still: any message — from anyone, including Paul himself, including an angel — that contradicts the gospel already delivered is to be rejected. Note the direction: the revealed gospel evaluates the new claim, not the other way around. The authority of a claimed messenger never overrides the authority of what Scripture has already established.

The appropriate response to a claimed word from God is not automatic acceptance or reflexive dismissal. It is patient, careful examination: Does it accord with Scripture? Does it accord with the gospel? Does it bear good fruit over time? These are the questions we'll develop in the days ahead.

Put It Into Practice

Next time you hear the phrase 'God told me' or 'the Lord showed me' — from a speaker, book, podcast, or conversation — don't dismiss it or accept it automatically. Hold it gently and ask: Does this accord with Scripture? Does it accord with the gospel? What fruit does it bear?

For reflection

How do you currently respond when someone claims a direct word from God? What has shaped that response? What's the difference between being appropriately skeptical about claimed revelation and being closed to the Spirit's work? Why does Paul's standard in Galatians 1 place Scripture above even angelic authority? What is he protecting?
