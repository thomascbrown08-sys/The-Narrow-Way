Day 22

When Truth Stays in the Head

Then he turned to the woman and said to Simon, "Look at this woman kneeling here. When I entered your home, you didn't offer me water to wash the dust from my feet, but she has washed them with her tears and wiped them with her hair. You didn't greet me with a kiss, but from the time I first came in, she has not stopped kissing my feet. You neglected the anointing oil, but she has anointed my feet with rare perfume. I tell you, her many sins have been forgiven — so she has shown great love. But a person who is forgiven little shows only little love." Luke 7:44–47 (NLT)

Simon the Pharisee knew more about God than almost anyone in the room. His theological education was substantial. His religious observance was meticulous. And he sat at the table and watched the sinful woman weep at Jesus' feet with a mild, condescending curiosity.

She knew far less doctrine than Simon. What she knew was that she had been forgiven much. And that knowledge had made its way from her head to her heart, from her heart to her hands and knees and tears. Her worship was embarrassing, costly, and real.

The danger of an exclusively intellectual faith is not that thinking about God is wrong — far from it. The danger is when theological knowledge becomes a substitute for encounter, when we can discuss the holiness of God with precision and be entirely unmoved by it, when we know all the right answers and none of them have touched us.

The cure is not less theology. It is allowing theology to do what it was meant to do: land. To let the truth that God is holy actually make us tremble. To let the truth that we are forgiven actually move us to gratitude. The woman in this story is the model — not because she was ignorant, but because what she knew had become real to her.

Put It Into Practice

Pick one theological truth you know well — the holiness of God, the resurrection, the forgiveness of sins — and sit with it today not as information but as reality. Ask: if this is actually true, what should I feel? What should I do? Let it land.

For reflection

Is there a theological truth you know in your head that has not yet reached your heart? What might be keeping it there? What's the difference between Simon's knowing and the woman's knowing? What did she have that he lacked? How do you guard against knowledge becoming a shield against genuine encounter with God?
