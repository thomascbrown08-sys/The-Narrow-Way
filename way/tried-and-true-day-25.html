Day 25

Spotting It in the Wild

He must hold firm to the trustworthy word as taught, so that he may be able to give instruction in sound doctrine and also to rebuke those who contradict it. Titus 1:9 (ESV)
Keep a close watch on yourself and on the teaching. Persist in this, for by so doing you will save both yourself and your hearers. 1 Timothy 4:16 (ESV)

Recognizing error in real time — in a sermon, a book, a conversation, a social media post — is a learned skill. It develops with practice. Here are three practical diagnostic questions.

First: What's absent? Sound Christian teaching consistently returns to certain themes: sin and its seriousness, the cross and its necessity, repentance, the exclusivity of salvation through Christ, the call to costly obedience. When teaching systematically avoids these — when the gospel is never offensive, never demanding, never exclusive — that pattern of absence is telling.

Second: What's overemphasized? Blessing, self-fulfillment, emotional comfort, personal flourishing, God's affirmation of whatever you already want — any of these, when they crowd out the whole counsel of God, is a sign of distortion.

Third: What does it cost the hearer? Teaching that costs nothing — no repentance, no cross, no death to self — should be examined carefully. The gospel is free, but it is not cheap. Jesus consistently described discipleship in terms of cost. Teaching that never gets to cost should raise questions.

Put It Into Practice

Evaluate one piece of popular Christian content this week — a book, podcast, social media account, or sermon — using today's three questions. What's absent? What's overemphasized? What does it cost the hearer?

For reflection

What's the difference between applying discernment to teaching and being uncharitable? How do you guard against both extremes? Can a teaching be pastorally warm and doctrinally sound at the same time? What does that look like? Who taught you to recognize false teaching? What did they model that you want to carry forward?
