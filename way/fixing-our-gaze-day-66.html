Day 66

The Via Dolorosa

Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, you carried the cross until you could not. Someone else carried it the rest of the way. On the way you turned to weeping women and spoke — not about yourself but about them, about their city, about their children. Even on the road to Golgotha you were attending to others. Let us walk this road with you today, at whatever pace you set. Amen.

"And as they led him away, they seized one Simon of Cyrene, who was coming in from the country, and laid on him the cross, to carry it behind Jesus. And there followed him a great multitude of the people and of women who were mourning and lamenting for him. But turning to them Jesus said, 'Daughters of Jerusalem, do not weep for me, but weep for yourselves and for your children.'" Luke 23:26–28, ESV

The flogging had been severe — Roman floggings typically were. He has been up all night. He has been in the garden in agony. He has been through two trials and back again. His body has been pushed to a limit that is visible to those walking behind him: the soldiers seize Simon of Cyrene as he is coming in from the country, a man with no part in any of this, a bystander caught at the wrong moment on the wrong road, and they lay the cross on him.

Simon carries the cross of Jesus through the streets of Jerusalem. He carries it behind Jesus — opisthen, behind, in the position that Luke elsewhere uses for the disciple's posture toward their teacher: "Whoever does not carry his own cross and come after me cannot be my disciple" (Luke 14:27). Simon did not volunteer for this. He was commandeered. But by the time the Gospels are written, his name is preserved — Mark identifies his sons as Alexander and Rufus (Mark 15:21), names that suggest they were known in the early church (cf. Romans 16:13). The man who was forced to carry the cross apparently encountered what the cross was carrying, and something changed.

The women mourning along the route are not unnamed background figures. Luke, alone among the evangelists, records what Jesus says to them — and it is startling. They are weeping for him, and he tells them to weep for themselves. Not as a rebuke but as a prophecy: the judgment coming on Jerusalem within this generation will be far worse than what they are witnessing now, and they should reserve their grief for that day (Luke 23:29–31).

What strikes us here is the direction of his attention. He is exhausted, beaten, carrying the sentence of death — and his focus is on these women and what is coming for them and their children. He is not self-absorbed in the last hours of his life. He is oriented outward, concerned for others, attending to what is real for the people around him even as his own body fails under the weight of the beam. The cross has not narrowed his vision. It has, if anything, widened it.

The Via Dolorosa — the Way of Sorrow — runs perhaps a third of a mile through the Old City of Jerusalem, from the Antonia Fortress (or the Praetorium) to Golgotha. Today it is a tourist route, marked with stations of the cross, lined with souvenir shops. Pilgrims walk it every year in their thousands. For the one who walked it that Friday morning, it was not a spiritual exercise. It was the final approach. The road that began in Bethlehem, that ran through Nazareth and along the Sea of Galilee and through the villages of Judea, that climbed to Jerusalem on Palm Sunday to the shouts of Hosanna — this road ends at a hill outside the walls, with a cross and nails and a sign that reads, in three languages: King of the Jews.

He is nearly there now. He has been walking toward this his whole life.

For reflection

  • Simon of Cyrene was commandeered — he did not choose this. And yet something happened between picking up the cross and setting it down, and his sons were known in the early church. Have there been tasks or sufferings forced on you that you did not choose, which turned out to be the place where you encountered something real? What do you make of that?
  • Jesus spoke to the weeping women about their future, not his own suffering. On the road to his own death, he was attending to others. When you are in the middle of your own suffering, do you find this kind of outward attention possible? What does it require — and what does his example suggest about its source?
  • He has been walking toward this his whole life. Looking back over sixty-five days of beholding him — the eternal Son, the servant, the teacher, the King — does the cross now feel like an interruption or a completion? What has changed in how you see it?

Going Deeper (optional)

Luke's unique preservation of the encounter with the daughters of Jerusalem (23:27–31) is part of his larger pattern of attending to the women in Jesus' story with particular care. Luke records more accounts of Jesus' engagement with women than any of the other Gospels — the widow of Nain, the sinful woman who anointed his feet, the women who followed and provided for him (8:1–3), Mary and Martha, the bent woman healed on the Sabbath. On the Via Dolorosa, these women are the last people Jesus speaks to before the crucifixion. That he speaks at all — in the condition he is in — is itself significant; that he speaks about their welfare rather than his own suffering is consistent with everything we have seen across sixty-six days.

The prophecy about Jerusalem — "if they do these things when the wood is green, what will happen when it is dry?" (23:31) — points to the destruction of Jerusalem in AD 70, which Jesus had predicted explicitly in Luke 19:41–44 and 21:20–24. The green wood/dry wood imagery is drawn from Ezekiel 20:47 and refers to himself (the living one, the innocent, the one who has not deserved judgment) and to Jerusalem (the dry, the guilty, the one under judgment). If even the innocent one is not spared the fire of Roman judgment, what will happen to the guilty city? The prophecy is fulfilled within a generation. His concern for the women of Jerusalem, expressed on the way to his own death, is the concern of one who loves the city that is about to destroy him — just as he had wept over it on Palm Sunday (Luke 19:41): "Would that you, even you, had known on this day the things that make for peace!"
