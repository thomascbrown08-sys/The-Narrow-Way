Day 72

The Road to Emmaus

Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, you drew near to two walking in the wrong direction, their faces downcast, and you walked with them without telling them who you were. You asked what they were talking about and listened to them describe their lost hope. Then you opened the Scriptures, and their hearts burned — and when you broke the bread their eyes were opened and you were gone. The whole arc of encounter with you is here. Give us burning hearts today. Amen.

"And beginning with Moses and all the Prophets, he interpreted to them in all the Scriptures the things concerning himself… When he was at table with them, he took the bread and blessed and broke it and gave it to them. And their eyes were opened, and they recognized him. And he vanished from their sight. They said to each other, 'Did not our hearts burn within us while he talked to us on the road, while he opened to us the Scriptures?'" Luke 24:27, 30–32, ESV

The two disciples walking to Emmaus are walking away from Jerusalem. This is the first detail to notice. They are not waiting at the empty tomb or gathered with the other disciples in the upper room. They are leaving — seven miles in the direction of their own village, carrying the wreckage of a hope that has not survived the weekend. The women's report about the empty tomb and the angels they heard but did not believe. They are done.

And a stranger falls into step beside them. He asks what they are talking about. They stop, faces downcast. One of them — Cleopas — asks incredulously whether the stranger can possibly be unaware of what has happened in Jerusalem these past days. And the stranger asks: What things?

They tell him everything. The prophet mighty in deed and word. The crucifixion. The hope that he was the one to redeem Israel — the hope now in past tense. The empty tomb, the angels, the women's words that seemed an idle tale. "But him they did not see" (Luke 24:24). They have all the evidence and none of the recognition. The stranger begins to speak, and the voice of the risen Christ opens the whole Bible to them as a story pointing at himself: beginning with Moses and all the Prophets, he interpreted to them in all the Scriptures the things concerning himself.

They do not yet know who he is. But their hearts are burning. This is not manufactured emotion; it is the response of people in whose interior something is being lit that was cold before. The Word of God, opened by the one who is the Word of God, produces in the hearer an unmistakable interior warmth — not always recognized in the moment, but recognizable in retrospect. They will say it when they return to Jerusalem that night: we should have known; our hearts were burning.

At Emmaus, at supper, he takes bread, blesses it, breaks it, and gives it to them. The gestures are unmistakable — the same gestures from the feeding of the five thousand, from the upper room, from every meal at which he presided. And their eyes are opened, and they know him. And he vanishes. The recognition arrives precisely as the presence withdraws — which is its own theology of the resurrection appearances: he does not stay permanently, appearing in the same place to be found repeatedly. He appears, he makes himself known, and he sends them back to bear witness.

They get up that same hour and walk the seven miles back to Jerusalem. The people who were walking away from the city of his death are now running toward it with a burning to share. The risen Christ has this effect: encounter with him produces urgency. You cannot quietly file the experience under "personal spiritual enrichment" and leave it there. Something has been lit, and lit things tend to spread.

The whole devotional is, in a sense, a road to Emmaus. You began walking. Someone fell into step. The Scriptures were opened. Hearts began to burn. The bread will be broken. Eyes will open. And the proper response to all of it is to get up — that same hour — and go tell someone.

For reflection

  • The disciples were walking away from Jerusalem — they had given up, heading home. The risen Christ drew near to people walking in the wrong direction rather than waiting for them to get it together first. Is there a direction in your life that is Emmaus-ward — away from where faith calls you? What would it look like for Christ to draw near to you there?
  • "Did not our hearts burn within us?" — the recognition came in retrospect. They felt the burning before they knew who was speaking. Have you experienced something like this — a moment when the Word of God worked in you before you fully understood what was happening? What was the quality of that burning, and what came from it?
  • They were sent back to Jerusalem — the burning was not for their private comfort but for communal witness. The risen Christ consistently sends those he encounters back to others with what they have seen. Is there someone in your life to whom you have been given something by Christ — a word, a recognition, a peace — that you have not yet brought back to them?

Going Deeper (optional)

The Emmaus road scene is the most complete account of what we might call "christological biblical interpretation" — reading the whole of Scripture as a unified narrative centered on and pointing toward Jesus Christ. "Beginning with Moses and all the Prophets, he interpreted to them in all the Scriptures the things concerning himself" (Luke 24:27) is a programmatic statement about how the Bible should be read: not as a collection of independent religious texts, but as one story moving toward and finding its fulfillment in the person of Jesus. This is what is sometimes called "the christological reading" or "redemptive-historical interpretation," which the Reformed tradition, following Calvin, has maintained as the proper mode of biblical interpretation.

The burning hearts of the disciples are the experiential counterpart of this interpretive method. When the Scriptures are read as a whole, with Christ as their center and goal, something happens to the reader that reading them as merely historical or ethical documents does not produce. This is the testimony that runs from the Emmaus disciples through Augustine's "our heart is restless until it rests in Thee" through the Reformers' insistence on Scripture's self-authenticating power (the testimonium Spiritus Sancti internum) to the present day: the Word read and heard and opened by the Spirit produces in the hearer a recognition that this is the voice of the one who knows them, and a warmth that corresponds to the presence of the living Word behind the written one. The burning is the sign. The recognition at the breaking of the bread is the confirmation. And the road back to Jerusalem is the inevitable consequence.
