Day 25

When Appetite Is Quieted

And when you fast, don't make it obvious, as the hypocrites do, for they try to look miserable and disheveled so people will admire them for their fasting. I tell you the truth, that is the all the reward they will ever get. But when you fast, comb your hair and wash your face. Then no one will notice that you are fasting, except your Father, who knows what you do in private. And your Father, who sees what you do in private, will reward you. Matthew 6:16–18 (NLT)
No, this is the kind of fasting I want: Free those who are wrongly imprisoned; lighten the burden of those who work for you. Let the oppressed go free, and remove the chains that bind people. Isaiah 58:6 (NLT)

Jesus doesn't say if you fast. He says when you fast — treating it as a normal part of the disciple's life, alongside prayer and generosity. Fasting is assumed. The only question is how.

Fasting is the practice of voluntarily setting aside a legitimate appetite — usually food, sometimes other things — in order to create space for God. It is not a hunger strike to get God's attention. It is not a work that earns merit. It is a physical act of telling your body that there is something more important than what it wants right now.

What fasting does, when it is genuine, is surface what has been hidden. When the noise of appetite is quieted, other things become audible. Fasting reveals what we actually depend on — and in the revealing, creates an opportunity for reordering.

Isaiah 58 expands the definition beautifully: the fasting God desires includes loosing the bonds of injustice, sharing bread with the hungry, housing the homeless. Fasting is not just about the body's appetites. It is about all the forms of withholding we practice — from God and from neighbors. True fasting makes us generous, not just hungry.

From Church History — Fasting in the Early Church

The Didache (a 2nd-century Christian document) instructed believers to fast twice a week — on Wednesdays and Fridays. This was not an ascetic excess but a communal practice that kept the body's appetites in their proper place and oriented the community toward dependence on God. The Desert Fathers developed rich teaching on fasting not as self-punishment but as apprenticeship in freedom — learning to want God more than food, more than comfort, more than anything the body insists upon. John Chrysostom wrote that the person who fasts without caring for the poor has not truly fasted at all.

Put It Into Practice

Consider a one-day or one-meal fast this week. As the hunger comes, instead of responding to it, turn it into prayer: "I want you more than this." If fasting from food isn't advisable for health reasons, fast from social media, entertainment, or something else that competes for your attention.

For reflection

What is your relationship with fasting? Have you practiced it, avoided it, or never considered it as a worship practice? What do you think would surface in you if you quieted your appetites for a day? What might God be waiting to say in the space that creates? Isaiah connects fasting to justice and generosity. How do these two things go together in your understanding of the Christian life?
