Day 1

What Is Discernment?

But solid food is for the mature, for those who have their powers of discernment trained by constant practice to distinguish good from evil. Hebrews 5:14 (ESV)
And it is my prayer that your love may abound more and more, with knowledge and all discernment, so that you may approve what is excellent, and so be pure and blameless for the day of Christ. Philippians 1:9–10 (ESV)

The word discernment can sound like a special ability reserved for particularly spiritual people. But the Bible presents it differently — as a trained capacity that grows through practice, available to every believer willing to develop it.

Think of it like the skill a doctor develops over years of seeing patients, or the ear a musician develops through years of practice. They can perceive things others miss — not because of raw talent alone, but because of long, repeated engagement with their subject.

Hebrews 5:14 is striking because it locates discernment in maturity and constant practice, not in a one-time gift. This is encouraging news: discernment can be grown. It is also sobering news: it requires patient effort.

There are three areas where discernment matters most. Doctrinal discernment helps us evaluate what we're taught — whether a teaching is true, distorted, or false. Ethical discernment helps us make wise decisions when the Bible doesn't give us a specific answer. Interior discernment helps us evaluate our own feelings and impressions — understanding what our inner experience is actually telling us. This thirty-day journey will help you grow in all three.

Put It Into Practice

Before you go further today, write down one area of your life where you feel confident in your discernment — and one area where you feel uncertain. Keep that list. We will come back to it.

For reflection

Before reading this, how would you have defined discernment? How has your understanding shifted? Which of the three types of discernment (doctrinal, ethical, interior) feels most challenging to you personally? Hebrews says discernment is trained by constant practice. What would that kind of regular, patient training look like in your daily life?
