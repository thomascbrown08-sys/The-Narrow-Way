The Captivity

Doubting Castle

The dungeon reached by an easy detour — doubt, despair, and the key that was in the pilgrim's pocket all along.

"Why are you cast down, O my soul, and why are you in turmoil within me? Hope in God; for I shall again praise him." Psalm 42:11 (ESV)

Just past Vanity Fair, the road grows rough underfoot, and beside it runs a green meadow — By-Path Meadow — with a stile leading into it and a path inside that seems to run parallel to the true way, only softer and easier. Christian persuades Hopeful that they can rest their tired feet there and rejoin the road further on. They cross the stile. And by nightfall they are lost, caught in a storm, and asleep on the grounds of a man named Giant Despair, who finds them trespassing, drags them off, and locks them deep in the dungeon of Doubting Castle.

It is the most psychologically precise sequence in the whole book, because of how small the first step was. They did not renounce the journey. They took an easier path that ran alongside the right one — and the small, reasonable compromise is exactly what delivered them into the giant's hands. This landmark is about doubt and despair: how a believer ends up in the dungeon, what the darkness does there, and the strange key that was in the pilgrim's own pocket the whole time.

Part OneHow a pilgrim ends up in the dungeon

Notice the mechanism, because despair rarely announces itself. Christian does not decide to abandon the way. He drifts onto a parallel path that looks close enough — and that is how it usually happens. A series of small compromises, each one defensible, none of them a clear renunciation, until one morning the pilgrim wakes up on the giant's grounds and cannot say exactly how he got there. The road to Doubting Castle is paved with reasonable shortcuts.

And Giant Despair's method, once he has them, is pure cruelty of a recognizable kind. He beats them, starves them of light, and — this is the heart of it — he counsels them to give up, even pressing them toward ending their own lives, telling them there is no point in going on because no rescue is coming. His whole power is the lie that the situation is hopeless. He does not argue that the pilgrims were never on the road; he argues that it no longer matters, that they are finished, that the door is locked for good. This is the same voice we have met before — the accuser from the valley, the condemnation of the Slough — wearing its heaviest disguise. Despair is accusation that has stopped shouting and started whispering that it is simply too late.

In plain terms

Spiritual despair usually isn't the result of one big decision. It's the cumulative weight of small drifts, plus a voice that tells you the situation is now hopeless and there's no use trying to get back.

That voice is lying — not about the drift, which may be real, but about the hopelessness. The door of Doubting Castle has never been as locked as the giant says it is.

Part TwoThe key in his own pocket

The pilgrims lie in the dungeon for days, and they pray — which is the one thing the giant cannot prevent — and it is in the middle of praying, near dawn, that Christian remembers something. He has, in his breast pocket, a key called Promise, and he is suddenly sure it will open any lock in Doubting Castle. They try it on the dungeon door, and it gives. The outer door, and the iron gate, and the castle gate — every lock turns to the same key. They walk out free.

It is one of the most quietly devastating images in the book, because the key was in his pocket the entire time. The promises of God — that he will never leave nor forsake, that nothing can separate us from his love, that the one who began a good work will finish it — were the pilgrim's possession all along, even in the dungeon, even when he had forgotten he was carrying them. Despair's deepest lie is that you have nothing; the truth is that the key was always on you. This is also why the road has insisted that assurance rests on God's promise rather than on your feelings: feelings cannot turn the lock, and in the dungeon they will tell you there is no door. The promise turns the lock.

And notice when Christian remembers the key: in the act of prayer. This is what prayer is for in the dark — not a technique that engineers a rescue, but the place where a despairing pilgrim, speaking honestly to the God who hears, is reminded of what he already holds. The old catechism calls prayer an offering up of our desires to God; in the dungeon it becomes the lifeline by which a forgotten promise is brought back to mind. The pilgrims did not argue themselves out of the castle. They prayed, and remembered, and the remembering set them free.

Part ThreeWhen the dungeon is depression

It must be said plainly, because some who read this are in the dungeon now and it is not a metaphor to them: sometimes the darkness of Doubting Castle is not the result of any drift at all, but of depression, or grief, or an illness of the mind that descends through no fault of the sufferer. Bunyan himself knew this darkness intimately; the castle is drawn from the inside. If that is you, hear two things together. The first is that the key is still Promise, not performance — your standing does not depend on climbing out by sheer will, and the God who holds you is not measuring the strength of your grip. The second is that God uses ordinary means, and a pilgrim in this kind of dark is right to reach for them: the companionship of others on the road, the care of those equipped to help the suffering body and mind, the patient return to the promises even when they cannot yet be felt. Hopeful, it is worth remembering, was in the dungeon too — and part of how Christian survived it was that he was not alone.

The key was in his pocket the whole time. Despair's deepest lie is that you have nothing left.

Once out of the castle, the pilgrims hurry back to the true road and set up a warning for those who come after, and the way begins to climb again — this time toward high, clear country, where the air is good and the views are long, and a pilgrim can finally see where he has been going all along.

✦For the Walka reading to carry

If you are in the dungeon right now — if some voice has been telling you it is too late, that you have drifted too far, that there is no use praying because no rescue is coming — then this is the page written for you, and here is the only thing that matters: the key is in your pocket.

Not a key you have to earn. Not a key you have to feel. The promises of God are your possession right now, in the dark, whether or not you can sense their weight: "I will never leave you nor forsake you." "Nothing can separate us from the love of God." The giant says the door is locked forever. The giant is lying. Every lock in that castle turns to the same key, and you are holding it.

And you are not in the cell alone. Hopeful was there too. Reach for the others on the road, and for those who can help — and keep praying, even badly, because praying in the dark is exactly how the pilgrim remembered what he already had.

The Key of Promise

A devotional on prayer and the promises of God — the key that opens the dungeon, turned even in the dark.

Open now

Lord, I have believed the giant. I have let the small drifts pile up and then believed the voice that says it is too late for me. Remind me what I am carrying. Turn the key. And if I cannot feel the promises yet, hold me to them anyway, and send me people to sit with me in the dark until the morning comes. Amen.
