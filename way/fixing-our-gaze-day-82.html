Day 82

To Know Him

Opening Prayer

Lord, Paul wrote this from prison and counted everything else as rubbish compared to knowing you — not knowing about you, but knowing you, the way one person knows another in the intimacy of shared life and shared suffering and shared hope. That is what we have been reaching for across eighty-one days. That is what we are reaching for still. Lead us further in. Amen.

"Indeed, I count everything as loss because of the surpassing worth of knowing Christ Jesus my Lord… that I may know him and the power of his resurrection, and may share his sufferings, becoming like him in his death, that by any means possible I may attain the resurrection from the dead." Philippians 3:8, 10–11, ESV

Paul is writing from prison. He has been beaten, shipwrecked, stoned, left for dead, and imprisoned more than once. He has lost the career, the reputation, the religious standing that once defined him. And he says that all of it — all that was gained and all that was lost — amounts to rubbish compared to the surpassing worth of knowing Christ Jesus his Lord.

The Greek word is skybala — translated "rubbish" in the ESV, "dung" in the KJV, something rather more emphatic in the original. Paul is not being politely modest about his former achievements. He is being specific: measured against knowing Christ, the things he once counted as gain are not neutral givens or respectable second-tier goods. They are worthless. Not because they were inherently worthless, but because they had been in the wrong position — competing with Christ for the throne of his life rather than flowing from a life already organized around him.

And then the most important word in the passage: that I may know him. After thirty years of following Jesus, after Damascus, after planting churches across the Roman world, after the vision of the third heaven (2 Corinthians 12:2), after writing the letters that will be read until the end of history — Paul's goal is still knowledge of Christ. Not a deeper expertise in theology about Christ, though that is a gift. Not more impressive religious credentials connected to Christ's name. Simply: to know him. The verb is gnō — not information-acquisition but personal, intimate, relational knowing. The kind of knowing that grows without ceiling and never reaches completion in this life.

Paul unpacks what this knowing involves, and the list is surprising in its inclusion of suffering: to know the power of his resurrection, and to share his sufferings, becoming like him in his death. The knowing moves through both. The power of the resurrection is not separable from the fellowship of the sufferings — you cannot have one while refusing the other. This is not masochism; Paul is not seeking suffering for its own sake. He is recognizing that the shape of Christ's life — the pattern of death-and-resurrection, of losing-in-order-to-gain, of descent before ascent — is the pattern that the Christian life takes as it conforms to him. To know him fully is to follow him fully, which includes the via dolorosa as well as the empty tomb.

This is the goal of the whole devotional, distilled into one sentence. Not that you would finish the ninety days having learned a great deal about Jesus. Not that you would have a more comprehensive theological understanding of the incarnation, the atonement, and the resurrection. But that you would know him — the way one person knows another, the way the disciples knew him on the road to Emmaus when their hearts burned, the way Mary knew him when he said her name, the way Thomas knew him when he saw the wounds and fell to his knees. That you would, across these ninety days and in everything after them, be drawn further into the knowledge of the one who is worth infinitely more than everything else combined.

Eight days remain. The knowing is not finished. It will not be finished in this life. But the direction is set.

For reflection

  • Paul counted everything as rubbish compared to knowing Christ — not as a renunciation of the world, but as a repositioning of what held first place. What still competes with Christ for first place in your own life — what do you still treat, in practice, as more worth having than the surpassing worth of knowing him?
  • "That I may know him" — the goal of someone who already knows him deeply, written after decades of following. What does it mean that knowing Christ has no arrival point in this life — that Paul after thirty years is still reaching for what he had not yet fully grasped? How does this shape the way you approach your own growth?
  • Knowing him includes sharing his sufferings and becoming like him in his death. Is there a suffering in your present life that you have not yet received as a form of participation in Christ — a place where the pattern of dying-to-live might be at work? What would it mean to receive it as such rather than simply endure it?

Going Deeper (optional)

Philippians 3:10 — "that I may know him and the power of his resurrection, and may share his sufferings, becoming like him in his death" — presents the Christian life as a participation in the paschal mystery of Christ: his death and resurrection become, by union with him, the pattern that the believer's life inhabits. This is not metaphorical. Paul describes it elsewhere as being "crucified with Christ" (Galatians 2:20), "buried with him" and "raised with him" (Romans 6:3–4; Colossians 2:12), and "suffering with him" in order to be "glorified with him" (Romans 8:17). The death-and-resurrection pattern is not a one-time event at conversion; it is the ongoing shape of the Christian life — the continual experience of dying to self and rising in Christ that characterizes every genuine act of repentance, every surrender of a competing love, every willingness to be last rather than first.

The Reformed tradition has sometimes been accused of being overly forensic in its soteriology — too focused on the legal transaction of justification and insufficiently attentive to the participatory and transformative dimensions of salvation. Passages like Philippians 3:10 are the corrective: Paul does not merely want to be declared righteous in Christ (though he wants that urgently — 3:9). He wants to know Christ, to share his sufferings, to be conformed to his death, to attain the resurrection. Justification is the legal ground; union and participation are the experiential reality. The two are inseparable. The one who is declared righteous in Christ is also, in being declared righteous, joined to Christ — and joined to him, they begin the lifelong process of being conformed to him from the inside out.
