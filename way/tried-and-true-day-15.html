Day 15

Motives All the Way Down

All the ways of a man are pure in his own eyes, but the LORD weighs the spirit. Proverbs 16:2 (ESV)
If I speak in the tongues of men and of angels, but have not love, I am a noisy gong or a clanging cymbal. And if I have prophetic powers, and understand all mysteries and all knowledge, and if I have all faith, so as to remove mountains, but have not love, I am nothing. If I give away all I have, and if I deliver up my body to be burned, but have not love, I gain nothing. 1 Corinthians 13:1–3 (ESV)

You can do the right thing for the wrong reason. This is among the most uncomfortable truths ethical discernment requires us to face, because we are very skilled at providing ourselves with noble-sounding motives for whatever we've already decided to do.

Proverbs 16:2 is blunt: all the ways of a man are pure in his own eyes. We almost always believe our own motives are good. The uncomfortable implication is that our self-assessment is systematically unreliable. What we tell ourselves about why we do things is often a story constructed after the fact.

Paul's great love passage isn't primarily about romantic relationships. It's a devastating critique of religious performance without love. You can give everything you own to the poor. You can perform miracles. You can master theological knowledge. And if it isn't rooted in love for God and people, it counts for nothing. The action and the motive are both part of the moral reality.

This doesn't mean we can never act until our motives are perfectly pure — they never will be in this life. It means we hold our actions and motives under regular examination, asking honestly: what am I actually seeking here? Approval? Control? Safety? The discipline of honest motive-examination is part of what it means to have a formed conscience.

Put It Into Practice

Think of a recent decision or action you're proud of. Spend two minutes being genuinely honest about the motive. Don't settle for the first answer — go one level deeper. What were you actually seeking?

For reflection

What virtuous-looking behavior might be serving a less virtuous motive in your life right now? How do you distinguish between healthy self-examination and unhealthy self-obsession when examining your motives? Paul says love must be the root of all spiritual action. What does it look like to act from love rather than from duty, fear, or desire for approval?
