The Escalation Trap

The Escalation Trap

A preference climbs the ladder until it feels like a salvation issue.

The tactic. A disagreement about a preference quietly becomes a disagreement about salvation — and no one can step back without feeling they are betraying the truth itself.

How it looksThe shape of the move

It starts with something genuinely real: a change to the order of service, a decision the elders made, a speaker someone invited. Positions harden. And then, by a series of small moves that each felt entirely reasonable, a difference of preference begins to climb a ladder — up to conviction, up to principle, up to essential doctrine, until someone says, ‘if this goes through, I don’t know what this church even believes anymore.’ Nobody planned the ascent. Each rung was small enough to feel justified at the time.

The virtue it twistsWhy good people fall for it

The virtue it exploits is seriousness — and it is a real virtue. A Christian who takes God’s Word seriously does not want to be the person who shrugged at something that mattered. So the moment a dispute gets framed as a question of faithfulness, the serious believer digs in, because backing down would feel like backing away from God. The stakes ratchet upward and no one ever names the move that is happening.

A scene — how it might run

Picture a worship-music change. It begins as taste — I find the new songs thin. A week later I’ve decided they’re theologically shallow. A week after that I’m wondering aloud whether the leadership still takes doctrine seriously at all. By the time I say it out loud, I genuinely believe the gospel itself is at stake — and I could not now retreat to ‘it’s just not my taste’ without feeling I’d abandoned something sacred. I climbed four rungs in three weeks and felt righteous at every step.

What it costsThe damage it does

The cost is that a Level 1 question gets handled with Level 5 gravity. A matter of preference is now freighted with the weight of heresy, and no one can step back down the ladder without feeling they are abandoning the truth itself. Relationships fracture over what began as a difference of taste.

Disarming itThe peacemaker’s move, up close

Name the rung, gently and out loud. The move that stops the climb is to ask, without accusation, what level the disagreement actually lives on: ‘Can I ask — is this a salvation issue, something that changes whether we’re preaching the same gospel? Or is it a faithfulness issue, where I can imagine serious Christians landing differently? I only ask because the conversation should go differently depending on the answer.’

Then hold the distinction kindly. If it really is first-order, treat it with full gravity — but say why it is. If it is secondary, you are not saying it doesn’t matter; you are saying it doesn’t carry salvation on its back, and so it can be discussed without the room catching fire. Watch for it in yourself first: the tell is the speed of your own ascent — if you went from ‘I’d prefer’ to ‘this is a betrayal of the gospel’ in a fortnight, the ladder is probably yours, not the issue’s. Ask whether you could still call the person on the other side a faithful brother. If the honest answer is yes, the matter is lower on the ladder than your feelings have placed it.

📖 The Scripture behind this Entailedwhat’s this?›

The claim: Not every disagreement carries equal weight; Scripture distinguishes the truths that define the gospel from disputable matters on which serious believers may differ — so escalating a secondary issue to a salvation issue misnames it.

  • Romans 14:1, 5 (ESV) — “As for the one who is weak in faith, welcome him, but not to quarrel over opinions … Each one should be fully convinced in his own mind.”Paul names a class of disputable matters where believers may differ and still receive one another.
  • 1 Corinthians 15:3 (ESV) — “For I delivered to you as of first importance … that Christ died for our sins … was raised.”Some things are ‘of first importance’ — which implies others are not of that rank.
  • Titus 3:9 (ESV) — “But avoid foolish controversies … for they are unprofitable and worthless.”Not every dispute is worth the weight of a salvation question.
The reasoning (entailed — good and necessary consequence)Marked entailed because the principle — that issues come in different ranks, and a preference must not be dressed as a gospel matter — is drawn from comparing texts rather than stated in one. Paul explicitly names ‘disputable matters’ (Romans 14) over which believers differ while welcoming one another; he marks certain things ‘of first importance’ (1 Corinthians 15:3), implying a second rank; and he tells Titus to sidestep worthless controversies. Together these warrant the disarming move directly: naming the rung is simply asking which biblical category a dispute belongs to before assigning it the gravity of the highest one.

The existence of ranked matters is good and necessary consequence from these texts; sorting a particular dispute is the discernment the tactic calls for.
