Day 85

Beholding as in a Mirror

Opening Prayer

Lord, the text that made this devotional possible stands near its end. We are being transformed into your image from one degree of glory to another as we behold your glory. This is the mechanism. This is why these eighty-five days have not been wasted. Whatever beholding we have done has already done something in us — and the beholding is not finished. It will never be finished on this side of eternity. Let us keep our eyes fixed. Amen.

"And we all, with unveiled face, beholding the glory of the Lord, are being transformed into the same image from one degree of glory to another. For this comes from the Lord who is the Spirit." 2 Corinthians 3:18, ESV

On Day 10 we paused at 2 Corinthians 4:6 — the light of the knowledge of the glory of God in the face of Jesus Christ — and called it the theological charter of this devotional. This verse, one chapter earlier, is its mechanism. The how behind the what. Not merely that the glory is available to be seen, but that the seeing itself is the instrument of transformation.

With unveiled face. Paul has just been reflecting on Moses, who put a veil over his face after descending from Sinai so that the Israelites would not see the glory fading (2 Corinthians 3:13). The veil concealed the transience of the old covenant's reflected glory. But in Christ, the veil is removed — not because the glory is hidden or fading, but because it is permanent and full, and there is no need to protect the viewer from the sight of its ending. We come to the face of Christ unveiled — with nothing between us and the sight, no protective screen, no intermediary veil. The access is direct because the atonement is complete and the Spirit has opened what was closed.

Beholding the glory of the Lord. The word — katoptrizomenoi — carries the sense of gazing into a reflective surface, seeing something as in a mirror. The image is of beholding that is sustained, attentive, returning. Not a glance. Not information-gathering. The kind of looking that stays with what it is looking at until what it is looking at begins to shape what is looking.

Are being transformed into the same image. Present passive participle — happening now, continuously, through the looking. The transformation is not something you produce by effort. It is something that happens to you as you look. The mirror reflects something back into you that was not there before. The Spirit — who is the Lord, who provides the beholding — works through the act of gazing to produce in the gazer something of what is gazed at. You become, gradually and imperfectly and by grace alone, something of what you have been looking at.

From one degree of glory to another. The transformation is incremental, cumulative, progressive. No single day of beholding produces the full result. But no day of beholding is wasted. Every day in which you look at Christ with an unveiled face — every time you come to Scripture with genuine openness, every time you pray with honest attention, every time you sit with these meditations and actually receive what is being shown — something happens that would not have happened without that looking. Glory produces glory: the glory of the indwelling Spirit, working through the beholding, producing in the believer more and more of the image of the one beheld.

For this comes from the Lord who is the Spirit. The transformation is entirely his work. You do not transform yourself by looking hard enough. The Spirit transforms you as you look — the looking is the condition, but the producing is his. This is the liberation from every form of spiritual striving that treats the Christian life as a project of self-improvement: the improvement is not yours to produce. It is the Spirit's work, through the means of beholding, by grace, from one degree of glory to the next. Your part is to keep looking. His part is everything else.

Eighty-five days of looking. Five more to go. And then the rest of your life.

For reflection

  • "Are being transformed" — present tense, ongoing, through the beholding. Do you believe that looking at Christ is actually doing something in you — that the beholding is not merely informational but transformational? What evidence, however partial and imperfect, have you seen in yourself across these eighty-five days?
  • "From one degree of glory to another" — incremental, cumulative, never complete this side of eternity. How does this patience built into the mechanism of transformation challenge the ways you tend to measure spiritual progress? What would it mean to trust the process rather than demanding visible results?
  • "For this comes from the Lord who is the Spirit." The transformation is his work through your looking. What is the difference between this and what you may have previously understood the Christian life to require of you? And what does releasing the production to him feel like — relief, or unsettling, or both?

Going Deeper (optional)

Second Corinthians 3:18 is the biblical anchor for the classic Reformed conviction that sanctification is primarily a work of the Spirit through the Word — that the means of grace (Scripture, prayer, sacraments, gathered worship) are not merely religious habits that maintain our connection to the community, but the Spirit's chosen instruments for producing Christlikeness in the believer. The Westminster Shorter Catechism asks: "What are the outward means whereby Christ communicateth to us the benefits of redemption?" and answers: "The outward and ordinary means whereby Christ communicateth to us the benefits of redemption are, his ordinances, especially the word, sacraments, and prayer; all which are made effectual to the elect for salvation." The effectuality is not in the means themselves but in the Spirit who uses them.

Thomas Chalmers' insight — the expulsive power of a new affection — is the experiential expression of what 2 Corinthians 3:18 describes mechanically. The heart is not changed by effort; it is changed by a vision that captivates it more completely than what previously held it. Beholding the glory of Christ with an unveiled face is the instrument through which the Spirit produces in the believer a love that increasingly displaces the lesser loves — not by suppressing them through willpower, but by presenting something incomparably better that makes the giving up seem obvious rather than painful. The merchant in the parable sells everything gladly because he has seen the pearl. The mechanism of that gladness is here: from one degree of glory to another, as the Spirit opens the eyes to see more and more of what the pearl actually is.
