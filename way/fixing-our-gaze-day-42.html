Day 42

Unless You Become Like a Child

Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, the disciples had been arguing about who was greatest in the kingdom. Your answer was to call a child into the middle of the circle. Not as a lesson in sentimentality, but as a lesson in standing: the greatest in the kingdom is the one who has descended to the position of least social leverage. Give us the grace to understand what you mean by this, and the courage to want it. Amen.

"Truly, I say to you, unless you turn and become like children, you will never enter the kingdom of heaven. Whoever humbles himself like this child is the greatest in the kingdom of heaven." Matthew 18:3–4, ESV

The disciples have been walking with Jesus long enough to begin jockeying. Their question — "Who is the greatest in the kingdom of heaven?" — is the question of men who have decided the kingdom is coming and have begun positioning themselves within it. It is the same impulse that will send James and John's mother to request the best seats (Matthew 20:20), the same impulse that will produce the argument at the Last Supper about who among them should be considered the greatest (Luke 22:24). They cannot stop reaching for the top rung.

Jesus calls a child into the center of the circle and sets the child before them. In first-century culture, children occupied the lowest rung of the social hierarchy. They had no status, no rights, no leverage. They could not contribute to the household economy in any meaningful way; they were entirely dependent on the provision and goodwill of adults. A child in the ancient world was, by definition, a dependent — someone whose whole existence rested on the willingness of others to sustain it. To become like a child was not, in this context, a flattering metaphor. It was a description of social descension.

Jesus says: unless you turn and become like children, you will never enter the kingdom of heaven. The word "turn" — strephō — carries the sense of a direction change, a reorientation, what the Greek New Testament elsewhere calls metanoia. This is not a mild adjustment of attitude. It is a turn. The disciples have been heading in one direction — up, toward greatness, toward recognized status — and Jesus is asking them to turn and head the other way.

But what precisely is childlike about children that the kingdom requires? Jesus specifies in the next verse: "Whoever humbles himself like this child." It is not innocence (children are not morally innocent), nor naivety (children are not theologically naive in some blessed way), nor intellectual simplicity (the kingdom requires the whole mind). It is humility — and humility in the specific form of the child's structural dependence. The child has nothing to leverage. The child can only receive. The child does not negotiate their standing; they accept the position of one who is entirely held by another.

This is the same posture as the poor in spirit (Day 32) and the tax collector at the back of the temple (Day 41). The kingdom has a consistent entry requirement: you must come as one who has no standing of your own and no currency with which to purchase it. The child does not resent this condition — the child does not yet know enough to resent it, which is what makes the child's posture the right model. The problem with adults is not that they have become more capable; it is that their capability has made them less willing to be held. The kingdom asks for the willingness back.

John Calvin noted that this passage is among the most searching Jesus ever spoke on the question of human pride — because the disciples who heard it were not bad people by any ordinary measure. They were genuinely devoted, genuinely self-sacrificing, following a leader who was heading toward danger. And still the upward reach was there. The longing for recognition does not wait for a comfortable life in which to flourish; it grows just as readily in the middle of genuine sacrifice. The person who has given much may be more tempted to measure greatness by what they have given than the person who has given little. Unless you turn applies to the devoted as much as the complacent.

The greatest in the kingdom, Jesus says, is the one who has descended most willingly — who has humbled themselves like the child, who has stopped reaching and started receiving. This is both a description of the kingdom's inversion of worldly hierarchy and, more profoundly, a description of Christ himself: the one who did not count equality with God a thing to be grasped, but emptied himself, taking the form of a servant (Philippians 2:6–7). He became the child. He is the model as well as the teacher.

For reflection

  • The child's defining characteristic in this passage is structural dependence — the inability to leverage one's own standing. Where in your relationship with God are you most tempted to leverage something — your history, your obedience, your sacrifice, your theological knowledge — rather than simply coming as a dependent child?
  • Jesus tells the disciples to "turn" — a direction change, not a mild correction. Is there a direction in your life that currently runs toward recognition, comparison, or self-established greatness? What would turning look like in that specific context?
  • The greatest in the kingdom is the one who has humbled themselves most willingly. Does this description of greatness sound appealing to you — or does it sound like a loss? What does your honest answer reveal about where your desires currently point?

Going Deeper (optional)

The call to become "like children" has sometimes been misread as a commendation of intellectual simplicity or theological minimalism — as if the kingdom rewards those who think least carefully about it. This reading is mistaken. Paul explicitly says "do not be children in your thinking; be infants in evil, but in your thinking be mature" (1 Corinthians 14:20). The intellectual life is not the target of Jesus' instruction here. What is targeted is the structural posture of dependence — the willingness to receive rather than achieve one's standing.

The Reformed tradition distinguishes between faith as intellectual assent (notitia — knowledge of the content), personal trust (fiducia — trust in the person), and the affective response (assensus — heartfelt agreement). The childlike faith Jesus calls for is primarily a matter of fiducia — it is the total trust of one who has no recourse but the trustworthiness of the one being trusted. This kind of faith is not less intelligent than other kinds; it may in fact be the most intelligent response available, once the situation is clearly seen. The child who trusts their parent's hand at the edge of the cliff is not failing to think; they are thinking clearly about the options and choosing the right one. Childlike faith is not the absence of reason. It is reason applied to the recognition of one's own limits — and the discovery that the one who holds the limits is utterly reliable.
