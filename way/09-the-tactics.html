The Tactics of Division

The Adversary's Nine Tactics

Nine recognizable ways the enemy fractures a church from within — the virtue each one twists, and the peacemaker's move against it.

"Eager to maintain the unity of the Spirit in the bond of peace." Ephesians 4:3 (ESV)

Down in the valley we said that the adversary's most effective work in the church almost never looks like a dragon in the road — it looks like a misunderstanding that festers, a reasonable concern that keeps escalating, two people who love Scripture each concluding the other cannot really love God. The night before the Cross, Jesus prayed that his people would be one, so that the watching world would believe; division is the precise undoing of that prayer, and so it is the strategist's favorite work. This page lays out nine of his most reliable plays.

It is the relational companion to Hill Error. There the counterfeits were of doctrine — truths pulled out of shape; here they are of fellowship — bonds pulled apart. And notice the family resemblance in how they operate: nearly every one of these tactics works not by tempting us toward an obvious vice but by exploiting a genuine virtue — seriousness, conviction, love of the truth, the longing for purity. That is exactly what makes them so hard to see from the inside. The defense, then, is never less of the virtue. It is discernment to see the move, and the peacemaker's posture to refuse it. For each, we name how it looks, the good thing it twists, what it costs, and the move that disarms it.

One word before the list, because it can be misused as easily as Hill Error's labels: naming a tactic is not a weapon to win an argument. It is a way to see clearly enough to stop one. The point of all of it is the thing Jesus prayed for — that we would be one.

Tactic 1The Escalation Trap

A disagreement about a preference quietly becomes a disagreement about salvation.

It starts with something real — a change to the service, a decision by the elders, a speaker someone invited. Positions harden. And by a series of small moves that each felt reasonable, a difference of preference climbs a ladder: to conviction, to principle, to essential doctrine, to “if this goes through I don't know what this church even believes.” Nobody planned it. Each step up was small enough to feel justified.

The virtue it exploits is seriousness. A Christian who takes God's Word seriously does not want to be the one who shrugged at something that mattered — so when a dispute is framed as a faithfulness question, the serious believer digs in, and the stakes ratchet without anyone naming the move. The cost is that a Level 1 question gets handled with Level 5 gravity, and no one can step back without feeling they are abandoning the truth itself.

The peacemaker's moveName the level out loud, gently: is this a salvation issue — something that changes whether we're preaching the same gospel — or a faithfulness issue where you can imagine serious Christians landing differently? I ask because the conversation should go differently depending on the answer. Naming the rung stops the climb.

Tactic 2The Vocabulary Trap

Two people use the same word to mean different things, and each concludes the other is dangerously wrong.

Two believers discuss grace, or authority, or accountability. Both are serious, both think they are faithful — and the more they talk, the more each suspects the other holds a dangerous position. Neither notices they are using one word in two different theological dialects. When someone formed in a Reformed world says grace, they mean sovereign, unmerited favor that precedes every human response; when someone formed in a Wesleyan world says grace, they also mean something precise, but not the same thing. A conversation that never establishes which definition is running is almost guaranteed to misdiagnose a different doctrine as a deficient one.

It is cruelest with those whose vocabulary was formed in harm. To someone wounded in a high-control church, words like submission or authority do not land as neutral terms — they arrive carrying memory. The cost is rupture between people who may not actually disagree, and fresh injury to the already-wounded.

The peacemaker's moveSlow down and define before you debate: when you say that word, what do you mean by it — here, in this conversation, not in general? And if a word seems to land harder than the moment warrants, ask gently whether there is history with the term, and listen before advancing a single step.

Tactic 3The Wounded Proxy War

A real theological argument is carrying a wound it was never built to carry.

The argument is genuine — church discipline, elder authority, how the church handled an abuse — and the person can cite Scripture for it. But something is off: the intensity runs higher than the question alone explains, and no amount of careful theological engagement reaches a resting point, because the person is not trying to win the argument so much as trying to be heard. Often this is a wound speaking through the only language the church made available for it. The position is shaped by the wound, but it is not therefore wrong; the intensity is exactly proportionate to the question plus the unprocessed pain beneath it.

The adversary's move is to keep both dimensions from ever being addressed at once — so the argument proceeds while the wound goes unseen, and the person never receives what they actually need: to be believed about what happened to them. This is the same care the Slough asked for, surfacing again inside a disagreement.

The peacemaker's moveSafety before tactics — and never lead with “this is really about your wound.” Validate the substance first: what you're raising is a real question, and it deserves a real answer. Then make room for the person beneath it to be heard. The wound has to be seen before the argument can rest.

Tactic 4The Uniformity Substitution

The body's God-given diversity is replaced with a demand for sameness.

Someone is simply different — a different conclusion on a secondary question, a different style of faith, a concern about a practice everyone takes for granted. The difference unsettles, not because it is heresy but because it is unfamiliar, and the community applies quiet pressure toward conformity: fewer invitations, gentle questions about their commitment, the feedback that they “don't quite fit.” When they conform, the community relaxes and calls it unity.

But Paul's picture of the body in 1 Corinthians 12 does not merely tolerate diversity; it insists on it — if the whole body were an eye, where would the hearing be? The cost of the substitution is a body that amputates the very parts it needs, mistaking sameness for health and losing the gifts only a different member could bring.

The peacemaker's moveDistinguish the unity Scripture actually asks for. Paul's unity in Philippians 2 is shared humility and a common orientation toward Christ — not agreement on every secondary matter. Ask plainly: does this community have room for someone who lands differently here? Because I think we lose something real if the answer is no.

Tactic 5The Certainty Weapon

Honest inquiry is shut down by treating uncertainty as spiritual weakness.

Someone asks a genuine question — not an accusation, just honest uncertainty — and the response does not engage it. It signals, without quite saying so, that a faithful person would not be asking. The questioner is reminded what the church believes; the tone implies that doubt is a defect; the conversation moves on as if the question never happened, leaving them to conclude their uncertainty is a spiritual problem rather than a theological prompt.

The virtue here is real: conviction is a true Christian good, and the church's refusal of relativism is not a mistake. The weaponization begins when certainty stops being the fruit of deep engagement and becomes a social signal — a badge of loyalty — so that questions read as disloyalty. The cost is a community that produces performers who have learned the safe answers, not disciples who actually believe.

The peacemaker's moveDistinguish honest inquiry from rejection, and reopen the door: I don't think they're suggesting we abandon the gospel — I think they're genuinely asking how this works. Can we engage the question on its merits? Paul says we know in part; uncertainty about a secondary matter is not the same as denying a central truth.

Tactic 6The Tradition Trap

Institutional habit is treated as if it carried the authority of Scripture.

Someone proposes a reasonable change, with a case from Scripture or pastoral wisdom. The reply is not a counter-argument from Scripture but from history: we have always done it this way. The weight being appealed to is not a biblical command but accumulated habit — the gravity of the past functioning as a theological argument against the present.

This is not inherently wrong; traditions exist for reasons, and wisdom honors what has been learned. The trap is the failure to distinguish what history requires from what Scripture requires. It is exactly the move Jesus confronted in Mark 7: he did not deny the tradition of handwashing existed, he denied its authority — “you leave the commandment of God and hold to the tradition of men.” The cost is that a human habit, granted divine weight, ends up silencing an actual good.

The peacemaker's moveSeparate the two kinds of weight, without embarrassment: can we locate this in Scripture specifically — is it a biblical requirement, or a practice that has served us well? Both deserve respect, but different kinds of respect. Then ask what the tradition was for, and whether the proposal might serve the same goal.

Tactic 7The Social Hostage

Belonging is made conditional on conformity, and the threat is loneliness.

There is no explicit argument and no accusation. The person simply began asking questions, or landed differently on a secondary matter — and what followed was a change in temperature. A little less warmth, a few fewer invitations, a subtle shift in how their comments are received. Nothing you could point to. Just a growing sense that belonging now has conditions it did not have before, and that the conditions are tied to what they believe.

The mechanism is the quiet attachment of belonging to agreement — not on the gospel, but on a widening list of positions the community treats as defining. The result is a closed system: you may ask questions in principle, but the social cost of the wrong answer is high enough that honest inquiry becomes dangerous. The cost is a community of performers who have learned which answers preserve their place — and, for the one frozen out, the specific cruelty of being threatened not with punishment but with isolation.

The peacemaker's moveIf it is being done to you, name it to yourself first and clearly: my belonging here has become conditional on my conclusions. Then test, gently, whether this community can hear that and change — some can. You are allowed to test it before despairing of it, and allowed to grieve and leave if the closed system proves load-bearing to who they are.

Tactic 8The Celebrity Fracture

A teacher's name becomes the thing people fight about, instead of the truth itself.

Two people are discussing theology, and one says a teacher's name — and the room shifts. Suddenly the question is not whether a position is true but whether the teacher who holds it is trustworthy, on the right side, one of us. The other answers with their own teacher's name, and the argument becomes a contest of tribes conducted by proxy. They are arguing about Paul and Apollos, and neither of them knows it.

Paul named this in Corinth with startling directness: “each of you says, ‘I follow Paul,' or ‘I follow Apollos' … Is Christ divided?” The tribalism has quietly moved the identity-forming role from Christ to the teacher, so that a challenge to the teacher now feels like an attack on the self. And the modern amplification is severe: where Corinth had a handful of teachers who had visited in person, we have an unlimited supply of podcasts and channels, each able to form a tribe. The cost is a faith anchored to a personality rather than to the Lord.

The peacemaker's moveSet the name aside and return to the substance: let's look at the argument itself — what exactly is being claimed, and what is the scriptural case? If the position stands on its own, it doesn't need the teacher's authority; and if it doesn't, the teacher's name shouldn't save it.

Tactic 9The Purity Spiral

A good standard ratchets ever stricter, until the original becomes the mark of the unfaithful.

It begins with something admirable: a community decides to take a real standard seriously, and in the first generation holding it costs something. Then the center of gravity starts to move. Someone states the standard more strictly and signals greater seriousness by it; others, not wanting to seem less committed, move stricter still. A generation on, positions the founders would have called extreme are the assumed baseline, and the original — itself reasonable — has become the badge of the insufficiently devoted. To question the current standard is no longer to debate a position; it is to question the community's very identity as serious people.

The impulse it exploits is genuinely admirable — the desire to honor God completely, without half-measures. The flaw is not the desire but the mechanism: in a group, ever-greater strictness becomes the way to signal seriousness, and a community that has defined itself by the movement toward strictness has no brake. The cost is a slow narrowing that consumes its own, always hunting the next compromise to purge.

The peacemaker's moveAsk the origin question as genuine inquiry, not challenge: can we locate this in Scripture specifically — is the stricter position drawn from the text, or from our developing sense of what seriousness looks like? And when the spiral is accelerating, name the dynamic without naming the people driving it.

The deeper patternThe unity that disarms all nine

Step back and the nine share a single seam. Every one of them attaches something — being right, belonging, seriousness, loyalty — to a place it does not belong, and then uses that misattachment to pull people apart. And every peacemaker's move, underneath its particular wording, does the same one thing: it gently puts the attached thing back where it belongs, and returns the room to the only center that can actually hold a community together.

That center is not agreement on every secondary question, and it is not the strength of anyone's tribe. It is Christ, and the gospel that made enemies into a family. The unity that defeats division is not uniformity — the body was made diverse on purpose — but a shared and humble orientation toward the One who prayed we would be one. The peacemaker is not the person who avoids every hard conversation; he is the person who keeps drawing the room back to that center when the adversary is straining to pull it apart. Blessed are the peacemakers, Jesus said, for they shall be called sons of God. It is no small calling. In a valley full of an enemy's schemes, it is frontline work.

Every tactic attaches something to where it doesn't belong. Every peacemaker's move puts it back.

Christian fought his way through the Valley of Humiliation and bound up his wounds, and the road went on — down, into a second and darker valley, where the trouble would no longer be an enemy to fight but a darkness to walk through.
