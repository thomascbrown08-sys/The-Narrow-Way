The Tactics of Division

The Adversary's Nine Tactics

Nine recognizable ways the enemy fractures a church from within — the virtue each one twists, and the peacemaker's move against it.

"Eager to maintain the unity of the Spirit in the bond of peace." Ephesians 4:3 (ESV)

Down in the valley we said that the adversary's most effective work in the church almost never looks like a dragon in the road — it looks like a misunderstanding that festers, a reasonable concern that keeps escalating, two people who love Scripture each concluding the other cannot really love God. The night before the Cross, Jesus prayed that his people would be one, so that the watching world would believe; division is the precise undoing of that prayer, and so it is the strategist's favorite work. This page lays out nine of his most reliable plays.

It is the relational companion to Hill Error. There the counterfeits were of doctrine — truths pulled out of shape; here they are of fellowship — bonds pulled apart. And notice the family resemblance in how they operate: nearly every one of these tactics works not by tempting us toward an obvious vice but by exploiting a genuine virtue — seriousness, conviction, love of the truth, the longing for purity. That is exactly what makes them so hard to see from the inside. The defense, then, is never less of the virtue. It is discernment to see the move, and the peacemaker's posture to refuse it. For each, we name how it looks, the good thing it twists, what it costs, and the move that disarms it.

One word before the list, because it can be misused as easily as Hill Error's labels: naming a tactic is not a weapon to win an argument. It is a way to see clearly enough to stop one. The point of all of it is the thing Jesus prayed for — that we would be one.

📖 The Scripture behind this Directwhat’s this?›

The claim: The unity of Christ’s people is something he himself prayed for, so that the world would believe — which is why division is the precise undoing of his prayer and the enemy’s favorite work.

  • John 17:20–21 (ESV) — “that they may all be one … so that the world may believe that you have sent me.”Jesus prays for his people’s oneness, tied directly to the world’s believing.
  • Ephesians 4:3 (ESV) — “eager to maintain the unity of the Spirit in the bond of peace.”Unity is a thing to be actively guarded — implying it is under threat.
  • 1 Corinthians 1:10 (ESV) — “that there be no divisions among you, but that you be united in the same mind…”Division is explicitly named as the thing to be refused in the church.
The reasoning (direct)Marked direct because the page’s foundation is Jesus’ own stated prayer. In John 17:20–21 he asks ‘that they may all be one…so that the world may believe’ — binding the church’s unity to its witness in plain terms. Ephesians 4:3 commands believers to maintain that unity ‘in the bond of peace,’ and 1 Corinthians 1:10 forbids divisions outright. Together they establish exactly what the page claims: since oneness is what Christ prayed for, division is the precise undoing of that prayer — which is why the adversary works at it, and why naming a tactic is meant to stop a fight, never to win one. The aim of the whole page is the thing Jesus prayed: that we would be one.

Christ's prayer for unity and the call to guard it are stated directly; the page's nine tactics are practical discernment in service of that stated aim.

The deeper patternThe unity that disarms all nine

Step back and the nine share a single seam. Every one of them attaches something — being right, belonging, seriousness, loyalty — to a place it does not belong, and then uses that misattachment to pull people apart. And every peacemaker's move, underneath its particular wording, does the same one thing: it gently puts the attached thing back where it belongs, and returns the room to the only center that can actually hold a community together.

That center is not agreement on every secondary question, and it is not the strength of anyone's tribe. It is Christ, and the gospel that made enemies into a family. The unity that defeats division is not uniformity — the body was made diverse on purpose — but a shared and humble orientation toward the One who prayed we would be one. The peacemaker is not the person who avoids every hard conversation; he is the person who keeps drawing the room back to that center when the adversary is straining to pull it apart. Blessed are the peacemakers, Jesus said, for they shall be called sons of God. It is no small calling. In a valley full of an enemy's schemes, it is frontline work.

Every tactic attaches something to where it doesn't belong. Every peacemaker's move puts it back.

Christian fought his way through the Valley of Humiliation and bound up his wounds, and the road went on — down, into a second and darker valley, where the trouble would no longer be an enemy to fight but a darkness to walk through.
