Open Hands

Open Hands

A 30-day devotional companion on stewardship — money, relationships, and forgiveness — and the open-handed posture beneath all three.

"We buy the truth," they said — and bought nothing else the Fair was selling. in the manner of Bunyan, after Proverbs 23:23

In the middle of Vanity Fair, where every stall cries out for the pilgrim to buy, to keep, to grasp, Christian and Faithful did a strange and provocative thing: they were not interested. Asked what they would buy, they answered that they would buy the truth — and bought nothing else the Fair was selling. This devotional lives in that refusal. It is a thirty-day study in stewardship, and its whole argument can be held in the difference between two gestures: the clenched fist that grasps, and the open hand that holds lightly what it knows it does not finally own.

The landmark above told us why a pilgrim can walk the Fair with open hands — because everything is a trust, and we are managers, not owners. This study is the slow, practical working-out of that posture across the three areas where it is hardest to keep: how we handle money, how we navigate relationships, and how we extend and receive forgiveness. It is not a teaching essay; it is a companion for the road, meant to be taken one short day at a time.

In plain terms

Open Hands is a 30-day devotional about money, relationships, and forgiveness — and the single posture underneath all three: holding what God gives you as a manager holds the owner's goods, not as a miser holds his own.

The days are short and the Scripture is central. It is built to be read slowly, ideally with another person, a day at a time.

The shape of the thirty daysOne posture, three proving-grounds

The journey opens by laying the foundation — that everything belongs to God, that we manage what is his, and that where our treasure is, our heart follows. From there it moves into money: the goodness of work, the peculiar danger of loving money, the slavery of debt, the learned skill of contentment, and generosity as the natural exhale of a grateful heart. Then into relationships: the rare worth of true friendship, what love actually is as opposed to what the Fair sells under that name, character over chemistry, and the patient work of becoming rather than merely waiting. And finally into forgiveness — perhaps the hardest open hand of all — distinguishing what forgiveness is not from what it is, and learning to release a debt the way one who has been forgiven everything learns to release a little.

Notice the thread: each section is the same open hand, tested in a different grip. The miser's clenched fist and the open palm are not two topics but one posture, learned in three classrooms.

How to use itSlowly, and not alone if you can help it

This study was made for the way it is best used: read aloud and unhurried, one short reading a day, with the Scripture given the central place and the commentary kept to a servant's role — there only when a word is unfamiliar or a context would help. It works beautifully in a quiet morning by oneself, and even better between two people, where one reads and both talk it over. The aim is not to finish thirty days but to loosen, slowly, the grip of a heart that was made to hold the world lightly and its God tightly — and so often gets it exactly backwards.

The clenched fist and the open hand are not two topics but one posture — learned in the three classrooms of money, love, and forgiveness.

The pilgrims passed through Vanity Fair without being bought by it, and the road carried them on. This companion is here so that you can learn to do the same — to walk through a world of stalls with your hands, and your heart, held open.
