Day 13

The School of the Psalms

Shout with joy to the LORD, all the earth! Worship the LORD with gladness. Come before him, singing with joy. Acknowledge that the LORD is God! He made us, and we are his. We are his people, the sheep of his pasture. Enter his gates with thanksgiving; go into his courts with praise. Give thanks to him and praise his name. For the LORD is good. His unfailing love continues forever, and his faithfulness continues to each generation. Psalm 100:1–5 (NLT)
O LORD, how long will you forget me? Forever? How long will you look the other way? How long must I struggle with anguish in my soul, with sorrow in my heart every day? Psalm 13:1–2 (NLT)

The book of Psalms is the Bible's own hymnal and prayer book — and it contains something that should surprise us more than it does: everything. Joy and rage. Confidence and confusion. Gratitude and despair. The full range of human experience before God, unedited.

Psalm 100 is pure praise — shout with joy, come singing, give thanks. It is the kind of worship we are comfortable with and aspire to. Psalm 13 opens with a question that sounds almost like an accusation: how long will you forget me? And both of these are in the Bible. Both are modeled as legitimate.

What this tells us about worship is profound: God does not require us to pretend. The Psalms are full of people bringing their actual emotional reality into God's presence — not sanitizing it first, not waiting until they feel better, not performing a state they haven't reached. They bring what is true, and they bring it to the right address.

The Psalms are a school in this sense: they teach us that worship is not a feeling we produce, but a conversation we enter. The Psalmists taught us how to enter it honestly — with joy when there is joy, with lament when there is lament, with confusion when we are confused — and always, eventually, with our faces turned toward the God who hears.

From Church History — The Psalms in Early Christian Worship

The earliest Christian communities inherited the Psalms from their Jewish roots and used them as their primary worship resource. Justin Martyr (2nd century) describes Sunday gatherings that included Scripture reading, teaching, prayer, and the Lord's Supper — with the Psalms woven throughout. Athanasius wrote that the Psalms have a unique place in Scripture because they do not merely tell us what to think about God but teach us how to speak to him in every condition of the soul. The Reformers, especially Calvin, prioritized the sung Psalms as the central act of corporate worship, calling them "the anatomy of all the parts of the soul."

Put It Into Practice

Open the Psalms to wherever your actual emotional state is today. If you're at peace, read Psalm 23 or 100. If you're struggling, read Psalm 13 or 22. If you're confused, read Psalm 73. Let the Psalm teach you how to say what is true.

For reflection

What emotions do you tend to leave at the door when you come to worship — privately or corporately? What would it mean to bring them in instead? The Psalms model lament as a form of worship. What would it change if you believed that bringing your grief or confusion to God counted as worship? Which Psalm has most formed your spiritual life? Why that one?
