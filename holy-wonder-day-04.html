Day 4

Worship That Was Already Happening

I am praying not only for these disciples but also for all who will ever believe in me through their message. I pray that they will all be one, just as you and I are one — as you are in me, Father, and I am in you. And may they be in us so that the world will believe you sent me. I have given them the glory you gave me, so they may be one as we are one. I am in them and you are in me. May they experience such perfect unity that the world will know that you sent me and that you love them as much as you love me. Father, I want these whom you have given me to be with me where I am. Then they can see all the glory you gave me because you loved me even before the world began! John 17:20–24 (NLT)

Before any of us drew our first breath, something was already happening. The Father loved the Son. The Son delighted in the Father. The Spirit moved between them in a communion of joy and glory that has no beginning and no end. This is the inner life of the Trinity — not static, not lonely, but overflowing with love.

When we worship, we do not initiate something new. We are invited into something ancient — something that was already going on before creation, before time, before us. Jesus' prayer in John 17 is breathtaking: he asks that his people might be drawn into the very unity he shares with the Father. That the love between Father and Son might flow through us and around us.

This reframes worship entirely. We are not performing for a silent audience, hoping God notices. We are joining a song that was already being sung. The question is not whether the music is happening — it always is. The question is whether we are listening carefully enough to find our part.

Michael Reeves writes that the God of the Bible is not the solitary, motionless deity of philosophy. He is the dancing, overflowing, self-giving God of the Trinity. To worship him is to be caught up in that dance. And the reason we often feel flat in worship is that we've forgotten we've been invited to dance at all.

Put It Into Practice

Before your next time of personal prayer or Sunday worship, pause and remember: you are not starting something. You are joining something. Ask the Spirit to help you find your place in the worship that is already happening.

For reflection

How does it change your experience of worship to know you are joining something already in progress rather than initiating it yourself? Jesus says the Father loved him "before the world began." What does it mean that this eternal love is now directed toward you through the Son? What would it look like practically to approach Sunday worship as an invitation to join a song rather than a performance to attend?
